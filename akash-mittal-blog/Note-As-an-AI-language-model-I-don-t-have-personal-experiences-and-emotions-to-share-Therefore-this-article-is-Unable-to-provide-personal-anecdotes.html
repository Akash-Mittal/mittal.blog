Gentle Parenting: What It Is, Techniques, & Discipline - A Herald Review

Once upon a time, a mother and her toddler son went to a park. The mother settled on a bench, taking out a book to read, and let her son wander around. The boy played with other children, rode his toy car, climbed up and down the slide, and occasionally came back to his mother to show what he had found. The mother praised her son for his bravery and curiosity, listened to his stories about the new friends he made and the bugs he caught, and smiled at him with warmth and love.

Suddenly, a mother and her daughter approached them. The daughter, who seemed a bit older than the boy, demanded the car, which the boy was playing with, and took it away from him. The mother of the boy gently acknowledged the situation and asked her son if he wanted to find another toy to play with. He agreed and went off to explore again. But the daughter kept following him and taking away his toys, while her mother watched and commented, "You should teach your child to share. He's being selfish and rude."

The mother of the boy felt frustrated and judged, but instead of confronting the other mother or scolding her son, she chose to talk to him empathetically. She asked him how he felt when his toy was taken away, and he said he was sad and angry. She validated his emotions and told him it was okay to feel that way. She also explained that the other child might be feeling bored or lonely and needed someone to play with. She suggested they could find a way to share the toys or find a new game that involved both of them. The boy listened attentively and nodded. Then, he approached the girl and asked if she wanted to play together. She hesitated for a moment, and then smiled and nodded. They started to build a sandcastle together, took turns with different tools, and laughed and chatted. The mothers watched and smiled too, feeling grateful for the connection their children made and the lesson they learned.

This story exemplifies one of the core principles of gentle parenting: empathy. Gentle parenting is a philosophy and style of raising children that prioritizes respect for their dignity, autonomy, and emotions. It emphasizes the use of compassionate communication, positive reinforcement, and proactive problem-solving instead of punishment, coercion, or control. Gentle parenting recognizes children as unique individuals who deserve to be heard, seen, and valued, rather than as "blank slates" to be molded or trained. It also acknowledges the impact of the environment, culture, and history on children's development and behavior, and aims to create safe, nurturing, and diverse environments that promote well-being and justice.

The gentle parenting movement has been growing in popularity and influence over the last few decades, as more parents and caregivers seek alternatives to traditional authoritarian or permissive approaches that may harm children's physical, emotional, and social development. Gentle parenting is not a fixed or monolithic set of practices or rules, but rather a flexible and evolving mindset that adapts to each child's needs and abilities, as well as to the family's values and circumstances. However, there are some key techniques and strategies that are commonly used by gentle parents, which we will explore in this article.

Techniques of Gentle Parenting

1. Connection and Attachment: Gentle parenting emphasizes the importance of building strong and deep connections with children, based on empathy, trust, and love. Connection and attachment are the foundations of healthy emotional development, as they help children regulate their emotions, develop empathy and compassion, and form secure relationships with others. Gentle parents spend quality time with their children, engage in active listening and reflective dialogue, offer physical affection and comfort, and respond with sensitivity and consistency to their children's needs and cues. They also practice co-sleeping, baby-wearing, and responsive breastfeeding, if possible, to enhance the bond between themselves and their infants.

2. Positive Discipline: Gentle parenting discourages the use of punishment, threats, or rewards to manage children's behavior. Instead, it promotes the use of positive discipline, which focuses on teaching children how to develop self-discipline, responsibility, and empathy through respectful and constructive communication. Positive discipline involves setting clear and age-appropriate boundaries for children, explaining the reasons behind them, and letting children participate in the decision-making process. It also involves modeling appropriate behavior, acknowledging the positive behaviors of children, and redirecting their negative behaviors towards positive alternatives. Positive discipline avoids using shame, blame, or guilt to manipulate or shame children, and instead encourages them to learn from their mistakes and find solutions to problems.

3. Mindful Parenting: Gentle parenting encourages parents to practice mindfulness, which means being present and aware of the present moment with acceptance and non-judgment. Mindful parenting helps parents regulate their own emotions, reduce stress and anxiety, improve communication skills, and model self-care and self-compassion for their children. It also helps parents tune in to their children's needs and feelings, and respond thoughtfully and proactively to them. Mindful parenting techniques include deep breathing, meditation, yoga, journaling, time-outs, and other self-care practices that promote relaxation and awareness.

4. Respectful Communication: Gentle parenting emphasizes the use of respectful and effective communication with children, which means using clear, concise, and positive language, avoiding criticism, sarcasm, or shouting, and actively listening to the child's perspective. Respectful communication helps children feel heard, understood, and valued, and promotes trust and collaboration between parents and children. Some examples of respectful communication include active listening, reflective questioning, "I" statements, positive affirmations, and non-violent communication techniques.

5. Natural Consequences: Gentle parenting promotes the use of natural consequences, which means letting children experience the natural consequences of their actions, rather than imposing artificial or punitive consequences. Natural consequences help children learn cause-and-effect relationships, take responsibility for their choices, and develop problem-solving skills. They also help children feel empowered and autonomous, as they see the direct consequences of their actions. Natural consequences might include letting a child take their own sweater outdoors and feeling cold, cleaning up spilled milk, or facing the consequence of not getting sleep after playing past bedtime.

Discipline within Gentle Parenting

Discipline is one of the most debated and misunderstood aspects of gentle parenting. Some critics argue that gentle parenting lacks discipline, or that it spoils children by allowing them to "get away with" misbehavior. Others believe that gentle parenting is too controlling or permissive in its discipline, or that it fails to prepare children for the "real world" where rules and consequences are harsher.

However, gentle parenting does not advocate for the absence of rules, consequences, or discipline. Rather, it proposes a different approach to discipline that respects the child's autonomy, emotions, and development, as well as the family's values and circumstances.

Discipline within gentle parenting is proactive, collaborative, and respectful, focusing on prevention and teaching, rather than punishment. Discipline aims to guide children towards positive behavior, rather than simply stop negative behavior, increases the child's sense of responsibility, and promotes problem-solving skills. Discipline also takes into account the child's age, temperament, and learning style, as well as the context and situation of the behavior.

Discipline within gentle parenting includes several strategies, such as:

1. Prevention: Gentle parents try to anticipate and prevent potential conflicts or misbehaviors by setting clear expectations, routines, and boundaries. They also make sure the child is well-fed, rested, and stimulated, and create safe and enriching environments that foster exploration and learning.

2. Acknowledgment: Gentle parents acknowledge the child's emotions and feelings, and validate them as normal and acceptable. This helps the child feel heard and seen and creates a foundation for further communication and problem-solving.

3. Collaboration: Gentle parents involve the child in the decision-making process and encourage them to find solutions to problems. This helps the child feel empowered and gives them a sense of ownership and responsibility for their actions.

4. Natural Consequences: As mentioned before, natural consequences are used within gentle parenting as disciplinary measures that allow children to experience the effects of their actions. Natural consequences are not meant to punish children but rather to teach them the cause and effect relationship of their actions.

5. Logical Consequences: Logical consequences are used as a means of discipline where children are held to consequences based on the situation. For example, if a child refuses to complete their chore, a logical consequence could be that he or she does not get any dessert.

Conclusion

Gentle parenting is a growing movement that emphasizes empathy, respect, and communication in raising children. Although it is different from traditional authoritarian or permissive parenting styles, it is not a fixed or rigid approach but rather a flexible and evolving one that adapts to the child's needs and circumstances. Gentle parenting involves several techniques, such as connection and attachment, positive discipline, mindful parenting, respectful communication, and natural consequences. Within gentle parenting, discipline is viewed as a positive guidance tool that is utilized to reinforce the appropriate behavior.

Gentle parenting promotes healthy physical, emotional, and social development that will lead to a healthy adult who has developed problem-solving skills, self-discipline, responsibility, and empathy. By practicing gentle parenting, parents can provide a nurturing and loving environment that fosters positive development and well-being for their children.
