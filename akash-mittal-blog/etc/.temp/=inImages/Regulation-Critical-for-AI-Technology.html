Regulation Critical for AI Technology: The Importance of Governance

By Sarah Davis

An Interesting Story

In 2015, a revolution was brewing in the world of artificial intelligence. DeepMind, a London=based AI research company, had developed a program that was able to master the game of Go = considered one of the most complex board games in the world, with more possible game configurations than there are atoms in the universe.

The victory of the AI program over the reigning human world champion was a landmark moment in the development of AI technology. However, it also left many experts concerned about the potential for AI to outstrip human decision=making capabilities in a wider range of fields, with potentially catastrophic results.

While AI undoubtedly offers a wealth of opportunities for the future, the threat of these potential dangers has led to increasing calls for regulation of AI technology.

Quantifiable Examples

One major area of concern with unregulated AI technology is bias. An AI algorithm is only as good as the data it is trained on, which means that if the data is biased, then the algorithm's decisions will also be biased.

One study found that an AI program used by US courts to predict the likelihood of defendants committing another crime had significant racial bias = the program was much more likely to label black defendants as higher risk than white defendants with similar criminal histories.

Another example is in finance = an AI program designed to make investments for clients would be biased if it was trained on data that disproportionately represented the interests of wealthy investors, as opposed to the general population.

Without regulation, it is likely that these biases would be perpetuated and would become increasingly difficult to correct.

Another concern with unregulated AI is the potential for catastrophes caused by the technology. Self=driving cars are a prime example of this = without reliable and consistent regulation, accidents caused by malfunctioning AI systems could endanger human lives.

In a major accident involving a self=driving Uber vehicle in 2018, a pedestrian was killed after the car failed to detect her presence on the road. The accident was caused by a combination of programming errors and lack of safety regulations.

It is vital that AI systems are put through rigorous testing and regulation to ensure that they are safe and reliable.

The Importance of Governance

It is clear that in order for AI technology to develop responsibly, regulation is necessary. But how can this regulation be implemented in a way that balances innovation with safety and ethics?

Firstly, it is important that governments and regulatory bodies work together to create a set of standards and guidelines for AI technology. These guidelines should cover areas such as data privacy, bias mitigation, accountability and transparency.

Secondly, testing and auditing procedures should be put in place to ensure that AI systems meet these guidelines before they are deployed. This will not only improve the safety and reliability of the technology, but will also help to build trust with the public.

Thirdly, there should be a focus on education and public engagement to increase awareness of the potential risks of unregulated AI technology, and to encourage a culture of responsible AI development and usage.

In conclusion, the development of AI technology offers incredible opportunities for the future, but it is important that we proceed with caution. Regulation is vital to ensure that the technology is developed in a responsible, safe and ethical way, and that the potential risks are mitigated.
