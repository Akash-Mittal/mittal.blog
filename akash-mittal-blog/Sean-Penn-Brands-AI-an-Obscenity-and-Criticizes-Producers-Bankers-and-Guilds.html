Sean Penn Brands AI an Obscenity and Criticizes Producers, Bankers, and Guilds

Sean Penn, the Hollywood actor and filmmaker, has recently made headlines with his outspoken criticism of artificial intelligence. Penn has gone on record to say that AI is an "obscenity" and that it is responsible for the "dehumanization" of society. He has also taken aim at producers, bankers, and guilds, accusing them of putting profit over people and contributing to the cultural decline. In this article, we will take a closer look at Penn's comments, examine his arguments, and explore the implications of his views.

An Interesting Story

In an interview with the French newspaper Le Figaro, Penn spoke passionately about his hatred of AI. He described it as a "cancer" that was rapidly spreading through society, and warned that it posed a threat to human values and emotions. Penn argued that AI was robbing people of their jobs, their dignity, and their sense of purpose. He also criticized the way it was being used to manipulate and control people's behavior, citing the example of social media algorithms that "are designed to keep you addicted and get you to spend more time scrolling through mindless content."

Quantifiable Examples

Penn's criticisms of AI are not without merit. There is ample evidence to suggest that AI is having a profound impact on society, and that some of its effects are not necessarily positive. For example, a recent report by the McKinsey Global Institute estimates that up to 375 million workers, or 14% of the global workforce, could be displaced by automation by 2030. This could lead to significant social and economic upheaval, as millions of people struggle to find new jobs or retrain for different careers.

Another worrying trend is the rise of AI-powered "deepfake" videos, which use computer algorithms to create convincing images and voices of people that never existed. These videos can be used to spread fake news, defame politicians, or manipulate public opinion. In one famous example, a deepfake video of former US President Barack Obama was created by a team at the University of Washington, sparking concerns about the potential misuse of the technology.
