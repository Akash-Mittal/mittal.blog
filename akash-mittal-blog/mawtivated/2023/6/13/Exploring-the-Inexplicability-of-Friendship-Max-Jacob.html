Friendship is a beautiful thing. It's what makes life worth living, what gives us strength and support in times of need, and what makes our happiest moments even sweeter. Max Jacob, a French poet, painter, and writer, once said, "Friendship is inexplicable, it should not be explained if one doesn't want to kill it." In this article, we'll explore the meaning behind this quote and try to understand why friendship is so hard to explain.

Max Jacob's quote is an acknowledgement that friendship is something special. It's an intangible thing that can't be measured or quantified, and yet we all know it when we feel it. Trying to explain friendship can be like trying to explain love - it's difficult to put into words, and doing so can actually take away from the experience.

Instead, let's try to illustrate the concept of friendship through some inspiring stories and personal anecdotes.

An Inspiring Story of Friendship

There are countless examples of inspiring friendships throughout history, but one that immediately comes to mind is that of Ruth and Naomi from the Bible. Ruth, a Moabite woman, married Naomi's son and moved to Israel with her new family. But when Naomi's husband and sons died, she decided to return to her homeland, and encouraged her daughters-in-law to stay behind and start new lives. Ruth refused, saying, "Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God." (Ruth 1:16)

Ruth's loyalty and devotion to Naomi is a beautiful example of the depth of friendship that can exist between two people. Even when it would have been easier to stay behind and start a new life, Ruth chose to stick by Naomi's side no matter what.

We all have our own stories of friendship, and I'm no exception. One that sticks out in my mind involves my best friend from high school. We were inseparable, spending lunch breaks, weekends, and after-school hours together. But when it came time to go to college, we ended up choosing schools on opposite sides of the country.

Despite the distance, we stayed in touch, calling each other regularly and writing letters (this was before the days of texting and social media). We made a pact to get together every year, no matter what, and over the past decade we've kept that promise. We've traveled to each other's cities, explored new places together, and supported each other through job changes, breakups, and other life events.

This friendship may not be as dramatic as Ruth and Naomi's, but it's no less valuable to me. It's a reminder that distance doesn't have to mean the end of a friendship, and that the bonds we form with others can endure through many years and life changes.

Why Is Friendship Inexplicable?

So why is friendship so hard to explain? One reason is that it's different for everyone. The qualities that make for a good friend - loyalty, honesty, empathy, humor, and so on - might be more or less important depending on our individual personalities and life experiences. Someone who values intellectual conversation might prioritize friends who challenge them intellectually, while someone who's going through a tough time might value friends who are good listeners and can offer emotional support.

In addition, friendship is often based on subtle cues and unspoken understandings. We might have a shared sense of humor, or know that we can rely on a friend for help even if they don't explicitly say so. The nuances of human relationships can be difficult to put into words, and trying to do so might miss the point entirely.

How to Appreciate the Inexplicability of Friendship

If you're someone who's been struggling to explain friendship to others - or even to yourself - it's important to remember that the beauty of friendship is in the experience itself. Rather than trying to analyze or quantify it, focus on the ways in which your friendships make you feel. Appreciate the moments of laughter, the shoulder to cry on, and the celebrations shared with those who love you.

In addition, it's important to value the diversity of friendships in your life. Recognize that different friends might meet different needs, and appreciate each for their own unique qualities. As with any relationship, it's also important to communicate openly and honestly with your friends, and to be willing to put in the effort to maintain your relationships over time.

Conclusion

In conclusion, Max Jacob's quote "Friendship is inexplicable, it should not be explained if one doesn't want to kill it" speaks to the intangible nature of the bonds we form with our friends. Rather than trying to quantify or explain these relationships, it's important to appreciate the experiences they bring to our lives, and to value the diversity and uniqueness of each friendship. By doing so, we can cultivate deeper, more meaningful relationships that bring us joy and support throughout our lives.
