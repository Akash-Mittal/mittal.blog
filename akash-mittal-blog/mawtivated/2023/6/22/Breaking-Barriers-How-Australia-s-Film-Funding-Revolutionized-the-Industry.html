#Breaking Barriers: How Australia's Film Funding Revolutionized the Industry



In 1980, Australian filmmaker Bruce Beresford found himself in a difficult position. Despite the critical success of his films like "Don's Party" and "The Club," he was struggling to secure funding for new projects. It was then that he learned of a special fund set up by the Australian government to support the development of local films. This fund, known as the Australian Film Development Corporation (AFDC), proved to be a game changer for the Australian film industry.

The AFDC was created in 1970 as part of a larger push to promote Australian culture and identity. At the time, Australian films were largely ignored by international audiences and investors, and there was little infrastructure in place to support local production. The AFDC changed all that by providing funding, advice, and resources to local filmmakers, helping to kickstart a new era of Australian cinema.

One of the key innovations of the AFDC was its willingness to fund films that might not otherwise have been made. This included projects from first-time directors, documentaries, and films with challenging subjects or experimental forms. The funding was not limited to commercial considerations, but also included creative and cultural goals. This approach led to the development of a unique and diverse Australian cinema, which tackled a wide range of topics and perspectives.

Over the years, the AFDC evolved into the Australian Film Commission (AFC) and then Screen Australia, but its legacy lives on. Today, the Australian film industry is a vibrant and thriving community, producing films that are celebrated around the world. From classic films like "Picnic at Hanging Rock" and "Gallipoli" to modern hits like "The Babadook" and "Lion," Australian cinema has firmly established itself on the global stage.

But what can other countries learn from Australia's experience? Here are three key takeaways:

1. Support and Fund Diverse Voices

One of the most important lessons from the Australian example is the value of supporting diverse voices and perspectives. By funding films that were unlikely to find support elsewhere, the AFDC helped to nurture a new generation of talent that reflected the country's unique culture and identity. This approach is just as relevant today, and can help to foster a rich and varied media landscape that serves all audiences.

2. Create Fair and Transparent Funding Systems

Another crucial element of the Australian system was its transparency and fairness. Filmmakers were not judged solely on their commercial prospects or connections, but on the merit of their ideas and storytelling abilities. This meant that even first-time directors or outsiders had a chance to secure funding and make their voices heard. By creating equitable and transparent funding systems, other countries can ensure that all voices are heard and that the most innovative and important projects are supported.

3. Embrace Creative Risk and Innovation

The final lesson from Australia's success is the importance of embracing creative risk and innovation. The AFDC was not afraid to fund films that were unusual or challenging, and this led to some of the most enduring and influential works in Australian cinema. By supporting creative risk and innovation, other countries can help cultivate a truly dynamic and forward-thinking media industry.

In conclusion, the Australian film funding model has lessons for all countries that seek to develop a thriving creative sector. By supporting diverse voices, creating transparent funding systems, and embracing risk and innovation, filmmakers and audiences can benefit and grow together.
