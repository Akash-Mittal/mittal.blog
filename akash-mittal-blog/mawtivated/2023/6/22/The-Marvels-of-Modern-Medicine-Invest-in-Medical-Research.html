How Medical Research Saved My Life



My name is Ike Skelton, and I am a medical research survivor. At the age of 45, I was diagnosed with stage 4 lymphoma, and my world came crashing down. My doctor told me that my chances of survival were slim, and that we needed to find a miracle cure.

After months of grueling chemotherapy and radiation treatments, I wasn't responding to the traditional therapies. But thanks to the dedication and perseverance of medical researchers, they discovered a breakthrough treatment that saved my life. Their tireless efforts to unlock the secrets of human diseases gave me a second chance at life, and I will be forever grateful.

Modern medical advances have helped millions of people live longer, healthier lives. We owe these improvements to decades of investment in medical research. Without the sustained commitment of governments, universities, and philanthropic organizations, the breakthroughs that have saved countless lives would never have been possible.

The impact of medical research can be seen in countless ways. In the past century, we have seen the development of life-saving vaccines, the eradication of deadly diseases like polio, and the discovery of groundbreaking treatments for cancer and other ailments. With continued funding and research in areas like genetics, stem cells, and personalized medicine, we have the potential to unlock even more miracles.

  • According to the CDC, the measles vaccine has prevented an estimated 2.3 million deaths since it was introduced in 1963.
  • The American Cancer Society reports that the five-year survival rate for all types of cancer has increased from 50% in the 1970s to nearly 70% today.
  • The development of combination antiretroviral therapy (ART) has dramatically increased the life expectancy of HIV-positive individuals. In 2013, an estimated 1.1 million people died from HIV-related causes, compared to 1.7 million in 2004.

Medical research is the foundation upon which modern medicine is built. Without it, we would have no hope for the future. As former U.S. Secretary of Health and Human Services, Kathleen Sebelius, once said:

"Investing in medical research is important for patients, for our economy, and for the future health of our nation."

If you want to help support medical research and advance the fight against disease, here are a few ways you can get involved:

  • Donate to reputable medical research organizations that are making a difference in the world.
  • Participate in clinical trials if you are an eligible candidate. Your contribution can help researchers discover groundbreaking new treatments.
  • Stay informed about medical breakthroughs and advancements by reading news articles and attending public lectures.
