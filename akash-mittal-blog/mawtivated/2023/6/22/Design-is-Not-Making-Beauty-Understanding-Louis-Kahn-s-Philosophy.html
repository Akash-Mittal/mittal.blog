Design is Not Making Beauty: Understanding Louis Kahn's Philosophy



Once upon a time, there was a renowned architect named Louis Kahn who believed that design is not making beauty. Kahn believed that beauty emerges from selection, affinities, integration, and love. In other words, a beautiful design is not created from scratch, but it emerges from the process of natural selection and the love for the project.

The Story of Louis Kahn

Louis Kahn was born in 1901 in Estonia, and his family moved to the United States when he was a child. His architecture career took off after he became a professor at Yale University, and he later founded his own firm in 1935. Kahn's work is characterized by the use of materials such as brick and concrete, and he was known for his focus on natural light and geometry.

Design is Not Making Beauty

Kahn's statement that "design is not making beauty" is an important reminder for anyone in the creative field. It's not about creating something that looks beautiful, but it's about the process that leads to that beauty.

  • Selection: Selecting the right materials, colors, and textures is crucial in the design process. It's like a puzzle, where each piece needs to fit perfectly with the others.
  • Affinities: The elements of a design need to have affinities with one another. They need to complement each other and work together to create a cohesive whole.
  • Integration: A design needs to be integrated with its surroundings. It shouldn't stick out like a sore thumb, but it should blend in seamlessly with the environment.
  • Love: Last but not least, a designer needs to love their project. The love for the project will show in the final product, and it's what makes a design beautiful.

Examples of Beauty in Design

There are countless examples of beautiful designs that emerged from the process of selection, affinities, integration, and love. Here are just a few:

"Architecture should speak of its time and place, but yearn for timelessness." - Frank Gehry

  • The Sydney Opera House: The iconic opera house in Sydney, Australia, is an example of a design that blends in seamlessly with its surroundings. The sail-like structure is meant to evoke the feeling of being on a boat, as it's located right by the water.
  • The Guggenheim Museum: Frank Lloyd Wright's masterpiece in New York City is a testament to the power of integration. The spiral structure of the museum allows for a continuous flow of art and visitors, as well as providing natural light throughout the space.
  • The Vietnam War Memorial: Maya Lin's memorial in Washington D.C. is a stunning example of a design that emerged from selection and love. Lin's design was chosen out of over 1,000 submissions, and she put her heart and soul into creating a tribute to the soldiers who lost their lives in the Vietnam War.

Conclusion

As Louis Kahn said, design is not making beauty. Instead, it's about the process that leads to that beauty. Remember to take the time to select the right materials, ensure that the elements have affinities with one another, integrate the design with its surroundings, and most importantly, love your project.
