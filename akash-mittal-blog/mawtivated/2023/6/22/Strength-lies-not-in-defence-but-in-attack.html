Strength lies not in defence but in attack



Picture a king, facing a mighty army, ten times larger than his own. The enemy had surrounded his fortress, and his people trembled in fear. But the king did not cower behind his walls. Instead, he called forth his army, took the fight to the enemy's doorstep. In one swift and unexpected move, he slayed the enemy's general, stampeded through their ranks with fire and sword, and emerged victorious.

This is what we call the art of attack. In this world of ours, where success is measured by how much you can take and how much you can give, the one who attacks is the one who prevails.

The Importance of Taking Initiative

Now, before you start raising objections, hear me out. I am not talking about mindless aggression or starting wars on a whim. What I am talking about is something deeper: the importance of taking initiative, of being the first to strike, of seizing opportunities before anyone else does.

Think of it this way. In a game of chess, the player who makes the first move has a slight advantage over the other. He can dictate the pace of the game, control the middle of the board, and force his opponent into making mistakes. To be the one who makes the first move is to have the initiative, and the initiative is power.

But how does this apply to real life? Simple. In any given situation, there are two types of people: those who wait for opportunities to come to them, and those who go out and create their own opportunities. The latter are the ones who get things done, who make things happen, who lead by example. The former are the ones who wait, watch, and hope for the best.

Take the example of Adolf Hitler. Regardless of what you may think of him, there is no denying his unmatched ability to seize the initiative, to take charge of situations, to bend reality to his will. He did not wait for opportunities to come to him. He created them through sheer force of will. And while his actions were certainly controversial, there is no denying that he was a master of the art of attack.

Examples of Attack in Practice

To give you some practical examples, here are a few cases where the art of attack was used to great effect:

  • Elon Musk, who founded SpaceX and revolutionized the space industry
  • Steve Jobs, who reimagined the personal computer, the music industry, and the phone industry
  • Malala Yousafzai, who fought for the education rights of Pakistani girls, and survived an assassination attempt at the age of 15

All of these individuals had one thing in common: they attacked their respective industries head-on, took on entrenched interests, and changed the world in the process.

Three Key Takeaways

So what can we learn from these examples? Here are three key takeaways:

  1. The initiative is power. If you want to make a difference, don't wait for someone else to give you permission. Create your own opportunities, and seize them with both hands.
  2. Attack with purpose. Mindless aggression is pointless. Before you attack, think about what you want to achieve, and how you can achieve it in the most effective manner possible.
  3. Be prepared to take risks. Attack is inherently risky, but the rewards can be enormous. Don't be afraid to take a chance, to step out of your comfort zone, to try something new.

With these words of wisdom, I leave you to contemplate the art of attack, and how you can apply it to your own life.
