The Power of Working Backward



Have you ever heard of the inspiring story of how Amazon's founder, Jeff Bezos, turned an online bookstore into one of the world's largest online retailers? When Bezos started Amazon in 1994, he had a vision for a company that focused on the customer experience and reinvented the way people buy things. He had a simple idea: to use the internet to sell books to customers around the world.

But Bezos knew that in order to succeed, he needed to be innovative and think ahead. He realized that the key to Amazon's success was not just selling books on the internet, but using the data he was collecting on his customers to personalize their experience and offer them products they might be interested in. Instead of focusing on what he was good at, Bezos worked backward, listening to his customers and learning new skills.

Working Backward

There are two ways to extend a business. You could take inventory of what you're good at and extend out from your skills. Or you could determine what your customers need and work backward, even if it requires learning new skills.

The first method, extending out from your skills, can be limiting. It assumes that you know what your customers want and that your skills are enough to meet their needs. But this approach doesn't leave much room for innovation and growth.

The second method, working backward, allows you to see your business from your customers' perspective. By putting yourself in their shoes, you can identify their pain points and find ways to solve them. This approach helps you to innovate and grow, and it allows you to stay ahead of the competition.

Examples of Working Backward

  • Kindle: Bezos realized that customers wanted a way to read books digitally, even if they didn't have a physical e-reader. He created an app that could be downloaded on any device, allowing customers to read Kindle books on their smartphones, tablets, or computers.
  • Amazon Prime: Bezos saw that customers wanted free and fast shipping. He created Amazon Prime, a subscription service that provides free two-day shipping on millions of items, in addition to other benefits like streaming of movies and music, and unlimited photo storage.
  • Alexa: Bezos recognized that customers wanted a smart speaker that could help them with everyday tasks. He created Alexa, a voice-activated assistant that can play music, answer questions, and control smart home devices.

When I started my own business, I focused on what I was good at and offered my services to customers. However, over time I realized that my customers were not always satisfied with what I was offering. I decided to take a step back and listen to their needs. I learned new skills and adapted my services to meet those needs. This allowed me to improve my business and attract new customers.

Another anecdote comes from a friend of mine who started a bakery. She thought that all her customers wanted was fresh, delicious bread. But one day a customer asked if she could make gluten-free bread. She had never made gluten-free bread before, but she worked backward and learned how to make it. This allowed her to offer a new product, attract new customers, and grow her business.

Conclusion

  1. Listen to your customers and work backward from their needs.
  2. Don't limit yourself to what you're good at, be willing to learn new skills.
  3. By working backward, you can innovate and stay ahead of the competition.
