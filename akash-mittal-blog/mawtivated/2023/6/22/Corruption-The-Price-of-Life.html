Corruption: The Price of Life



By Saul Alinsky

The Beginning of Corruption

The corruption of life begins at an early age. I remember when I was a child, my parents would tell me to go to bed at a certain time and I would always try to play one parent off the other to get what I wanted. I saw nothing wrong with it, but looking back, I realize it was the start of my experience with corruption. This is a common theme in childhood and it continues into adult life in even more significant situations.

An Inspiring Story

There was once a young woman who grew up in a poor family in a small town. She was determined to make a better life for herself and so she worked hard and got a scholarship to a prestigious university. However, when she got there, she found herself surrounded by wealthy students who could afford anything they wanted, including good grades.

She found herself unable to compete and was failing her classes. That is when she decided to take matters into her own hands. She began to offer her professors favors in exchange for good grades, from running errands to even doing their laundry. It was not long before she was at the top of her class and graduating with honors.

This may seem like a success story, but it is an example of how corruption can become a part of everyday life. This young woman had to sacrifice her own values to succeed in a system that was unjust. Although she ended up on top in the short term, it is often the case that corrupting oneself can lead to long term negative consequences.

and Case Studies

In my own experience, I have seen corrupt systems at work in politics. I have seen politicians bow to the will of special interest groups and wealthy donors in exchange for campaign contributions and favors. These deals often hurt the public and benefit a select few.

One personal case I recall was when I was working as a community organizer in Chicago. I was working with a group of tenants who were being evicted from their apartments so the landlord could rent the units out for higher prices. The landlord was a wealthy donor to a local politician who was blocking legislation that would protect the tenants from eviction.

Our organization decided to confront the politician at a public event and demand their support for the legislation. After some tense moments, the politician agreed to support the bill and the tenants were able to stay in their homes. This was a small victory against a corrupt system, but it showed that people have the power to make change.

Conclusion

Corruption is a part of life, but it does not have to be. It is important to recognize corruption and fight against it whenever possible. Here are three practical tips on how to fight against corruption:

  • Hold politicians and other leaders accountable for their actions
  • Speak up when you see corruption or injustice taking place
  • Support organizations and movements that work to promote justice and fairness
