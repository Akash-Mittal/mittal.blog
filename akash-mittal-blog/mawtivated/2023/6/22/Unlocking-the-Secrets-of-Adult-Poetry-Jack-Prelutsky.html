Unlocking the Secrets of Adult Poetry



It was a dark and stormy night when Jack Prelutsky first fell in love with poetry. As a child, he found solace in the rhythmic beauty of words, and soon began penning his own verses. When a teacher read one of his poems aloud in class, he was hooked. From then on, he spent nearly every waking moment reading, writing, and breathing poetry.

Why Some Adults Struggle With Poetry

Despite his early success, Prelutsky knows that not everyone shares his love of poetry. In fact, many people find it confusing, intimidating, or just plain boring. As he once admitted, "Otherwise I don't read much adult poetry at all, because I'm not smart enough and mostly I don't get it."

If you're one of those adults who struggles with poetry, you're not alone. The good news is that there are some practical tips and strategies you can use to unlock the secrets of this art form and start enjoying it for yourself.

How to Start Appreciating Poetry

One of the best ways to start appreciating poetry is to find a poet who speaks to you personally. Prelutsky, for example, was drawn to the work of Ogden Nash, who wrote witty, playful poems about animals and everyday life. By finding a poet whose style and subject matter resonates with you, you may find it easier to connect with the poetry on a deeper level.

Another strategy is to study the form and structure of poetry. While some poets may intentionally break the rules, most follow certain guidelines or patterns that provide structure and meaning to their work. By learning about these patterns, such as rhyme, meter, and stanza structure, you can start to see how the poetry is constructed and how it contributes to the meaning of the piece.

Finally, it can be helpful to read poetry aloud. Many poets intentionally craft their work to be spoken or performed, and hearing the rhythm and flow of the words out loud can help you better understand and appreciate the poetry. Plus, the act of reading aloud can be a meditative, calming experience that allows you to fully immerse yourself in the language.

Examples of Great Poetry

  • "Do Not Go Gentle Into That Good Night" by Dylan Thomas - A powerful poem that urges us to fight against death and to live fully until the end.
  • "The Waste Land" by T.S. Eliot - One of the most influential modernist poems, this work weaves together a complex web of literary and cultural allusions to explore themes of decay and fragmentation.
  • "The Road Not Taken" by Robert Frost - Perhaps Frost's most famous poem, this work explores the idea of choice and the paths we take in life.
"Poetry is not a form of entertainment and not a luxury. It's a necessity, and it's the fastest way we have of reminding ourselves of what's important." - Jeanette Winterson

How to Make Poetry a Part of Your Life

If you want to start integrating more poetry into your life, there are a few strategies you can try:

  • Spend a few minutes each day reading poetry.
  • Join a poetry reading group or attend a local poetry event.
  • Start a journal to write your own poems or reflections on the poetry you read.

Conclusion

In conclusion, while poetry may seem intimidating at first, it can be a beautiful, rewarding art form that invites us to explore the language and meaning of the world around us. By finding a poet you connect with, learning about the form and structure of poetry, and reading it aloud, you can start to appreciate and enjoy poetry for yourself.

In summary:

  1. Find a poet who speaks to you personally.
  2. Study the form and structure of poetry.
  3. Read poetry aloud and let it speak to you.
