The Endless Pursuit of Architectural Knowledge



It all started with a childhood dream where I would draw buildings on pieces of paper, and imagine myself living there. My parents always encouraged my creativity and told me that one day, I will become an architect. This seemed so far fetched at the time but as I grew older, my love for architecture grew too. It was then that I realized that my parents had sown a creative seed in me that will blossom when the time is right.

Fast forward to today, as I sit in my studio, surrounded by blueprints and drawings, I reflect on how much I have learned about architecture over the years. It is a field that is ever-evolving, and one can never stop learning. I can still remember the words of Richard Rogers who said, "Of course, I know very little about architecture, and the older I get the less I know." This statement emphasizes that in architecture, there is always something new to learn.

The Life Story of Richard Rogers

Richard Rogers is a renowned British architect and urban designer, born on 23 July 1933. He is a recipient of the Pritzker Architecture Prize for his contributions to the field of architecture. One of his most iconic creations is the Pompidou Centre in Paris. Richard's design philosophy is all about creating spaces that are built for people. He believes in creating buildings that are sustainable and environmentally friendly.

Learning through and Case Studies

Through my journey as an architect, I have learned a lot by studying the works of other architects and observing how they design and build their structures. But I have also learned so much from my personal experiences and those of my colleagues. For example, I once designed a building that looked great on paper but was a nightmare to construct. This experience taught me to design structures that are not only beautiful but also practical and doable. In another instance, I was working on a building project and had to deal with unforeseen obstacles. This experience taught me to stay solution-focused and think outside the box.

Practical Examples

One practical example that I always give my students is to always work with the site you have. I know this may sound obvious, but a lot of architects forget this. For example, if you are working on a site that has a lot of shade, it wouldn't make sense to design a building that relies heavily on natural light. Another example is to always design with the occupants in mind. A building that looks stunning but is uncomfortable to live in, is not good design. Good architectural design should be a blend of beauty, functionality, and practicality.

The Importance of Continuous Learning

Architecture is a field that is always growing and evolving, and there is always something new to learn. However, learning doesn't have to be formal. It can come from reading books or attending lectures by other architects. But it is also important to attend workshops and conferences to keep up with the latest trends. As an architect, you must be willing to learn new things and continuously evolve your skills.

Conclusion

So, what have we learned? As architects, we must not only focus on the beauty of our designs but also on their practicality and functionality. We must design buildings that cater to the occupants' needs, and we must always work with the site we have. Lastly, we must never stop learning and growing.
