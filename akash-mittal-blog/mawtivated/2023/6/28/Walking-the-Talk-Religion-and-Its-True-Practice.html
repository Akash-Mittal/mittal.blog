Walking the Talk: Religion and Its True Practice



The Story of Two Monks

Once upon a time, there were two monks traveling together. As they walked, they stumbled upon a stream where they saw a woman trying to cross. One of the monks, without hesitation, offered to carry the woman across the stream on his back.

The woman was grateful for the help and they went their separate ways. As the two monks continued their journey, the other monk started to grumble and complain about the fact that the other had broken the rules of their monastery by touching a woman. The monk who had carried the woman simply replied, "I put the woman down back at the stream, but you are still carrying her."

This story is often told to illustrate the difference between religion and its practice. It is easy to talk about one's beliefs, but difficult to live those beliefs and put them into action.

The Challenge of Practicing Religion

Religion has been a part of human civilization for thousands of years. It has shaped the way we live, our values, our relationships, and our societies. It provides us with a framework to make sense of the world and our place in it. Yet, despite its importance, religion is often seen as something that is difficult to practice.

Part of the challenge of practicing religion lies in the fact that beliefs and values are easy to articulate, but difficult to put into action. It is easy to say that one believes in compassion, love, and forgiveness, but it is much harder to actually act on those beliefs.

Another challenge lies in the fact that religion often requires us to go against our natural inclinations. It may require us to be kind to those who have wronged us, to forgive those who have hurt us, and to put the needs of others before our own. These are all difficult things to do, but they are the very things that religion asks of us.

The Example of Ramakrishna

"The goal of human life is to seek and realize God."

Ramakrishna was a 19th-century Indian mystic and philosopher who is particularly famous for his teachings on the practice of religion. He believed that there was only one God, but that this God could be reached through many paths. His teachings emphasized the importance of experiential knowledge of God, rather than just intellectual knowledge.

One of the things that makes Ramakrishna an interesting figure is that he didn't just talk about religion, he practiced it. He was known for his deep devotion to God and his willingness to live out his beliefs in his everyday life. He was kind to everyone he met, regardless of their social status or religion.

One particular story about Ramakrishna illustrates this point well. One day a man came to him and berated him for his devotion to the Hindu goddess Kali. The man started to spit at Ramakrishna, but Ramakrishna simply smiled and offered to loan the man his handkerchief so he could wipe off the spit. This act of kindness and compassion caused the man to have a change of heart and become a disciple of Ramakrishna.

Practical Tips for Practicing Religion

If we want to live out our religious beliefs, there are a few practical tips that can help us along the way:

  • Practice humility and always be willing to learn
  • Look for opportunities to serve others and put their needs before your own
  • Cultivate a sense of gratitude for the gifts and blessings in your life
  • Regularly set aside time for prayer or meditation
  • Avoid judging others and instead strive to see the divine in everyone

Conclusion: Walking the Talk

In conclusion, it is easy to talk about religion, but difficult to practice it. However, by following the example of spiritual figures like Ramakrishna and by using practical tips, we can begin to live out our beliefs in our everyday lives. By doing so, we can create a more compassionate and just world for ourselves and others.

  1. Practice humility and always be willing to learn
  2. Look for opportunities to serve others and put their needs before your own
  3. Cultivate a sense of gratitude for the gifts and blessings in your life
