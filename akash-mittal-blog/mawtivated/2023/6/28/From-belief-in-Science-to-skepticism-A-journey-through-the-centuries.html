The Journey of Belief in Science: 19th Century to 20th Century



An Inspiring Story

It was a bright sunny day in the early 19th century when a young aspiring scientist was experimenting in his lab. He had been working on a theory that could potentially change the world of science forever, but he had encountered a roadblock.

As he sat pondering his next move, one of his colleagues walked in and asked him what was wrong. The young scientist explained his dilemma to his colleague who then replied, "Don't give up now. You are on the brink of a great discovery. Keep pushing forward, and you will succeed."

Those words of encouragement resonated with the young scientist, and he went on to make the discovery that would change the course of science forever. That young scientist was Louis Pasteur, and the discovery he made was the germ theory of disease, which has saved countless lives ever since.

Belief in Science in the 19th Century

"The nineteenth century believed in science but the twentieth century does not."

Robert Staughton Lynd

The words of Robert Staughton Lynd still hold true today. In the 19th century, science was seen as the solution to many of the world's problems. Scientists like Charles Darwin, Alexander Bell, Thomas Edison, and Marie Curie were revered for their contributions to science.

People believed that science held the key to unlocking the mysteries of the universe. From finding a cure for diseases to understanding the complexities of the human mind, science was seen as the answer to everything.

It was during this time that the idea of progress through science first emerged. Scientists believed that they could use their knowledge to create a better world, free of disease, poverty, and ignorance.

This belief in science led to great advancements in fields like medicine, technology, and transportation. People had faith in science and trusted that scientists would always act in the best interest of society.

Skepticism in the 20th Century

However, as we entered the 20th century, people's views towards science began to change. The two World Wars and the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki showed the world the destructive power of science, and people began to question whether science was always a force for good.

The rise of conspiracy theories and pseudoscience also contributed to the growing skepticism towards science. People no longer took scientists at their word, and some began to view science as just another ideology with its own biases and interests.

The globalization of corporations and the increasing influence of money in research also led to skepticism towards science. People questioned whether the research was being funded to benefit society or to further the interests of corporations and governments.

While advancements in science continued at a rapid pace, the public trust in science continued to decline, leading to a growing disconnect between scientists and the general public.

Three Points to Consider

  • Education: One important way to increase trust in science is to improve science education. If people understand the scientific process and how scientific research is conducted, they are more likely to trust the findings and recommendations of scientists.
  • Transparency: Another way to increase trust in science is to increase transparency in research. Scientists should be open about their funding sources, research methods, and results to build trust with the public.
  • Collaboration: Collaboration between scientists and the public can also help to increase trust in science. Scientists can engage with the public through social media, public talks, and citizen science projects to build relationships and enhance understanding between scientists and the public.
