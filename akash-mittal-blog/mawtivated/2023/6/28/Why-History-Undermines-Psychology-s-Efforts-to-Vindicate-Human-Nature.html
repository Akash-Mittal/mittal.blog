The Parable of the Two Wolves



One evening, an elderly Cherokee brave told his grandson about a battle that goes on inside people. He said, "My son, the battle is between two wolves inside us all. One is Evil. It is anger, envy, jealousy, sorrow, regret, greed, arrogance, self-pity, guilt, resentment, inferiority, lies, false pride, superiority, and ego. The other is Good. It is joy, peace, love, hope, serenity, humility, kindness, benevolence, empathy, generosity, truth, compassion, and faith."

The grandson thought about it for a minute and then asked, "Grandfather, which wolf wins?"

The old Cherokee simply replied, "The one you feed."

This parable highlights an important aspect of human nature – the notion of dualism, the idea that we each possess within us the potential for good and evil, and that we must constantly choose which one we nurture and which one we suppress. Psychology has long been concerned with explaining and understanding the nature of human behavior and mental processes, and much of this work has focused on vindicating human nature by emphasizing its innate goodness. However, history provides ample evidence that, more often than not, human nature is far from ideal.

The Evolution of Psychology's View of Human Nature

Psychology, as a scientific discipline, has evolved over time, and so too has its understanding of human nature. Early on, psychologists tended to adopt a pessimistic view of humanity, asserting that individuals were born with certain innate traits and tendencies that made them prone to destructive behavior. Some even went so far as to argue that human beings were little more than animals, driven purely by instinctual drives and impulses.

However, by the mid-20th century, there emerged a school of psychology that was more optimistic about human nature. This perspective emphasized the potential for growth and development that exists within each of us, and suggested that individuals have the ability to transcend their base impulses and become more enlightened and compassionate individuals.

More recently, a growing body of research has emerged that supports the idea that humans are not only capable of living fulfilling lives, but actually have an innate drive towards social connection, meaning, and purpose. Positive psychology, for instance, emphasizes the importance of cultivating positive emotions and experiences in order to promote well-being and flourishing.

The Challenges of Vindicating Human Nature

Despite these efforts to vindicate human nature, however, the lessons of history suggest that we are far from perfect beings. The 20th century alone witnessed some of the darkest periods in human history – World War II, the Holocaust, and the genocides in Cambodia and Rwanda, to name just a few. These atrocities were perpetrated not by monsters, but by human beings like ourselves, driven by fear, hatred, and prejudice.

Moreover, even in everyday life, we are confronted with examples of dysfunction and suffering. We see relationships marked by conflict and betrayal, addictions that lead to self-destruction, and all manner of psychological and emotional disorders.

While it is certainly true that much of our behavior is shaped by our environment and the circumstances in which we find ourselves, the fact remains that individuals have the power to make choices, and these choices can either lead to greater good or to greater harm.

The Promise and Perils of Positive Psychology

This is not to say that the efforts to vindicate human nature are without merit. On the contrary, positive psychology has opened up new avenues for research and practice, and has helped to promote greater understanding of the factors that contribute to human flourishing.

However, there is a danger in promoting an overly optimistic view of human nature. When we focus solely on the positive aspects of human psychology, we run the risk of ignoring the darker side of our nature – the biases, prejudices, and irrational tendencies that can lead to harm and suffering.

Moreover, by emphasizing the individualistic aspects of human nature – the idea that each of us is a unique and special snowflake – we may unwittingly overlook the importance of social context and the role that interpersonal relationships and cultural norms play in shaping our behavior.

The Importance of Personal Responsibility

Ultimately, the challenge we face is to find a way to balance the potential for growth and development that exists within each of us with the sobering reality of human imperfection and fallibility. This requires a recognition that we each bear a personal responsibility for our actions, and that our choices can have profound effects on ourselves and others.

It also requires a humility and an openness to the possibility that we may be wrong, and that our beliefs and biases may be hindering our ability to see the world clearly. By recognizing these limitations, we can begin to cultivate a more nuanced and compassionate understanding of human nature – one that recognizes both its potential and its challenges.

Conclusion

  1. We should recognize the importance of personal responsibility and the fact that our choices have real consequences for ourselves and others.
  2. We should be open to the possibility that our beliefs and biases may be limiting our ability to fully understand ourselves and others.
  3. We should strive to cultivate a more nuanced and compassionate understanding of human nature – one that recognizes both its potential and its flaws.

In conclusion, while psychology continues to strive to vindicate human nature, the lessons of history suggest that we must remain vigilant in our efforts to understand and improve the human condition. By acknowledging the duality of human nature, and by recognizing both its potential and its challenges, we can begin to cultivate a deeper understanding of ourselves and the world around us.
