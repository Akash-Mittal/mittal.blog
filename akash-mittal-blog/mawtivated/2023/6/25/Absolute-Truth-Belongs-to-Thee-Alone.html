The Search for Absolute Truth



There was once a man who was searching for absolute truth. He traveled far and wide, talked to many wise men and scholars, read books and scriptures, but he still could not find the answers he was looking for. One day, he met a monk who asked him, "Why are you searching for absolute truth, my friend?" The man replied, "I want to know the purpose of my existence, the meaning of life, and the path to true happiness." The monk smiled and said, "Absolute truth belongs to Thee alone."

The Story of Gotthold Ephraim Lessing

Gotthold Ephraim Lessing was a German writer, philosopher, and critic in the 18th century. He was known for his works that questioned religious dogmas and emphasized reason and humanism. Lessing was a seeker of truth, but he believed that absolute truth was unattainable by human means. He wrote, "There is no absolute truth, but only a multitude of approximations, which must be modified by the circumstances and the prejudices of the observer."

The Elusiveness of Absolute Truth

Many thinkers and philosophers throughout history have debated the nature of truth and its accessibility to human beings. Some have claimed that truth can be obtained through faith, intuition, or revelation, while others have argued that truth can only be discovered through reason, evidence, or empirical observation. Still, others have questioned whether there is such a thing as absolute truth or whether truth is relative to each individual's perspective or culture.

Examples of Relative and Absolute Truth

One example of relative truth is the concept of beauty. Some people find certain things beautiful, while others find them ugly. Beauty is in the eye of the beholder, as they say. On the other hand, an example of absolute truth is the law of gravity. Gravity affects everything in the universe, regardless of one's opinion, belief, or culture. Another example of absolute truth is mathematical and logical principles, such as 2+2=4 or A=A.

The Limits of Human Knowledge

The search for truth is a noble endeavor, but humans are limited by their cognitive abilities, biases, and cultural conditioning. We can only perceive a small fraction of the electromagnetic spectrum, and our brains have evolved to make quick judgments based on incomplete or ambiguous information. Moreover, humans tend to interpret reality through their own filters of language, faith, values, and emotions. Therefore, our perception of truth is always partial and subjective.

The Need for Humility and Openness

Given the limits of human knowledge and the diversity of perspectives, it is important to approach truth-seeking with humility and openness. We should acknowledge the possibility of error and learn from our mistakes. We should also be willing to listen to different viewpoints and challenge our own assumptions. This does not mean that we have to abandon reason or critical thinking, but rather that we should use them in a flexible and nuanced way.

The Three Points of Conclusion

  1. Absolute truth is a concept that has fascinated and eluded humans throughout history.
  2. While some claim that there is absolute truth, others argue that truth is relative and contextual.
  3. Whether absolute truth exists or not, the quest for truth can be a fulfilling and humbling journey that requires openness, curiosity, and intellectual honesty.
