The Truth About Marriage: My Take as Lindsay Lohan



Let me tell you a story. When I was 19, I was swept off my feet by a guy I had been dating for less than a month. He was charming, successful, and everything I thought I wanted in a partner. One night, he whisked me away to Vegas, and before I knew it, we were hitched. That "minute" I mentioned in the quote? It wasn't even that long.

The marriage, unsurprisingly, didn't last. We were both young, immature, and didn't understand the institution we had entered into. Looking back, I know that I wasn't ready for that kind of commitment. But it taught me valuable lessons about what marriage is really about - and what it isn't.

When it comes to marriage, everyone has an opinion. Some people swear by it, saying it's the ultimate sign of love and commitment. Others think it's outdated and pointless, and would rather focus on building a life with someone without tying the knot.

As someone who's been through a marriage (albeit a short-lived one), I can tell you that the truth probably lies somewhere in between. Here are my takes on the myths and realities of marriage that I've come to understand over the years:

Myth #1: Marriage is the ultimate fulfillment of love.

This is probably the most common myth about marriage - that it's the ultimate symbol of love and commitment, and that those who get married are the truly devoted ones. But the truth is, marriage is just a piece of paper. It doesn't automatically make you more in love with your partner, nor does it mean that you're more committed to the relationship. Love and commitment come from the heart, not from a legal document.

Example:

"I was convinced that getting married was the ultimate goal in life - that somehow, everything would be better if I was someone's wife. But when I married someone I barely knew, I realized that nothing had really changed. We were still two people with our own individual problems, trying to make it work together."

Myth #2: Marriage solves problems.

Another common myth about marriage is that it solves problems - that once you're married, all the little things that used to bug you about your partner magically disappear. But anyone who's been in a long-term relationship knows that's not the case. Marriage doesn't solve problems, it just amplifies them. All the little struggles and disagreements you had before are still there, only now you're legally bound to each other and forced to confront them head-on.

Example:

"I used to think that getting married would mean my partner and I would never fight. But the reality is, we still argue about silly things like whose turn it is to do the dishes. The difference is that now, we've committed to working through our problems together, even if it takes a little bit of compromise."

Myth #3: Marriage is the end goal.

One of the biggest myths about marriage is that it's the end goal - that once you get married, you can cross "finding love" off your to-do list and move on to other things. But the truth is, marriage is just the beginning. It's a starting point, not a destination. Once you're married, you still have to work every day to keep the relationship strong and healthy. You still have to put in effort, compromise, and communicate effectively.

Example:

"I used to think that getting married would be the finish line of my relationship with my partner. But now that we're married, I see that it's just the starting line. We still have goals and dreams we want to achieve together, and marriage doesn't change that."

Conclusion:

So what's the bottom line when it comes to marriage? Here are three key takeaways:

  • Marriage isn't the end-all, be-all of relationships. It's just one way to express love and commitment, and it's not for everyone.
  • Getting married won't solve your problems - if anything, it'll make them more pronounced. But if you're willing to work through those problems together, marriage can be a rewarding experience.
  • Marriage isn't the end goal - it's just the beginning. You still have to work to maintain your relationship and keep it healthy and strong.

At the end of the day, the decision to get married (or not) is a personal one, and there's no right or wrong answer. What's important is that you make the choice that's right for you, and that you approach marriage with a clear understanding of what it really means and what you're getting into.
