The Art of Film Reviewing: When You Just Don't Get It



Everyone has their own taste in movies. What one person finds entertaining, another might find dull. And as film reviewers, it's our job to express our thoughts on a movie to help viewers make decisions on what to watch. But what happens when you just don't get it? When a movie receives critical acclaim and fills theaters around the world, but it just doesn't resonate with you? This is the challenge faced by many film reviewers, including myself.

My Story

My name is Stephen Hunter, and I've been in the film reviewing business for over twenty years. I've had many successes in my career, but there's one review that people still bring up to this day - my review of 'Hudson Hawk.' The movie received overwhelmingly negative reviews from critics, but for some reason, it tickled my funny bone. I laughed throughout the movie and appreciated the humor. But my review stood out like a sore thumb among the negativity, and it became a running joke in certain circles. 'Hey, you're that guy who liked 'Hudson Hawk',' people would say.

The experience taught me a valuable lesson about the impact of reviews on a critic's career. While I received some praise for thinking outside the box and daring to be different, it also hurt my credibility in some circles. People questioned my judgment and wondered if I could be trusted to give accurate insights into movies. It's a tough situation to be in for any film critic.

The Impact of Not 'Getting' a Movie

There are a few different scenarios that can happen when a movie doesn't strike a chord with a reviewer:

  • The reviewer feels like a fraud because they're unable to appreciate a movie that the majority of people love.
  • The reviewer enjoys the movie, but it's panned by other critics and doesn't do well in theaters, potentially hurting the reviewer's credibility.
  • The reviewer dislikes the movie, but it's a critical or commercial success, potentially putting the reviewer at odds with their readership or media outlet.

All of these scenarios can be difficult to navigate, and it's important for film reviewers to remember that their opinion is just that - an opinion. It doesn't necessarily reflect the views of every viewer, and that's ok. However, it's still important to express one's thoughts clearly and honestly.

One experience that stands out to me happened just a few years ago. A critically acclaimed movie was released, and I was eager to see it. However, as I watched it, I found myself struggling to stay engaged. The visuals were stunning, the acting was top-notch, but something just didn't click for me. I left the theater feeling disappointed and frustrated that I wasn't able to appreciate the movie like so many others seemed to.

Later that week, I was approached by a reader who had seen the movie and loved it. They asked me what I thought, and I hesitated, unsure of how to express my thoughts. But then I remembered that it's important to be honest with oneself and one's readers. I told the reader that the movie just didn't do it for me, but that didn't mean it wouldn't be enjoyable for them. After all, taste is subjective.

Conclusion

In conclusion, here are three important takeaways for film reviewers when they just don't get it:

  1. Remember that taste is subjective. Just because you don't enjoy a movie doesn't mean it's a bad movie or that others won't appreciate it.
  2. Be honest with oneself and one's readers. It's better to express one's true thoughts on a movie than to pretend to like it or to avoid talking about it altogether.
  3. Understand that reviews can have a lasting impact on one's career. While it's important to express one's honest thoughts, it's also important to consider the larger context of a movie's critical and commercial success.

"I never feel so utterly fraudulent as when I review a movie whose charms impress all in the world and I simply do not get it. The other variant is that I love something the world disdains. This has had severe career consequences: I am still famous - or notorious - in certain quarters where I am recalled as the man who liked 'Hudson Hawk.'"

- Stephen Hunter
