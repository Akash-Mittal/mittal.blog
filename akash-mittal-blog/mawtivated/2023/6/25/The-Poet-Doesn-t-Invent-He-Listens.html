The Poet Doesn't Invent. He Listens.



How listening can be the cornerstone of creativity and poetry.

The Poet Who Listened

In 1922, French poet, playwright, and filmmaker Jean Cocteau published a book called Le Coq et l'Arlequin, in which he set forth his thoughts on art and literature. One of his most famous postulates was this: "The poet doesn't invent. He listens."

What did Cocteau mean by that? Simply put, he believed that poetry was not something that originated solely from the poet's imagination. Rather, the poet was like a receiver, tuned to the frequencies of the world around them, picking up signals and translating them into words.

"The poet is a radio, not an inventor."

This idea may seem counterintuitive to our preconceptions of creativity and authorship. Don't great works of art come from a well of inspiration deep within the artist's soul?

But Cocteau's point was that the true wellspring of poetry was not the ego or the will, but the receptive spirit of the poet - the part of them that was open to the world around them, that could absorb and transform the perceptions of their senses into something transcendent. In this sense, the poet was not so much a creator as a translator - someone who could weave together words and images to capture the essence of a moment or a feeling.

Listening to the World

So what does it mean to listen as a poet? For Cocteau, it was not a passive or idle activity. It demanded a heightened state of awareness - an ability to attune oneself to the rhythms and nuances of the world. In his view, the poet was not just someone who heard things, but who felt them, who could sense the underlying currents that connected all things.

This was not a mystical or supernatural skill, but rather a matter of training oneself to pay attention. Cocteau believed that any being capable of seeing, hearing, touching, tasting, or smelling was already in possession of all the senses they needed to create great poetry. The challenge was to refine those senses, to cultivate a deep and abiding appreciation for the world and all its manifestations.

Case Study: Mary Oliver

One contemporary poet who exemplifies Cocteau's philosophy of listening is Mary Oliver. Oliver, who passed away in 2019, was a Pulitzer Prize-winning writer known for her clear, evocative nature poetry. But what many readers found most striking about her work was its intense attentiveness to the natural world.

Oliver spent much of her life in New England, exploring the woods and fields around her home and observing the creatures that lived there. Her poems often read like field guides or journals, with detailed descriptions of birds, mammals, and plants. But there was also a deep emotional resonance to her work - a sense that by describing the world around her in such exacting detail, she was also revealing something essential about her own inner life.

Practical Tips for Listening

If you're a poet or writer interested in cultivating your listening skills, here are a few practical tips to get you started:

  • Spend time in nature. Whether it's a walk in the park or a camping trip, being in the outdoors can help attune you to the sights, sounds, and rhythms of the world.
  • Practice mindfulness. Set aside a few minutes each day to simply be present, observing your surroundings without judgment or expectation.
  • Engage your senses. Try to notice the details of the world around you - the way the light falls on a leaf, the sound of water flowing over rocks, the smell of freshly-baked bread.
  • Read widely. Expose yourself to a variety of styles, voices, and genres. Pay attention to how different writers use language to capture different moods and emotions.

Conclusion

To some, the idea that the poet doesn't invent may be unsettling or even blasphemous. But to those who understand Cocteau's message, it can be liberating - a reminder that the creative act is not solely the province of the talented or the privileged, but an innate human capacity available to all. By learning to listen - to the world, to ourselves, to each other - we can tap into that wellspring of inspiration and create something beautiful.

So, in summary:

  1. The poet doesn't invent - they listen.
  2. Listening demands a heightened state of awareness.
  3. Practical tips for cultivating listening skills include spending time in nature, practicing mindfulness, engaging your senses, and reading widely.
