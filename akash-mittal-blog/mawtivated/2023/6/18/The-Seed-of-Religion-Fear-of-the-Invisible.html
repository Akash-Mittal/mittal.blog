The Seed of Religion: Fear of the Invisible



There once was a man named John, who lived in a small village in the mountains. He was known as a pious man who spent a great deal of his time in prayer and contemplation. One day, as he was walking through the forest, he heard a rustling in the bushes. Thinking it was a wild animal, he became afraid and began to pray for protection. As the rustling grew louder, he closed his eyes and continued to pray. When he opened them again, he saw a beautiful bird perched on a nearby branch. He felt a sense of awe and wonder at the beauty of the creature and realized that his fear had blinded him to the beauty of the natural world around him.

This experience reminded John of a quote he had once heard from Thomas Hobbes, a famous philosopher of the 17th century:

"Fear of things invisible in the natural seed of that which everyone in himself calleth religion."

John realized that his fear of the unknown had led him to seek comfort and protection in religion. This is a common experience for many people, who find solace in the rituals and doctrines of their chosen faith.

The Story of Thomas Hobbes

Thomas Hobbes was born in 1588 in England, at a time of great religious turmoil. The Protestant Reformation had led to a split in the Christian Church, and religious wars were raging across Europe. Hobbes was a philosopher, best known for his work on political theory and social contract theory. He was also a firm believer in the power of the state to control religious dissent and enforce conformity.

Fear and Religion

Hobbes believed that fear was the driving force behind religion. He argued that people are naturally afraid of the unknown and seek comfort in religious beliefs that offer explanations for the mysteries of life. This fear of the invisible is the seed of religion, and it is what drives people to seek answers to the questions of life.

Religion offers a sense of security and comfort in a world that is often uncertain and frightening. It gives people a sense of community and shared beliefs, which can be comforting in times of crisis. However, Hobbes believed that this fear can also be dangerous, particularly when it leads to religious conflict and intolerance.

Examples of Fear-Driven Religion

Throughout history, fear has been a driving force behind many religious movements. One example is the rise of fundamentalist Christianity in the United States in the 20th century. This movement was fueled by a fear of social change and a desire to return to traditional values. Another example is the rise of militant Islam in the Middle East, which has been fueled by a fear of Western imperialism and a desire to defend Islam against perceived threats.

Conclusion: Breaking Free from Fear

In conclusion, fear is a natural human emotion that can drive people to seek comfort and security in religion. While religion can provide a sense of community and shared beliefs, it can also be a source of conflict and intolerance when driven by fear. To break free from fear-driven religion, we must learn to embrace the beauty and mystery of the natural world around us, letting go of our need for certainty and control.

Three practical tips for breaking free from fear-driven religion include:

  • Practice mindfulness and meditation to cultivate a sense of inner peace and stillness
  • Engage in dialogue with people of different faiths and beliefs to broaden your perspective and challenge your assumptions
  • Explore the natural world and connect with the beauty and wonder of the universe
