The Ultimate Purpose: Improving Mankind



An Inspiring Story on Planning and Architectural Design

The Power of Purpose

There is a story of a man who used to work as a janitor in a hospital. Every day, he would patiently clean the corridors and rooms, and he would sometimes chat with some of the patients and their families. One day, a young girl asked him what his job was and why he did it. He answered: "My job is to make sure that the sick people here feel better, and I do it because it makes me happy to know that I'm helping them." The girl smiled and said: "You're not just a janitor, you're a healer."

This story illustrates the power of purpose. When we have a clear and inspiring purpose, even the simplest job can become meaningful and fulfilling. But when we lack purpose, even the most complex and prestigious job can feel empty and frustrating.

"For many years, I have lived uncomfortably with the belief that most planning and architectural design suffers for lack of real and basic purpose. The ultimate purpose, it seems to me, must be the improvement of mankind."

James Rouse

James Rouse was an American real estate developer and urban planner who believed in the power of purpose. He saw planning and architectural design not as mere technical tasks or artistic expressions, but as means to improve the quality of life for people. He believed that the ultimate purpose of planning and design was to enhance the physical, social, and psychological well-being of individuals and communities.

The Purpose of Planning and Design

  • Planning and design must serve the needs and aspirations of people, not just the interests of developers, investors, or politicians.
  • Planning and design must promote equity, diversity, and inclusiveness, not just exclusivity, homogeneity, or segregation.
  • Planning and design must respect the natural environment and the cultural heritage, not just exploit or destroy them.

These three principles can guide us in creating planning and design that are purposeful and meaningful. Let's see some examples of how these principles can be applied in practice.

Examples of Purposeful Planning and Design

Universal Design

Universal Design is a concept that aims to create products, environments, and systems that are accessible and usable by all people, regardless of their abilities, ages, and backgrounds. Universal Design is based on the belief that diversity is a strength, not a limitation, and that designing for the extremes can benefit the mainstream. Universal Design principles can be applied to buildings, public spaces, transportation, products, and communication.

For example, the "Curb Cut Effect" shows that the curb cut, which is a sloped or ramped sidewalk curb that allows wheelchair users, strollers, and bicyclists to safely and easily cross the street, has also benefited pedestrians, delivery people, and others who use wheeled devices or who have difficulty climbing stairs. The curb cut is not just a solution for a minority group, but a universal solution that improves the quality of life for everyone.

Biophilic Design

Biophilic Design is a concept that recognizes the innate human affinity for nature and seeks to incorporate natural elements and patterns into the built environment. Biophilic Design is based on the belief that exposure to nature can reduce stress, enhance creativity, and improve well-being. Biophilic Design principles can be applied to buildings, interiors, landscapes, and urban spaces.

For example, the "Central Park Effect" shows that the proximity and access to nature, such as parks, gardens, and green spaces, can have positive effects on physical health, psychological well-being, and social interactions. The Central Park in New York City is not just a luxury for the wealthy or a tourist attraction, but a public good that benefits the entire community.

Active Design

Active Design is a concept that promotes physical activity and healthy behaviors through the design of the built environment. Active Design is based on the belief that physical inactivity, poor nutrition, and sedentary lifestyles are major risk factors for chronic diseases, such as obesity, diabetes, and heart disease. Active Design principles can be applied to buildings, streets, neighborhoods, and cities.

For example, the "Stair Use Effect" shows that the visibility, attractiveness, and convenience of staircases can encourage people to use them more often instead of elevators or escalators. The Stair Use Effect is not just a way to save energy or reduce congestion, but a way to improve public health and well-being.

The Importance of Purposeful Planning and Design

Purposeful planning and design can have a transformative impact on society. Purposeful planning and design can:

  1. Transform the physical environment into a healthy, safe, and resilient habitat for all species.
  2. Transform the social fabric into a diverse, egalitarian, and inclusive community for all people.
  3. Transform the psychological landscape into a meaningful, fulfilling, and joyful experience for all individuals.

But purposeful planning and design requires more than technical expertise or artistic talent. Purposeful planning and design requires empathy, curiosity, and humility. Purposeful planning and design requires listening to people's stories, understanding people's needs, and valuing people's diversity. Purposeful planning and design requires learning from nature's wisdom, respecting nature's limits, and restoring nature's balance.

Conclusion

To sum up, purposeful planning and design can be a powerful tool for improving mankind. Purposeful planning and design can enhance our physical, social, and psychological well-being. Purposeful planning and design can celebrate our diversity, honor our heritage, and protect our environment. To create purposeful planning and design, we need to adhere to three principles: serving the needs and aspirations of people, promoting equity, diversity, and inclusiveness, and respecting the natural environment and the cultural heritage. To apply these principles, we can use some examples of purposeful planning and design, such as Universal Design, Biophilic Design, and Active Design. And to embody these principles, we need to nurture our empathy, curiosity, and humility, and learn from people and nature.
