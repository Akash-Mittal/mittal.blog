The Nature of People: A Lesson from Xun Zi



As a young boy, Xun Zi was a mischievous troublemaker. His parents had a hard time controlling him, and they hoped that sending him to school would help him become better behaved.

And it did. Xun Zi flourished under the guidance of his teachers, who taught him the importance of morality and ritual principles. He went on to become a famous philosopher and one of the most important thinkers in ancient China.

In his writings, Xun Zi acknowledged that the nature of people is bad. He believed that in order to become good, we need to be taught by teachers and acquire moral principles.

The Nature of People

According to Xun Zi, the nature of people is not inherently good. Instead, we are born with primitive instincts that lead us to pursue our own interests and desires.

Left to our own devices, we are likely to behave badly, engaging in acts of greed, selfishness, and violence. Xun Zi believed that it was only through the acquisition of moral and ritual principles that we could overcome these baser instincts and become civilized, orderly members of society.

The Importance of Teachers

Xun Zi believed that it was the role of teachers to guide students in the acquisition of moral and ritual principles. He believed that teachers had a duty to inspire and instruct their students, using their knowledge and wisdom to mold young minds.

Xun Zi himself was deeply influenced by his teachers, who instilled in him a sense of reverence for moral principles and a deep respect for ritual. He believed that without their guidance, he would have remained a troublemaker all his life.

The Role of Moral and Ritual Principles

Xun Zi believed that moral and ritual principles were essential for a harmonious society. Without them, people would behave in a selfish and chaotic manner, leading to conflict and disorder.

Moral principles, such as the importance of honesty, charity, and compassion, help to guide our behavior and ensure that we treat others with respect and dignity. Ritual principles, such as the performance of ceremonies and festivals, help to build a sense of community and shared values.

Examples

  • A teacher who instills the importance of honesty and integrity in her students, leading them to become more trustworthy and responsible members of society.
  • An organization that encourages its members to participate in community service, leading to a greater sense of social responsibility and civic engagement.
  • A family that observes cultural festivals and traditions, strengthening their sense of identity and shared values.

Conclusion

In summary, Xun Zi believed that the nature of people is bad, and that we need to be taught by teachers and acquire moral and ritual principles in order to become good members of society.

To apply this philosophy to our own lives, we can seek out teachers and mentors who can guide us in our personal growth, and we can embrace moral and ritual principles that promote positive behavior and build a sense of community.

Ultimately, it is through the cultivation of these principles that we can become better, more compassionate, and more contributing members of society.
