The Revolution of Cathy: A Milestone in Comic Strips



An Inspiring Story

It's the year 1976 in Dayton, Ohio. A young woman named Cathy Guisewite is struggling to find her place in the world. She has a degree in English and a job as a secretary, but she feels unfulfilled. One day, she decides to try her hand at creating a comic strip based on her own experiences as a single woman in her 20s.

Cathy works tirelessly on her strip, making sure every detail is perfect. When she finally submits it to the syndicates, she is met with rejection after rejection. But Cathy refuses to give up. She knows her strip has potential, and she is determined to get it out there for the world to see.

Finally, after months of hard work and perseverance, Cathy's strip gets picked up by Universal Press Syndicate. It's called "Cathy" and it stars a relatable, funny, and emotionally honest character who quickly becomes a hit with readers around the country.

The Story Behind Cathy Guisewite

Cathy Guisewite was born in 1950 in Dayton, Ohio. She grew up in a family of artists and writers, and she always knew she wanted to do something creative with her life. After graduating from the University of Michigan with a degree in English, she moved to Milwaukee and worked as a secretary while pursuing her dream of becoming a cartoonist.

Guisewite's experiences as a single woman in her 20s were the inspiration for "Cathy." The strip's protagonist was a spunky and independent woman who struggled with her weight, her relationships, and her career. Like Guisewite herself, Cathy was relatable and endearing, and readers quickly fell in love with her.

"I just drew what I knew, and I knew the life of a single woman trying to make it in the world," Guisewite once said.

For 34 years, Guisewite drew "Cathy" and gave voice to the struggles and triumphs of modern women. Along the way, she won three Emmy Awards and a National Cartoonist Society Reuben Award for Outstanding Cartoonist of the Year.

The Impact of "Cathy"

"Cathy" was a revolutionary strip for its time. It was the first widely syndicated comic strip created by a woman, and that fact alone made it groundbreaking. But beyond that, "Cathy" was also emotionally honest in a way that few strips were in the 1970s.

"Cathy" tackled a wide range of topics that were relevant to women at the time, including body issues, workplace discrimination, and relationship woes. Guisewite never shied away from the hard stuff, and her willingness to confront these issues head-on helped her readers feel less alone in their struggles.

Another groundbreaking aspect of "Cathy" was its willingness to represent all types of women. In a time when most comic strips featured predominantly white, male characters, "Cathy" showed a diverse cast of women with different ages, shapes, and backgrounds. This was especially meaningful for women of color, who rarely saw themselves represented in the media.

Examples of "Cathy" Comic Strips

  • "On Second Thought": In this strip, Cathy debates whether or not to return a shirt she bought that's too small. She tries it on one last time, and it gets stuck on her, leaving her trapped in the dressing room. The strip is a hilarious and relatable look at the struggles of finding clothes that fit.
  • "Enough is Never Enough": In this strip, Cathy tries to stick to a diet but finds herself constantly giving in to temptation. She eats a piece of chocolate cake for breakfast, a donut for lunch, and a bag of chips for dinner, all while telling herself that she'll start the diet tomorrow. It's a funny and honest take on the difficulties of weight loss.
  • "The Single Life": This recurring theme in the strip centers around Cathy's struggles with dating and relationships. In one strip, she wonders why she can't find a man who is kind, funny, intelligent, and good-looking. Her friend responds, "Sweetie, that's what we all want. Adolf Hitler could have found that in the bunker." The strip is a humorous and relatable look at the ups and downs of the dating world.

Conclusion

  1. "Cathy" was a groundbreaking comic strip for its time, featuring a relatable and emotionally honest female protagonist.
  2. Guisewite's experiences as a single woman inspired the strip and gave voice to the struggles and triumphs of modern women.
  3. "Cathy" was one of the first comic strips to feature diverse characters and represent women of all ages, shapes, and backgrounds.

Overall, "Cathy" was a milestone in the world of comic strips. It showed that women could be funny, relatable, and emotionally honest in a way that resonated with readers of all genders. Guisewite's legacy lives on today in the many female cartoonists who have been inspired by her groundbreaking work.
