The Major Advantage of Age - Learning to Accept People Without Passing Judgment



Once upon a time, there was a woman named Liz Carpenter who worked as a journalist and speechwriter for Lady Bird Johnson, the first lady of the United States. When Carpenter was in her sixties, she wrote a book about her life experiences called "Unplanned Parenthood". One passage resonated with many readers:

"It is a sad fact that we have to observe age limits in our society, especially in regard to younger people's trust in older people. Only time really tests a relationship. There are no shortcuts, and 'grandparenting' must be earned . . . I will remind you that there is no aging in heaven and that the experience of life that comes with age can make this planet a better place to live."

Carpenter's words reflect the major advantage of age: learning to accept people without passing judgment. Here are some of the reasons why this advantage is so valuable and how you can cultivate it in your own life.

The Value of Acceptance

Acceptance means seeing people as they are, without trying to change them or hold them to your own standards. It means acknowledging that everyone has their own unique path in life, and that no one is completely good or completely bad, but a mixture of both.

When you're young, it's easy to be judgmental. You haven't had enough life experience to understand that everyone makes mistakes and that people are complex. But as you get older, you begin to see people with more nuance and empathy. You recognize that everyone has struggles and that some are bigger than others. You learn that what you see on the surface isn't always the whole story.

Cultivating Acceptance

If you'd like to cultivate the ability to accept people without passing judgment, here are some tips that might help:

  • Practice empathy: try to put yourself in other people's shoes and understand their perspective.
  • Avoid using stereotypes: recognize that every person is an individual and that there is no one-size-fits-all label.
  • Show compassion: when someone is struggling, offer a listening ear or a helping hand.
  • Be open-minded: instead of assuming you know all there is to know about a person, ask questions and seek to learn more.
  • Practice gratitude: focus on the good in people rather than their flaws.

Examples of Acceptance

There are many examples of people who have cultivated the ability to accept others without passing judgment. Here are a few:

  • Mother Teresa: a Catholic nun who devoted her life to serving the poor, sick, and dying in India. She saw everyone as a child of God and treated them with respect and compassion.
  • Nelson Mandela: a South African anti-apartheid revolutionary and politician who became the country's first black president. He fought against racism and discrimination and worked to unite his nation.
  • Malala Yousafzai: a Pakistani activist for female education and the youngest Nobel Prize laureate. She survived an assassination attempt by the Taliban because of her advocacy for girls' education.

Conclusion

In conclusion, learning to accept people without passing judgment is a major advantage of age. It allows us to see people with empathy, compassion, and open-mindedness. To cultivate this trait, we can practice empathy, avoid using stereotypes, show compassion, be open-minded, and focus on gratitude. When we embrace acceptance, we become better human beings and make the world a better place.

Three key takeaways from this article are:

  1. Acceptance means seeing people as they are, without trying to change them or hold them to your own standards.
  2. Cultivating acceptance involves practicing empathy, avoiding stereotypes, showing compassion, being open-minded, and focusing on gratitude.
  3. Examples of people who have cultivated acceptance include Mother Teresa, Nelson Mandela, and Malala Yousafzai.
