The Fear That Doesn't Include Me



An Inspiring Story of Overcoming Fear

by Andrew Motion

When I was a young boy, I used to be scared of the dark. It was an irrational fear, I knew that, but I couldn't help it. I used to lie in bed, staring at the ceiling, imagining all kinds of monsters lurking in the shadows. It wasn't until I discovered poetry that I began to overcome my fear.

One of my favorite poems is called "Bomb Damage" by Dylan Thomas. It's a haunting piece, written during World War II, about a man who is hiding in his cellar while bombs are falling all around him. He describes the fear he feels, the way the explosions sound like "sudden gusts of light." But what I love about the poem is the way the man overcomes his fear. He realizes that the bombs can't include him in their fear. They're just mechanical devices, doing what they're designed to do. It's up to him to find the strength to overcome his fear and carry on.

"Each sudden gust of light explains itself as flames, but neither they, nor even bombs redoubled on the hills tonight can quite include me in their fear."

That's the key, I think, to overcoming fear. It's all about perspective. We have to realize that the things we're afraid of are usually just mechanical devices. They don't have any real power over us, unless we give it to them. We have to find the strength within ourselves to take control of our fear.

Of course, that's easier said than done. Fear is a powerful emotion, and it's not always easy to overcome. But there are tricks we can use to help us.

Tip 1: Visualization

When we're afraid of something, it's usually because we're imagining the worst possible outcome. We're picturing all the ways things could go wrong. But we can use visualization to our advantage. Instead of imagining the worst possible outcome, we can imagine the best possible outcome. We can picture ourselves succeeding, and feeling proud of ourselves for overcoming our fear. This can help to reframe our thinking and make us feel more confident.

Tip 2: Exposure Therapy

Another trick is to gradually expose ourselves to the thing we're afraid of. This is called exposure therapy, and it's a technique used by psychologists to help people overcome phobias. The idea is to start small, and gradually work your way up to facing your fear head-on. For example, if you're afraid of spiders, you might start by looking at pictures of spiders, before moving on to holding a toy spider, and eventually holding a real spider in your hand. The key is to take it slowly, and to keep reminding yourself that nothing bad is actually happening.

Tip 3: Mindfulness

Mindfulness is the practice of focusing on the present moment, without judgment. It can be a helpful tool for overcoming fear, because it allows us to observe our thoughts and emotions without getting caught up in them. When we're afraid of something, our thoughts tend to spiral out of control, and we get caught up in a cycle of worry and anxiety. But by practicing mindfulness, we can step back from our thoughts and observe them objectively. We can realize that our thoughts are not reality, and that we have the power to change them.

These are just three tips, but there are many more out there. The key is to find what works for you, and to keep practicing. Overcoming fear is not something that happens overnight, but with time and effort, it is possible.

Conclusion:

  • Overcoming fear is all about perspective.
  • We can use tricks like visualization, exposure therapy, and mindfulness to help us overcome our fear.
  • It takes time and effort, but it is possible.
