The Honor of Trying



It was a cold, early morning in November. I can still picture the sun rising over the horizon as we stood in silence along with the rest of our battalion, waiting for the signal to charge. I was scared, of course, but there was also a sense of duty that propelled me forward. And so, we charged.

This was the reality for millions of young men who fought and died in the trenches of World War I. It was supposed to be the war to end all wars, but instead, it ushered in a new era of conflict and human suffering. However, amidst the chaos and destruction, there were stories of incredible bravery and humanity. One such story is that of Rene Cassin.

Who was Rene Cassin?

Rene Cassin was a French legal scholar and judge who dedicated his life to promoting human rights and world peace. He was a veteran of World War I, where he served as an artillery captain and was awarded the prestigious Croix de Guerre for his bravery in battle.

After the war, Cassin became involved in politics and social activism. He was a major contributor to the development of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, which was adopted by the United Nations in 1948. Cassin went on to receive the Nobel Peace Prize for his work in the field of human rights.

The Honor of Trying

As a survivor of World War I, Cassin understood the immense sacrifice that soldiers and civilians alike had made in the name of peace. In a speech he gave in 1945, he said:

"As a privileged survivor of the First World War, I hope I may be allowed to interject here a deeply felt tribute to those who were not fortunate enough to succeed, but who shared the signal honor of trying to the last to salvage peace."

The honor of trying. It's a phrase that encapsulates the spirit of Cassin's life's work. It's the idea that even in the face of seemingly insurmountable odds, it is our duty as human beings to strive for a better world.

Practical Tips for Making a Difference

So how can we, as individuals, make a difference in the world? How can we honor the legacy of those who came before us and fought for a better future?

  • Get involved in your community. Volunteer your time and resources to local organizations that are working to promote social justice and equality.
  • Educate yourself and others. Read books, watch documentaries, and attend lectures that address issues of human rights and the promotion of peace. Share what you've learned with your friends and family.
  • Advocate for change. Write letters to your elected officials, sign petitions, and participate in protests and rallies. Use your voice to speak out against injustices and advocate for a better world.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the honor of trying is not just a phrase, but a call to action. We must honor the sacrifices of those who came before us by actively working towards a world that is more just, peaceful, and equitable. By getting involved, educating ourselves and others, and advocating for change, we can make a difference. As Rene Cassin said, "Freedom is not a gift received from the State or leader, but a possession to be won every day by the effort of each and the union of all."
