Writing for Money: Balancing Art and Business



Several years ago, a friend of mine attended a writing workshop hosted by the legendary poet Robert Frost. When the workshop ended, Frost took the participants aside and asked each of them what they hoped to achieve with their writing. One by one, they talked about their aspirations of literary fame and recognition for their craft. But when Frost came to my friend, she admitted that she didn't really care about those things, that all she wanted was to make a living as a writer.

Frost looked at her for a moment, then smiled and said, "That might just be the most honest answer I've heard all day."

This anecdote illustrates a tension that many writers feel: the conflict between the desire to create art and the need to make a living. Some writers recoil at the mere idea of commercial success, seeing it as a betrayal of their artistic integrity. Others embrace the idea of writing as a business, determined to make a career out of their craft.

Regardless of which side of the fence you fall on, the truth is that most writers need to make money from their writing at some point. Even poets, who often consider themselves above the crass concerns of commerce, have bills to pay. As the quotation above suggests, writers are "subjected to trial by market" - that is, they are judged not only by the quality of their writing but also by its commercial viability.

The Business of Writing

So how can you balance the art and business of writing, creating work that is both creatively fulfilling and financially viable? Here are a few key strategies:

  • Think about your audience: One mistake that many writers make is assuming that their work will automatically find an audience. But in a crowded marketplace, you need to be intentional about who you are writing for and how you can reach them. Consider factors such as age, gender, interests, and genre preferences.
  • Build your brand: When a reader buys a book, they aren't just buying a story - they're also buying into the author's brand. Think about how you can create a consistent image and message across your website, social media, book covers, and author bio. This can help you attract and retain readers over time.
  • Market like a pro: Even if you create the best work in the world, it won't make a difference if nobody knows about it. That's why it's important to learn basic marketing and promotional techniques, such as building an email list, pitching to influencers in your niche, and running targeted ads. Marketing doesn't have to be slimy or manipulative - it's simply about getting your work in front of the right people.
  • Create multiple income streams: Writing is unlikely to be a steady, reliable source of income, especially in the beginning. That's why it's important to diversify your income streams, such as by writing for multiple publications, offering coaching or editing services, or monetizing your blog or podcast. This can help you weather the ups and downs of the writing life.
  • Embrace collaboration: Writing can be a solitary pursuit, but that doesn't mean you have to go it alone. Building connections with other writers, editors, agents, and publishers can help you learn from others' experiences, find new opportunities, and create a support system for yourself. Don't be afraid to reach out and ask for help or advice.
"The truth is that most writers need to make money from their writing at some point. Even poets, who often consider themselves above the crass concerns of commerce, have bills to pay."

Conclusion

In conclusion, writing for money doesn't have to be a compromise of your artistic integrity. By thinking about your audience, building your brand, marketing like a pro, creating multiple income streams, and embracing collaboration, you can create a sustainable career in creative writing. Remember to be patient, persistent, and passionate about your work, and don't be afraid to experiment and try new things along the way.

Takeaways:

  • Writing for money is a necessity for most writers.
  • You can balance the art and business of writing by thinking about your audience, building your brand, marketing like a pro, creating multiple income streams, and embracing collaboration.
  • Be patient, persistent, and passionate about your work, and don't be afraid to experiment and try new things.
