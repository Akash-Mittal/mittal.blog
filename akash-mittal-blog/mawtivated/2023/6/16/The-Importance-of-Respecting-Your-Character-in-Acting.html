The Importance of Respecting Your Character in Acting



An Inspiring Story

Imagine yourself playing the lead role in a play. You've rehearsed for months, memorized your lines, and you're feeling confident about your performance. But on opening night, something changes. As soon as you step onstage, you start to feel like you're not really the character you're playing. You start to mock their quirks and make fun of their flaws. You're not taking the role seriously, and it shows in your performance.

Now imagine the opposite. You've been cast in a supporting role, but you're determined to give it your all. You research the character, study their behaviors and mannerisms, and really try to understand their motivations. And when you step onstage, you feel like you've truly become the character. The audience can feel it, too - they're riveted by your performance, even though you're not the lead.

The difference between these two scenarios is simple: respect. When you respect the character you're playing, you bring depth and nuance to your performance. But when you disrespect the character - whether intentionally or unintentionally - you create a shallow, forgettable performance that falls flat.

The Steve Coogan Story

Steve Coogan is a British actor and comedian known for his work on shows like "Alan Partridge" and "The Trip." One of Coogan's most famous characters is a socially awkward radio host named Alan Partridge. Coogan has played Partridge in various forms for over 25 years, and has become known for his uncanny ability to stay in character even when he's not performing.

According to Coogan, the key to playing Alan Partridge (and any character, for that matter) is respect. In an interview with The Guardian, Coogan said:

"If I didn't respect Partridge, then there would be no character, because I wouldn't be able to sustain him. His attitudes are so appalling. But he's a good character because he's a complete person. He's not just his views, he's not just his taste."

Coogan's dedication to his characters - and his respect for their complexity - is what makes his performances so memorable. Like a method actor, he immerses himself in the character's world, understanding their motivations and quirks in a way that allows him to fully inhabit them.

Examples

  • Jim Carrey in "The Cable Guy": When Jim Carrey played the titular role in "The Cable Guy," he dove into the character with abandon. He stayed in character between takes, even when the cameras weren't rolling. His respect for the character allowed him to create a performance that was both hilarious and disturbing.
  • Meryl Streep in "Sophie's Choice": When Meryl Streep played concentration camp survivor Sophie Zawistowski in "Sophie's Choice," she did extensive research on the psychological effects of trauma. Her respect for the character's history and experiences allowed her to create a nuanced and heartbreaking performance.
  • Robert De Niro in "Raging Bull": When Robert De Niro played boxer Jake LaMotta in "Raging Bull," he gained over 60 pounds to play the character in his later years. His dedication to the role - and his respect for LaMotta's struggles - allowed him to create a performance that is widely regarded as one of the greatest in cinematic history.

Practical Tips

  • Do your research: Read the script and do your research on the character's background, personality, and motivations. Try to put yourself in their shoes and understand their perspective.
  • Avoid parody: While it can be tempting to play a character for laughs, avoid pushing them into parody territory. Characters who are played solely for laughs can be funny, but they won't leave a lasting impression on the audience.
  • Stay in character: Whether you're performing on stage or on camera, try to stay in character between takes. This will help you stay in the character's headspace, and make it easier to get back into character when it's time to perform again.
  • Collaborate with your director: Your director is there to help you bring the character to life. Collaborate with them to create a performance that is both true to the character and compelling for the audience.

Conclusion

  1. Respecting the character you're playing is essential to creating a compelling, memorable performance.
  2. Researching the character, staying in character, and collaborating with your director can help you create a nuanced, believable performance.
  3. Avoiding parody and playing the character solely for laughs can lead to a shallow, forgettable performance.
