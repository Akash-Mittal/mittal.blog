The Black Agenda: Towards a Better America



By Cornel West

It was a bright, sunny day in Harlem. I was walking down the street, lost in thought, when I saw a young black boy sitting on the steps of a building, crying. I approached him and asked what was wrong. He told me that he had failed his science test and was afraid of what his parents would say. As he wiped away his tears, I sat down next to him and told him a story.

"When I was your age," I said, "I failed a math test. I was so scared to tell my parents that I kept it a secret. But you know what? The secret got so heavy that I couldn't keep it anymore. So, I told my parents the truth. And you know what they said? They said, ‘We still love you, and we know you'll do better next time.' And you know what? I did do better next time."

I looked at the boy, who was now watching me intently. "Do you know why I'm telling you this story?" I asked. He shook his head. "Because there are going to be times when you fail. But that doesn't mean you're a failure. It means you're human. And humans make mistakes. But that doesn't mean you can't learn from them and do better next time."

The boy wiped away his tears and smiled at me. "Thank you, sir," he said. "I feel better now." And with that, he got up and ran off to play with his friends.

What is the Black Agenda?

The black agenda is a vision for a better America. At its core, it is a set of policies and principles that seek to address the historical and ongoing injustices experienced by black Americans. It is a call for social justice, economic opportunity, and democratic representation. It is a demand for a just and equitable society for all.

According to the National Urban League, the black agenda prioritizes:

  • Jobs, jobs, jobs
  • Quality education
  • Investment in infrastructure
  • Strong democratic regulation of corporations

These priorities are essential for achieving racial equity and social justice in America. They reflect the urgent needs of black communities, which have been marginalized and oppressed for centuries. But more than that, they represent a vision for a better America, one that is built on fairness, equity, and responsibility.

Why is the Black Agenda Important?

The black agenda is important because it is necessary to address the systemic and historical injustices faced by black Americans. These injustices include:

  • Slavery and Jim Crow
  • Redlining and discriminatory housing policies
  • Mass incarceration and police violence
  • Health and wealth disparities
  • Environmental racism

These injustices have contributed to the marginalization and impoverishment of black communities, who continue to face disproportionate rates of poverty, unemployment, and social inequality. The black agenda seeks to change this by addressing the root causes of these injustices and creating a more just and equitable society for all.

Examples of the Black Agenda in Action

The black agenda is not just a set of policies and principles—it is a movement for change. Here are some examples of the black agenda in action:

Black Lives Matter

The Black Lives Matter movement is a powerful example of the black agenda in action. It is a movement against police brutality and systemic racism, and a call for accountability and justice. It has sparked protests, community organizing, and policy reforms across the country, and has brought attention to issues of racial inequality and police violence.

The Movement for Black Lives

The Movement for Black Lives is a coalition of organizations and activists who are working for racial justice and social change. It has developed a comprehensive policy platform that includes demands for economic justice, environmental justice, and democratic representation. Its goal is to create a just and equitable society where black lives matter.

Voting Rights Advocacy

Voting rights have been a key issue for the black agenda since the civil rights era. Today, voting rights advocates are working to protect the right to vote and expand access to voting for marginalized communities. This includes fighting against voter suppression, supporting early voting and mail-in voting, and advocating for automatic voter registration.

Conclusion: The Black Agenda in Three Points

  • The black agenda is a call for social justice, economic opportunity, and democratic representation for black Americans.
  • The black agenda seeks to address the historical and ongoing injustices faced by black Americans, including systemic racism, economic inequality, and political disenfranchisement.
  • The black agenda is a movement for change, and its principles and policies are essential for creating a just and equitable society for all.
