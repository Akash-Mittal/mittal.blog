Truth and Politics



It was the stifling heat of a midsummer day in Mississippi, and Will Rogers was feeling the heat. He had been traveling the country for months, delivering speeches and making appearances, and he longed for a cold drink and a shady spot to rest. As he walked down the dusty road, he noticed a small group of people gathered around a makeshift podium.

Curiosity getting the best of him, Rogers approached the crowd and listened in as a local politician railed against his opponent, a man the speaker claimed was untrustworthy, corrupt, and beholden to powerful interests.

"If you ever injected truth into politics you have no politics."

The words hung in the air, and the crowd erupted in applause.

Will Rogers would never forget that moment, nor would he forget those words. They spoke volumes about the state of politics in America, and they still do today. But what do they mean, exactly?

Politics Without Truth

At its core, politics is a game of persuasion. Candidates and parties try to convince voters to support them by making promises, presenting their views, and attacking their opponents. And a certain amount of spin, hyperbole, and exaggeration is to be expected in any election campaign. After all, politics is a battle for hearts and minds, and every weapon in the arsenal is fair game.

But what happens when the battle becomes divorced from reality? What happens when candidates and parties are no longer content to simply spin the facts, but instead create their own version of reality altogether? This is the point at which truth and politics become irreconcilable.

The Consequences of Lying

The consequences of this "post-truth" era of politics are dire indeed. When politicians can make up their own facts, when they can dismiss criticism and evidence as "fake news," when they can inflame prejudice and fear for political gain, democracy itself is imperiled. When voters cannot trust what their leaders say, when the media cannot hold politicians accountable, when the very notion of objective truth is called into question, our most fundamental institutions are at risk.

A Way Forward

So what can be done to reverse this trend, to reintroduce truth into politics, and to restore faith in our democratic institutions? Here are three practical steps we can all take:

  • Fact-check everything: Whether you are reading a news story, watching a political ad, or scrolling through social media, always ask yourself: is this true? Look for credible sources, cross-check information, and don't believe everything you read or hear.
  • Hold politicians accountable: We have the power to make our voices heard. Write letters, attend town halls, call your representatives, and demand that they be truthful and transparent in their actions and statements.
  • Support independent media: In an era of "fake news" and partisan media, it is more important than ever to support impartial, independent journalism. Subscribe to reputable news sources, donate to local media outlets, and spread the word about the importance of independent reporting.
