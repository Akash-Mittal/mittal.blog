Planting Vines: A Lesson for Architecture



An Inspiring Story

It was a hot summer day when the young architect, John, went to meet his new client, Mr. Smith. Mr. Smith had inherited a large piece of land from his grandfather, and he wanted to build a spacious house there.

John was excited about his first big project, and he saw this as an opportunity to create something truly remarkable. He had spent countless hours preparing and designing, and he was ready to present his plans to Mr. Smith.

As John started to show Mr. Smith the blueprints, he noticed that there was something wrong with the measurements. He had made a critical mistake, and he had no idea how to fix it without starting over.

John felt the sweat running down his forehead, and he knew that he had let his client down. He was ready to apologize and take all the blame when Mr. Smith said something unexpected.

"Don't worry about it," said Mr. Smith, "I trust your judgement. Just make sure that the house is surrounded by vines."

John was confused, but Mr. Smith explained his philosophy of life to him. He said that mistakes and failures are a natural part of life, but they can also be an opportunity for growth. He believed that by planting vines around the house, he could turn a mistake into beauty, and he encouraged John to do the same with his work.

The Wisdom of Frank Lloyd Wright

Mr. Smith's advice may have seemed unconventional, but it was based on a timeless wisdom that has been passed down from one generation of architects to the next.

One of the most famous practitioners of this philosophy was Frank Lloyd Wright, the American architect who designed some of the most iconic buildings of the 20th century. Wright believed that architecture should be in harmony with nature, and he used natural elements like light, water, and greenery to create a sense of peace and tranquility.

Wright also believed that mistakes were an inevitable part of the creative process. He once said, "An expert is a man who has made all the mistakes which can be made in a very narrow field."

Wright's advice to young architects was to embrace their mistakes and learn from them. He encouraged them to experiment and take risks, and he believed that by doing so, they could create something truly unique and beautiful.

The Importance of Going Far from Home

Another piece of advice that Wright often gave to young architects was to go as far away from home as possible to build their first buildings. He believed that by exploring new cultures and environments, architects could broaden their horizons and develop a deeper understanding of human nature.

This advice is just as relevant today as it was in Wright's time. In today's globalized world, architects have access to a wealth of information and resources from all around the world. By traveling and experiencing different cultures firsthand, they can gain insights and perspectives that would be impossible to achieve through books or online research.

Examples of Embracing Mistakes

There are many examples of architects who have embraced their mistakes and turned them into opportunities for growth and beauty. One such example is the Sagrada Familia in Barcelona, Spain.

The Sagrada Familia is a massive church that has been under construction since 1882. Over the years, it has been plagued with setbacks and delays, and it has undergone many changes in design and leadership.

One of the most significant changes occurred in 1926, when the original architect, Francisco de Paula del Villar, resigned, and the work was taken over by a young, unknown architect named Antoni Gaudi. Gaudi's vision for the church was radically different from Villar's, and it required a complete overhaul of the existing design.

Despite this setback, Gaudi saw it as an opportunity to create something truly unique and beautiful. He spent the rest of his life working on the Sagrada Familia, and he poured his heart and soul into every detail of the building.

Today, the Sagrada Familia is considered one of the most beautiful and awe-inspiring buildings in the world, and it is a testament to the power of embracing mistakes and turning them into opportunities for growth and beauty.

Conclusion

  1. Embrace your mistakes and learn from them.
  2. Explore new cultures and environments to broaden your horizons.
  3. Use natural elements like light, water, and greenery to create a sense of peace and tranquility.

By following these principles, you can create truly remarkable buildings that not only inspire but also bring joy and beauty to those around you.
