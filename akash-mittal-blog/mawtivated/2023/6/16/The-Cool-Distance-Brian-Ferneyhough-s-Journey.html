Background

Brian Ferneyhough was born in the UK in 1943, where he began his formal music education with the violin. In the mid-1960s, he studied with the composer Ton de Leeuw, where he became interested in composing. He pursued further education at the Royal Academy of Music in London and later at the University of California, San Diego.

"When I left Europe in 1987, I did so with the thought that my relevance as a composition teacher would benefit from a certain cool distance to certain tendencies I had been observing for several years with increasing disquiet."

Ferneyhough's music is heavily influenced by his love of literature, as well as his interest in philosophy and linguistic theory. His style is often referred to as 'complexity' or 'new complexity,' characterized by intricate rhythms, complex harmonies, and elaborate notation. Ferneyhough's music is performed internationally and has been recorded by many orchestras worldwide. In addition to composition, Ferneyhough has also served as a professor of music at various universities, including the University of California and Stanford University.

I had the privilege of attending one of Ferneyhough's lectures during my graduate studies in music. I still remember his passion for the subject and his willingness to push his students to think beyond the boundaries of traditional music theory. He encouraged us to explore different musical styles, and to find our unique voice within the vast world of music. His approach to teaching was always honest and direct, even if it meant telling a student that their work wasn't quite up to par. Ferneyhough's dedication to his art and his students continues to inspire me.

Examples and Tips

If you're a composer or musician looking to push boundaries, there are a few key things you can do to bring your work to the next level:

  • Read widely: expose yourself to various forms of literature and non-musical arts to inspire your creative process.
  • Experiment: don't be afraid to try new things, even if they're unconventional or challenging.
  • Collaborate: work with other musicians and artists to broaden your perspective and learn new concepts and techniques.
  • Refine your skills: take lessons or attend workshops to continue improving your abilities.
  • Break down conventions: challenge the norms of music composition by exploring new techniques, and forms of expression.

By following these steps, you can grow your potential and discover a unique voice that is both authentic and innovative.

Conclusion: Three Takeaways

  1. Embrace complexity: Ferneyhough's work has shown us that music doesn't always need to be straightforward or predictable. By pushing boundaries and experimenting, we can create work that is unique and inspiring.
  2. Never stop learning: even if you're a seasoned artist, there's always room for improvement. Attend workshops, learn new techniques, and refine your skills to grow in your craft.
  3. Find your unique voice: don't be afraid to take risks and explore unconventional methods. By breaking down conventions, we can discover our own authentic and innovative style.
