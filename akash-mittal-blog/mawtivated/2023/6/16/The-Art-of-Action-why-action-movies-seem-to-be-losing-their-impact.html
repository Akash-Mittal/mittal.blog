The Art of Action



Why action movies seem to be losing their impact

The problem with modern action scenes

A lot of action movies today seem to have scenes that just lead up to the action. Explosions, chases, and fights are often the focus, leaving little room for character development, plot, or emotional resonance.

This is not to say that action movies can't be great. Some of the best action movies of all time, from Die Hard to The Matrix, combine thrilling action sequences with compelling characters, meaningful themes, and unforgettable moments. What sets them apart from many modern action movies is their ability to balance spectacle with substance.

Unfortunately, too many action movies today seem to be content with just delivering the spectacle. They're like fireworks displays: impressive in the moment, but forgettable soon after.

The Jason Statham story

"I didn't want to just be a physical entity, and I didn't want to be in a summer movie where I'm just CGI or a fantasy character."

Perhaps no one understands the art of action better than Jason Statham. The British actor, known for his tough-guy roles in movies like The Transporter and Crank, has been a fixture in Hollywood for over two decades.

What sets Statham apart from many other action stars is his commitment to doing his own stunts. He's known for performing dangerous feats like hanging off a helicopter, jumping out of a moving car, and fighting on top of a plane. But Statham doesn't just rely on his physical prowess to carry his movies.

"I'm always trying to find the human angle, the thing that an audience can relate to. The action is there to serve the story, not the other way around."

Statham knows that the action in his movies needs to have a purpose beyond just looking cool. It needs to advance the story, reveal something about the characters, or create an emotional connection with the audience.

The power of a well-crafted action scene

So what makes a great action scene? There's no one-size-fits-all answer, but there are certain elements that tend to make action scenes more memorable:

  • Stakes: The action needs to have real consequences for the characters. If there's no sense of danger or risk, the audience won't be invested.
  • Emotion: The action needs to be grounded in the characters' emotions. If we don't care about what they're fighting for, the action will feel hollow.
  • Imagination: The action needs to be creative and surprising. If we've seen it all before, the action will feel stale.
  • Story: The action needs to move the plot forward or reveal something new about the characters. If it's just there for its own sake, the audience will tune out.
  • Character: The action needs to reflect the personalities and motivations of the characters. If it's generic or impersonal, the action will feel disconnected from the story.

When all of these elements come together, the result can be an action scene that is not only thrilling, but also meaningful and memorable. Some examples of great action scenes that balance spectacle with substance include:

  • The hallway fight in Oldboy
  • The opening chase in Mad Max: Fury Road
  • The final battle in Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon
  • The train sequence in The Fugitive
  • The rooftop chase in Casino Royale

The way forward for the action genre

So what can action movies do to regain their impact? Here are three suggestions:

  1. Think beyond the action: Action scenes are important, but they shouldn't be the only thing that matters. Focus on developing characters that the audience cares about, and find ways to use action to reveal their strengths, flaws, and motivations.
  2. Push the boundaries: Don't be afraid to take risks and experiment with new forms of action. Whether it's through innovative camera work, unexpected twists, or unconventional settings, a willingness to be creative can set an action movie apart from the crowd.
  3. Remember why we care: Ultimately, action movies are about more than just explosions and fight scenes. They're about the triumph of heroism over evil, the thrill of adventure, and the hope that good can prevail against all odds. Make sure your movie speaks to these fundamental human desires, and you'll have a chance at creating something truly special.
