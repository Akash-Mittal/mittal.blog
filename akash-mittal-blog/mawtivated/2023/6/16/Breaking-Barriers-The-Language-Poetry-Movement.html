Breaking Barriers: The Language Poetry Movement



As a young girl growing up in a small town in Michigan, Diane Wakoski always had a passion for words. She spent hours writing poems in her diary, imagining a world where women had a voice and could break free from the constraints of conventional beauty standards. Little did she know, her words would eventually become part of a movement that challenged those very standards.

The Language Poetry Movement emerged in the late 1960s and early 1970s as a response to the dominant literary culture of the time. It rejected the idea that language should be used solely for communication and instead embraced language as an art form in its own right. Language poets sought to break down the barriers between words and reality, exploring the ways in which language shapes our perceptions of the world around us.

One of the key aims of the Language Poetry Movement was to challenge conventional ideas of beauty. As Wakoski puts it, "those ideas contain a certain attitude toward women, certain attitudes toward sex, certain attitudes toward race, etc." Language poets sought to create a new aesthetic that was more inclusive and diverse, reflecting the complexity and diversity of human experience. They rejected the idea that beauty should be defined by a narrow set of standards, and instead embraced the beauty of the unconventional.

Feminism and the Language Poetry Movement

Many of the poets involved in the Language Poetry Movement were feminists, and they saw their work as a way of challenging the patriarchal structures of society. They sought to create a space where women's voices could be heard, and where the experiences of women could be explored and celebrated. Through their poetry, they protested against the ways in which women had been objectified and silenced by the dominant culture, and they created a new language that reflected the realities of women's lives.

For Wakoski, the Language Poetry Movement was particularly significant in that it allowed her to explore her own experiences of trauma and suffering. Through her poetry, she was able to confront the difficulties she had faced as a survivor of sexual abuse, and to find a way of expressing the pain and anger that had been silenced for so long.

Examples of Language Poetry

The Language Poetry Movement produced some of the most innovative and challenging poetry of the twentieth century. Here are just a few examples:

  • Charles Bernstein's "Artifice of Absorption" (1987), which challenges the idea that language can be a transparent medium for communication, and instead sees it as a complex and unstable system of signs.
  • Lyn Hejinian's "My Life" (1987), a long poem that explores the relationship between language, memory and identity, and challenges the idea that our lives can be neatly divided into discrete moments and events.
  • Leslie Scalapino's "The Woman Who Could Read the Minds of the Dogs" (1990), a powerful poem that confronts the reader with the violence and oppression that lie at the heart of our culture.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the Language Poetry Movement was a significant moment in the history of American literature, and it continues to influence poets and artists today. Through their commitment to breaking down barriers and challenging conventional ideas of beauty, the language poets created a new language that reflects the richness and diversity of human experience. As we continue to grapple with issues of gender, race, and power, the language poets offer us a powerful reminder of the transformative potential of language and art.

"Poetry evokes the experience of painting and the visual arts. It is language freed from the need to make sense, language unbound from the requirement to be linear, to move logically from point A to point B."

- Diane Wakoski
