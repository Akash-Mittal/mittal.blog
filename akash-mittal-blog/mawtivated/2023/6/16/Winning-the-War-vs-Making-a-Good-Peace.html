The Paradox of War and Peace: How to Win the War and Make a Good Peace



It was a sunny day in Afghanistan, where I had the honor of serving as a peacekeeping soldier. My team and I were assigned to a remote village in the northern region of the country. Our mission was to provide security and support to the local population, and help them rebuild their lives after years of conflict. As we walked through the rugged terrain, we could see the scars of war all around us: burned-out buildings, abandoned fields, and empty villages.

But amid the devastation, we found something inspiring: the resilience and determination of the Afghan people. They were eager to work with us, learn from us, and build a better future for their children. They showed us that, even in the midst of war and chaos, there is always hope for peace and prosperity.

This experience taught me a valuable lesson: that winning a war is not enough to create lasting peace. To achieve true peace, we need to adopt a different approach, one that focuses not only on military power but also on diplomacy, dialogue, and compassion. As Winston Churchill once said, "Those who can win a war well can rarely make a good peace and those who could make a good peace would never have won the war." In this article, we will explore the paradox of war and peace, and discover the key strategies for winning wars and making good peace.

The Fine Line between Victory and Defeat

Winning a war is a complex and challenging task, one that requires a combination of military prowess, strategic planning, and operational excellence. From ancient battles to modern conflicts, history has shown us that the key to victory lies in several factors, including superior firepower, well-trained troops, and sound leadership. However, even the best-laid plans can falter in the face of unforeseen challenges, such as guerrilla warfare, asymmetric threats, or political instability. In such cases, it is not enough to rely on brute force alone; rather, a more nuanced and flexible approach is needed to achieve the desired outcome.

One example of this approach is the successful campaign against ISIS in Iraq and Syria. The coalition forces led by the United States combined aerial bombardments and special operations with local ground forces, diplomatic pressure, and economic sanctions, to defeat the terrorist group and restore stability to the region. This strategy not only targeted the military capabilities of ISIS but also its ideology, propaganda, and support networks, thus addressing the root causes of the conflict and preventing its resurgence.

The Art of Making a Good Peace

Despite the challenges of winning a war, the real test comes after the fighting stops. The transition from war to peace is a delicate and complex process, one that requires patience, sensitivity, and creativity. To make a good peace, we need to address the underlying causes of the conflict, promote reconciliation and forgiveness, and build sustainable institutions and systems that can ensure social justice and economic development.

Unfortunately, many peace processes have failed or faltered due to a lack of political will, inadequate resources, or external interference. One example of this is the failed peace talks between the Colombian government and the FARC rebels, which collapsed several times before a final agreement was reached in 2016. The challenges of this process included trust-building, disarmament, and socio-economic integration, as well as the need to address the legacy of violence and injustice that fueled the conflict. Despite these obstacles, the parties were able to negotiate a comprehensive peace agreement that addressed the root causes of the conflict and paved the way for a more stable and peaceful society.

Conclusion: How to Win the War and Make a Good Peace in 3 Steps

  • Step 1: Define your objectives and strategy. Before engaging in a war or peace process, it is crucial to be clear about your goals, values, and priorities. This includes assessing the strengths and weaknesses of your adversary, as well as identifying your allies, partners, and resources.
  • Step 2: Build trust, communication, and cooperation. Whether you are fighting a war or negotiating a peace agreement, it is essential to establish a constructive and respectful dialogue with all parties involved. This requires active listening, empathy, and flexibility, as well as a willingness to compromise and find common ground.
  • Step 3: Empower the people and address the root causes of conflict. To achieve lasting peace, we need to empower the local communities, promote human rights, and address the root causes of conflict, such as poverty, inequality, and injustice. This includes providing education, health care, and social services, as well as supporting economic development and environmental sustainability. By doing so, we can create a more just, equitable, and peaceful world for all.
