The Power of Destruction - Honoring Walt Whitman's Style



Imagine a world where we all wrote the same way. A world where every sentence was structured the same and every story sounded the same. It would be dull and monotonous. Luckily, the world of creative writing is filled with unique styles and voices that add interest and diversity to the art. But how can we develop our own style and voice?

According to Walt Whitman, the key to developing your own unique style is to learn from your teacher's style and then destroy it. In his words, "He most honors my style who learns under it to destroy the teacher." Let's dive into what this means and how it can help you in your writing journey.

Story Time

Before we jump into the nitty-gritty, let me share a story that illustrates the idea of destroying your teacher's style.

In high school, I had a creative writing teacher who had a very specific style of writing. She loved long and flowery sentences, filled with metaphors and similes. At first, I tried to emulate her style, thinking that it was the "right" way to write. But as I read more and explored my own interests and passions, I realized that her style didn't fit with my own voice.

One day, I submitted a short story to her that was written in a completely different style - short, choppy sentences with no metaphors or flowery language. I was nervous about how she would react, but when she returned my story, she wrote, "This is your best work yet. Your voice is finally shining through." It was in that moment that I realized the power of destroying your teacher's style.

Walt Whitman

Now, let's talk about the man who inspired the idea: Walt Whitman. Whitman was an American poet who lived in the 19th century and is considered one of the most influential poets in American history. His most famous work, "Leaves of Grass," is known for its free verse style and celebration of nature and democracy.

Whitman believed that the key to becoming a great poet was to find your own voice and style. He encouraged his students and readers to learn from his own style, but not to copy it. Instead, he wanted them to take what they learned from him and use it as a foundation to create their own unique style.

Why Destroy?

So, why destroy your teacher's style? It seems like a harsh term, but it's not about disrespecting your teacher or their style. Instead, it's about taking what you've learned and making it your own. When you try to copy someone else's style exactly, you're not allowing your own voice to shine through. You're not being true to yourself.

By destroying your teacher's style, you're breaking down the walls that have been built up around your own creativity. You're giving yourself permission to experiment with different styles and find what works for you. It's a process of trial and error, but it's worth it in the end when you find your own unique voice and style.

Examples

Let's take a look at some examples of famous writers who have destroyed their teacher's style:

  • Ernest Hemingway - Hemingway's writing style was known for its simplicity and straightforwardness. He learned from his journalism background and destroyed the flowery language and complex sentences that were popular in his time.
  • Margaret Atwood - Atwood's writing style is known for its feminist themes and dystopian settings. She destroyed the traditional structures and narratives that she learned in her early education, allowing her to create unique and thought-provoking stories.
  • J.K. Rowling - Rowling's writing style is known for its magical, whimsical tone. She destroyed the traditional conventions of children's literature, creating a new genre that has captured the imaginations of millions.

Conclusion

In conclusion, here are three key takeaways from Whitman's idea of destroying your teacher's style:

  1. Learn from your teacher's style, but don't copy it exactly. Use it as a foundation to create your own unique voice and style.
  2. Be true to yourself. Your writing should reflect your personality, interests, and passions.
  3. Experiment with different styles and structures. Breaking down the walls around your creativity can lead to great discoveries.

    4. Remember, the beauty of creative writing lies in its diversity of styles and voices. By destroying your teacher's style, you're contributing to the rich tapestry of literature.
