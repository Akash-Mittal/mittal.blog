The Power of WASP and the Impact of Social Class



It was a beautiful day in New York City. I was on my way to a meeting with a potential client when I spotted a homeless man asking for money. I usually walk right past them, but for some reason, I stopped to listen to his story. It turns out that he was a former Wall Street executive who lost everything during the 2008 financial crisis. His story left me feeling inspired and motivated to use my platform to bring attention to the often-overlooked issue of social class.

As Christopher Hitchens once said, "WASP is the only ethnic term that is in fact a term of class." The acronym stands for White Anglo-Saxon Protestant and is often associated with the elite upper class in America. However, the term has a long and complex history, making it difficult to fully grasp its true meaning.

The Origins of WASP

The term WASP first emerged in the 1960s as a way to describe the dominant culture and social class in America. The acronym was used to refer to wealthy, white, Anglo-Saxon Protestants who were part of the traditional power structures in the country.

While the term WASP may seem innocuous at first glance, it is a loaded term that carries with it a complex history. For many, the term represents a rigid and elitist class system that favors those who come from specific backgrounds and families.

The Power of Social Class

The power of social class cannot be underestimated. Those who come from wealthy and influential families often have access to resources and connections that are out of reach for others. The concept of "old money" is a perfect example of how social class can be passed down through generations, creating a cycle of privilege that perpetuates itself.

Furthermore, those who are born into wealthy families often have access to the best education, health care, and other opportunities. This can create a stark contrast between those who come from less privileged backgrounds, who may struggle to access even basic necessities.

The Impact of WASP on Society

The impact of WASP on society cannot be ignored. For many, the acronym represents a class system that is inherently unfair and favors those who are already privileged. This can lead to feelings of resentment, frustration, and anger among those who feel that they are left out of the elite social structures in America.

Furthermore, the impact of WASP can be seen across multiple avenues, from education to politics to religion. Those who come from privileged backgrounds often hold positions of power and influence, which can perpetuate the cycle of privilege and inequality that exists today.

Examples of WASP in Action

  • The Skull and Bones society at Yale University: This elite secret society is made up of only 15 members per year and has produced some of the most influential people in American history.
  • The Bush family: The Bush family is a perfect example of the power of old money. From Prescott Bush, who made his fortune in finance, to George W. Bush, who served as President, the Bush family has been a major force in American politics for generations.
  • Ivy League Schools: Ivy League schools are well-known for their exclusivity and are often seen as a symbol of the elite upper class in America. Only a small percentage of applicants are accepted each year, and the vast majority of students come from wealthy families.

Conclusion: What We Can Do to Address Social Class

  1. Education: Education is key when it comes to addressing social class. We need to focus on providing equal opportunities to all students, regardless of their backgrounds.
  2. Diversity: We need to work to create more diversity in positions of power and influence. This means actively seeking out and promoting individuals from diverse backgrounds.
  3. Empathy: Finally, we need to work on developing empathy for those who come from different backgrounds than ourselves. By understanding the experiences of others, we can work to break down the barriers that separate us.
