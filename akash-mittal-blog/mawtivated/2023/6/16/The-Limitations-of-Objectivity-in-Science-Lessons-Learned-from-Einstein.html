The Limitations of Objectivity in Science: Lessons Learned from Einstein



Albert Einstein is one of the most recognizable names in science. He is remembered for his theory of general relativity, the formula E=mc^2, and his highly quotable remarks on the nature of the universe. But despite his many contributions to the field, there is one aspect of Einstein's worldview that remains problematic: his commitment to the objective view of the physical world.

An Inspiring Story

John Polkinghorne, a former professor of mathematical physics at Cambridge University, has a unique perspective on this issue. Prior to his career in academia, Polkinghorne was a member of the Royal Army Chaplains' Department, serving in the British Army during the Korean War. It was during this time that he first became interested in the intersection of science and theology.

After leaving the Army, Polkinghorne pursued a PhD in physics and began a long and distinguished career as a scientist, earning numerous accolades and awards. But he never lost his interest in theology, and eventually left academia to become an Anglican priest. Today, he is a leading voice in the field of science and religion, and has authored several books on the subject.

The Objectivity of the Physical World

As Polkinghorne notes, Einstein was famously committed to the idea that the physical world was objective. That is, he believed that the laws of physics existed independently of human observers, and that their truth could be determined through empirical observation and experimentation.

On the surface, this may seem like a reasonable and even admirable view. After all, science has given us incredible insights into the workings of the universe, from the behavior of subatomic particles to the movements of galaxies. It is responsible for life-saving medical treatments, advanced technologies, and an ever-increasing understanding of the natural world.

But as Polkinghorne argues, there are limitations to the objective view of the physical world. For one thing, human beings are not passive observers of the universe. Our observations are inevitably influenced by our own biases, perspectives, and experiences. This can lead to cherry-picking data, confirmation bias, and other types of errors in scientific research.

Moreover, the objective view of the physical world inherently neglects the role of subjectivity in scientific discovery. As Polkinghorne puts it, "The scientist is like a man climbing a mountain who slowly discovers the various contours of its surface as he ascends, rather than like a god surveying the whole range from above."

and Case Studies

To illustrate his point, Polkinghorne shares several personal anecdotes and case studies from his own career. One of the most notable involves his work in the field of quantum mechanics. As he notes, this branch of physics is notoriously difficult to interpret, and has often been the subject of heated debates among scientists.

But rather than viewing this as a limitation of the field, Polkinghorne argues that it is precisely because of its subjectivity that quantum mechanics has been so fruitful. By allowing for multiple interpretations and viewpoints, it has spurred new discoveries and breakthroughs.

He also points to the role of intuition and creativity in scientific discovery. While empirical observation is certainly important, it is often the case that scientific breakthroughs come from flashes of insight or "aha!" moments. These cannot be simply reduced to objective data, but are rather the product of a scientist's subjective intuition and imagination.

Conclusion: Three Key Points

So what can we learn from Einstein and Polkinghorne's views on the objectivity of the physical world? Here are a few key takeaways:

  1. The objective view of the physical world may be useful, but it is not the whole story. Human subjectivity and perspective are also important factors in scientific research.
  2. The limitations of the objective view can actually be a source of creativity and innovation. By allowing for multiple interpretations and perspectives, science can often make breakthroughs that would not be possible otherwise.
  3. Finally, it is important to remember that science does not exist in a vacuum. Ethical considerations, cultural values, and political realities can all influence scientific research and conclusions.

Practical Tips

Here are some practical tips for scientists and science enthusiasts looking to explore the limitations of the objective view:

  • Be open to multiple interpretations and viewpoints. Don't assume that the objective view is the only valid one.
  • Embrace subjectivity and intuition. These are often powerful tools in scientific discovery.
  • Consider the larger context in which science operates. Don't neglect the role of culture, ethics, and politics in shaping scientific research and conclusions.
