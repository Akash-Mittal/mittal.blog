The Conscience: To What Extent Should We Follow It?



What Is Conscience?

In simple terms, conscience is an inner voice that tells us right from wrong based on our moral and ethical values. It's that little voice in our head that nudges us to do the right thing, even when it's difficult or unpopular. As human beings, we're wired to have a conscience, but the extent to which we listen to it varies from person to person.

The Inspiring Story of Maria

Maria was a single mother who worked hard to provide for her two children. She had a job as a customer service representative, which paid her enough to cover the rent and buy groceries, but not much else. One day, she received a call from a customer who had accidentally overpaid his bill by $500. The customer asked her to refund the money to him, but Maria noticed that the customer had a history of late payments and seemed to be struggling financially. She felt a pang of conscience and decided to waive the $500 instead of refunding it.

"I knew it was the right thing to do, even though it went against company policy," Maria later said. "I would rather lose my job than betray my values."

Maria's decision to follow her conscience paid off in unexpected ways. The customer was grateful for her kindness and recommended her to his friends and family. Within weeks, Maria was flooded with phone calls from new customers who had heard about her exceptional service. Her manager also heard about her act of kindness and was impressed. Instead of firing her, he promoted her to a higher-paying position that allowed her to give her children a better life.

Examples of Conscience in Action

The following are some examples of how conscience can guide our decision making:

  • A doctor who refuses to prescribe opioids to a patient, even though it would be easier than exploring alternative treatments, because he or she knows the risks of addiction.
  • A politician who speaks out against a bill that would harm the environment, even though it would be politically expedient to support it.
  • A student who reports a cheating classmate, even though it could lead to social ostracism or retaliation.
  • A journalist who refuses to publish a story that's based on unverified sources, even though it would generate more clicks.

Practical Tips for Listening to Your Conscience

  • Take time to reflect on your values and principles. What's most important to you in life?
  • When faced with a difficult decision, ask yourself what you would tell a friend or family member to do in the same situation.
  • Consider the consequences of your actions on others. Will your decision harm anyone else?
  • Try to distance yourself from external pressures or biases. What do you really believe, independent of what others might think?
  • If you're still unsure, seek advice from someone you trust who shares your values.

Conclusion: Striking the Right Balance

So, to what extent should we follow our conscience? The answer, like most things in life, is that it depends. While conscience can be a guiding force for good, it's important to balance it with reason, critical thinking, and empathy for others. Blindly following our conscience without considering the consequences can lead to harmful or impractical outcomes. But ignoring our conscience altogether can lead to a life devoid of purpose and meaning.

In summary, here are three key takeaways:

  1. Conscience is an important aspect of decision making, but it should be balanced with other factors such as reason, critical thinking, and empathy.
  2. Practical tips such as self-reflection and seeking advice can help us listen to our conscience more effectively.
  3. We should strive to make decisions that align with our values and principles while also considering the impact on others.
