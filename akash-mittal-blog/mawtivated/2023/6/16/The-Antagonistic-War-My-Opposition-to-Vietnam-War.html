The Antagonistic War: My Opposition to Vietnam War



It was the 1960s, a time of great turmoil in American society. We were in the middle of the Cold War, fighting against Communism, and the government's involvement in the Vietnam War was escalating rapidly. Many young Americans were being drafted into the military, and their families and friends were seeing them off with a heavy heart.

I was one of those people who was opposed to the war in Vietnam from the beginning. In fact, I was so opposed to it that I initially refused President Nixon's urgings for me to go there. My story of opposition to the war is one that needs to be told, to inspire others that the power of resistance can change the course of history.

Background

I was born in 1925 in New York City, the youngest of three children. From an early age, I was exposed to the world of entertainment, as my parents were both entertainers. I grew up singing and dancing, and made my professional debut at the age of three in the vaudeville circuit.

As a young man, I served in the United States Army during World War II, and when I returned to civilian life, I resumed my career in entertainment. I became a multi-talented performer, known for my singing, dancing, acting, and comedy. I performed on Broadway, in nightclubs, and in films and television shows. I also became involved in the civil rights movement, supporting the cause of racial equality and justice.

Reasons for Opposing the War

It was during the 1960s that I became intensely involved in opposing the war in Vietnam. I couldn't see any justification for sending young American men to a foreign country to fight and die in a war that seemed to have no end in sight. I saw it as a waste of young lives, and as a betrayal of the trust that the people had placed in their government.

Moreover, I knew that the war disproportionately impacted African-American and other minority communities. It seemed that the draft was unfairly targeting young men of color, who were already facing discrimination and inequality in their everyday lives. I felt a moral obligation to stand up for their rights and oppose the war that was unjustly impacting their communities.

Finally, I was inspired by the anti-war movements that were gaining ground across the country. I saw the power of the people when they united against a common goal, and I knew that I had to contribute to this movement in any way I could. I joined other entertainers, such as Jane Fonda and Marlon Brando, in speaking out against the war and supporting the anti-war cause.

Examples of Anti-war Movements

  • March on Washington: In November 1969, I joined over half a million people in the nation's capital for a peaceful anti-war demonstration. I sang "The Theme from Zorba the Greek" and "What the World Needs Now is Love" to a rapturous crowd, and I was proud to be part of this historic moment.
  • Entertainers and Musicians Against the War in Vietnam: I, along with many other entertainers, joined this organization that was dedicated to using the power of the entertainment industry to oppose the war. We organized concerts and events that raised awareness and funds for the anti-war cause, and we used our fame and influence to draw attention to the issue.
  • Draft Resistance: I also supported young men who were resisting the draft, and who were risking imprisonment and other legal consequences for their beliefs. I knew that this was a difficult decision for them, but I respected their courage and conviction.

Conclusion

My opposition to the war in Vietnam was a deeply personal and principled stance, and one that I have never regretted. Looking back on that time, I am proud to have been part of a movement that helped bring an end to a war that should never have happened in the first place. To summarize, my reasons for opposing the war were:

  1. Opposition to the waste of young lives
  2. Concern for the disproportionate impact of the war on young men of color
  3. Inspiration from the anti-war movements of the time

I hope that my story will inspire others to take a stand for their beliefs, and to use their talents and abilities to make a positive difference in the world. We must never forget the lessons of the past, and we must always fight for justice and peace.
