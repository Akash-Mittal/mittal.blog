The Power of a Moratorium: Halting Landfill Permits



It was a hot summer day in 1981 when Ed Rendell, then the mayor of Philadelphia, decided to take a walk to the city's Fairmount Water Works. As he strolled along the Schuylkill River, he couldn't help but notice how polluted the water looked and how bad it smelled. He knew something had to be done to protect this vital resource.

Over the next decade, Rendell initiated a massive overhaul of Philadelphia's water system, including the construction of state-of-the-art sewage treatment plants and the laydown of miles of new distribution pipes. These efforts paid off, and today, the quality of water from the Schuylkill River has improved dramatically.

But Rendell's work on behalf of the environment didn't stop there. During his two terms as governor of Pennsylvania, he was a leading advocate for sustainable energy development and conservation, and he championed policies to protect the state's natural resources from pollution and overuse. One of his biggest achievements was the enactment of a moratorium on new landfill permits in the state, which he signed into law in 2005.

"I think a moratorium probably is legal, and we should probably for a short period of time impose a moratorium so that we don't permit any additional landfill permits for the time being, so we don't exacerbate the problem," Rendell said at the time.

Rendell's decision to impose a moratorium on landfill permits was a bold one, but it was also a necessary one. Landfills are a major source of environmental pollution, from the leaching of harmful chemicals into the soil and groundwater to the release of methane gases into the air, which contribute to climate change.

By stopping the flow of new landfill permits, Rendell was able to give communities and policymakers time to develop more sustainable waste management systems. This allowed them to explore alternatives like recycling, composting, and waste-to-energy technologies, which can help reduce the amount of waste going into landfills and minimize their impact on the environment.

The Benefits of Imposing a Moratorium on Landfill Permits

There are several legal and practical benefits to imposing a moratorium on landfill permits, including:

  • Protection of natural resources: By halting the construction of new landfills, communities can protect their groundwater, soil, and air quality from harmful pollutants.
  • Encouragement of sustainable waste management practices: A moratorium on landfill permits can provide communities with the breathing room they need to explore alternatives like recycling and waste-to-energy technologies, which are better for the environment and can save money over the long term.
  • Opportunity for public input and engagement: A moratorium on landfill permits can give communities and stakeholders the opportunity to have a say in the future of their waste management systems and to work collaboratively with policymakers and industry leaders to find solutions.

Case Study: The Pennsylvania Moratorium

When Rendell signed the moratorium on new landfill permits in 2005, it was greeted with some skepticism from industry leaders and policymakers who worried about the impact on economic development in the state. But over time, the benefits of the moratorium became clear.

Today, Pennsylvania has one of the highest rates of landfill diversion in the country, with over 45% of its municipal solid waste being diverted from landfills through recycling and other sustainable methods. This not only helps protect the environment, but it also creates jobs in industries like recycling and composting, which can be more labor-intensive than landfilling.

The success of the Pennsylvania moratorium has also inspired other states to take action. In 2019, for example, New York State enacted a temporary moratorium on new landfill permits in certain areas to address concerns about environmental justice and public health.

Conclusion

In summary, imposing a moratorium on landfill permits can be an effective way to protect the environment and encourage more sustainable waste management practices. By giving communities and stakeholders time to explore alternatives like recycling and waste-to-energy technologies, a moratorium can help reduce the amount of waste going into landfills and minimize their impact on natural resources. If you're interested in working on this issue in your own community, here are three practical steps you can take:

  1. Research the current state of waste management in your community, including landfill usage rates and diversion rates.
  2. Explore alternatives to landfilling, such as recycling, composting, and waste-to-energy technologies, and reach out to local policymakers and industry leaders to discuss these options.
  3. Advocate for a moratorium on new landfill permits in your community or state, and mobilize others to support this policy change.
