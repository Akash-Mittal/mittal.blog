The Danger of Tribal Politics in the United States



Once upon a time, there was a small town where people from different backgrounds lived harmoniously. The town had a problem with a pothole on one of its main roads. The townspeople decided to hold a meeting to discuss the issue. At the meeting, people from different backgrounds came together to offer solutions.

A young man suggested that the town should pool its resources and hire a contractor to fix the pothole. An old woman suggested that the townspeople should take turns filling the pothole themselves. A middle-aged man suggested that the town should petition the government to help with the problem.

The townspeople discussed these solutions and decided to combine them. They would take turns filling the pothole themselves while they petitioned the government for funds to hire a contractor to fix it permanently. The problem was solved, and the town continued to prosper.

In today's America, however, this kind of harmony seems almost impossible. The country has become sharply divided along ideological and tribal lines, with people more concerned about their perceived differences than their shared goals.

The Rise of Tribal Politics

The story of tribalism in American politics is a complex one. In a sense, it has always been with us: the country's two major political parties have existed for more than 150 years, and politics has always been a rough-and-tumble business.

However, in the last few decades, the divide between the parties has grown wider, and the rhetoric has grown sharper. Polarization has increased, and people have become more entrenched in their positions.

The result is a political landscape in which each side sees the other as an existential threat. Each side has its own news outlets and social media bubbles, and each side has its own language and values.

The result is that politics has become less about the issues and more about identity. People are increasingly defined by their political affiliations, and these affiliations become part of their self-conception.

The Cost of Tribalism

The cost of this tribalization is high. First, it makes compromise and negotiation more difficult. If one side sees the other as evil or illegitimate, it becomes almost impossible to reach an agreement.

Second, it reduces the diversity of opinion. If people only listen to others who share their views, they will never hear alternatives. They will become more entrenched in their positions and less open to change.

Third, it leads to a lack of trust. If people see those who disagree with them as an enemy, they will not trust them. This can lead to a breakdown of social cohesion and a sense of alienation and isolation.

Former Senator Evan Bayh has lived through the transformation of American politics. When he began his career in the 1980s, the parties were not as polarized, and there was more room for compromise.

However, over time, he saw the rise of tribalism and the cost it had on the country. He saw how politicians became less interested in solving problems and more interested in winning elections.

Bayh decided not to seek re-election in 2010, citing his frustration with the dysfunction of the Senate. Since then, he has become an advocate for centrist politics and bipartisanship.

Examples of Tribalism

There are many examples of tribalism in American politics, but here are a few:

  • Democrats and Republicans increasingly live in separate geographic locations, making it less likely they will interact with those who hold different views
  • The impeachment of President Trump was largely along party lines, with few exceptions
  • People often vote for a candidate based on their party affiliation rather than their policies or track record

Conclusion

Here are three practical tips for combating tribalism:

  1. Expose yourself to different perspectives by reading news outlets that don't agree with you or attending events where you can meet people with different views
  2. Acknowledge that people who disagree with you are not your enemies. Try to find common ground and work towards compromise
  3. Vote based on issues and values, not just party affiliation
