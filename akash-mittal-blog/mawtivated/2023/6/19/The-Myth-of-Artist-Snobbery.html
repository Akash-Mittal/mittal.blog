The Myth of Artist Snobbery



An inspiring story

David Byrne, the singer-songwriter and former lead of the Talking Heads, often reflects on his career and the role of artists in society. In a recent interview, he shared a story that stuck with him over the years.

When I was starting out, I played in a band that did a lot of gigs in cafes and bars. One night, a businessman came up to me and offered me a gig at a corporate event. I sneered at him and turned down the offer. I thought corporate gigs were beneath me and my art. A few months later, I was struggling to pay my rent, and I regretted my decision. I realized that I had created this false narrative in my head about artists being somehow above commercial work. In reality, we all need to make a living, and there's nothing wrong with accepting gigs from the business community.

Byrne's story highlights a common misconception about artists. Many outsiders view them as elitist or snobby, looking down on anything that doesn't fit their particular brand of creativity. But the truth is much more nuanced.

The reality of artist-business relationships

Artists and businesses have had a complicated relationship for centuries. Some artists, like Byrne, reject any association with the corporate world. Others are more willing to collaborate, seeing it as a way to expand their reach and make a living. The reality is that there's no one-size-fits-all answer to the question of whether artists should work with businesses.

On the one hand, some argue that artists have a social responsibility to remain independent and uncorrupted by commercialism. They see art as a form of resistance against the status quo and believe that any association with businesses would compromise that vision. But others argue that artists have the right to make a living and that aligning themselves with businesses is a way to achieve that goal. They see art as a form of entrepreneurship and believe that partnering with businesses is a way to grow their brand and reach a wider audience.

The risks and rewards of collaboration

For those artists who decide to work with businesses, there are both risks and rewards. On the risk side, there's the danger of being co-opted by a company's agenda, losing one's artistic integrity, or being seen as a sell-out by the artistic community. But on the reward side, there's the potential for financial stability, exposure to new audiences, and the chance to influence business practices for the better.

One example of a successful artist-business collaboration is the recent partnership between musician Childish Gambino and Adidas. The rapper designed a line of sneakers for the sportswear brand, incorporating his unique sense of style and creativity into the shoe designs. The collaboration was a hit, with the shoes selling out quickly and generating buzz in both the music and fashion worlds.

The way forward

So what's the way forward for artists and businesses? Instead of seeing each other as adversaries, they should view each other as potential collaborators. Artists bring a unique perspective and creativity to the table, while businesses bring resources and exposure. By working together, they can create something that's greater than the sum of its parts.

Here are three practical tips for artists and businesses who want to collaborate:

  • Be clear about your vision and values. Before entering into any partnership, make sure that you and your collaborator share the same goals and values.
  • Communicate openly and honestly. Don't be afraid to speak up if you feel like your artistic integrity is being compromised or if your collaborator is not living up to their end of the bargain.
  • Think beyond the transaction. A successful collaboration should be more than just a financial exchange. It should be a meaningful and long-lasting relationship that benefits both parties.
