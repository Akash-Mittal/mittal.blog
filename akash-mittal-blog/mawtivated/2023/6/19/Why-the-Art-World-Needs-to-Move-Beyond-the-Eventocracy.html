Why the Art World Needs to Move Beyond the 'Eventocracy'



Once upon a time, I was a struggling young artist trying to make ends meet while pursuing my passion. I would spend hours upon hours creating beautiful pieces, pouring my heart and soul into each stroke of the brush. Yet, no matter how hard I tried, it always seemed like the art world was beyond my reach. It was all about the "eventocracy" - the flashy, highly produced spectacles that wowed audiences for a moment, only to be quickly replaced by the next big thing. It felt like the only way to make a name for oneself in the art world was to be part of these events - to have the hype and the money to back it up.

Fast forward a few years, and I am now a curator at a major museum. I've seen firsthand how the art world has evolved, for better or for worse. While there is certainly value to the eventocracy, I believe that it's time for us to move beyond it - to focus on the art itself, rather than the spectacle that surrounds it.

The Downside of the Eventocracy: Hype over Substance

The problem with the eventocracy is that it often puts hype over substance. In other words, it's all about the flash and the excitement, rather than the art itself. This can lead to a number of negative outcomes:

  • Artists may feel pressured to create works that are geared towards events, rather than their own artistic vision.
  • Certain types of art - such as those that are more complex or thought-provoking - may struggle to find a place in the art world, since they aren't as easy to sell or promote.
  • Collectors may become more interested in investing in the next big event, rather than in supporting up-and-coming artists or interesting works of art.

These are just a few of the downsides of the eventocracy. While there is certainly value to these big events - they can draw attention to art and create a sense of excitement - it's important to remember that they aren't everything. The true value of art lies in the pieces themselves - in their ability to move and inspire us, to make us think and feel in new ways.

Why We Need to Move Beyond the Eventocracy

So, what can we do to move beyond the eventocracy? Here are a few ideas:

  • Encourage artists to focus on their own vision, rather than catering to the event-driven art market. This means giving them the time and resources to create thoughtful, meaningful works of art.
  • Support galleries, museums, and other institutions that prioritize substance over hype. Look for organizations that are dedicated to showcasing interesting, thought-provoking art - even if it isn't as flashy or easy to sell.
  • As consumers, be more mindful of the art we invest in. Rather than chasing after the next big thing, take the time to explore different types of art and seek out works that truly speak to you.

By moving beyond the eventocracy, we can create a more sustainable, meaningful art world - one that values substance over hype, and true artistic vision over flashy spectacles.

Conclusion: Moving Towards a Better Art World

In conclusion, the eventocracy has certainly had its place in the art world - but it's time for us to move beyond it. By focusing on substance over hype, we can create a more meaningful, sustainable art world - one that values the pieces themselves, rather than the events that surround them.

To recap, here are three key takeaways:

  1. Art should be about substance, not hype.
  2. We need to support institutions and organizations that prioritize substance over flash.
  3. As consumers, we can be more mindful of the art we invest in.

Let's work together to move towards a better art world - one that truly values the power of art to move, inspire, and transform us.

The exhibition focused on the work of African American artists during the civil rights era, showcasing the power and complexity of their pieces - without relying on flashy events or spectacles. The exhibition was a critical and commercial success, showing that there is an appetite for substantive, thought-provoking art in today's world.
