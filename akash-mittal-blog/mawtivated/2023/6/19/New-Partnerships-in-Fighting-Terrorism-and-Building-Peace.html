New Partnerships in Fighting Terrorism and Building Peace



On a sunny day of May 2003, a group of young students were sitting at a caf√© in Casablanca, Morocco, discussing their future and dreaming of a better world. Suddenly, a series of bomb blasts shattered their dreams and shattered the lives of dozens of innocent people in the heart of the city. It was one of the deadliest terrorist attacks in the history of the country, and it left a deep scar on the minds and hearts of the Moroccan people.

But out of this tragedy emerged a new spirit of resilience, solidarity, and hope. People of all walks of life, from all corners of the country, came together to condemn the atrocities, to comfort the victims, and to seek ways to prevent such acts of violence from ever happening again. They knew that they could not do it alone, that they needed new partnerships in fighting terrorism and building peace.

The Need for New Partnerships

Terrorism is a complex and multifaceted threat that cannot be defeated by military force alone. It is a social, political, economic, and ideological phenomenon that requires a comprehensive and integrated approach. It is a threat that affects not only the security and stability of countries, but also the dignity and well-being of individuals and communities. It is a threat that cannot be eradicated by isolation, but only by collaboration.

Building partnerships is essential to fighting terrorism and building peace. Partnerships can help to share information, expertise, and resources. They can help to coordinate policies, strategies, and actions. They can help to foster trust, respect, and understanding. They can help to empower people, especially the most vulnerable and marginalized, to resist the lure of extremism and to embrace the values of tolerance, diversity, and human rights.

Partnerships should not be limited to governments and security agencies, but should also involve civil society organizations, religious leaders, business communities, and academic institutions. They should be based on a shared vision and a common purpose, and should respect the principles of equality, accountability, and transparency. They should be inclusive, participatory, and responsive to the needs and aspirations of all stakeholders.

Examples of Successful Partnerships

There are many examples of successful partnerships in fighting terrorism and building peace. One of them is the Global Counterterrorism Forum (GCTF) which was launched in 2011 as a multilateral platform to strengthen cooperation and capacity-building among countries and stakeholders on all aspects of counterterrorism.

Another example is the Radicalization Awareness Network (RAN) which was established by the European Commission in 2011 as a network of practitioners, policymakers, and experts to share best practices and expertise on the prevention of radicalization and violent extremism.

Yet another example is the Strong Cities Network (SCN) which was launched by the Institute for Strategic Dialogue in 2015 as a global network of cities and municipalities to collaborate and exchange ideas and strategies on preventing and countering violent extremism at the local level.

The Importance of Personal Engagement

But partnerships are not only about institutions and structures, but also about individuals and their personal commitment and engagement. Personal engagement can make a real difference in preventing and countering violent extremism and in promoting peace and tolerance.

Personal engagement can take many forms, such as volunteering, mentoring, advocacy, and education. Personal engagement can help to build bridges of understanding and empathy, to create opportunities for dialogue and cooperation, and to empower people to become agents of change and ambassadors of peace.

Personal engagement requires courage, perseverance, and creativity. It requires the willingness to step out of one's comfort zone, to listen to diverse voices, and to challenge stereotypes and prejudices. It requires the belief in the power of human dignity, diversity, and unity.

"The biggest enemy of tolerance is indifference. When we stop caring, we stop hoping, we stop acting. So let us care, let us hope, let us act. Let us be the change that we want to see in the world." - Anna Lindh

The late Anna Lindh was a Swedish politician and minister of foreign affairs who was known for her strong commitment to human rights, democracy, and peace. She was a firm believer in the power of partnerships and personal engagement in building a better world. Sadly, she was assassinated in 2003 by a lone attacker who opposed her pro-European views.

Conclusion

  • New partnerships are essential to fighting terrorism and building peace.
  • Partnerships should involve diverse stakeholders and be based on shared values and common goals.
  • Personal engagement is a powerful tool to prevent radicalization and promote tolerance and empathy.
