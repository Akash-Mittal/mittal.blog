The Poetry of Philosophy: The Art of Poetic Poetry



An Exploration into the Works of Karl Wilhelm Friedrich Schlegel

Imagine this: You are in a concert hall, surrounded by people from all walks of life, eagerly anticipating the start of the show. The lights dim, and suddenly, a lone figure steps onto the stage. He begins to speak, at first in a soft and measured tone, his words weaving a tapestry of emotion and thought that hypnotizes the audience, drawing them ever closer to his world of wonder and discovery.

Such is the power of the spoken word, and indeed, the written word as well. It is the essence of poetry, the art of capturing the essence of the human soul and distilling it into a form that resonates with all who experience it.

In the world of poetry, there are many genres and styles, each with its own unique flavor and appeal. Some focus on the beauty of language itself, such as the sonnets of Shakespeare or the haikus of Basho. Others delve into the workings of the mind and soul, such as the confessional poetry of Sylvia Plath or the beat poetry of Allen Ginsberg.

But what about the poetry of philosophy?

"The poetry of this one is called philosophical, of that one philological, of a third rhetorical, and so on. Which is then the poetic poetry?" - Karl Wilhelm Friedrich Schlegel

This quote by German philosopher and writer Karl Wilhelm Friedrich Schlegel encapsulates the challenge of defining what it means for poetry to be truly poetic. What makes a poem poetic?

For Schlegel, the answer lies in the connection between poetry and philosophy. He believed that true poetry is a form of philosophical inquiry, a search for meaning and truth that goes beyond mere wordplay and imagery.

At the heart of this idea is the concept of poiesis, which can be loosely translated to mean "creation" or "bringing forth." In the context of poetry, poiesis refers to the act of creating something new and meaningful through language. It is the art of shaping words and ideas into a form that speaks to the human experience in a profound and lasting way.

This brings us back to the question of what makes a poem poetic. Is it the use of metaphor and imagery? The musicality of the language? The emotional resonance it inspires in its readers?

The answer, according to Schlegel, is all of the above. True poetic poetry is a synthesis of many different elements, each working together in harmony to create something greater than the sum of its parts.

To illustrate this point, let's look at a few examples of works that embody the essence of poetic poetry:

  • The Waste Land by T.S. Eliot - This modernist masterpiece blends literary allusions, vivid imagery, and fragmented narrative to create a haunting portrait of the spiritual decay of modern society.
  • The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock by T.S. Eliot - Another work by Eliot, this poem explores the inner turmoil and feelings of inadequacy experienced by a middle-aged man as he contemplates his place in the world.
  • Songs of Innocence and of Experience by William Blake - This collection of poems explores the themes of innocence, experience, and the loss of childhood wonder through deceptively simple language and powerful imagery.
  • The Divine Comedy by Dante Alighieri - A sweeping epic poem that explores the nature of sin, salvation, and divine justice through a series of vividly imagined scenes and characters.

These works, and many others like them, demonstrate the power of poetic poetry to inspire, challenge, and transform the human spirit.

So how can you incorporate the principles of poetic poetry into your own writing? Here are a few tips to get you started:

  • Embrace complexity: True poetic poetry is not afraid to tackle big ideas and complex themes. Don't be afraid to grapple with challenging concepts and explore the depths of human experience in your writing.
  • Show, don't tell: The best poetry does not simply state its meaning outright; it shows us through vivid descriptions and powerful imagery. Use concrete details and sensory language to bring your ideas to life.
  • Experiment with form: The form of a poem can be just as important as its content. Play around with different structures, such as sonnets, haikus, or free verse, to find the one that best suits your ideas.
  • Revise, revise, revise: Writing poetry is a process of constant refinement. Don't be afraid to revise your work extensively until it reaches its full potential.

In Conclusion:

In summary, poetic poetry is the art of blending philosophy and language to create something greater than the sum of its parts. It encompasses elements such as metaphor, imagery, and emotional resonance, and is a powerful tool for exploring the depths of the human experience. By embracing complexity, showing rather than telling, experimenting with form, and revising extensively, you can create works of poetic poetry that inspire, challenge, and transform your readers.

  • True poetic poetry is a form of philosophical inquiry, a search for meaning and truth that goes beyond mere wordplay and imagery.
  • Poiesis, or the act of creating something new and meaningful through language, is at the heart of poetic poetry.
  • Examples of works that embody the principles of poetic poetry include The Waste Land, The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock, Songs of Innocence and of Experience, and The Divine Comedy.
