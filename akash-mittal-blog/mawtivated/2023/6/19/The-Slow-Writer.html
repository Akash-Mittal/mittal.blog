The Slow Writer: An Inspiring Story



There was once a writer named Shelby Foote who took 20 years to write a million and a half words about the Civil War. Foote had what some might consider a fatal flaw for a writer: he wrote slowly. He would only manage to write between five and six hundred words on a good day. This might seem like a small output, but Foote did not let it get to him.

Despite his slow writing pace, he managed to accomplish what few writers have done before: he became a published author. In 1952, Foote published his first novel, "Tournament," and he continued to write fiction until he became interested in history and began his long journey of writing about the Civil War. His monumental work, "The Civil War: A Narrative," was widely praised for its meticulous attention to detail and its insightful analysis of the conflict.

If you are a slow writer yourself, you can take inspiration from Foote's example. He did not let his slow pace get in the way of his writing goals. Instead, he focused on writing a little bit every day, and over time, his efforts paid off in a big way.

Take, for example, the case of Jane Austen. She wrote her novels at a glacial pace, taking years to complete each one. Despite this, her novels are still read and studied today, proving that quality is more important than quantity when it comes to writing.

Practical Tips for Slow Writers

If you're a slow writer, don't despair. There are things you can do to increase your productivity and make the most of your writing time:

  • Set a writing schedule and stick to it. Even if you can only write for a short amount of time each day, make it a habit.
  • Eliminate distractions. Turn off your phone, close your email, and find a quiet place to write.
  • Don't try to write a perfect draft. Instead, focus on getting your ideas down on paper and worry about editing later.

Conclusion

In conclusion, being a slow writer is not a barrier to success. With discipline, dedication, and a willingness to focus on quality over quantity, anyone can achieve their writing goals. Just remember to set a schedule, eliminate distractions, and focus on your ideas rather than your output. Slow and steady wins the race.
