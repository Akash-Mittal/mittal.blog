The Imperfection of Taxation: Why Equality is almost Impossible



Once upon a time, a small town was in dire need of money to fund their local school. The town mayor called for a meeting and proposed a new taxation system that would ensure everyone paid their fair share, no matter their income. The townspeople were ecstatic and quickly agreed. The mayor, eager to implement the new system, divided the town into ten districts based on their property values. Each district was to pay an equal amount of tax that was deemed fair by the mayor.

However, a year later, the town was still in need of money. The mayor, upon investigating their tax system, discovered that certain districts were paying a significantly higher amount of tax than others. The initial excitement and agreement of the townspeople quickly disappeared. Some were angry at the mayor, while others were angry at their fellow townspeople for not paying their fair share.   • The Problem with Equality: The concept of equality is often confused with the concept of fairness. While equality is about treating everyone the same, fairness is about treating everyone according to their needs. What may be equal may not be fair, especially in terms of taxation where people have different incomes and abilities to pay. A flat tax that requires everyone to pay the same percentage of their income may be equal, but it is not fair because it places a greater burden on low-income earners.
  • The Lack of Consensus: Taxation is an issue that affects everyone, but not everyone agrees on what is fair. Some believe that high-income earners should pay more tax to fund public services, while others believe that everyone should pay the same amount regardless of their income. Even within the same group, there may be different opinions on what is fair. It becomes a challenge to create a taxation system that is agreeable to everyone.
  • The Complexity of Taxation: Taxation is a complex subject that requires a thorough understanding of economics and politics. It involves not only collecting revenue but also influencing behavior and promoting social objectives. Taxation can be levied on income, property, consumption, or services, and each form of taxation has its own advantages and disadvantages. Thus, designing a tax system that is simple, effective, and fair is a difficult task.
The wisdom of man never yet contrived a system of taxation that would operate with perfect equality.

- Andrew Jackson

Andrew Jackson, the seventh president of the United States, was renowned for his efforts to reduce taxes and increase the power of the common man. He believed that the wealthy should not be exempt from paying their fair share but also did not want to burden the poor with excessive taxation. Jackson's perspective reflects the difficult balance between equality and fairness in taxation.

To illustrate this point, take the case of two individuals who earn the same amount of money. The first one is a single mother with three children who struggles to make ends meet, while the second one is a bachelor who enjoys an extravagant lifestyle. If a flat tax is imposed, the mother would have to sacrifice basic necessities to pay the same amount of tax as the bachelor. This is neither fair nor just.

On the other hand, if a progressive tax system is applied where the bachelor pays a higher percentage of his income in tax, some may argue that this is punishing success and discouraging hard work. Ultimately, there is no fully satisfying system of taxation that can accommodate everyone's preferences.

In conclusion, the imperfection of taxation lies in the inherent difficulty of balancing equality and fairness, the lack of consensus among taxpayers, and the complexity of taxation matters. However, there are some tips that can make the system more efficient:

  • Broaden the tax base to include more taxpayers rather than increasing the tax rate on existing taxpayers
  • Use technology to simplify tax compliance and reduce errors
  • Provide tax credits and deductions targeted at low-income earners to reduce their tax burden
