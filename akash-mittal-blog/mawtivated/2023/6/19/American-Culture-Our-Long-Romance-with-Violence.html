Let me start this article with a story. You might have heard about the mass shooting that happened at the Route 91 Harvest country music festival in Las Vegas in 2017. A friend of mine was there enjoying the music with a group of people, including his girlfriend. When the shooting started, they all ran in different directions. My friend ended up under a table, terrified for his life.

After the shooting ended, he got out from under the table only to find that his girlfriend was missing. He searched everywhere, but couldn't find her. The panic was unbearable. He didn't know whether she was alive or dead. He called her phone, but it went straight to voicemail. Finally, after hours of waiting and worrying, he got a call from her. She was safe, but had been hiding behind a food truck with a group of strangers.

This story is just one of many that illustrate the violence that permeates American culture. We live in a society that lionizes guns, glorifies war, and revels in revenge. We watch movies and TV shows that are full of violence, play video games that simulate killing, and consume news that bombards us with images of death and destruction.

American Culture: A Love-Hate Relationship with Violence

American culture is torn between our long romance with violence and our terror of the devastation wrought by war and crime and environmental havoc. On the one hand, violence has always been part of our national mythology. We see ourselves as rugged individualists who have triumphed over adversity through force of will and the barrel of a gun. We celebrate the cowboys and outlaws who tamed the Wild West, and we idolize the soldiers who fought and died in wars from the American Revolution to the present day.

On the other hand, we are all too aware of the cost of violence. We know that wars and crime and environmental destruction have left millions dead, injured, or displaced. We recognize that violence begets violence, and that revenge is a never-ending cycle. And we feel the personal toll of violence in our own lives, whether it's from domestic abuse, sexual assault, or street crime.

Examples of American Culture's Love Affair with Violence

Here are some examples of how American culture glorifies violence:

  • The popularity of action movies and TV shows that feature guns, explosions, and martial arts
  • The prevalence of video games that simulate killing and encourage players to be more violent
  • The high rate of gun ownership and the resistance to gun control laws
  • The admiration of military heroes and the constant references to the "sacrifice" of soldiers
  • The acceptance of police brutality and the lack of accountability for police officers who abuse their power

The Devastating Consequences of American Culture's Love Affair with Violence

Here are some of the devastating consequences of American culture's love affair with violence:

  1. The high rate of gun deaths, particularly in mass shootings and suicides
  2. The long-term effects of trauma and PTSD on survivors of violence
  3. The perpetuation of cycles of revenge and retaliation
  4. The erosion of empathy and compassion for others
  5. The normalization of violence as a means of resolving conflicts

Conclusion

American culture's love affair with violence is a complex and multifaceted issue, and there are no easy solutions. However, here are three things we can do to start shifting the culture:

  1. Encourage empathy and understanding for others, particularly those who are marginalized or oppressed
  2. Promote nonviolent means of conflict resolution, such as mediation and dialogue
  3. Support policies and programs that reduce access to guns and address the root causes of violence, such as poverty and mental illness
