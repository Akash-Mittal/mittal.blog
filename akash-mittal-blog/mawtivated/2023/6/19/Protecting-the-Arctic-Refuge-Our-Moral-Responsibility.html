Protecting the Arctic Refuge: Our Moral Responsibility



By Lois Capps

An Inspiring Story

When I was a young girl, my family took a summer trip to the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge. As we explored the stunning landscapes and encountered the wildlife unique to the region, my parents taught me about the importance of protecting the environment for future generations. That experience inspired me to dedicate my life to environmental advocacy and to fight for the preservation of places like the Arctic Refuge.

Our Moral Responsibility

We have a moral responsibility to save wild places like the Arctic Refuge for future generations, and that is why our country has remained committed to its protection for nearly 50 years.

These words, spoken by former Secretary of the Interior Sally Jewell, speak to the core of why we must continue to fight for the preservation of the Arctic Refuge. This pristine wilderness area, located in the northeastern corner of Alaska, is home to a unique collection of plants and animals that are found nowhere else on earth. It is a critical breeding and feeding ground for hundreds of species, including polar bears, caribou, and millions of migratory birds.

But the Arctic Refuge is also under threat. For decades, there have been attempts to open the area to oil and gas drilling, which would irreparably damage this fragile and biodiverse ecosystem. Despite the fact that there is no evidence that drilling in the area would significantly reduce energy prices or make us less reliant on foreign oil, industry interests continue to push for access to the Refuge.

It is up to us, as citizens and stewards of the earth, to protect the Arctic Refuge and ensure that it remains a wild, unspoiled landscape for generations to come.

and Case Studies

One of the most powerful ways to illustrate the importance of protecting the Arctic Refuge is through stories and experiences. For example, there are countless indigenous communities in Alaska whose way of life is intimately tied to the natural rhythms of the Arctic landscape. These communities rely on the caribou and other wildlife in the Refuge for food and cultural practices that have been passed down for generations. If the Refuge were to be opened to drilling, it would not only have devastating environmental consequences, but would also threaten the very survival of these communities.

Another example is the story of the Porcupine caribou herd, which migrates through the Arctic Refuge every year. This herd is a critical source of subsistence for indigenous communities, but it is also a favorite target of predators like wolves and bears. By disturbing the area in which the herd calve and rear their young, drilling activities could severely impact the population and lead to a cascading series of negative consequences throughout the ecosystem.

Conclusion

  1. The Arctic Refuge is a unique and valuable wilderness area that deserves protection from the threats of oil and gas drilling.
  2. We have a moral responsibility to preserve this unspoiled landscape for future generations of plants, animals, and humans.
  3. By engaging in active advocacy and supporting environmental organizations working on the issue, we can make a difference in the fight to protect the Arctic Refuge.
