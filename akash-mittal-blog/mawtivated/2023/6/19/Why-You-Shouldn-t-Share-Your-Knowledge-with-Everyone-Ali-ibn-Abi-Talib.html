Why You Shouldn't Share Your Knowledge with Everyone



There was once a wise man who lived in a village. He had many followers who would come to him for guidance and advice. One day, a young villager came to him and asked, "Master, why do you not share your knowledge with everyone, like other teachers do?"

The wise man smiled and said, "Come with me." He led the young man to a nearby field and plucked a flower. "Who should I give this flower to?" he asked.

"To me, Master!" the young man replied eagerly.

The wise man then plucked another flower and asked the same question. This time, the young man wasn't the only one around, and he saw other villagers nearby who were also eager to speak up.

The wise man chose one of them and said, "I will give this flower to this person because they appreciate its beauty, and I trust they will take care of it. The others do not possess the same qualities, and so I cannot entrust them with something so precious."

The young man understood the lesson. Not everyone is equipped to handle the knowledge that is being shared, and it is up to the giver to discern who is worthy of receiving it.

One of the most famous proponents of this lesson was Ali ibn Abi Talib, a cousin and son-in-law of the Prophet Muhammad, and the fourth caliph of the Islamic empire. He was known for his courage, piety, and wisdom, and was said to have feared no mortal man but Allah himself.

What Knowledge Should You Keep Hidden?

Ali ibn Abi Talib believed that not all knowledge should be shared with everyone. Some examples of this include:

  • Sensitive or Confidential Information: If you've been entrusted with sensitive or confidential information, it's your responsibility to keep it safe. Sharing it with someone who doesn't have a need to know could be disastrous.
  • Personal Failures: Sometimes our failures and mistakes can be too personal to share. You may want to keep these to yourself or restrict the audience to a trusted few.
  • Protected Trade Secrets: If you're in a certain industry, there may be certain trade secrets or competitive advantages that you're privy to. Sharing these could be detrimental to the business, and sometimes even illegal.
  • Proprietary Knowledge: If you've taken the time to learn something valuable that sets you apart from others, you may want to keep this to yourself to ensure that you remain unique and valuable.

Practical Tips for Sharing Knowledge

If you do decide to share your knowledge with others, it's important to do it responsibly. Here are three practical tips to follow:

  1. Assess the recipient: Take the time to assess whether the person is capable and trustworthy to receive the knowledge. Do they have the skills and experience to apply it? Can you trust them to keep it confidential?
  2. Be Clear about Expectations: When sharing information, be clear about what you expect from the recipient. What do they need to do with the knowledge? What are the consequences of not keeping it confidential?
  3. Show Gratitude: If someone has shared valuable knowledge with you, be grateful and show appreciation. This not only acknowledges their effort, but it also encourages them to keep sharing with you in the future.

Conclusion

As you can see, not all knowledge should be shared with everyone. By being discerning about who you share your knowledge with, you can ensure that the information is safeguarded and put to good use. Remember to assess the recipient, be clear about expectations, and show gratitude.
