The Remarkable Progress in Caring for Returned Veterans



While returning home from a long and hard-fought war can bring immense relief to veterans, it can be a daunting challenge to integrate back into civilian life. And, unfortunately, the veterans of Vietnam War were not well received and appreciated for their services. But, as with most things, time brings changes for the better. This is very apparent in the treatment and caring for returned veterans in modern times.

An Inspiring Story of Resilience and Support

Private First Class Joseph Paulus, a returned veteran of the Iraq War, never expected what happened when he went to buy a used car. A salesman at the car dealer not only offered him a 25% discount but also refused to let Paulus pay any money upfront. The salesman's father served in the Korean War, and he knew the sacrifices that Paulus and his fellow veterans have made to protect our country.

This one act of kindness meant the world to Paulus, who had been struggling to adjust to civilian life due to the trauma he experienced during his service. It reminded him that there were people who cared about him and appreciated what he had done. Small acts of kindness like this do wonders, and it is heartening to know that more and more people are following this path.

The Significance of Caring for Returned Veterans

The simple act of welcoming home a returned veteran warmly can go a long way in helping them feel appreciated and valued. It's not just about thanking them for their service; it's about creating a supportive environment where their physical and mental well-being is prioritized.

This is essential because veterans often face unique challenges related to their service, such as physical disabilities, mental health issues, and social isolation. Services that address these issues are critical in helping veterans acclimate to civilian life, find employment opportunities, and attain financial stability. It also helps them heal from the emotional scars of their service.

Examples of How We're Caring for Returned Veterans

We've come a long way in ensuring that our veterans receive the care and support they deserve when they return home. The following are just a few examples of how organizations and individuals are making a difference:

  • The Wounded Warrior Project - A nonprofit that offers veterans a range of services such as mental health counseling, physical rehabilitation, combat stress recovery, veterans benefits counseling, and connecting them with employment opportunities.
  • Veterans Affairs - Provides health care services and benefits to eligible veterans.
  • Employer Support of the Guard and Reserve (ESGR) - Helps veterans find employment opportunities and educates employers on the value of hiring veterans, as they have valuable skills and experience.
  • Operation Homefront - Provides financial assistance and other resources to military families who are struggling financially or facing other challenges.

Barbara Bush's Efforts in Improving the Lives of Veterans

Barbara Bush, the former First Lady of the United States, was a well-known advocate for veterans and their families. She founded the Barbara Bush Foundation for Family Literacy in 1989, which improved the literacy levels of thousands of families in need. She also established the Barbara Bush Foundation for Family Literacy Military Initiative to help military families with young children build literacy skills.

In her own words, Bush once said, "The American dream is about equal access to quality education, and I don't think higher education should be reserved for only the very rich. We need to find ways to help others get their education without taking on burdensome and onerous debt."

The Three Key Takeaways

Here are three key points to remember when thinking about the remarkable progress we've made in caring for returned veterans:

  1. Small acts of kindness can go a long way in making veterans feel appreciated and valued.
  2. We need to prioritize services that address the unique challenges our veterans face and help them acclimate to civilian life.
  3. Organizations and individuals have made significant progress in improving the lives of veterans, but there is still much more to do.

Conclusion

The treatment of veterans in the aftermath of the Vietnam War was not kind or gentle. However, we have come a long way since then. Today, initiatives like the Wounded Warrior Project, Veterans Affairs, ESGR, and Operation Homefront, among others, are doing vital work to make a difference in the lives of returned veterans. But there is still much more we can do. Let's continue to work towards building a kinder and gentler world for these brave men and women, who have sacrificed so much for us.
