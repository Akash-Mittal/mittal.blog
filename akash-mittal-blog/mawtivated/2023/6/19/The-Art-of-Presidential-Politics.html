The Art of Presidential Politics



A Guide to Mastering the Game

It was the pivotal moment in the 2008 presidential campaign – the question posed to Barack Obama and Hillary Clinton during a debate in Texas. Amid the tense exchange, something amazing happened.

Barack Obama, facing criticism from Clinton, responded with an inspiring story. He spoke of a 106-year-old woman he had met while campaigning. She had been born just a generation past slavery, yet had cast her vote for the first black president. She had lived through the civil rights era, segregation, and Jim Crow – and yet maintained her faith in America despite the country's troubled history.

The story was a master class in how to inspire others. It spoke to the voters' emotions and values, earning Obama their trust and admiration. It was proof that the art of presidential politics is about more than just throwing mud.

As John Podhoretz, a visionary political commentator, once wrote: "This has always been the way of presidential politics. The president rises above the fray while his surrogates go on the attack. They throw the spears and fling the mud, he sits upon the throne."

The Importance of Storytelling in Politics

Stories have the power to bring people together or tear them apart. In politics, stories can be used to inspire, to motivate, and to elicit strong emotions from the electorate. A compelling story can be the difference between a candidate's victory and defeat.

Take, for example, George W. Bush's speech on September 20, 2001, in which he spoke of the heroism of rescue workers and the bravery of passengers on Flight 93. "The nation sends its love and compassion to everyone who is here," he said. "Thank you for your hard work. Thank you for making the nation proud, and may God bless America." His story transformed him overnight from a divisive president to a unifying leader.

The Role of Surrogates in Presidential Politics

Presidents must remain above the fray, but their surrogates are free to throw spears and mud on their behalf. Surrogates are allies of the candidate, with a vested interest in their success. As such, they can be more aggressive in their approach. They can attack the opponent, promote their candidate's message and highlight their strengths.

Surrogates can also be a double-edged sword. If they go too far, their candidate can lose credibility and trust. Surrogates must remain on-message and on brand, or risk doing more harm than good.

The Art of Political Dialogue

Presidential politics is a game of dialogue – between the candidate and the voter, between the candidate and the media, and between the candidate and their opponent. Dialogue is not just about what is said, but how it is said, where it is said and when it is said.

Dialogue can be used to create connections, challenge assumptions and build bridges. When Reagan said: "Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall," he was not just challenging the Soviet leader, but the entire world. He was using dialogue to create a new reality, one in which all barriers to freedom were erased.

Conclusion: How to Master the Art of Presidential Politics

  • Start with a compelling story that speaks to the voters' emotions and values.
  • Use surrogates to your advantage, but do not allow them to damage your credibility.
  • Master the art of dialogue, and use it to build bridges and create new realities.
