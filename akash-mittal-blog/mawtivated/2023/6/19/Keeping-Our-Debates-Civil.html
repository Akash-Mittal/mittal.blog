Keeping Our Debates Civil



An Inspiring Story About Respecting Differences and Cultivating Tolerance

In the summer of 2020, a young boy named Jason sat under a shade tree in his local park, eating his favorite sandwich and watching people come and go. As he was admiring the view, he noticed an old man with a hat and a cane walking slowly toward him. Jason was a curious boy, so he asked the old man, "What's the hat for?"

The man replied, "Well, my boy, this hat reminds me of what my mother used to say when I was your age. She used to tell me that when I wear a hat, I have to take it off when I meet someone I respect and want to show respect to."

Jason thought it was a cool tradition, and he decided to adopt it as his own. Later that day, he came across two boys playing near the swings. As he approached them, he took off his hat and greeted them with a smile. The boys were surprised, but they smiled back and introduced themselves. They told Jason they had somewhat different views, but they were still friends. Jason felt happy that he could make new friends and respect different perspectives.

The Importance of Civility in Modern Debates

The story of Jason and the old man is a simple example of how showing respect and tolerance for differences can lead to positive interactions. This is an important lesson for many Americans today, especially when it comes to political discussions and debates. In the past years, tensions have been high, and it seems that people are struggling to communicate effectively with one another. But why is this happening?

According to many experts, there are several reasons. One of them is that people are more exposed to extreme opinions on social media, cable news, and other digital platforms. This creates filter bubbles and echo chambers where people only hear what they want to hear. They also tend to attack and demonize those who have different opinions or beliefs. This pattern of behavior is unhealthy, and it can lead to more division and anger between different groups.

Another reason is that people lack the skills or willingness to have civil conversations. They are quick to judge others, dismiss their perspectives, and resort to name-calling or other forms of disrespectful behavior. This approach is not only unfair but also unproductive. When people feel attacked or ridiculed, they shut down, and their ability to listen and learn from others diminishes.

Finally, people may not see the value or benefits of civility and tolerance. They may think that being "politically correct" or "nice" is a sign of weakness or conformity. They may also feel that their voice is not heard or respected, so they resort to aggressive or irrational tactics to get attention. This attitude not only alienates others but also damages their own reputation and credibility.

Applying Civility in Our Daily Lives

So what can we do to promote civility and tolerance in our daily lives, especially when it comes to politics and debates? Here are some tips:

  • Listen more than you talk. Try to understand the other person's perspective, even if you don't agree with it.
  • Avoid personal attacks. Stick to the issue, not the person. Don't make assumptions or stereotype others.
  • Be curious and respectful. Ask questions, seek clarification, and acknowledge the other person's feelings or experiences.
  • Find common ground. Look for areas of agreement, no matter how small, and build on them.
  • Set achievable goals. Don't try to change someone's mind or win an argument. Aim for mutual understanding and respect.

By practicing these habits, we can create a more harmonious and productive society, where people can coexist even if they have different values, beliefs, or lifestyles. We can also promote democracy and freedom by engaging in civil discourse and promoting civic education.

Conclusion

In summary, civility and tolerance are essential qualities for a healthy and democratic society. They require patience, empathy, humility, and a willingness to learn from others. By cultivating them, we can build bridges of understanding and mutual respect, and overcome the divisions that hold us back. As Joe Scarborough said:

"We can disagree without being disagreeable. We can challenge each other's ideas without being hateful. We can find common ground without sacrificing our values."

Let's all strive to be more like Jason and less like the angry, divisive rhetoric that we see too often.
