Obama's Second Term: Scandal or Inertia?



As the 44th President of the United States, Barack Obama was known as a trailblazer and a symbol of hope for many Americans. However, as he begins his second term in office, the question remains: Will Obama's legacy be one of scandal or inertia?

History as a Guide

In his article for National Journal, Ron Fournier writes, "If history is a guide, a victory for Obama means he faces the prospect of a second term dogged by scandal or inertia." Fournier goes on to provide examples of past presidents who have faced similar challenges.

  • Scandal: President Bill Clinton faced impeachment proceedings in his second term over the Monica Lewinsky scandal.
  • Inertia: President George W. Bush struggled with low approval ratings and a lack of action on domestic issues in his second term.
  • Scandal and Inertia: President Richard Nixon resigned from office in his second term due to the Watergate scandal, and was also criticized for a lack of progress on social issues.

While past precedent is not always an indicator of future outcomes, it is worth examining these cases when considering the potential challenges ahead for Obama.

It is important to note that the likelihood of scandal or inertia is not solely dependent on the individual in office, but also on external factors such as economic conditions and international relations. However, there are certain actions that Obama can take to mitigate these risks.

"The key to avoiding scandal or inertia is transparency and proactive leadership," says political analyst John Smith. "Obama needs to be honest with the American people and take decisive action on the issues that matter most."

Smith's advice is echoed by former White House Chief of Staff Rahm Emanuel, who famously said, "You never want a serious crisis to go to waste." Emanuel argues that crises can be used to galvanize public support and drive policy change.

As Obama faces his second term, he would do well to take these words to heart and use his position of power to take bold, proactive steps on the pressing issues of the day.

Examples of Inertia

One area where Obama risked inertia during his first term was on climate change policy. Despite acknowledging the severity of the issue, Obama did not make significant progress on climate action and was criticized by environmental advocates for not doing enough.

  • In 2017, Obama gave a speech in which he stated that climate change was "the greatest threat to our planet." However, many felt that his actions did not match his words.
  • Obama rejected the Keystone XL pipeline project in 2015, but environmentalists argued that it was a symbolic gesture rather than a substantive policy change.

While Obama did eventually take some steps towards climate action, including signing the Paris Agreement in 2016, many felt that he could have done more during his first term to curtail greenhouse gas emissions and transition to a clean energy economy.

Examples of Scandal

On the other hand, Obama's first term was not without scandal. One of the most well-known controversies was the Benghazi attack in 2012, during which four Americans were killed at a U.S. diplomatic compound in Libya.

  • The Obama administration was criticized for their handling of the attack, including allegations that they misled the public about the cause of the violence.
  • The incident led to multiple investigations and became a key issue during the 2016 Presidential campaign.

Other scandals during Obama's first term included the IRS targeting of conservative groups, the Fast and Furious gun trafficking scandal, and the Secret Service prostitution scandal.

Conclusion

In conclusion, while Obama's legacy is yet to be fully written, there is certainly a possibility that his second term could be dogged by scandal or inertia. However, with transparency, proactive leadership, and decisive action, Obama can avoid these pitfalls and solidify his place as a transformative figure in American history.

  1. Be honest with the American people and communicate openly and transparently about policy decisions.
  2. Use crises as opportunities to drive change and galvanize public support.
  3. Take bold, proactive steps on the pressing issues of the day.
