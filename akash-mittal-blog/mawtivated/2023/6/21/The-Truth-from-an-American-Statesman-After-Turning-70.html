The Truth from an American Statesman After Turning 70



There is a famous quote that goes, "You can always get the truth from an American statesman after he has turned seventy, or given up all hope of the Presidency." This quote, attributed to Wendell Phillips, suggests that political aspirations can cloud one's ability to speak the truth. It is only after reaching the age of 70, or after recognizing the impossibility of achieving certain goals, that someone can speak with complete honesty. While this quote may appear cynical, there are lessons to be learned from it.

Perhaps the most important lesson is the value of honesty. Honesty is the foundation of trust, and trust is essential for any successful relationship, whether it be personal or professional. Yet, it can be difficult to be completely truthful in certain situations, particularly when there are competing interests at play. In politics, for example, there may be pressure to say what is politically expedient, rather than what is actually true. This is where the quote from Wendell Phillips becomes relevant.

Phillips was an American abolitionist, lawyer, and orator who lived from 1811 to 1884. He was known for his fervent speeches against slavery, as well as his support for women's suffrage and labor rights. Phillips was a strong advocate for honesty, even when it was unpopular or inconvenient. He believed that only by confronting uncomfortable truths could society make progress. It is perhaps for this reason that he made the observation about truth-telling and aging statesmen.

So, what can we learn from Wendell Phillips and his quote about American statesmen? Here are three key takeaways:

  • Honesty is a virtue: This may sound like a cliché, but it is true. Honesty is essential for personal and professional success, and it should be promoted and rewarded. When we tell the truth, even when it is difficult or unpleasant, we build trust with those around us.
  • Age brings wisdom: While it is not necessarily true that all elderly people are wise, it is often the case that life experience brings with it a certain level of wisdom. As we get older, we have more experience to draw on and we may be less influenced by external factors, such as social pressures or personal ambition. This can make us more likely to speak the truth, even when it is uncomfortable to do so.
  • Context matters: While there may be some truth to the idea that aging statesmen are more truthful, it is important to consider the specific context in which they are speaking. For example, a retired politician who no longer has any political power may be more willing to speak the truth than someone who is currently in office and has a vested interest in maintaining the status quo. It is important to take into account the specific circumstances of each individual situation.
"You can always get the truth from an American statesman after he has turned seventy, or given up all hope of the Presidency." – Wendell Phillips

In conclusion, the quote from Wendell Phillips about aging American statesmen and truth-telling is a reminder of the importance of honesty and the value of life experience. While it may not be true that all people become more truthful as they age, it is often the case that wisdom comes with experience. We should strive to be honest in all aspects of our lives and to promote honesty in others. By doing so, we can build trust and make progress towards a better future.

To sum up, here are the three key takeaways from this article:

  • Honesty is a virtue that should be promoted and rewarded.
  • Age can bring wisdom, which may make people more likely to speak the truth.
  • Context matters when it comes to truth-telling, and it is important to consider the specific circumstances of each situation.
