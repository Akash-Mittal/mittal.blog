Why Thanksgiving dinners take 18 hours to prepare?



Once upon a time, there was a young girl who loved Thanksgiving. It was her favorite holiday, and she couldn't wait to spend it with her family. Her mother would wake up at 4 am to start cooking the turkey, and the girl would join her in the kitchen, helping her peel potatoes, chop onions, and mix the stuffing. As the day went on, more family members would arrive, and the house would fill with the smells of roasting turkey, sweet potatoes, and pumpkin pie. And when the dinner was finally ready, everyone would sit down at the table and give thanks for the food, the family, and the blessings of life.

Years went by, and the girl grew up, got married, and had children of her own. But she continued the tradition of Thanksgiving, and every year she would wake up early, just like her mother used to, and start preparing the feast. And every year, her husband, her children, and her guests would marvel at the delicious food, the warm atmosphere, and the love that went into every dish.

Why does Thanksgiving take so much time to prepare?

Erma Bombeck, a famous American humorist, once said that "Thanksgiving dinners take eighteen hours to prepare. They are consumed in twelve minutes. Half-times take twelve minutes. This is not a coincidence." What did she mean by that?

  • Thanksgiving dinners take a long time to prepare because they involve many dishes, many ingredients, and many steps. A typical Thanksgiving menu may include:
    • Roast turkey
    • Mashed potatoes and gravy
    • Stuffing
    • Cranberry sauce
    • Green bean casserole
    • Sweet potato casserole
    • Pumpkin pie
    • Apple pie
    • And more...
  • Thanksgiving dinners are consumed in a short time because people are hungry, and they have been waiting for this meal for a long time. They may have skipped breakfast, and they may have traveled for hours to get to the host's house. They may have also been smelling the food cooking for hours, and their appetites have been stimulated by the aroma.
  • Halftime takes twelve minutes because it is a break from the eating, the drinking, and the conversation. It is a time to watch football, to digest the food, and to prepare for the next round. It is also a time to be grateful for the good things in life, just like the Pilgrims did when they first celebrated Thanksgiving with the Native Americans.

How to make Thanksgiving more enjoyable?

Although preparing Thanksgiving dinner may be tiring and stressful, there are ways to make it more enjoyable and less demanding:

  1. Plan ahead: Make a list of the dishes you want to prepare, and buy the ingredients in advance. This will save you time, money, and headaches.
  2. Delegate: Ask your family members or your guests to bring a side dish, a dessert, or a drink. This will not only lighten your load but also make everyone feel involved and appreciated.
  3. Be flexible: Don't be too picky about the recipes or the presentation. Remember that the most important thing is to enjoy the company of your loved ones and to give thanks for the blessings of life.

Erma Bombeck

Erma Bombeck was a wise and funny woman who understood the paradox of Thanksgiving: the time it takes to prepare the feast vs. the time it takes to consume it. But she also knew that Thanksgiving was more than just a meal. It was a way to connect with our families and our friends, to express our gratitude for the good things in life, and to create meaningful memories that would last a lifetime.

In conclusion

Thanksgiving dinners may take eighteen hours to prepare, but they are worth every minute. They may be consumed in twelve minutes, but they leave a lasting impression on our souls. They may have halftime breaks, but they remind us of the value of rest, gratitude, and togetherness.

3 things to remember:

  1. Thanksgiving is a time to express gratitude, connect with family, and create memories.
  2. Thanksgiving dinners involve many dishes, many ingredients, and many steps, but they can be made more enjoyable by planning ahead, delegating tasks, and being flexible.
  3. Thanksgiving is not just a meal, but a celebration of life's blessings, a reminder of our shared humanity, and a call to kindness, love, and compassion.
