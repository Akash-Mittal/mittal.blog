Have you ever wondered what makes the economy grow or why some countries are richer than others? One of the keys to understanding this is realizing the importance of interest rate. Interest rates act as a link between income-value and capital-value. In this article, we will discuss how Irving Fisher, one of the greatest economists of the 20th century, came up with this concept and how it has influenced the way we think about the economy.

Irving Fisher: A Brief History

Irving Fisher was born in 1867 in upstate New York. In his early life, he was interested in mathematics and became a professor of political economy at Yale University. Fisher's work on the theory of interest rates has been highly influential in economic theory. He was known for his wide range of interests, including economics, mathematics, physics, and chemistry.

Fisher is known for many contributions to economics, but his work on the link between income-value and capital-value stands out as one of his most important. Fisher recognized that there is a connection between interest rates, which determine the value of savings, and income, which represents the amount of money people receive from their labor. By understanding the relationship between these two, Fisher was able to develop a new way of thinking about how the economy grows.

The Link Between Income-Value and Capital-Value

The link between income-value and capital-value is simple: interest rates influence the value of capital, while income influences the value of labor. When interest rates are low, people are more likely to borrow money to make capital investments that will increase their returns. For instance, if a person can borrow money at a low interest rate, they may choose to take out a loan to start a business or buy shares in the stock market, hoping to earn a higher return on their investment.

On the other hand, when interest rates are high, people are less likely to take out loans because the cost of borrowing is more expensive. When this happens, people tend to save more because they want to earn interest on their savings. This leads to a decrease in consumption and a decrease in the amount of money flowing through the economy, which can lead to a recession.

Examples of Interest Rates and Economic Growth

There are many examples of how interest rates can impact economic growth. In the 1990s, Japan experienced a period of low interest rates and high economic growth. This was because the low interest rates made it more affordable for people to borrow money to invest in capital, which stimulated economic growth.

In contrast, during the Great Depression, the US government raised interest rates to try and stimulate the economy. However, this ended up having the opposite effect, as it made it more expensive for people to borrow and reduced economic growth. Today we see the United States government reducing interest rates in the hopes of generating further economic growth and act as a positive wealth effect.

Conclusion:

  • Understanding the link between income-value and capital-value is essential to understanding economic growth.
  • The interest rate plays a critical role in determining the economic activity of a country or an individual.
  • It's important to remember that interest rates can have both positive and negative impacts on the economy, depending on the situation

It's crucial to keep an eye on interest rates and understand how they can impact economic growth. By doing so, you can make informed decisions about investments, savings, and other financial activities that can help you succeed in the economy.
