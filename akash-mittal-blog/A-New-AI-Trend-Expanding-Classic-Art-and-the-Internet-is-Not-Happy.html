Imagine seeing a beautiful piece of art at a museum and feeling awe-inspired by the talent, creativity, and detail that went into its creation. And then imagine seeing that same piece of art being distorted, manipulated, and changed by an AI program to create something entirely new and different. This is the reality of a new AI trend that is expanding classic art - and the internet is not happy about it.

The Quantifiable Examples

Many artists and art enthusiasts are worried about this trend because it disrespects the original artist's vision and intent. Computer algorithms are essentially taking the work of a master artist and turning it into something unrecognizable and potentially meaningless.

One example of this is the recent AI-generated portrait of a fictional aristocrat named Edmond de Belamy. The original image was created by artist Ian Goodfellow using a GAN (generative adversarial network) algorithm. The painting ended up selling for over $400,000 at Christie's auction house, which is a testament to the growing interest in AI-generated art.

Another example is the AI-generated version of Leonardo da Vinci's famous Mona Lisa painting that was created by developer Nathan Shipley. The AI was able to manipulate the colors and textures of the original painting to create a surreal, dream-like version that some found intriguing but ultimately disrespectful.

These examples may seem harmless at first, but they are indicative of a larger trend that could have negative implications for the art world as a whole.

As an aspiring artist myself, I understand the importance of respecting the work of those who came before me and using their art as inspiration to create something new and unique.
