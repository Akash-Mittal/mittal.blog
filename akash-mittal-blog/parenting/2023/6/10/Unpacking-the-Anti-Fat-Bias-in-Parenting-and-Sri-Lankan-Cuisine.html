Unpacking the Anti-Fat Bias in Parenting and Sri Lankan Cuisine



Image Credits https://www.kcrw.com/culture/shows/good-food/diet-culture-parenting-sri-lankan-cuisine-farmland-water

As a child growing up in an upper-middle-class household, Sarah struggled with feelings of emptiness and inadequacy as she watched her mother yo-yoing between different diets and exercise regimes to lose weight. Even though Sarah was a healthy weight, her mother's preoccupation with thinness and the constant stream of messages from media and peers about the importance of a narrow beauty ideal left her feeling insecure and anxious about her body image.

Unfortunately, Sarah's experience is not unique. Studies have consistently shown that children who are exposed to messages promoting thinness and negative attitudes toward fatness are more likely to develop disordered eating behaviors, low self-esteem, and poor body image, regardless of their own weight status. Furthermore, the emphasis on individual responsibility and moral judgment when it comes to health and weight gain ignores the complex societal and environmental factors that contribute to these issues, from poverty and access to healthy food options to discrimination and social exclusion.

The Complexities of Parenting in a Diet Culture

As parents, it's important to be aware of the ways in which both explicit and implicit anti-fat bias can influence our attitudes and behaviors around food, exercise, and self-care, and how this can affect our children's perception of themselves and their bodies. Opting out of the diet culture altogether is not always feasible, but there are steps we can take to cultivate a positive relationship with food and our bodies that doesn't rely on shame, guilt, or deprivation.

  • Instead of making weight and appearance the focus of conversation around health and wellbeing, talk about how different foods provide energy, nutrients, and flavor, and try to involve your children in meal planning and preparation.
  • Avoid making negative comments about your own body or other people's bodies, and emphasize the importance of respecting and taking care of oneself without judgment or comparison.
  • Encourage your children to engage in physical activity that they enjoy, rather than framing exercise as a punishment or a way to lose weight.

Tasting the Diversity of Sri Lankan Cuisine

Karan Gokani, a restaurateur and food enthusiast, has always been fascinated by the richness and diversity of world cuisine, and how food can be a way of connecting with different cultures and traditions. Recently, he discovered Sri Lankan cuisine and fell in love with its bold flavors, fragrant spices, and unique dishes like hoppers, a type of pancake made of fermented rice batter and coconut milk that's crispy on the edges and fluffy in the middle.

For Karan, the experience of discovering new flavors and ingredients is not just a matter of personal enjoyment, but also a way of expanding his worldview and supporting local communities and small-scale producers. By sourcing ingredients from local farms and markets, he can ensure that the food he serves is fresh, sustainable, and free from harmful chemicals and practices.

The Importance of Farmland Water for Clean Drinking Water

However, access to clean and safe water is not a given in many parts of the world, including Sri Lanka, where pollution and industrialization have led to the contamination of water sources and the depletion of natural reserves. This has increased the risk of waterborne diseases, such as cholera and diarrhea, and the need for expensive and inefficient methods of water treatment and purification.

By supporting sustainable farming practices and protecting the quality of farmland water, we can help to preserve this precious resource and ensure that future generations have access to clean and healthy drinking water. This includes reducing the use of chemical fertilizers and pesticides, promoting soil conservation and biodiversity, and investing in infrastructure and education that support sustainable agricultural practices.

Conclusion

Unpacking the interrelated issues of anti-fat bias, cultural diversity, and environmental sustainability can seem overwhelming, but there are tangible and effective ways to address them. By fostering a positive and holistic relationship with food, we can promote a sense of body positivity and self-care that extends beyond narrow beauty standards and societal pressures. By exploring and celebrating the richness and relevance of different food cultures, we can connect with others and expand our horizons, while supporting local producers and reducing our ecological footprint. By advocating for sustainable farming practices and protecting the quality of farmland water, we can safeguard the health and wellbeing of communities and ecosystems around the world.

  1. Body positivity and self-care are important for promoting a healthy relationship with food and our bodies, particularly in the context of a diet culture that values thinness over health.
  2. Cultural diversity and gastronomic exploration can be a way of expanding our worldview and connecting with different traditions and communities, while supporting local producers and reducing our environmental impact.
  3. Environmental sustainability and access to clean water are crucial for the health and wellbeing of communities and ecosystems around the world, and can be supported through sustainable farming practices and policy advocacy.
