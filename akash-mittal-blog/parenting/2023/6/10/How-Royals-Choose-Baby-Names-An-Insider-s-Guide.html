How Royals Choose Baby Names: An Insider's Guide

When Prince Harry and Meghan Markle announced they were expecting their first child, the world eagerly awaited the news of what the royal couple would name their baby. Names like Alexander, Elizabeth, and Victoria were among the top contenders, but when Archie Harrison Mountbatten-Windsor was finally revealed, many were surprised by the unconventional choice.

So how do Royals choose baby names? Is it based on tradition, family history, or personal preference? As it turns out, there are several factors that go into the decision-making process.

Tradition and Family History

For centuries, Royals have carried on family names and traditions through their choice of baby names. The British Royal Family, in particular, has a long history of naming their children after ancestors and royals who came before them. For example, Prince Charles was named after his grandfather, King George V, while Prince William's full name is William Arthur Philip Louis, a nod to his great-grandfather King George VI and grandfather Prince Philip.

But it's not just about royalty. Royals also look to their own family history for inspiration. When the Duke and Duchess of Cambridge named their first child, Prince George, they chose a name that had special meaning to both of them. William's great-grandfather was named George VI, while Kate's father's middle name is also George.

Quantifiable example: A survey conducted by YouGov found that 18 percent of Britons believe family tradition is the most important factor in choosing a baby name, while 17 percent believed it should be a name that is "personal and meaningful to the parents."

Personal Preference

While tradition and family history play a big role in royal baby name choices, personal preference cannot be overlooked. The Influence of Society and Culture

Finally, it's important to consider the role that society and culture play in shaping baby name choices for Royals. Just like any celebrity or public figure, Royals are aware of the impact their choices can have on the wider population. Whether it's a name that becomes popular due to its royal association, or a name that sparks controversy and criticism, Royals are mindful of how their baby name choices will be received by the public.

This can also extend to cultural considerations. When the Duke and Duchess of Sussex welcomed their son Archie, they chose a name that reflects Meghan's American background. The name Archie is a nickname for Archibald, a name that has Gaelic roots and means "genuine and bold." This choice not only pays homage to Meghan's heritage but also reflects a growing trend towards multiculturalism and diversity.

Quantifiable example: In the United States, the Social Security Administration tracks the popularity of baby names each year. In 2020, the top five most popular boys' names were Liam, Noah, Oliver, Elijah, and William, while the top five girls' names were Olivia, Emma, Ava, Charlotte, and Sophia.

Conclusion

In conclusion, there is no one-size-fits-all approach when it comes to how Royals choose baby names. Tradition and family history, personal preference, and society and culture all play a role in the decision-making process. However, what is important is that the name chosen is meaningful and significant for the parents and their child.
