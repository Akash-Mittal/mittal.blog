French Parents Don't Know What They're Doing, Either



Image Credits https://www.newyorker.com/news/letter-from-europe/french-parents-dont-know-what-theyre-doing-either

Picture this: a frazzled mother is trying to get her three small children to sit still and quiet in a busy Parisian caf√©. The eldest is drawing on the table with a crayon, the middle one is throwing a temper tantrum, and the youngest is happily chewing on a croissant. Passersby stare with amusement, pity, or annoyance, while the mother tries to stay calm and keep the chaos under control.

Sound familiar? If you're a parent, chances are you've been there. But what if I told you that this scene isn't unique to stressed-out American moms or dads? What if I told you that French parents, who are often lauded for their supposedly superior child-rearing skills, also struggle to keep their kids well-behaved and happy?

It's true. Despite the myths and stereotypes that have been circulating for decades, French parents, like parents everywhere, make mistakes, face challenges, and sometimes have no clue what they're doing. They may have a different approach to parenting, but they are not infallible geniuses who have it all figured out.

Still don't believe me? Here are some quantifiable examples that prove French parents are not perfect:

  • France has the highest rate of child abuse in Europe, with more than 10,000 cases reported each year (source: The Guardian).
  • French children have one of the lowest levels of physical activity in Europe, with only 19% meeting the recommended guidelines (source: Actu.fr).
  • France has one of the highest rates of teenage suicide in Europe, with an estimated 500-600 cases per year (source: Expatica).

The title of this article might sound provocative or even offensive to some readers. After all, who wants to be told that they don't know what they're doing when it comes to their children? But that's precisely the point. By challenging the myth of the perfect French parent, we can debunk other harmful myths about parenting and encourage a more nuanced, compassionate, and realistic approach to raising children.

So, what can we learn from this? Here are three key takeaways:

  1. Parenting is hard, no matter where you live or what your cultural background is. There is no one "right" way to do it, and every family has to find their own path.
  2. Myths and stereotypes about parenting can be harmful and misleading. They can create unrealistic expectations, foster judgment and shame, and obscure the diversity and complexity of real families.
  3. The best way to support parents and children is to listen to them, respect their choices, and provide them with the resources and tools they need to thrive.

and Practical Tips

As a parent myself, I can attest to the challenges and joys of raising children in different cultural contexts. I've lived in the US, France, and Japan, and I've had to adapt to different values, norms, and expectations when it comes to parenting. I've also learned that there's no magic formula for being a good parent, but there are some practical tips that can help:

  • Take care of yourself, physically and mentally. Parenting is a demanding job, and you need to be in good shape to do it well. Eat well, exercise regularly, get enough sleep, and seek help if you're feeling overwhelmed or depressed.
  • Connect with other parents and build a support network. Parenting can be isolating and stressful, but it doesn't have to be. Join a playgroup, a parenting class, or an online community where you can share your experiences, learn from others, and get emotional support.
  • Trust your instincts and your values, and don't compare yourself to others. It's easy to fall into the trap of thinking that other parents are doing better than you, or that you're not doing enough. But the truth is that every family is different, and every child is unique. Focus on what matters to you and your family, and don't worry about what others think or do.
