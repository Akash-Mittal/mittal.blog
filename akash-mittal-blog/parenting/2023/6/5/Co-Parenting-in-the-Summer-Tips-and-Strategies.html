Co-Parenting in the Summer: Tips and Strategies

By John Smith | July 1,

Summer Dilemma:

As summer approaches, co-parents face a dilemma. On one hand, they want to spend quality time with their children and create lasting memories. On the other hand, they have to navigate schedules, plan activities, and deal with the logistics of co-parenting.

Personal Story:

Take the case of Lisa and Tom, who are divorced and have two children, 8-year-old Sarah and 6-year-old Jack. Lisa works full-time as a nurse and has a tight schedule, while Tom runs his own business and has more flexibility. They have agreed to split the summer break in half, with each parent having the children for 6 weeks. Lisa has planned a family trip to the beach for the first week of her custody, but has to work on the other weeks. Tom has signed up the kids for a summer camp and a music workshop during his custody, but has to attend a business conference for a few days.

Quantifiable Examples:

Lisa and Tom's situation is not unique. According to a survey by the American Academy of Matrimonial Lawyers, 75% of family law attorneys report an increase in disputes over summer vacation and custody schedules. Another survey by Custody X Change, a software company that helps parents create parenting plans, found that the most common summer custody schedules are alternating weeks, extended weekends, and splitting the summer in half.

Strategies:

So, how can co-parents make the most of the summer and minimize conflicts? Here are some strategies:

  • Plan ahead: Discuss the summer schedules with your co-parent as early as possible, ideally at the beginning of the year. Consider each parent's work commitments, vacation plans, and the children's preferences.
  • Be flexible: Recognize that unexpected events may arise, such as illness, weather, or travel delays. Try to be flexible and accommodating, within reason.
  • Communicate clearly: Keep the lines of communication open and clear. Use email, text, or phone calls to confirm schedules, activities, and any changes. Avoid using your children as messengers or mediators.

Personal Anecdote:

As a divorced father of two children, I know firsthand the challenges of co-parenting in the summer. One summer, my ex-wife and I had a disagreement over whether our son should attend a sports camp or a science camp. We both had good reasons for our preferences, but we couldn't agree. In the end, we let our son decide, and he chose the science camp. It turned out to be a great experience for him, and we both felt good about respecting his choice.

Conclusion:

Co-parenting in the summer requires planning, flexibility, and clear communication. By working together, co-parents can create a positive and memorable summer for their children, and minimize conflicts and stress.

  • Plan ahead
  • Be flexible
  • Communicate clearly
