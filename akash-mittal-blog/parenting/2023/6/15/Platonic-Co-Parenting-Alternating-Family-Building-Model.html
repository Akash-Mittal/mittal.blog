Platonic Co-Parenting: Alternating Family Building Model

When Emily and Jack realized that they wanted children, they knew that traditional family models did not fit them. They had met in college and became best friends, but there was no romantic chemistry between them. They had both had failed relationships in the past and did not want to rush into such a commitment. They wanted to take their time to find the perfect partner, but they also did not want to miss the chance of becoming parents. That's when they discovered platonic co-parenting.

Platonic co-parenting is a model in which two (or more) people, who are not romantically involved, decide to have and raise a child/children together. They share the responsibilities and joys of parenthood, but they do not have a romantic commitment. It may sound unconventional, but it is gaining popularity among people who want to build a family in a non-traditional way.

Examples of Platonic Co-Parenting

The idea of platonic co-parenting is not new, but it is becoming more visible and accepted. Here are some examples of famous people who have embraced this model:

  • Actress Mindy Kaling and her friend B.J. Novak announced in 2019 that they were raising Kaling's daughter together.
  • Vlogger Casey Neistat and his friend Candice Pool co-parent their two children, even after Pool remarried.
  • Ethan and Elizabeth Diamond, a brother and sister, decided to raise Ethan's daughter together after his ex-wife moved away with their child.
  • Singer-songwriter Sia adopted two teenage boys who were about to exit the foster care system, and she plans to co-parent them with a close friend.

These examples show that platonic co-parenting can work for different types of people and situations.

Benefits of Platonic Co-Parenting

Why would someone choose to co-parent with a friend instead of pursuing a romantic partnership or a traditional adoption?

  • It offers a way to have children without compromising one's values or preferences. For example, someone who does not want to have a child alone or in a romantic relationship can still become a parent through co-parenting.
  • It allows for shared responsibilities and experiential learning. Two (or more) people can divide the tasks of parenting and rely on each other for support and guidance. They can also model healthy cooperative relationships for the children.
  • It creates a unique family structure that can be flexible and inclusive. Co-parents can involve other family members, friends, or community members in their child's life, and they can design their own rituals and traditions.
  • It fosters a deep and lasting friendship between the co-parents. Sharing the experience of raising a child can create a bond that goes beyond the usual friendship boundaries.

Of course, co-parenting also has its challenges, such as legal, financial, and emotional issues. But with proper planning, communication, and support, these challenges can be overcome.

Tips for Successful Platonic Co-Parenting

If you are considering platonic co-parenting, here are some tips to help you make the best out of this model:

  • Have an open and honest conversation with your potential co-parent(s) about your expectations, values, and boundaries. Make sure you are on the same page regarding parenting styles, discipline, education, health, religion, and finances.
  • Create a written agreement that covers the legal and practical aspects of co-parenting, such as custody, visitation, child support, health insurance, schooling, and extracurricular activities.
  • Seek professional advice from lawyers, therapists, or mediators who specialize in co-parenting arrangements. They can help you navigate the legal, financial, and emotional aspects of co-parenting and prevent or resolve conflicts.
  • Involve the child in the co-parenting process as soon as they are developmentally ready. Explain the situation in a child-friendly way and answer their questions honestly and age-appropriately.
  • Develop a support network of friends, family members, or other co-parents who can help you and your child. They can provide emotional support, practical assistance, and social interaction.

These tips are not exhaustive and may vary depending on your situation, but they can give you a sense of what to expect and how to prepare for platonic co-parenting.

Conclusion

Platonic co-parenting offers an alternative way of building a family that can meet the needs and desires of people who do not fit into the traditional family mold. It can provide a supportive, enriching, and fulfilling experience for both the co-parents and the child. It may not be for everyone, but it deserves consideration and respect as a viable option.

To summarize, here are three key takeaways:

  • Platonic co-parenting is a model in which two (or more) people, who are not romantically involved, decide to have and raise a child/children together.
  • Platonic co-parenting offers benefits such as providing a way to have children without compromising one's values, creating a unique and flexible family structure, and fostering a deep and lasting friendship between the co-parents, but it also has challenges that require careful planning, communication, and support.
  • Tips for successful platonic co-parenting include having an open and honest conversation with your potential co-parent(s), creating a written agreement, seeking professional advice, involving the child in the co-parenting process, and developing a support network.
