The Danger of Obsessive Love

It started innocently enough.

A young girl, let's call her Lily, met a boy in high school and they began dating. It was nothing serious at first, just a few dates here and there, but soon Lily became infatuated. She thought about him all the time, talked about him to her friends, and wanted to spend every moment with him.

But the boy was not as invested in the relationship as Lily was. He started to distance himself, cancel dates, and hang out with his friends instead of her. Lily felt hurt and rejected, but she refused to give up on him.

Things escalated quickly from there. Lily began calling him constantly, showing up uninvited at his house and at his school, and sending him dozens of messages a day. She became possessive, jealous, and controlling. She didn't want him to talk to other girls, have any hobbies or interests that didn't involve her, or spend any time apart from her.

The boy tried to break up with her several times, but Lily always found a way to convince him to stay. She threatened to hurt herself, told him that she couldn't live without him, and promised to change her behavior. But nothing worked.

One day, the boy couldn't take it anymore. He told Lily that he was done and that he never wanted to see her again. Lily was devastated. She felt like her whole world had ended. She couldn't imagine living without him.

That's when things turned violent.

Lily started stalking the boy, following him around town, and breaking into his house when he wasn't there. She threatened him and his family, vandalized his property, and even attacked him physically. The boy was terrified and didn't know what to do.

The situation ended in tragedy when Lily killed the boy and then herself in a murder-suicide.

Unfortunately, this is not an isolated case. Obsessive, dangerous love can happen to anyone, at any age, and in any gender. It's a serious problem that can lead to violence, abuse, and even death.

But there are things you can do to protect your child from becoming a victim or a perpetrator of obsessive, dangerous love.

Signs of Obsessive Love

Obsessive love can manifest in many ways, but there are some common signs and behaviors that you should be aware of. Here are a few examples:

  • Constant calling, texting, and messaging
  • Show-up uninvited or unexpectedly
  • Keeping tabs on someone's whereabouts
  • Displaying intense jealousy and possessiveness
  • Trying to control someone's behavior or decisions
  • Isolating someone from friends and family
  • Threatening self-harm or harm to others
  • Making unwanted physical advances
  • Stalking or harassing someone online or in person

If your child is exhibiting any of these behaviors, it's important to talk to them and seek professional help if necessary. Don't dismiss it as "just a phase" or "young love." Obsessive love can have serious consequences and should be taken seriously.

Prevention and Protection

There are several things you can do to prevent your child from falling into a pattern of obsessive love and to protect them from dangerous relationships.

  1. Teach Healthy Relationship Skills

    2. Start young and teach your child healthy relationship skills from an early age. This can include things like communication, empathy, respect, and consent. Help them understand what it means to be in a healthy relationship and how to set boundaries.

  2. Be Involved and Supportive

    3. Be involved in your child's life and have open and honest communication with them. Create a safe and supportive environment where they feel comfortable sharing their thoughts and feelings with you. Take an interest in their friends and partners and look out for any warning signs of obsessive love.

  3. Set Boundaries and Limits

    4. Set clear boundaries and limits with your child around relationships and dating. Encourage them to have a healthy balance between their relationships and their other interests, hobbies, and responsibilities. Talk to them about healthy breakups and how to let go when a relationship is no longer working.

  4. Seek Professional Help

    5. If you're concerned that your child may be exhibiting signs of obsessive love, seek professional help. This could include counseling, therapy, or intervention programs. Don't wait until it's too late to seek help.

Resources and Support

If you or your child is experiencing obsessive love or dangerous relationships, there are resources and support available. Here are a few examples:

  • The National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-SAFE (7233)
  • The Love Is Respect Hotline: 1-866-331-9474
  • Rainn (Rape, Abuse, & Incest National Network): 1-800-656-HOPE (4673)
  • The National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 1-800-273-TALK (8255)
  • Counseling or therapy services in your area

Remember, obsessive, dangerous love is a serious problem that can have devastating consequences. Don't wait until it's too late to protect your child from the dangers of unhealthy relationships.
