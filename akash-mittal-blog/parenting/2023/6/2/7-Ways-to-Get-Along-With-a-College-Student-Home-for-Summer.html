7 Ways to Get Along With a College Student Home for Summer

When Sarah came home from college for summer break, she was excited to catch up with her parents and siblings. However, within a few days of being home, she started feeling claustrophobic and frustrated with her family's routine. Her parents were constantly asking questions about her classes and social life, and her younger siblings were always around, demanding attention. Sarah started feeling like she didn't have any privacy or independence, and tensions started rising.

Does this sound familiar? If you're a parent of a college student, you know how tricky it can be to adjust to having your child back home for a few months. It can be a time of joy, but also a time of stress and conflict. In this article, we'll share some tips and tricks for getting along with your college student during summer break.

  1. Plan ahead for family time and alone time. Schedule activities and outings that everyone can enjoy, but also carve out some time for your student to do their own thing.
  2. Communicate openly and honestly. Discuss expectations, boundaries, and any potential sources of conflict before they become an issue.
  3. Show interest in your student's life, but don't interrogate them. Ask open-ended questions and be a good listener.
  4. Respect your student's independence, but also provide structure and guidance. Help them stay on track with summer jobs, internships, or other activities that will help them prepare for the future.
  5. Be realistic about your student's maturity level and responsibility. Don't expect them to suddenly become a fully-functioning adult overnight.
  6. Set ground rules for household chores and responsibilities. Make sure everyone is contributing equally to the household, and hold each other accountable.
  7. Practice patience and empathy. Remember that this is a time of transition for everyone, and it's okay to make mistakes and learn from them.

Get Along with Your College Student During Summer Break: 7 Essential Tips

Practical Tips and

As parents, it's natural to want to spend as much time as possible with your college student when they're home for summer break. After all, you miss them and you want to catch up on everything that's happened since they left for school. However, it's important to remember that your student has likely changed and grown in ways that might surprise you, and they may need some space and independence to continue that growth.

One practical tip for getting along with your college student during summer break is to plan ahead for both family time and alone time. Set aside specific days or times for family outings or activities, but also make sure your student has some time to themselves to recharge and pursue their own interests. This can help prevent claustrophobia and conflict.

Communication is also key to a successful summer break. Be honest and transparent about your own expectations and boundaries, and encourage your student to do the same. Ask open-ended questions and really listen to their answers, without jumping to conclusions or judgment. This can help you better understand your student's experiences and feelings, and reduce tension and miscommunication.

Another tip is to respect your student's independence, while also providing structure and guidance. Help them find summer jobs or internships that align with their career goals, or encourage them to take classes or pursue hobbies that interest them. This can help them build skills and experience that will benefit them in the long run, while also giving them a sense of purpose and direction.

Of course, no summer break is without its challenges. Your student may still act like a teenager in some ways, even though they're technically an adult now. They may have different priorities or values than you do, or they may struggle with homesickness or anxiety. It's important to be patient and empathetic, and to recognize that this is a time of transition for everyone. Above all, try to keep the lines of communication open and focus on building a positive and respectful relationship with your college student.

To illustrate these tips, let's return to our example of Sarah and her family. After a few tense days at home, Sarah and her parents sat down and had a frank conversation about their expectations and needs for the summer. Sarah expressed her desire for more privacy and independence, while her parents shared their hopes for spending quality time together as a family. They agreed to a schedule of family outings and activities, but also set aside specific times for Sarah to pursue her own interests. They also discussed household chores and responsibilities, and made a plan for how everyone would contribute. While there were still some bumps along the way, this open communication and compromise helped the family get along much better during the summer break.

  • Communicate openly and honestly
  • Respect your student's independence and maturity level
  • Set boundaries and expectations, but also allow for flexibility and compromise

By following these tips, you can help build a positive and constructive relationship with your college student during summer break. Remember that this is a time of transition for everyone, and that it's okay to make mistakes and learn from them.
