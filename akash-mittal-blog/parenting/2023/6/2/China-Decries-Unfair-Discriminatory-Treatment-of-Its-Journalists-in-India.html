China Decries Unfair Discriminatory Treatment of Its Journalists in India

It was a sunny morning in Delhi when Chinese journalist Liu Yong arrived at the airport with his team, all set to cover a press conference. However, his joy was short-lived when he was stopped by Indian immigration officials and his passport was confiscated. Yong was subjected to a 5-hour interrogation and was not allowed to proceed with his work.

This is not an isolated incident. China has been voicing its concerns over the unfair treatment meted out to its journalists in India. The Chinese Foreign Ministry has accused India of "discriminating" against its journalists and has demanded that the Indian government take immediate steps to address the issue.

The Indian government, on the other hand, has maintained that it is committed to the freedom of the press and that it does not differentiate between journalists on the basis of their nationality. However, the reality on the ground seems to be quite different.

There have been many instances where Chinese journalists have been denied visas or have had their visas revoked without any explanation. In 2018, three Chinese journalists from the state-run Xinhua news agency were denied permission to cover a public meeting in Dharamshala, the headquarters of the Tibetan government-in-exile. The Indian government had reportedly cited "national security reasons" for the denial.

Similarly, in 2019, Chinese journalist Lu Kang was denied a visa to cover the Indian general elections. Lu, who was based in New Delhi, had applied for an extension of his visa, which was due to expire in February 2019. However, his application was rejected without any explanation.

These incidents are not limited to Chinese journalists alone. In 2016, two French journalists were denied visas to cover the violent clashes in Kashmir. The Indian government had reportedly cited "security reasons" for the denial.

The lack of transparency in the visa application process is a major concern for foreign journalists. The Indian government does not provide any reasons for denying visas or revoking them. This not only creates uncertainty for journalists but also undermines the freedom of the press.

Another issue that Chinese journalists face in India is the hostility towards them. There have been instances where Indian journalists have targeted Chinese journalists for being "state-run" or "propagandists". This kind of stereotyping is not only unfair but also creates a hostile environment for foreign journalists.

The Indian media's coverage of China has also been criticized by the Chinese government. The Chinese Foreign Ministry has accused the Indian media of being "irresponsible" and of "spreading rumors" about China.

In a recent statement, the Chinese Embassy in India said, "We urge the Indian side to immediately stop the restrictions on Chinese media, rectify the discriminatory practices against Chinese journalists and guarantee their safety and legitimate rights and interests in India."

Conclusion:

The unfair treatment of Chinese journalists in India is a worrying trend. The Indian government needs to be more transparent in its visa application process and provide valid reasons for denying visas or revoking them. The Indian media also needs to be more responsible in its coverage of China, and avoid stereotypes and rumors. Creating a safe and welcoming environment for foreign journalists is essential for maintaining the freedom of the press.

Three key points:

1. The Indian government needs to be more transparent in its visa application process and provide valid reasons for denying visas or revoking them.

2. The Indian media needs to be more responsible in its coverage of China, and avoid stereotypes and rumors.

3. Creating a safe and welcoming environment for foreign journalists is essential for maintaining the freedom of the press.
