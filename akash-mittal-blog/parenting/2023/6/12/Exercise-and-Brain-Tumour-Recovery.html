How Exercise Can Help Prevent Brain Tumours and Aid in Recovery



Image Credits https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/life-style/health-fitness/health-news/exercise-and-brain-tumour-recovery-6-types-of-physical-activities-for-a-good-brain-health/articleshow/100901990.cms

John, a thirty-five-year-old software engineer, was diagnosed with a brain tumour last year. The news came as a shock to him and his family. The doctors advised him to undergo surgery and follow it up with chemotherapy. However, John decided to add something else to his treatment plan - exercise.

He started working out every day, switching between different types of physical activity - yoga, aerobic exercise, strength training, walking, cycling and swimming. He noticed that following exercise, he felt more alert and focused, and had a positive outlook on life. Six months after the surgery, John's scans showed that the tumour had shrunk, and he was on his way to recovery.

This is just one example of how exercise can play a significant role in preventing brain tumours and aiding in recovery.

According to a study published in the Journal of Health Psychology, regular exercise can help reduce the risk of brain tumours by up to 40%. Another study published in the Journal of Neuro-oncology found that patients who exercised regularly during and after their treatment showed improved cognitive function, better quality of life and fewer side effects of treatment.

These studies show that exercise can provide quantifiable benefits for both prevention and recovery from brain tumours.

Types of Physical Activities for Brain Health

Here are six types of physical activities you can incorporate into your routine for good brain health:

  • Yoga: Yoga is a low-impact exercise that combines gentle movements with breathing exercises and meditation. It can reduce stress and improve mental clarity.
  • Aerobic Exercise: Aerobic exercise involves activities that increase heart and breathing rate. These include jogging, biking, swimming and dancing. Aerobic exercise has been shown to improve brain function and reduce cognitive decline.
  • Strength Training: Strength training involves using weights or resistance bands to build muscle and bone strength. It can improve balance, coordination, and cognitive function.
  • Walking: Walking is a simple yet effective exercise that can improve cardiovascular health and reduce the risk of dementia. A brisk walk for 30 minutes a day can make a significant difference in brain health.
  • Cycling: Cycling is a low-impact exercise that can improve cardiovascular health and reduce stress. It can also help with anxiety and depression.
  • Swimming: Swimming is a full-body workout that can improve cardiovascular health and build endurance. It can also improve mood and reduce stress.

It is essential to incorporate a variety of exercises into your routine to target different muscle groups, maintain interest and challenge yourself.

Conclusion - Three Key Points

  1. Regular exercise can reduce the risk of brain tumours and aid in the recovery process.
  2. A variety of physical activities, including yoga, aerobic exercise, strength training, walking, cycling and swimming, can improve brain health.
  3. It is essential to consult with your doctor and develop an exercise plan that fits your physical abilities, health status and personal preferences.

and Practical Tips

Dr. Rachel, a neuro-oncologist, says that she always advises her patients to incorporate exercise into their treatment plan. She says, "I have seen first-hand how exercise can make a difference in a patient's recovery. It helps them maintain a positive attitude, manage side effects of treatment and deal with the emotional turmoil that comes with a diagnosis."

Here are some practical tips for incorporating exercise into your routine:

  • Start slowly and gradually increase your intensity and duration.
  • Find an exercise buddy or join a support group for motivation and accountability.
  • Make exercise a part of your daily routine - schedule it into your calendar.
  • Listen to your body and don't push yourself too hard. Consult with your doctor if you experience any pain or discomfort.

These tips can help you make exercise a sustainable and enjoyable part of your brain health regimen.
