'Sell China, Buy India' policy of FPIs cannot continue for long



Image Credits https://www.siasat.com/sell-china-buy-india-policy-of-fpis-cannot-continue-for-long-2642047/

Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) have been implementing a 'Sell China, Buy India' policy for some time now. However, experts believe that this strategy may not be sustainable in the long run. FPIs are international investors who invest in the financial markets of different countries, including emerging economies like China and India.

The 'Sell China, Buy India' policy gained traction due to the ongoing geopolitical tensions between China and several other countries, including India. FPIs saw India as a viable alternative to China and started shifting their investments from Chinese companies to Indian ones. This trend was accelerated during the COVID-19 pandemic, which exposed the vulnerabilities of global supply chains heavily reliant on China.

While the policy initially had positive effects on India's stock markets, experts argue that it may not be sustainable in the long term. One of the primary concerns is the economic and market outlook of both countries.

Economic and Market Outlook

China is the world's second-largest economy and has a robust industrial sector. Despite the ongoing tensions, it continues to attract significant investments due to its strong manufacturing capabilities and favorable business environment. On the other hand, India is still an emerging economy and faces challenges such as infrastructure gaps and bureaucratic hurdles.

Additionally, China's market is more mature and liquid compared to India's. It offers a diverse range of investment options, including tech giants such as Alibaba and Tencent. India's market, while growing, still lacks such diversified opportunities for investors.

Regulatory Challenges

Another crucial aspect to consider is the regulatory environment in both countries. China has a relatively stable regulatory framework for foreign investors, whereas India has faced criticism for frequent policy changes and regulatory uncertainty. These uncertainties pose risks for foreign investors and may deter them from placing long-term bets on Indian companies.

Moreover, the Indian government's recent measures to curb Chinese investments and promote self-reliance have affected overall investor sentiment. While the intention behind these policies is to boost the domestic economy, it has given rise to concerns among FPIs regarding the stability and consistency of India's business environment.

Capacity Constraints

India also faces capacity constraints in various sectors, including infrastructure, manufacturing, and labor. While the country has shown resilience and potential for growth, it still needs significant investments to bridge these gaps. China, on the other hand, has already built extensive infrastructure and possesses advanced manufacturing capabilities.

Addressing these capacity constraints will require long-term investments and policy reforms, which may take time to materialize. FPIs may find it challenging to allocate substantial funds to India without immediate returns or clear visibility on the resolution of these challenges.
