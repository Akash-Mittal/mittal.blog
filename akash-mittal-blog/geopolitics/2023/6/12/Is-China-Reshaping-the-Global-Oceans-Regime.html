Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

The oceans cover more than two thirds of the Earth's surface, and they are a vital part of our planet's ecosystem and economy. However, they also represent a complex web of international laws, agreements, and disputes, which are increasingly affected by geopolitical tensions and maritime activities. In particular, the rise of China as a sea power has raised questions about how it is reshaping the global oceans regime and what are the consequences for the international community.

The increasing maritime activities and disputes

China's maritime activities have been growing steadily in the past decades, as it seeks to protect its economic and security interests, expand its influence, and project power abroad. Some of the most notable developments include:

  • Building militarized artificial islands in the South China Sea, which have been condemned by the international community for violating the UNCLOS (United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea) and threatening the freedom of navigation and overflight.
  • Expanding its naval fleet and capabilities, including the construction of aircraft carriers, ballistic missile submarines, and modernized surface ships and submarines.
  • Asserting its sovereignty claims and exclusive economic zones (EEZs) in disputed areas of the East China Sea and the South China Sea, which have resulted in confrontations and incidents with other coastal states, such as Japan, Vietnam, the Philippines, and Indonesia.
  • Participating in international forums and initiatives related to maritime governance, such as the UN Convention on Biological Diversity, the International Seabed Authority, and the Belt and Road Initiative, which aim to promote sustainable development, resource exploitation, and connectivity, but also raise concerns about transparency, accountability, and environmental risks.

These and other activities and disputes have raised serious questions about China's adherence to international law, norms, and standards, and its intentions or capabilities to challenge the existing maritime order. They have also triggered various responses from the international community, ranging from diplomatic protests and legal actions to military deployments and cooperation.

Case studies

Several case studies can illustrate the dynamics and implications of China's reshaping of the global oceans regime:

South China Sea

The South China Sea is one of the most controversial and contested maritime areas in the world, due to its strategic location, rich resources, and overlapping jurisdictional claims. China claims most of the sea, based on its "nine-dash line" map, which overlaps with the EEZs and continental shelves of other countries, such as Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, and Brunei. China has been building artificial islands in the Spratly Islands since 2013, by dredging and filling coral reefs, and transforming them into military bases, airstrips, and radar stations. These activities have been criticized by the US, Japan, Australia, and other countries as a violation of international law and a threat to regional security and stability. The Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague ruled in 2016 that China's claims and actions in the South China Sea are unlawful, but China rejected the verdict and continued its activities, claiming them as legitimate and peaceful.

Belt and Road Initiative

The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) is a massive infrastructure and development program launched by China in 2013, that aims to connect Asia, Europe, Africa, and the Middle East through a network of roads, railways, ports, pipelines, and digital platforms. The BRI involves dozens of partner countries, including many coastal states, that receive loans, grants, investments, and services from China, in exchange for access to their resources, markets, and geopolitical influence. The BRI has significant implications for maritime governance, as many of its projects involve the development of ports and shipping facilities, that could enhance China's maritime connectivity and presence, but also raise concerns about debt sustainability, environmental impact, and national security. The BRI has also prompted debates about the compatibility of its standards and norms with those of the existing international regimes, such as the UNCLOS, the International Maritime Organization, or the World Trade Organization.

Illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing

China is the world's largest fishing nation, with a fleet of more than 200,000 vessels, that operate in both domestic and international waters. However, China has been accused of engaging in illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing practices, that harm the marine environment, deplete fish populations, and threaten the livelihoods of local communities. China has also been criticized for its lack of transparency, enforcement, and compliance with international agreements and standards on IUU fishing. The IUU fishing issue highlights the tensions between China's economic interests and its responsibilities as a coastal state and a global stakeholder in marine conservation and sustainable development. It also shows how IUU fishing can exacerbate existing conflicts and disputes over maritime resources, as seen in the case of the South China Sea.

Conclusion

The consequences of China's actions

China's reshaping of the global oceans regime has several consequences for the international community:

  • Increased maritime tensions and disputes, that could lead to conflicts, accidents, or environmental disasters, and undermine the stability and security of the Asia-Pacific region and beyond.
  • Challenges to the existing maritime order, norms, and standards, that could erode the authority and legitimacy of the UNCLOS and other legal frameworks, and create a more fragmented and unpredictable governance landscape.
  • Opportunities and risks for cooperation and competition, that could shape the economic, political, and social dynamics of the region and the world, and determine the winners and losers of the global maritime competition.

Therefore, it is crucial for the international community to engage with China constructively and comprehensively, and to seek ways to manage and resolve the maritime disputes, promote the sustainable use and conservation of the oceans, and enhance the cooperation and coordination among all stakeholders. The future of the global oceans regime depends on the willingness and ability of all actors to adapt to the changing realities and challenges of the maritime world.

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

When we talk about China's global impact, we often think about its economy, trade, and military power. However, there is another aspect of China's influence that we need to pay attention to – the country's role in shaping the global oceans regime.

China is the world's largest fish producer, the second-largest fish importer, and has the longest coastline of any country in the world. Given these factors, it is not surprising that China has become a major player in the global oceans regime. In this article, we will explore how China's actions are reshaping the global oceans regime and what implications this has for the world.

China's Maritime Silk Road Initiative

China's Maritime Silk Road Initiative is a massive infrastructure and investment project that aims to create a network of ports, railways, highways, and other infrastructure across Asia, Africa, and Europe. The initiative is a major part of China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), which aims to boost economic growth and connectivity across the region.

The Maritime Silk Road Initiative has a significant impact on the global oceans regime. By investing heavily in ports and other infrastructure in countries around the world, China is gaining strategic influence over key maritime chokepoints and strategic sea lanes. For example, China has invested heavily in the port of Gwadar in Pakistan, which is just 180 miles from the Strait of Hormuz – a strategic chokepoint that is crucial for the global oil trade. China's investments in ports in Myanmar, Sri Lanka, and Djibouti also give it significant strategic leverage in the Indian Ocean region.

China's territorial disputes in the South China Sea

Another way in which China is reshaping the global oceans regime is through its territorial disputes in the South China Sea. China claims nearly all of the South China Sea as its own, despite competing claims from neighboring countries such as Vietnam and the Philippines. China has been building artificial islands in the region and militarizing these islands, which has raised concerns from the international community.

China's actions in the South China Sea have significant implications for the global oceans regime. By claiming such a large portion of the South China Sea, China is challenging the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), which recognizes the exclusive economic zones (EEZs) of coastal states. China's actions also threaten the freedom of navigation in the region, as the South China Sea is a crucial sea lane for global trade.

China's development of deep-sea mining technologies

Finally, China's development of deep-sea mining technologies is another way in which the country is reshaping the global oceans regime. China is investing heavily in developing technology for deep-sea mining, which could give it a significant advantage in accessing the vast mineral resources found on the ocean floor. China's deep-sea mining efforts have expanded into international waters, which has raised concerns from other countries.

China's deep-sea mining activities have significant implications for the global oceans regime. As countries around the world seek to develop their own deep-sea mining technologies, China's early mover advantage could give it a significant advantage in this area. Additionally, deep-sea mining activities have the potential to cause significant environmental damage, which could have long-lasting consequences for the health of the world's oceans.

Conclusion

China's actions are reshaping the global oceans regime in significant ways. The country's investments in ports and infrastructure, its territorial disputes in the South China Sea, and its development of deep-sea mining technologies are all examples of how China is exerting influence over the world's oceans.

The implications of China's actions are vast. China's influence over key maritime chokepoints and sea lanes could have significant effects on global trade, while its territorial disputes in the South China Sea threaten the stability of the region. Additionally, China's development of deep-sea mining technologies could have significant environmental consequences.

As China continues to exert its influence over the world's oceans, it is clear that the global oceans regime is changing. The implications of these changes are vast and will impact the world for years to come. It is important for the international community to carefully consider these implications and work towards a sustainable and equitable global oceans regime.
