China Criticizes PM Modi's US Trip



Image Credits https://www.indiatoday.in/world/video/china-slams-america-over-pm-modis-us-trip-calls-it-attempt-to-harass-chinas-economic-progress-2395913-2023-06-21

On September 24, 2019, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi embarked on a week-long visit to the United States to attend the United Nations General Assembly and to engage in bilateral talks with President Donald Trump. This visit was seen by many as an attempt to strengthen India's strategic partnership with the US and to raise its global profile.

However, China was quick to criticize this visit, calling it an attempt to "harass China's economic progress". According to an article published by India Today on September 18, "China strongly opposes any country's attempt to use any excuses to damage China's interests and obstruct China's development."

The article goes on to say that China believes that any attempt to "contain" its economic progress will fail, and that "anyone who tries to do so will eventually be left with a bruised face and broken bones". This statement has been seen by many as a threat to India and to other countries that seek to challenge China's economic and political dominance in the region.

But why is China so concerned about India's partnership with the US? And what are the implications of this conflict for the future of Asia-Pacific region?

The Rise of India

Over the past few decades, India has emerged as a major player in the global economy, thanks in part to its large and rapidly growing population, its educated workforce, and its strategic location at the crossroads of Asia. India has also been proactive in developing its political and military clout, seeking to assert itself as a regional power in its own right.

But India's rise has not gone unnoticed by China, which sees India as a potential competitor for economic and political influence in the region. China has long sought to maintain its dominance in Asia by using its economic and military power to suppress rival powers and to take advantage of smaller, weaker countries.

As India grows stronger, it poses a threat to China's aspirations of regional hegemony. This is why China is so keen to criticize India's close ties with the US, which it sees as an attempt to build a counterbalance to its own power.

Case Study: The South China Sea

One of the most significant flashpoints in the conflict between China and India is in the South China Sea, where China has been aggressively expanding its territorial claims in recent years. The South China Sea is a key shipping route for trade between Asia and the rest of the world, and it is rich in natural resources such as oil and gas.

In 2014, China began constructing artificial islands and military installations in the South China Sea, despite protests from other countries in the region, including Vietnam, the Philippines, and Indonesia. India became involved in the conflict when it signed a strategic partnership with Vietnam, which includes agreements on defense cooperation and joint exploration of oil and gas in the South China Sea.

This move was seen by China as a provocation, and it responded by warning India to stay out of its territorial disputes in the region. However, India has continued to assert its right to defend its strategic interests in the South China Sea, and it has even conducted joint naval exercises with the US and Japan in the area.

The conflict in the South China Sea is likely to escalate in the coming years, as China seeks to consolidate its control over the region and to challenge the influence of the US and its allies. India's role in this conflict is likely to be significant, as it seeks to protect its strategic interests and to maintain a balance of power in the region.

The Future of Asia-Pacific Region

The conflict between China and India is just one example of the complex and interconnected challenges facing the Asia-Pacific region in the coming years. As countries in the region seek to assert their own interests and to protect their strategic priorities, tensions are likely to rise, and competition is likely to escalate.

However, it is also possible that these tensions could lead to greater cooperation and collaboration between countries, as they seek to find common ground and to address shared challenges such as climate change, economic inequality, and political instability.

The future of the Asia-Pacific region is uncertain, and much will depend on the ability of countries in the region to find ways to work together and to navigate the complex and sometimes treacherous waters of international relations.
