Climate Displacement in South Asia and India's Imperative to Act



Image Credits https://southasianvoices.org/climate-displacement-in-south-asia-and-indias-imperative-to-act/

Sheela had lived in the small village of Gopalpur all her life. She had a modest but comfortable home, a few cows and goats that she reared with great care, a small patch of land where she grew vegetables and fruits, and a close-knit community that celebrated festivals with great enthusiasm.

One year, the monsoon rains came with greater intensity than usual, and the river that flowed near Sheela's village burst its banks. The water gushed in, destroying homes and fields, killing people and livestock, and creating a sea of mud and debris.

Sheela barely managed to escape with her family and a few belongings, but her home and her livelihood were gone. They took shelter in a nearby school building that had been turned into a relief camp. The authorities provided them with some food and water, but there was no privacy, no sanitation, no security.

Weeks turned into months, and the relief camp became a permanent refuge for Sheela and hundreds of others who had lost everything to the floods.

The Scale of Climate Displacement

Sheela's story is not unique. Climate displacement, or the forced migration of people due to the impacts of climate change, is a growing global challenge, affecting millions of people every year. In South Asia, a region that is highly vulnerable to climate change, the problem is particularly acute.

According to a recent report by the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, South Asia accounted for more than half of the 17.2 million people globally who were newly displaced by disasters in 2018. India alone was responsible for 2.8 million new displacements, the highest in the world.

The main causes of displacement in South Asia are floods, cyclones, droughts, landslides, and other extreme weather events that are becoming more frequent and severe due to climate change. These events not only destroy homes and infrastructure, but also disrupt livelihoods, displace people from their ancestral lands, and create social and economic tensions.

For example, in the Sunderbans delta of India and Bangladesh, where more than four million people live on mangrove islands and riverbanks, rising sea levels and salinity intrusion are making it difficult to grow crops or rear fish and livestock. As a result, many families are migrating to cities or neighboring countries in search of work, education, and healthcare.

The Need for Action

Climate displacement is not a new phenomenon, but it is becoming more frequent and intense, and there is a need for urgent action to protect the rights and well-being of those who are affected. In this context, India has a critical role to play, both as a major emitter of greenhouse gases and as a country that is highly vulnerable to the impacts of climate change.

There are several steps that India can take to address the challenge of climate displacement:

  • 1. Mitigate greenhouse gas emissions: India can adopt ambitious targets for reducing its greenhouse gas emissions, particularly from the energy, transport, and industrial sectors. This will require investments in renewable energy, energy efficiency, public transport, and low-carbon technologies, as well as regulatory measures such as emissions standards and carbon pricing.
  • 2. Enhance disaster preparedness and response: India can improve its capacity to anticipate, prevent, and respond to disasters, including early warning systems, evacuation plans, risk assessments, and emergency shelters. This will require the involvement of multiple stakeholders, including government agencies, civil society organizations, and the private sector.
  • 3. Develop policies and programs for climate migrants: India can develop policies and programs that recognize the rights and needs of climate migrants, provide them with adequate social protection, facilitate their access to education and health services, and support their integration into host communities. This will require a holistic and rights-based approach, as well as consultations with affected communities and other stakeholders.

Conclusion

  • Climate displacement is a growing global challenge, particularly in South Asia, where millions of people are forced to migrate due to the impacts of climate change.
  • India has a critical role to play in addressing the challenge of climate displacement, by mitigating its greenhouse gas emissions, enhancing its disaster preparedness and response, and developing policies and programs for climate migrants.
  • A concerted effort by India and other countries can help to ensure that the rights and well-being of climate migrants are protected, and that the world is better prepared to cope with the impacts of climate change.
