The Changing Landscape of Turkey's Economy and China's Energy Deals

In a bustling market in Istanbul, a middle-aged Turkish woman stands amidst a crowd of tourists and locals, selling hand-woven baskets, carpets and ceramic plates. She has been running this small business for over two decades, but she says that in recent years, things have become much harder. She is not alone in her struggles - Turkey's economy has been undergoing a massive transformation, and it is causing both challenges and opportunities for businesses and investors alike, both in Turkey and around the world.

The State of Turkey's Economy

Over the past few years, Turkey's economy has gone through a rollercoaster ride. In 2016, the country experienced a failed coup attempt, which resulted in an economic downturn. However, the country's economy started picking up again, and the growth rate has been impressive over the past two years. As of 2021, Turkey ranks as the 19th largest economy in the world and the 7th largest economy in Europe.

However, despite the positive news, Turkey's economy has been facing significant challenges. The country is highly dependent on foreign trade, which has been impacted by global economic tensions, including the trade war between the US and China, Brexit and the COVID-19 pandemic. The Turkish lira has also depreciated significantly, making imports more expensive and causing inflation.

The Changing Landscape of China's Energy Deals

Meanwhile, China has been busy building its energy empire, investing in oil and gas deals all around the world. In the first half of 2021, China secured a flurry of deals with countries such as Russia, Iraq, Angola, and Kazakhstan, among others, to import energy resources. While China's energy deals have been massive and diverse, the country has also been investing in renewable energy, such as wind, solar and hydro power sources.

As the biggest energy producer and consumer in the world, China's energy policies, deals, and investments have significant implications for global geopolitics and the environment.

Case Studies: The Impact of Economic Changes on Businesses

Case Study 1: Manufacturing Industry in Turkey

The manufacturing industry is a significant contributor to Turkey's economy, and it has been facing significant challenges in recent years. The sector is quite dependent on exports, and the trade tensions have made it harder for manufacturers to sell their goods abroad. Additionally, the pandemic has disrupted the supply chain, making it harder for manufacturers to source raw materials and ship finished products. Furthermore, the weakening Turkish lira has made imports of raw materials more expensive, impacting the cost of production.

Despite these challenges, some manufacturers have been finding new opportunities for growth. For example, some companies have started producing COVID-19 related products, such as masks and medical devices, which have been in high demand in Turkey and abroad. Additionally, some companies have been investing in automation and digitalization to increase efficiency, reduce costs and achieve greater sustainability.

Case Study 2: Energy Sector in China

China's energy sector has been rapidly growing, and the country has been investing in both fossil fuels and renewable energy sources. This has led to both opportunities and challenges for energy companies in China.

One example is the state-owned China National Petroleum Corporation (CNPC), which has been exploring energy deals all around the world. In 2021, CNPC signed a deal with Iraq to develop the country's oil fields, which could produce up to 500,000 barrels of crude oil per day. Elsewhere, the company has been investing heavily in renewable energy, aiming to increase its renewable capacity by 50% by 2025.

Despite these deals and investments, there are also growing environmental concerns over the impact of China's energy sector. The country is highly dependent on coal, which is a significant contributor to air pollution, greenhouse gas emissions and climate change. Furthermore, China's investments in clean energy sources, such as solar and wind power, have sometimes come at the expense of human rights and environmental protection, as the country imports raw materials, such as rare-earth metals, from countries with poor labor standards and weak environmental regulations.

Conclusion: What Does the Future Hold?

As the examples of Turkey's economy and China's energy deals demonstrate, the global economic and environmental landscape is rapidly changing, bringing both challenges and opportunities for businesses, investors and policymakers. However, there are three key takeaways:

  1. The COVID-19 pandemic has accelerated existing trends, such as digitalization, automation, and sustainability, and businesses must adapt accordingly to survive and thrive.
  2. The changing energy landscape has significant geopolitical, economic and environmental implications for the world, and policymakers must work together to ensure a sustainable and equitable energy transition.
  3. The global economy is more interconnected than ever before, and businesses must be prepared to navigate volatile and unpredictable situations, such as economic downturns and geopolitical tensions.
