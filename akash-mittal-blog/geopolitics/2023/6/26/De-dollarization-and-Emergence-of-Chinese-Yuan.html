From the Dollar Dominance to the Emergence of Chinese Yuan



Image Credits https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/de-dollarization-and-emergence-of-chinese-yuan/

It was a sunny day in 1971 when Richard Nixon stood in front of the cameras and announced the end of the Bretton Woods system, which had tied the value of the US dollar to gold. With this move, the US dollar became the dominant reserve currency in the world, with countries stockpiling dollars as a form of financial security. But fast forward to 2021, and the world is seeing a gradual shift away from the US dollar towards alternatives such as the Chinese yuan.

Examples of De-dollarization

One of the biggest examples of de-dollarization was seen in 2014, when the Russian government and some of its allies started using their own national currencies in their mutual trade. This move was seen as a retaliation against Western sanctions imposed on Russia, but it also showed that countries were looking towards other currencies beyond the US dollar.

Similarly, in 2018, the Trump administration reimposed sanctions on Iran, leading the country to look for alternatives to the US dollar. Iran started trading oil with other countries using currencies other than the US dollar, mainly the Chinese yuan.

Case Study: China's Rise

China has been gradually increasing its economic influence around the world, and its currency, the yuan, has been a big part of this strategy. The Chinese government has been promoting the yuan as an alternative to the US dollar, and has been encouraging other countries to use it in their trade dealings.

China has been signing currency swap agreements with many countries, allowing them to trade directly in yuan, bypassing the need to convert to US dollars. In addition, the Chinese government has been investing heavily in infrastructure projects in other countries, which has led them to use the yuan as a form of payment.

Another Case Study: Venezuela's Crypto Push

Another country that has been looking for alternatives to the US dollar is Venezuela. The country has been in the midst of an economic crisis for a few years now, and has been hit hard by US sanctions. In response, the country launched its own cryptocurrency, the Petro, which it hopes will become an alternative to the US dollar in international trade.

Venezuela has been promoting the use of the Petro in its trade dealings with other countries, and has been signing agreements with other countries to accept the cryptocurrency in exchange for goods and services.

Conclusion

  • The world is gradually moving away from the US dollar as the dominant reserve currency.
  • China's rise as an economic superpower has led to the promotion of its currency, the yuan, as an alternative to the US dollar.
  • Countries like Russia and Venezuela are actively looking for alternatives to the US dollar, which has led to the rise of alternative currencies such as the Chinese yuan and the Venezuelan Petro.
