Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

Imagine you are on a boat in the South China Sea, minding your own business, when suddenly a Chinese naval ship appears. The crew demands to know what you are doing in Chinese waters and orders you to leave immediately. This scenario has become a common occurrence in recent years as China asserts its territorial claims over much of the South China Sea, despite international opposition.

China's aggressive behavior in the South China Sea is just one example of how the country is reshaping the global oceans regime. Oceans cover over 70% of the Earth's surface and are a vital resource for food, energy, and transportation. The global oceans regime is the collection of rules, institutions, and norms that govern the use and management of these resources. China's actions have significant implications for the global oceans regime and the future of ocean governance.

Case Studies

One of the key ways that China is reshaping the global oceans regime is through its massive infrastructure projects in the South China Sea. In recent years, China has built artificial islands, military bases, and research stations in disputed waters. These projects enable China to extend its territorial claims and exert greater control over the surrounding waters.

Another example is China's use of its fishing fleet to advance its maritime interests. China has the world's largest fishing fleet, and its vessels frequently operate in the Exclusive Economic Zones (EEZs) of other countries, often violating national regulations. This has led to tensions with other countries, particularly in Southeast Asia.

Finally, China's rejection of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) arbitration ruling in 2016 is a clear example of how China is challenging the global oceans regime. The UNCLOS ruling upheld the Philippines' territorial claims in the South China Sea and declared that China's nine-dash line was invalid. China refused to accept the ruling, citing its historical claims to the region.

Conclusion

China's actions in the South China Sea and beyond have significant implications for the global oceans regime. China's territorial claims, its infrastructure projects, and its fishing fleet all contribute to the country's efforts to assert greater control over the oceans. This has led to tensions with other countries, particularly in Southeast Asia, and raised concerns among ocean governance experts.

So, is China reshaping the global oceans regime? The answer is yes, but to what extent remains to be seen. As China continues to assert its territorial claims and push for greater control over the oceans, it will be up to the international community to respond and shape the future of ocean governance.
