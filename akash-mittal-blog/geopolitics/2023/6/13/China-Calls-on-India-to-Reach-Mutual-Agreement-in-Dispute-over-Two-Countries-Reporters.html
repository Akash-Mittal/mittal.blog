China Calls on India to Reach Mutual Agreement in Dispute over Two Countries' Reporters



Image Credits https://www.aa.com.tr/en/asia-pacific/china-calls-on-india-to-reach-mutual-agreement-in-dispute-over-2-countries-reporters/2920855

China has called on India to reach a mutual agreement in the dispute over the treatment of Chinese media outlets' reporters in the country. According to Foreign Ministry spokesman Wang Wenbin, India has treated Chinese media outlets in "an unfair and discriminatory manner." China has raised concerns that Indian authorities have unfairly targeted Chinese media reporters and subjected them to harassment and restrictions.

Instances of Harassment and Restrictions

Several incidents have brought attention to the tensions between the two countries' media outlets. In October 2020, two journalists from China's state-run Xinhua news agency were asked to leave India. Indian officials accused them of violating visa regulations and engaging in activities beyond their journalistic duties. The journalists denied the allegations and called for transparency in the visa regulations.

In March 2021, three Chinese journalists from the China Daily newspaper were denied permission to cover the upcoming assembly elections in the northeastern state of Assam. Indian officials stated that the journalists failed to get the necessary clearances and permissions to cover the elections. The Chinese Embassy in India expressed disappointment at the decision and accused India of violating the agreements between the two nations.

Furthermore, India has implemented restrictions on Chinese apps and websites, which has hampered communication and reporting between the two countries. In June 2020, the Indian government banned 59 Chinese apps, including TikTok, WeChat, and UC Browser, citing national security concerns. Several Chinese media outlets, including Global Times and People's Daily, faced disruptions in their online presence in India.

Case Studies

Case Study 1: The Xinhua Journalists

The case of the two Xinhua journalists highlights the ongoing tensions between the two countries' media outlets. The journalists, Wang Wenjie and Tang Lu, were asked to leave India in October 2020. The Indian officials accused them of violating visa regulations and engaging in activities beyond their journalistic duties. However, the journalists denied the allegations and called for transparency in the visa regulations.

Furthermore, Xinhua called on Indian authorities to provide evidence to support the allegations against the journalists. Xinhua stated that it had always adhered to the laws and regulations of India and respected the country's sovereignty and territorial integrity. The incident sparked outrage among Chinese media outlets, with China Daily stating that the move was a clear violation of press freedom.

Case Study 2: The China Daily Journalists

In March 2021, three Chinese journalists from the China Daily newspaper were denied permission to cover the upcoming assembly elections in the northeastern state of Assam. Indian officials stated that the journalists failed to get the necessary clearances and permissions to cover the elections. However, the Chinese Embassy in India expressed disappointment at the decision and accused India of violating the agreements between the two nations.

Furthermore, China Daily stated that the denial was a clear violation of the "consensus reached between leaders of the two countries on promoting people-to-people exchanges and cultural cooperation, as well as media exchanges and communication." The incident highlighted the growing tensions between the two nations and raised concerns about the state of media freedom in India.

Conclusion

The dispute over the treatment of Chinese media outlets' reporters in India underscores the wider tensions between the two countries. India and China have long been engaged in a border dispute, which has resulted in military clashes and tensions. The treatment of Chinese media reporters in India is just one aspect of the broader conflict between the two nations.

In order to resolve this dispute, both countries need to engage in constructive dialogue and reach a mutual agreement that supports media freedom and ensures fair and equal treatment of all journalists. By doing so, they can pave the way for greater understanding and cooperation between their two nations.
