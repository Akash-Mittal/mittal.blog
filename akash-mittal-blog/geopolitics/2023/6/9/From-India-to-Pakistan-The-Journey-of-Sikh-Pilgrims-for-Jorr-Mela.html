From India to Pakistan: The Journey of Sikh Pilgrims for Jorr Mela



Image Credits  https://www.indiatoday.in/india/story/indian-sikh-pilgrims-pakistan-jorr-mela-guru-arjun-dev-ji-2390635-2023-06-08

It was a sunny day in Amritsar, India when a group of Sikh pilgrims gathered at the Golden Temple. They had been waiting for this day for months, packing their bags with clothes, food, and other essentials. They were excited to embark on a journey to Pakistan to attend the Jorr Mela at Gurdwara Punja Sahib, Hasanabdal. As they made their way to the Wagah Border, they felt a sense of camaraderie and excitement, knowing that they were part of a larger community of Sikhs who were making the same journey.

This journey is not just a pilgrimage, but a testament to the strength of the Sikh community. Despite the political tensions and border disputes between India and Pakistan, these Sikh pilgrims continue to cross the border to attend religious festivals. They believe that their faith is greater than their nationality and that they have a right to visit their sacred sites.

Case Studies: The Importance of Jorr Mela

The Jorr Mela is an annual festival that marks the death anniversary of the fifth Sikh Guru, Arjun Dev Ji. It is one of the most important festivals for the Sikh community, and attracts pilgrims from all over the world. The festival is held at Gurdwara Punja Sahib, Hasanabdal, which is located in the Punjab province of Pakistan.

For the Sikh community, attending Jorr Mela is not just an act of religious devotion, but a way to connect with their heritage. It is a chance to learn about their history and culture, and to pay homage to their spiritual leaders. The festival is a symbol of unity and solidarity, as Sikhs from different countries and backgrounds come together to celebrate their faith.

Despite the challenges of crossing the border between India and Pakistan, many Sikhs continue to attend Jorr Mela every year. One such pilgrim is Gurbachan Singh, who has been attending Jorr Mela for over 40 years. He believes that the festival is a way to keep the Sikh spirit alive and to pass on the traditions to the next generation.

Another pilgrim, Balwinder Kaur, traveled with her family from Punjab, India to attend Jorr Mela. She believes that the festival is a way to connect with her roots and to feel a sense of belonging to the Sikh community.

Conclusion: The Strength of the Sikh Community

Despite the political tensions and border disputes between India and Pakistan, the Sikh community remains resilient and united. They continue to cross the border to attend religious festivals, such as Jorr Mela, to connect with their heritage and to pass on their traditions to the next generation. The journey to Pakistan is not just a pilgrimage, but a symbol of the Sikh community's strength and perseverance.
