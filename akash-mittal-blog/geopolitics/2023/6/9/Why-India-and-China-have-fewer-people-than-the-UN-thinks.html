Why India and China have fewer people than the UN thinks



Image Credits https://www.bangkokpost.com/opinion/opinion/2588239/china-india-have-fewer-people-than-the-un-thinks

Population projections are important for governments, international organizations, and businesses to plan for future growth and development. However, these projections are often subject to uncertainties and inaccuracies due to various factors such as migration, fertility rates, and mortality rates. One recent example is the United Nations' estimate of India's population surpassing China's, which may be revised down in the future based on upcoming census data.

The UN's projection and its implications

In April 2021, the United Nations Population Division released its latest estimates of world population, which showed that India had 1.39 billion people and China had 1.34 billion people. This was a significant milestone for India, which had been projected to overtake China for years due to its higher fertility rate and younger population. However, some experts questioned the accuracy of the UN's projection, citing various factors such as incomplete data, sampling errors, and undercounting of migrants and informal workers.

The implications of India's population overtaking China's are far-reaching, as it may affect not only political and economic power dynamics but also environmental and social welfare issues. For example, India's youth bulge may provide a demographic dividend of a large workforce and a growing consumer market, but it also poses challenges of providing education, healthcare, and employment opportunities for millions of young people. On the other hand, China's aging population may lead to a shrinking workforce and a greater burden on social security and healthcare systems, but it also implies a lower demand for energy and resources.

The challenges of accurate population measurement

Accurate population measurement is a complex and ongoing process that requires various methods and tools. One of the primary sources of population data is the national census, which provides detailed and comprehensive information on age, sex, education, occupation, household size, and other demographic and socioeconomic factors. However, conducting a census is a costly and time-consuming operation that may involve a large number of enumerators, supervisors, and administrative staff.

Moreover, some populations may be harder to count than others, such as those in remote or conflict-affected areas, migrant workers, homeless people, or ethnic and linguistic minorities. In addition, the definition and boundaries of a population may also vary depending on the purpose and scope of the measurement. For example, some countries may only count citizens as their population, while others may include permanent residents or foreigners.

Another method of population measurement is the demographic surveillance system, which involves continuous monitoring of a selected population over time through various data sources such as health facilities, schools, or community surveys. This method allows for more frequent and flexible data collection and can capture changes in population dynamics such as births, deaths, migrations, and disease outbreaks. However, it also requires a high level of coordination and standardization among different data sources and may not be representative of the whole population.

Case studies of population measurement in India and China

India and China are the two most populous countries in the world, with a combined population of over 2.7 billion people. Both countries have implemented various methods and strategies to measure their populations, but they also face similar challenges of accuracy and completeness. Here are some examples of population measurement in India and China:

India's National Population Register

The National Population Register (NPR) is a digital database of residents of India that was established in 2010. The NPR is based on a house-to-house enumeration by a trained enumerator or surveyor, who collects the name, age, sex, marital status, occupation, education, and nationality of each person in a household. The NPR is intended to serve as a basis for various schemes and policies related to social welfare, law enforcement, and national security. However, the NPR has been criticized for its potential use for discriminatory purposes and for its reliance on self-declaration of nationality, which may exclude certain groups such as refugees and stateless persons.

China's hukou system

The hukou system is a household registration system in China that dates back to the 1950s. The hukou system is based on the principle of permanent residence, which means that a person's social benefits, rights, and obligations are tied to the place of their registered household. The system divides the population into rural and urban hukou, and it regulates migration between these two categories through a quota system and various restrictions on employment, housing, and education. The hukou system has been criticized for perpetuating inequality and discrimination, as it limits the mobility and opportunities of rural migrants who seek better jobs and living conditions in urban areas.

Conclusion

Population measurement is a crucial task that requires continuous improvement and innovation to account for changing demographics, social structures, and economic conditions. Accurate and timely population data can inform policies and programs that aim to improve people's well-being, reduce inequality, and promote sustainable development. However, it is also important to recognize the limitations and challenges of population measurement, such as incomplete data, sampling errors, and undercounting of marginalized populations. By acknowledging these challenges and investing in better data collection and analysis, we can create a more equitable and inclusive world for everyone.
