170 Indian Sikh pilgrims reach Pakistan's Lahore for Jorr Mela



Image Credits https://www.indiatoday.in/india/story/indian-sikh-pilgrims-pakistan-jorr-mela-guru-arjun-dev-ji-2390635-2023-06-08

Around 170 Indian Sikh pilgrims arrived in Pakistan via Wagah Border to attend the festivities of the Jorr Mela to be held at Gurdwara Punja Sahib, Hasanabdal, marking the death anniversary of the fifth Sikh Guru Arjun Dev Ji.

A Celebration of Guru Arjun Dev Ji

The Jorr Mela is an annual Sikh festival held at the Gurdwara Punja Sahib in Hasanabdal, Pakistan. It marks the death anniversary of the fifth Sikh Guru Arjun Dev Ji who was martyred in 1606. The festival is attended by thousands of pilgrims from all over the world, including India, Pakistan, and other parts of the world.

The Arrival of 170 Indian Sikh Pilgrims

The arrival of 170 Indian Sikh pilgrims to participate in the Jorr Mela is a significant event for the Sikh community, as it signifies the strong cultural and religious ties between India and Pakistan. The pilgrims were warmly welcomed by the Punjabi authorities and members of the Pakistani Sikh community.

Examples of Cultural and Religious Ties Between India and Pakistan

Despite the political tensions between India and Pakistan, the two countries share a long history of cultural and religious ties. The Sikh community, in particular, has strong roots in both countries. Here are some examples:

  • Guru Nanak Dev Ji: The founder of Sikhism, Guru Nanak Dev Ji, was born in 1469 in what is now known as Pakistan. He spent most of his life in India, but his teachings and philosophy have had a profound impact on both countries.
  • The Partition of India: In 1947, India and Pakistan were partitioned into two separate countries. The decision led to the displacement of millions of people and the loss of countless lives. However, despite the violence that accompanied the partition, many Sikh families remained in Pakistan and have continued to maintain strong cultural ties with their Indian counterparts.
  • Gurdwaras: There are numerous gurdwaras (Sikh temples) in both India and Pakistan that are considered holy sites by the Sikh community. Many of these gurdwaras are located in areas that are now part of the other country. For example, the Gurdwara Darbar Sahib in Kartarpur, Pakistan, is located just 4 km from the Indian border and is visited by thousands of Indian Sikh pilgrims every year.
  • Sikh Festivals: Sikh festivals such as Vaisakhi, Diwali, and Guru Nanak Jayanti are celebrated with great enthusiasm in both India and Pakistan. These festivals provide an opportunity for Sikhs to come together, irrespective of national boundaries, and celebrate their common heritage.

Conclusion

The arrival of 170 Indian Sikh pilgrims to participate in the Jorr Mela is a testament to the enduring cultural and religious ties between India and Pakistan. Despite the political tensions between the two countries, the Sikh community has remained a bridge of sorts, connecting people on both sides of the border through shared history and heritage.
