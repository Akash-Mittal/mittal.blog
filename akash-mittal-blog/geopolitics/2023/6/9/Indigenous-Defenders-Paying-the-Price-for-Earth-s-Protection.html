Indigenous Defenders: Paying the Price for Earth's Protection



Image Credits  https://www.usip.org/publications/2023/06/earths-best-defenders-are-indigenous-they-pay-price-violence

It was a typical early morning in the Amazon rainforest when a group of Indigenous people spotted a group of illegal loggers cutting down trees in their ancestral land. The loggers were armed, but the Indigenous people were determined to protect their forest, their home, their source of life. They stood between the trees and the machines, refusing to back down to the aggressors.

For centuries, Indigenous peoples have been the most committed defenders of the Earth. They have lived in harmony with their surroundings, respecting and protecting the natural resources that sustain life. They have developed a rich knowledge of the flora and fauna, of the soil and the water, of the cycles and the rhythms of the land. They have passed down this knowledge from generation to generation, along with a deep sense of connection to the Earth, a profound spirituality that recognizes the interdependence of all life.

However, the rise of modern civilization has brought about an era of unprecedented environmental destruction, as the pursuit of profit and power has led to the exploitation and degradation of the planet's resources. This destruction has hit the Indigenous peoples the hardest, as their lands and waters have been invaded, polluted, and destroyed by extractive industries, monoculture agriculture, damming and mining projects, and other forms of corporate and state violence.

The defenders of the Earth have become the targets of violence, intimidation, harassment, and persecution, as they resist the forces of destruction and demand respect for their rights, their territories, and their ways of life. They pay a heavy price for their defense, as their communities are threatened, displaced, and decimated, their leaders are jailed, tortured, and killed, and their cultures are eroded, suppressed, and forgotten.

Case Studies

The Philippines

The Indigenous people of the Philippines have been fighting against mining, logging, and other forms of resource exploitation for decades. They have established barricades, roadblocks, and other forms of collective resistance to prevent the entry of mining companies and other extractive industries into their territories. They have also taken legal action to challenge the granting of permits and licenses to these companies, arguing that they violate their constitutional and customary rights.

One of the most prominent cases of Indigenous resistance in the Philippines is that of the Lumad people of Mindanao, who have been subjected to a brutal campaign of militarization, paramilitarization, and displacement by the Philippine government and the military. The Lumad schools, which provide alternative education based on Indigenous knowledge and values, have been attacked, threatened, and closed down by the military, which accuses them of being fronts for the Communist Party of the Philippines. The Lumad leaders who have defended their land and their culture have been labeled as terrorists and subjected to harassment, detention, and extrajudicial killings.

Canada

Canada, often regarded as a progressive and peaceful country, has a dark history of violence against Indigenous peoples, from the residential school system to the forced relocation and assimilation policies. Today, the Indigenous peoples of Canada are still fighting for their rights and their survival, as they face ongoing discrimination, marginalization, and dispossession.

One of the most pressing issues for the Indigenous peoples of Canada is the exploitation of their lands and waters by the oil and gas industry, which threatens their health, their culture, and their future. The Indigenous peoples of the Wet'suwet'en Nation in British Columbia have been leading a peaceful resistance against the construction of a pipeline that would carry fracked gas from the northeast of the province to the coast. The Wet'suwet'en hereditary chiefs, who have the authority over the traditional territories, have rejected the pipeline and demanded the recognition of their sovereignty and their right to free, prior, and informed consent.

Brazil

The Amazon rainforest, the largest tropical rainforest on Earth, is home to hundreds of Indigenous peoples who have lived in harmony with the land for millennia. However, the Amazon is also a target of industrial-scale deforestation, as loggers, miners, farmers, and ranchers clear vast areas of forest for profit. The situation has worsened under the current Brazilian president, Jair Bolsonaro, who has encouraged the destruction of the Amazon and the persecution of the Indigenous peoples and their allies.

One of the most inspiring examples of Indigenous resistance in Brazil is that of the Yanomami people, who have been defending their land and their culture for decades. The Yanomami, one of the largest Indigenous groups in the Amazon, have faced a history of violence, exploitation, and disease, as their land has been invaded by gold miners, who bring diseases, violence, and pollution. The Yanomami have established a network of Indigenous health agents who provide basic healthcare services, including vaccination, sanitation, and education, to their communities. They have also partnered with NGOs and scientists to raise awareness of their struggle and to protect their territory from illegal mining.

Conclusion

"The defenders of the Earth have become the targets of violence, intimidation, harassment, and persecution, as they resist the forces of destruction and demand respect for their rights, their territories, and their ways of life."

The Indigenous peoples are at the forefront of the struggle for the protection of the Earth and its biodiversity. They are the ones who hold the key to the sustainable management of the land and the resources, as they have demonstrated for centuries. They are the ones who remind us of our dependence and our responsibility towards the planet that sustains us all.

However, the Indigenous peoples are also the ones who pay the highest price for their defense, as they face violence, intimidation, harassment, and persecution from the forces of destruction. Their struggle is not only a matter of human rights, but also of environmental justice and global sustainability. We need to support and amplify their voices, to recognize and respect their knowledge and their culture, and to include them in the decision-making processes that affect their lives and their territories.

Three points to take away:

  • Indigenous peoples are the most committed and effective defenders of the Earth's biodiversity and resources.
  • Indigenous peoples are also the most vulnerable and persecuted groups in the face of environmental destruction and corporate and state violence.
  • Supporting the Indigenous peoples' struggle for their rights, their territories, and their ways of life is essential for environmental justice and global sustainability.
