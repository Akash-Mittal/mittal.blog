Hindu Teen Kidnapped, Converted to Islam, and Married Off by Tutor in Pakistan



Image Credits https://www.indiatoday.in/world/story/minor-hindu-girl-kidnapped-converted-to-islam-married-pakistan-2390583-2023-06-08

Imagine waking up one day to find out that your teenage daughter has been kidnapped, forcibly converted to Islam, and married off to a stranger. This nightmare became a reality for the parents of a 15-year-old Hindu girl in Pakistan.

The girl's father said that three armed men entered their home, looted gold ornaments, and kidnapped the teen at gunpoint. She was taken to a nearby madrasa (Islamic seminary) where she was forcibly converted to Islam and married off to her tutor.

This incident is not an isolated one. Forced conversions of religious minorities, particularly Hindus and Christians, have been a longstanding issue in Pakistan. Despite laws that criminalize forced conversions, the practice continues with impunity.

Examples of Forced Conversions in Pakistan

A 2014 report by the Movement for Solidarity and Peace in Pakistan estimated that every year, about 1,000 Christian and Hindu women are forcibly converted to Islam through abduction, coercion, and fraud. Many of these women are forced to marry their abductors, who are often much older than them.

One such case is that of Arzoo Raja, a 13-year-old Christian girl who was abducted and forcibly converted to Islam and married off to her 44-year-old abductor in October 2020. Despite protests from her family and human rights organizations, the Sindh High Court upheld the marriage and ruled that Arzoo had willingly converted and married.

In another case, a 14-year-old Christian girl, Maira Shahbaz, was abducted and forcibly converted to Islam and married off to her abductor in April 2020. After seeking refuge in the UK, she spoke out about her ordeal and called for justice.

Impact on Religious Minorities

The issue of forced conversions has had a devastating impact on religious minorities in Pakistan. Many families live in fear of their daughters being abducted and forcibly converted. Even if they are able to recover their daughters, they often face discrimination and threats from their communities.

Forced conversions also have a chilling effect on interfaith relations in Pakistan. They deepen the mistrust and suspicion between different religious communities and undermine efforts to promote tolerance and harmony.

Conclusion

The issue of forced conversions in Pakistan is a complex and sensitive one. On the one hand, there are concerns about the human rights of religious minorities and the need to protect vulnerable girls and women. On the other hand, there are concerns about respecting the religious beliefs and practices of Muslims and not portraying Islam as a religion that promotes violence and oppression.

To address this issue, there needs to be a multi-stakeholder approach that involves religious leaders, civil society organizations, law enforcement agencies, and the government. This approach should aim to promote dialogue, dispel myths and stereotypes, and strengthen the legal framework for preventing and addressing forced conversions.
