Despite Geopolitical Tension...Can Robust Bilateral Trade Exist? Australia And China, A Good Example Perhaps



Image Credits https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/international-trade-amp-investment/1327504/despite-geopolitical-tensioncan-robust-bilateral-trade-exist-australia-and-china-a-good-example-perhaps

Australia and China have a complex relationship that has been marked by geopolitical tension, but despite that, the economic ties between them have remained strong. In fact, Australia is China's sixth-largest trading partner, and China is Australia's largest two-way trading partner.

The relationship between the two countries dates back to the 18th century when Chinese migrants first arrived in Australia, mainly as gold miners. Since then, trade between Australia and China has steadily grown, and despite some ups and downs, particularly in recent years, the economic relationship between them remains robust.

Examples with Case Studies

One of the reasons behind the robust economic relationship between Australia and China is the wide range of goods and services that they trade. Some of the major Australian exports to China include iron ore, natural gas, and agricultural products such as beef, wool, and wine. On the other hand, China exports a range of products to Australia, including electronics, clothing, and machinery.

Australia is particularly reliant on China as a buyer of its resources. For instance, iron ore is Australia's largest export industry, and China is the biggest market for Australian iron ore. In recent years, China's rapidly growing economy has had a significant impact on Australia's exports, with the demand for natural resources driving much of the growth.

Despite the strong economic relationship, there have been some challenges in bilateral trade between Australia and China. In recent years, there have been several diplomatic and trade disputes between the two countries, including a ban by China on certain Australian exports such as barley, beef and wine, which has caused significant disruption to some Australian industries.

However, both countries have continued to emphasize the importance of their economic relationship and have taken steps to manage their differences. For example, in November 2020, the two countries signed a major trade deal, the China-Australia Free Trade Agreement (ChAFTA), which aims to reduce barriers to trade and increase market access for goods and services. This agreement has helped to improve bilateral trade relations between the two countries.

  • The example of Australia and China shows that despite geopolitical tension, robust bilateral trade can exist between two countries. Economic interests often transcend political differences, and trade can provide a way to manage international tensions.
  • The key to maintaining a solid economic relationship during periods of tension is for both countries to show restraint and focus on the long-term benefits of trade. It is vital that both sides approach disputes with a willingness to negotiate and find mutually beneficial solutions.
  • The international community should encourage more bilateral trade between countries, even when relationships are strained. By focusing on building economic ties, countries can find ways to work together and create mutually beneficial outcomes despite their political differences.
