A Chance for a Defence Partnership with Germany: India's Quest for Diversification



Image Credits https://www.ips-journal.eu/topics/foreign-and-security-policy/a-question-of-defence-6767/

Once again, India is facing a difficult question of defence. It is no secret that Russia has been its main supplier of military equipment for decades. However, after Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2014 and its annexation of Crimea a month later, India has sought to diversify its military suppliers. In this context, Germany stands out as a potential partner for India's defence needs. This article explores the prospects and challenges of a defence partnership between India and Germany.

Examples of India's Quest for Diversification

India's need for diversification in defence procurement is not new, but the urgency grew after the Ukrainian crisis. India and Russia are bound together by a close strategic relationship that has spanned over six decades. Russia has been India's main supplier of military equipment since the end of the Cold War and remains the biggest supplier of major weapon systems.

However, India's dependence on Russian military equipment has been a strategic vulnerability, which was exposed during the 1999 Kargil War with Pakistan. The Indian Air Force's fleet of MiG-21s, purchased from Russia in the 1960s, proved to be ineffective against Pakistani F-16 fighters. Since then, India has been looking for alternatives.

In the last few years, India has signed defence deals with several other countries. For instance:

  • In 2015, India and France signed a contract for the purchase of 36 Rafale fighter jets, worth €7.8 billion.
  • In 2018, India signed a deal with the United States to purchase 24 MH-60R Seahawk helicopters for the Indian Navy, worth $2.6 billion.
  • In 2019, India and Russia signed a deal for five S-400 air defence systems, worth $5.4 billion.

However, these deals are not enough to meet India's defence needs. India's military is still heavily dependent on Russian equipment. For instance, the Indian Air Force operates over 270 Russian Sukhoi Su-30MKI fighter jets, which were ordered under a $12 billion deal in 2000. The Indian Navy also operates Russian submarines and aircraft carriers.

The Prospects and Challenges of a Defence Partnership between India and Germany

Germany's defence industry is one of the most advanced in the world. It boasts of companies like Airbus, Rheinmetall, and ThyssenKrupp, which have expertise in aerospace, defence electronics, and naval and land systems. German companies have a reputation for high quality and innovation. They also offer advanced technologies that are not available from Russia or other countries that India has deals with.

Germany and India have been cultivating a strategic partnership since the 1990s. In 2000, they signed a joint declaration on a "strategic partnership in the 21st century". Over the years, their cooperation has expanded to several areas, including trade, investment, science and technology, and defence.

A defence partnership between India and Germany has several advantages:

  • Germany can offer India high-quality military equipment and advanced technologies that are not available from Russia.
  • India can benefit from Germany's expertise in defence production, including joint ventures and technology transfers.
  • The partnership can help reduce India's dependence on Russia and increase its strategic autonomy.

However, there are also several challenges:

  • The two countries have different defence procurement procedures and standards, which could pose a hurdle in joint projects.
  • Germany's strict export control regulations could limit the transfer of sensitive technologies to India.
  • The partnership could face political opposition in Germany, especially if it involves arms exports to a country like India, which has a poor human rights record.

Conclusion

India's quest for diversification in defence procurement is understandable, given its strategic vulnerability and the changing global security environment. Germany's defence industry has the potential to offer India high-quality military equipment and advanced technologies that it needs. A defence partnership between the two countries can benefit both sides and contribute to regional and global security.

However, the challenges cannot be ignored. The two countries need to address the differences in their defence procurement procedures and standards and find ways to overcome the hurdles. Germany also needs to strike a balance between its strict export control regulations and India's need for advanced technologies. Moreover, the partnership needs to be based on shared values, including respect for human rights and international law.

In conclusion, a defence partnership between India and Germany is a chance worth exploring. It has the potential to strengthen their strategic partnership and contribute to a more secure and stable world.
