Two Nations, One Lesson: Press Freedom in India and China



Image Credits  https://www.telegraphindia.com/opinion/two-nations-one-lesson-press-freedom-in-india-and-china/cid/1943489

One of the hallmarks of press freedom is the ability of journalists to report on stories without fear of persecution or harassment. However, this is not always the case in some countries. For instance, China and India, two of the world's oldest civilizations, have very different approaches to media freedom. While India is considered one of the freest countries in the world when it comes to press freedom, China is often ranked very low, sometimes at the bottom of the list.

Examples with Case Studies

India

In India, journalists are generally free to cover any story they wish, and there is a lively and diverse media ecosystem. However, that does not mean that they don't face challenges. For example, in 2020, several journalists who reported on sensitive topics were attacked or harassed by police officials. In one case, journalist Prashant Kanojia was arrested and charged with sedition after he posted a tweet critical of the Uttar Pradesh Chief Minister. This led to widespread condemnation from the media fraternity and civil society, and he was eventually released on bail.

China

China, on the other hand, has a very different approach to media freedom. The government controls all media, and it is illegal for journalists to report on sensitive topics without official approval. As a result, many journalists who report on sensitive topics, such as human rights abuses or corruption, are often harassed, arrested, or imprisoned. In its latest press freedom index, Reporters Without Borders ranked China 179th out of 180 countries and described it as the world's biggest jailer of journalists, with 100 currently behind bars.

Conclusion in 3 points

  • India has a free press, with journalists generally free to report on any story they want, subject to certain restrictions.
  • China has a highly controlled media environment where journalists are subject to censorship, harassment, and imprisonment for reporting on sensitive topics.
  • Both India and China can learn from each other when it comes to media freedom. India can learn from China's ability to control the spread of 'fake news,' whereas China can learn from India's more open approach to reporting on a wide range of topics.

Two nations, one lesson: Press freedom in India and China

Journalism is often described as the Fourth Estate, the watchdog that keeps a check on those in power. But in countries like India and China, this watchdog is often muzzled by those in power. While India is considered one of the world's largest democracies, its press freedom is not without challenges. China, on the other hand, is a one-party communist state that has a long history of controlling the media. Let's delve deeper into the state of press freedom in these two countries.

India's Press Freedom Landscape

India ranks 142nd out of 180 countries in the Reporters Without Borders' World Press Freedom Index 2020. The rationale behind this ranking is the backlash faced by journalists who speak out against the ruling government or those in positions of power. The killing of journalist Gauri Lankesh in 2017 made headlines around the world. Lankesh was known for her criticism of the government's policies and her work as an activist. The fact that no arrests have been made in the case yet speaks volumes about the state of press freedom in India. The arrest of journalist Siddique Kappan in 2020 while covering the Hathras rape case also raised concerns about the freedom of the press in India.

China's Press Freedom Landscape

China's press freedom is often described as non-existent. Reporters Without Borders ranks China at 179 out of 180 countries in the World Press Freedom Index 2020. China is the world's biggest jailer of journalists, with over 100 currently behind bars, according to Reporters Without Borders. The Chinese government controls all media outlets which means that they can control the narrative and suppress any information that goes against their interests. The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted China's lack of press freedom, as the authorities have been accused of suppressing information about the virus.

Conclusion

The state of press freedom in India and China is a cause for concern. The press plays a crucial role as a watchdog in any democracy, and it is important that they are able to perform their role without fear of retribution. The lack of press freedom in these two countries is a threat to democracy and must be addressed by the respective governments. It is only when journalists are allowed to report without fear that the people can be informed and hold those in power accountable.

An analysis of press freedom in two of the world's largest countries

In India, journalists have been subject to violence, harassment, and pressure from the government in recent years. One of the most notable cases is that of Gauri Lankesh, a prominent journalist who was assassinated in 2017 for speaking out against right-wing extremism in India. Her death sparked outrage and led to renewed calls for press freedom in the country.

In China, the situation for journalists is even more dire. Reporters Without Borders ranked China 179th out of 180 countries in its latest press freedom index, describing it as the "world's biggest jailer of journalists." With 100 currently behind bars, the Chinese government has shown little tolerance for dissenting voices.

Case Studies

India

Gauri Lankesh was just one of many journalists in India who have been targeted for their reporting. In 2018, journalist Rana Ayyub faced death threats and harassment after publishing a book critical of the government. In another case, journalist Nikhil Wagle was attacked by a group of men for speaking out against right-wing extremism in Maharashtra.

China

In China, censorship and government control of the media are widespread. Journalists who report on sensitive topics, such as corruption or human rights abuses, risk arrest and imprisonment. In 2018, the Chinese government detained at least a dozen journalists for their reporting on issues such as labor rights and the treatment of ethnic minorities in Xinjiang.

Conclusion

Press freedom is critical for a healthy democracy. Without a free and independent press, citizens are at the mercy of those in power. Both India and China have a long way to go in terms of protecting press freedom. However, the recent protests in India in support of farmers' rights have shown that people are willing to speak out against government overreach and demand greater transparency and accountability.
