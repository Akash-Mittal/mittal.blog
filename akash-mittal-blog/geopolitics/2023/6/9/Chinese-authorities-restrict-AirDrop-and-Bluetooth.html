Chinese censors take aim at AirDrop and Bluetooth



Image Credits https://www.bbc.com/news/world-asia-china-65830185

The Chinese government has been blocking access to foreign websites and social media platforms such as Google and Facebook for years under the ever-tightening "Great Firewall" of China. However, recently the authorities have found yet another way to monitor and control information flows: by restricting AirDrop and Bluetooth usage.

What are AirDrop and Bluetooth?

AirDrop and Bluetooth are two wireless file-sharing technologies built into Apple devices. AirDrop works by facilitating peer-to-peer file sharing among different Apple devices, while Bluetooth is a protocol for wireless communication between devices.

The new restrictions

The Chinese authorities are concerned that these technologies can be exploited to spread subversive content and therefore have started to block or restrict their use. For example, users in China can no longer use AirDrop in public spaces such as train stations and airports, and Bluetooth must be disabled in some hospitals and government buildings.

Case studies

These new regulations are already having an impact on people's lives in China. For example, a photographer in Shanghai is unable to transfer her images to clients via AirDrop while in public areas due to the restrictions. A journalist in Beijing cannot use Bluetooth to transfer files to sources who prefer to remain anonymous.

The implications

The new restrictions on AirDrop and Bluetooth are part of a wider crackdown on dissent in China. By limiting access to these file-sharing technologies, Chinese authorities can better monitor and control the flow of information and potentially prevent their citizens from accessing unauthorized content.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the Chinese government's restrictions on AirDrop and Bluetooth are just the latest in a trend of tightening their control of the Internet. However, as history shows, information always finds a way to flow, and these restrictions will not be able to stop the spread of information entirely. The Chinese government must recognize that, in today's world, free access to information is a fundamental human right that cannot be suppressed.
