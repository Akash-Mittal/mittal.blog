Revival of TAPI Pipeline: Profit, Geopolitics, and Security Challenges



Image Credits https://oilprice.com/Geopolitics/Asia/Why-Russia-Wants-To-Revive-The-TAPI-Pipeline.html

It was a hot summer day in 1995 when Turkmenistan's President Saparmurat Niyazov and Pakistan's Prime Minister Benazir Bhutto signed a memorandum of understanding to build a gas pipeline that would connect the energy-rich Central Asian country with Pakistan via Afghanistan. The ambitious project, known as the TAPI pipeline, was expected to provide an alternative route for Turkmenistan's gas exports, which were then heavily dependent on Russia.

However, the project faced numerous challenges, including political instability in Afghanistan, security concerns, and financial constraints. It took more than two decades for the TAPI pipeline to start construction, and it still faces uncertainties and risks.

Why Russia Wants to Revive the TAPI Pipeline?

The revival of the TAPI pipeline could be highly profitable for the involved parties, but Russia has its own reasons to support the project. Russia has been a dominant player in the energy sector of Central Asia, and it has long used its influence on the region to maintain its control over the European gas market. However, the rise of alternative gas suppliers, such as the United States and Qatar, has challenged Russia's dominance and forced it to look for new markets.

Reviving the TAPI pipeline would allow Russia to diversify its gas export routes and weaken its competitors' positions. It would also enable Russia to maintain its influence over Central Asia, which is a vital region for its geopolitical and security interests. Moreover, Russia has been facing increasing pressure from the European Union to reduce its gas exports, which account for a significant portion of its revenues. By promoting the TAPI pipeline, Russia could reduce its dependence on the European market and secure new customers.

Geopolitical and Security Challenges of the TAPI Pipeline

The TAPI pipeline project faces numerous geopolitical and security challenges, which could delay or even derail the project. One of the main challenges is the volatile situation in Afghanistan, which is a key transit country for the pipeline. The Afghan government controls only a small portion of its territory, and the rest is under the influence of various armed groups, including the Taliban. The security risks associated with the project are high, and any disruption could have severe consequences for the entire region.

Furthermore, the TAPI pipeline crosses through regions with a history of ethnic and religious conflicts, which could fuel tensions and create new security risks. The project also faces financial challenges, as the construction costs are estimated to be around $10 billion, which is a significant amount for the participating countries. The economic viability of the project depends on the gas prices and the demand for gas in the region, which are subject to fluctuations.

Conclusion

The TAPI pipeline project presents both opportunities and challenges for the involved parties. It could provide a new source of gas for Pakistan and India, and diversify the gas export routes for Turkmenistan. It could also weaken Russia's gas dominance in the region and reduce its dependence on the European market. However, the security and geopolitical risks associated with the project are high, and the financial viability of the project is uncertain.
