The Impact of Geopolitics and Trade on Shipping: Analysis and Case Studies



Image Credits https://www.seatrade-maritime.com/regulation/podcast-geopolitics-and-trade-punit-oza-maritime-nxt

The world of shipping is not immune to the ripple effects of geopolitical decisions and trade tensions. Maritime trade is a vital area of interest for any nation. It not only supports their economy but also affects their diplomatic relations with trading partners around the world.

One of the most recent and severe examples of this nexus is the current military conflict in Ukraine. The disruption in supply chains and the threats to the broader security environment of the region are the primary reasons why companies now seek to understand and prepare for geopolitical and trade tensions' impacts on shipping.

Analysis of the Impact of Geopolitics and Trade on Shipping

Geopolitics and trade impact shipping in several ways. One of the most critical effects is the disruption of shipping routes. Seizure of ships, piracy, and sanctions make sea transport increasingly challenging, dangerous, and expensive. Trade disputes often result in ship detentions, port stay, and delays; causing additional costs for the shippers.

When a country imposes sanctions or trade restrictions, it significantly affects the shipping lanes and shipping companies' revenues and operations. For example, the United States' sanctions on Iran in 2018 led to a significant drop in the Iranian oil exports that helped many tanker shipping companies worldwide.

On the other hand, sanctions or trade restrictions promoting regional markets or non-sanction goods may provide an opportunity for local shipping companies. For example, Turkey has benefited from Russia's food import ban, which has resulted in the expansion of local agricultural production and significant export growth.

Case Studies

1. Impact of geopolitics on LNG transportation

Global tensions often lead to restrictions in the LNG trade. One such example is the 2019 gas crisis, where the US imposed sanctions on two Chinese shipping firms, COSCO Shipping Tanker (Dalian) and COSCO Shipping Tanker (Shanghai), responsible for transporting Iranian crude oil. The sanctions significantly impacted the shipping industry as many LNG, and other companies canceled their cargoes and contracts with the companies.

As a result, the loading, sailing, and discharging of cargoes delayed worldwide, which adversely affected the shipping companies' revenues. Such incidents not only lead to a shortage of supply but also significantly increase transportation costs, which, in turn, affects the end-users' prices.

2. Impact of Trade Tensions on Shipping

Trade policies and geopolitical tensions that impose tariffs on specific materials now impact shipping companies substantially. The dispute between the US and China over trade and tariffs have significantly impacted the shipping industry. With increasing tariffs and sanctions, many container-shipping companies feel that the risks outweigh the benefits.

Maersk Line, the largest container-shipping company in the world, between August and September 2018, faced the increased costs of fuel. They decided to increase tariffs, which put off many customers, and as a result, Maersk had to lower volumes.

Tariffs and trade tensions not only limit trading activities but also increase the probability of retaliatory measures. In 2019, the US-China trade war resulted in many ships having to spend an extended amount of time anchored offshore awaiting clearance to discharge their cargo.

Conclusion

The world of shipping is interdependent on geopolitical relations and trade policies. There will be consequences of the political decisions taken, and they could be either a blessing or a curse for the shipping industry.

Logistics companies and shippers must be aware of the complexities of geopolitics and trade and their impact on shipping. They should prepare to manage the immediate costs and disruptions while also preparing long-term strategies.

The volatile nature of geopolitics and trade will impact shipping operations regularly. However, the key to mitigating these risks is by proactive planning.
