Indian Rupee Ends Flat After RBI Policy; Focus Turns to Fed



Image Credits  https://finance.yahoo.com/news/india-rupee-indian-rupee-ends-101628591.html

Once upon a time, the Indian rupee was seen as a volatile currency that could swing dramatically in response to global economic events. But that reputation has been changing in recent years, as the country has implemented policies to make the rupee more stable and attractive to foreign investors.

One of the biggest factors contributing to this stability has been the Indian government's efforts to reduce the country's current account deficit. This deficit, which represents the difference between export earnings and import expenses, had been a major concern for investors who worried that it could lead to a depreciation in the rupee's value. However, India has been successful in reducing its current account deficit to around 1% of GDP, down from 5.8% in 2012.

Another important factor has been the steady inflow of foreign funds into India's economy. These funds represent a vote of confidence in India's economic prospects, and have helped to support the value of the rupee. According to the Securities and Exchange Board of India, foreign portfolio investors have pumped around 30 billion USD into the country's stock market in 2021 alone.

Today, the Indian rupee remains stable despite uncertainties elsewhere in the global economy. In fact, on September 8th, it ended mostly flat after the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) kept the key policy rate unchanged, as widely expected. This move was largely discounted by the market, indicating that investors are confident in the stability of the rupee.

Looking ahead, many investors are now turning their attention to the U.S. Federal Reserve rate outcome next week. This event has the potential to impact global currencies, including the rupee. However, experts predict that the rupee will remain stable due to its lower current account deficit and continued foreign fund inflows in the current financial year.

Case Studies: How India Improved Rupee Stability

India's success in stabilizing the rupee can be attributed to a range of policies and initiatives. Here are a few examples:

Opening up the Indian economy to foreign investors

In the early 1990s, India began a process of economic liberalization that involved opening up the country to foreign investment and reducing government regulation of business. This move helped to attract more foreign investors to India, which in turn increased demand for the rupee and supported its value.

Reducing the current account deficit

As mentioned earlier, reducing the current account deficit has been a major focus for the Indian government in recent years. This has involved measures such as encouraging more exports, reducing imports of non-essential goods, and attracting more foreign investment in sectors that generate export earnings. These efforts have helped to stabilize the rupee and demonstrate India's commitment to maintaining a healthy economic environment for investors.

Raising interest rates

When the rupee was facing significant volatility in 2013, the RBI responded by raising interest rates in an effort to attract more foreign investment and support the value of the currency. This move proved effective, as it helped to reduce the volatility of the rupee and increase confidence among investors.

Conclusion: Rupee Stability Reflects India's Economic Progress

The fact that the Indian rupee remains stable despite global uncertainties is a testament to India's economic progress in recent years. The country has implemented a range of policies and initiatives to attract foreign investment, reduce the current account deficit, and support its currency. As a result, the rupee is now seen as a relatively stable currency that is attractive to investors looking for opportunities in emerging markets.

Looking ahead, India will likely continue to implement policies aimed at improving economic stability and attracting foreign investment. These efforts will be key to ensuring that the rupee remains stable and that India's economy continues to grow in the years ahead.
