Turning a New Page in Pakistan-India Relations



Image Credits https://www.nation.com.pk/09-Jun-2023/cass-organises-seminar-on-managing-pakistan-s-relations-with-india

Once upon a time, there were two neighboring countries, Pakistan and India, who shared a common history, culture and traditions. But their relations were marked with hostility, mistrust, and acrimony. Decades of animosity and conflict have left imprints on the psyche of both nations, affecting not only the bilateral relations but also regional peace and stability.

Despite numerous attempts to revive the stalled peace process and resolve the long-standing disputes, the two countries remain locked in a vicious cycle of violence and enmity. The recent episode of tensions between India and Pakistan, which peaked in February 2019 after a terrorist attack in Indian-administered Kashmir, has aggravated the situation further.

The Need for Managing Pakistan-India Relations

Given the complexities and challenges of the Pakistan-India relations, there is a dire need for adopting a prudent and constructive approach to manage them. The recent seminar organized by the Centre for Aerospace and Security Studies (CASS), Lahore on the theme of "Managing Pakistan's Relations with India," provided an insightful and thought-provoking platform to discuss the ways forward for improving the bilateral relations.

The seminar brought together a diverse and distinguished group of policymakers, academics, and experts from Pakistan, India, and beyond to share their perspectives and experiences on various issues related to the Pakistan-India relations, such as Kashmir, terrorism, trade, people-to-people contacts, and regional dynamics.

The discussions and presentations at the seminar highlighted the following three points as the key takeaways for managing Pakistan-India relations:

1. Addressing the Core Issues of Dispute

The first and foremost challenge for managing Pakistan-India relations is to address the core issues of dispute that have bedeviled the bilateral relations since their inception. The most prominent issue is Kashmir, which has been at the center of the tensions and conflicts between the two countries for over seven decades.

The speakers at the seminar emphasized that the resolution of the Kashmir issue is a prerequisite for the normalization of the Pakistan-India relations. They stressed that the solution should be based on the aspirations of the Kashmiri people and the principles of international law, as enshrined in the relevant UN resolutions.

Moreover, the speakers also highlighted that terrorism and extremism are other critical challenges that need to be tackled collectively by Pakistan and India. They underscored that both countries have suffered from the scourge of terrorism and that cooperation and dialogue are the only viable options to address this menace.

2. Exploring New Avenues for Cooperation

The second important point raised at the seminar was the need for exploring new avenues for cooperation between Pakistan and India beyond the traditional areas of contention and conflict. The speakers emphasized that trade, investment, energy, education, culture, and sports are the potential areas for enhancing the bilateral relations and building confidence and trust.

They cited examples of other countries, such as Germany and France, who had long-standing disputes but had managed to overcome them through economic interdependence and cultural cooperation. The speakers also highlighted the role of civil society, media, and academia in promoting people-to-people contacts and changing the narrative of hostility and suspicion.

3. Engaging Regional Countries and International Community

The third and final point raised at the seminar was regarding the role of regional countries and the international community in managing Pakistan-India relations. The speakers emphasized that regional countries, such as China, Russia, Iran, and Afghanistan, have a stake in the stability and security of the South Asian region.

They pointed out that the regional countries, particularly China, could play a constructive role as a mediator between Pakistan and India to resolve their disputes and promote cooperation. Similarly, the international community, including the United States and Europe, could also contribute to the peace process by supporting dialogue and diplomacy and discouraging coercive measures.

Conclusion

The seminar on "Managing Pakistan's Relations with India" provided a valuable platform for discussing the challenges and opportunities for the normalization of the bilateral relations between the two countries. The seminar underscored the need for addressing the core issues of dispute, exploring new avenues for cooperation, and engaging regional countries and the international community.

The seminar highlighted that despite the daunting challenges and the negative narratives, there were still voices of reason and pragmatism on both sides who were committed to building a peaceful and prosperous future for the people of Pakistan and India.

It is time for the leaders and policymakers of both countries to heed these voices and turn a new page in their relations, based on the principles of mutual respect, cooperation, and dialogue.
