Is India Becoming Aatmanirbhar With Modern Weapons?

"Necessity is the mother of invention."

This age-old proverb holds true for India, a country that has faced numerous challenges in its quest for independence and progress. One such challenge is the need for modern weapons to defend its sovereignty and deter external aggression.

For decades, India has been dependent on foreign countries for its defense needs, which has resulted in various issues such as limited technology transfer, high costs, and political pressures. However, in recent years, India has taken significant steps towards becoming self-reliant in modern weapons technology. Let's analyze some of the examples and case studies.

Examples of India's Aatmanirbhar Initiative

One of the most significant initiatives in this regard is the Defense Production and Export Promotion Policy (DPEPP). It aims to achieve a turnover of INR 1,75,000 Crore (USD 24 billion) in defense manufacturing by 2025, including an export target of INR 35,000 Crore (USD 4.8 billion).

Another noteworthy example is the production of the indigenously developed Tejas fighter aircraft by Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL). HAL has also collaborated with Russia's Rostec State Corporation to manufacture Kamov-226T helicopters in India.

Similarly, in the naval sector, the Indian Navy has commissioned the INS Kavaratti, the last of 4 indigenously-built anti-submarine warfare corvettes under Project 28. The navy has also launched the first-scorpene class submarine, INS Kalvari.

Case Studies of India's Aatmanirbhar Success

One of the most successful examples of India's Aatmanirbhar initiative is the BrahMos cruise missile, jointly developed with Russia. It is one of the world's fastest supersonic cruise missiles and has significant export potential.

Another success story is the Akash surface-to-air missile, developed by the Defense Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and manufactured by Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL). It has the capability to neutralize aerial threats such as fighter aircraft, helicopters, and drones.

India has also made substantial progress in developing unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), with the Rustom-II and Nishant drones being test-flown successfully.

Conclusion

In conclusion, India's journey towards Aatmanirbhar in modern weapons technology has been long and arduous, but the recent efforts have shown promising results. India has made significant strides in indigenous weapons development, with notable successes in missile technology and UAVs. However, there is still a long way to go, and sustained efforts are needed to achieve complete self-reliance in this area. As a nation with a rich technological heritage and immense potential, India must continue to focus on indigenous innovation and research and development to become a global leader in modern weapons technology.
