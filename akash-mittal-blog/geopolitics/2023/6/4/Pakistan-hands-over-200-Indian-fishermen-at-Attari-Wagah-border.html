Pakistan hands over 200 Indian fishermen at Attari Wagah border

On July 10, 2021, Pakistan handed over 200 Indian fishermen to India at the Attari Wagah border. The Indian fishermen were arrested for allegedly violating the country's territorial waters in the Arabian Sea. This is not the first time that Pakistani authorities have arrested and detained Indian fishermen. The long-standing dispute over maritime boundaries between India and Pakistan has led to the detention of thousands of Indian fishermen over the years.

Impact on the lives of fishermen and their families

The detention of fishermen has a devastating impact on their families. In many cases, the fishermen are the sole breadwinners for their families, and their detention can result in a significant loss of income. The families of the detained fishermen are often left without any means of support and are forced to rely on friends and relatives for financial assistance. The emotional toll of detention can also be significant, as the families are left worrying about the safety and wellbeing of their loved ones.

One such case is that of Arjun Singh, a fisherman from Gujarat who was arrested along with 18 others by Pakistani authorities in December 2020. Singh's wife, Rekha Ben, said that the family was struggling to make ends meet after his arrest. "We have no source of income now. I have to take care of our three children and my elderly parents-in-law. It's extremely difficult," she said.

The need for a resolution to the maritime boundary dispute

The detention of Indian fishermen by Pakistan underscores the urgent need for a resolution to the maritime boundary dispute between the two countries. The dispute centers around the Sir Creek area, a 96-kilometer strip of water between India's Gujarat state and Pakistan's Sindh province. Both countries claim the area, which has rich fishing grounds and potential oil and gas reserves.

However, experts say that the dispute is more about politics than resources. "The dispute is really about the larger issue of India-Pakistan relations," said Michael Kugelman, deputy director of the Asia Program at the Wilson Center. "The territorial dispute is used as a proxy for larger political issues that the two countries can't seem to resolve."

Conclusion

The detention of Indian fishermen by Pakistan is a recurring problem that has a devastating impact on the lives of the fishermen and their families. The dispute over maritime boundaries between India and Pakistan needs to be resolved to ensure that fishermen can operate without fear of arrest and detention. The two countries need to work towards a peaceful resolution to the dispute, one that takes into account the needs and aspirations of both nations. Only then can the cycle of detention and suffering be broken.

Pakistan Hands Over 200 Indian Fishermen at Attari-Wagah Border

It was a heartwarming scene at the Attari-Wagah border as the Pakistani authorities handed over 200 Indian fishermen who were arrested for illegally fishing in Pakistani waters. The fishermen were received by the officials from the Indian government who ensured that they were well taken care of.

India and Pakistan share a maritime boundary in the Arabian Sea which is often disputed, leading to numerous instances of fishermen from both countries accidentally crossing over into each other's territory. While both countries take a tough stance on the issue, arresting fishermen and impounding their boats, it is the fishermen who suffer the most.

Case Studies

The plight of fishermen caught between India and Pakistan has been highlighted by numerous case studies over the years. The following are just a few examples:

The Kutchi Memons

The Kutchi Memons are a Muslim community who have been involved in fishing for generations. Many of them have relatives on both sides of the India-Pakistan border and, until a few decades ago, were free to fish in the Arabian Sea as long as they did not cross over into territorial waters. However, with the demarcation of the maritime boundary, their lives have become more difficult.

In 2015, a group of Kutchi Memons were arrested by the Pakistani authorities for allegedly crossing over into Pakistani waters. They were detained for more than a year before being released and allowed to return to India. However, their boats, worth several lakhs, were impounded and could not be recovered.

Anthony and Xavier

In 2012, Anthony and Xavier, two Indian fishermen, were arrested by the Sri Lankan Navy for allegedly crossing over into Sri Lankan waters. They were later sentenced to death by a Sri Lankan court, sparking outrage in India. The case was eventually taken up by the Indian government and the fishermen were released in 2014 after two years in prison.

Pakistani Fishermen

It's not just Indian fishermen who suffer in these disputes. Pakistani fishermen have also been arrested by Indian authorities for crossing over into Indian waters. In 2015, 87 Pakistani fishermen were released by India in a goodwill gesture ahead of talks between the two countries. They had been held in Indian jails for several months.

Conclusion

The arrest and detention of fishermen by India and Pakistan is a complex issue that requires a nuanced solution. While both countries have a right to protect their territorial waters, the plight of innocent fishermen caught in the middle needs to be addressed.

The release of the 200 Indian fishermen by Pakistan is a positive step towards resolving the issue. However, more needs to be done to ensure that fishermen are not subject to undue harassment or hardship and that disputes over the maritime boundary are resolved in a peaceful manner.
