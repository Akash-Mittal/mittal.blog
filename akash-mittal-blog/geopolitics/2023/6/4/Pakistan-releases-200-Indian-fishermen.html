Pakistan releases 200 Indian fishermen

Picture this - you are enjoying a day at the beach, fishing with your friends and family when suddenly a group of armed men approach you and take you into custody. You are accused of crossing the international border and are thrown into jail without any evidence to support the claim.

This is exactly what happened to over 200 Indian fishermen who were recently released by Pakistan after being detained for violating their maritime border. Many of these fishermen were languishing in Pakistani jails for months, and some even for years.

One such fisherman, Shiva Das, was detained in Pakistan for 13 long years before finally being released in 2019. He had no idea why he was being held and his family back home in India had given up hope of ever seeing him again.

Das is not alone. There are many fishermen from both India and Pakistan who are caught in the crosshairs of geopolitics and are often used as pawns in the political game. They are left to suffer in inhumane conditions, without any access to legal aid or their families.

Case study #1: Vignesh

Vignesh, a fisherman from Tamil Nadu, was arrested by the Pakistani authorities in 2017 along with five other fishermen. They were accused of crossing the maritime boundary between India and Pakistan and were charged under the Pakistan Maritime Security Agency (PMSA) Act.

Despite repeated appeals from his family and the Indian government, Vignesh was kept in jail for two years with no proper trial. His family did not have the means to hire a lawyer in Pakistan and were at the mercy of the Indian embassy for updates on his case.

After much diplomatic pressure, Vignesh was finally released along with 115 other Indian fishermen in 2019. However, his ordeal had taken a toll on his mental and physical well-being.

Case study #2: Mahendar Singh

Mahendar Singh, a fisherman from Gujarat, was arrested by the Pakistani authorities in 2018 along with eight others. They were accused of smuggling drugs and were charged under the Anti-Narcotics Force (ANF) Act.

However, Singh's family maintains that he is innocent and was falsely implicated in the case. They allege that he was not even on the boat that was intercepted by the Pakistani authorities and that his name was added to the list of accused because he was a well-known fisherman in the area.

After spending over two years in jail, Singh was finally released along with 199 other Indian fishermen as a goodwill gesture by the Pakistani government. His family is overjoyed to have him back, but they are aware that the trauma of his incarceration will stay with him for a long time.

Conclusion

The release of the Indian fishermen by Pakistan is a welcome step towards building trust between the two countries. It is important to remember that these fishermen are not criminals, but are merely trying to earn a livelihood for their families. They should not be caught in the crossfire of political tensions.

The governments of India and Pakistan need to work towards finding a permanent solution to this issue, such as delineating the maritime boundary or allowing fishermen from both countries to fish in each other's waters under a mutually agreed upon arrangement.

Until such a solution is found, innocent fishermen like Vignesh, Mahendar Singh, and Shiva Das will continue to suffer the consequences of geopolitical tensions. It is our responsibility as human beings to ensure that their voices are heard and their rights are protected.
