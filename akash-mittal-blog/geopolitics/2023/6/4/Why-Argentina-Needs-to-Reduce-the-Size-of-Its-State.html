Why Argentina Needs to Reduce the Size of Its State

Imagine a company that has grown too big and is now struggling to maintain its operations at a high level. It has too many employees, too many departments, and too many expenses. This scenario is not uncommon in the corporate world, and it is also applicable to countries that have an extensive public sector, like Argentina.

In recent years, Argentina has faced significant economic challenges, such as high inflation, negative growth rates, and an increasing fiscal deficit. These issues have raised concerns about the sustainability of Argentina's public sector and its ability to deliver essential services to its citizens. Alejandro Werner, the director of the Western Hemisphere Department of the International Monetary Fund (IMF), has pointed out that Argentina cannot sustain the current size of its state. In this article, we will explain why reducing the size of the state is essential to prevent an economic crisis.

Examples of Oversized Public Sector in Argentina

Argentina's public sector is currently one of the largest in Latin America, accounting for around 45% of the country's GDP. The high number of employees and the extensive bureaucracy have created several inefficiencies and bottlenecks that affect the quality and timeliness of public services. Here are some examples:

In 2015, the average time to open a new business in Argentina was 52 days, compared to 9 days in Chile and 11 days in Uruguay.

The slow process of obtaining permits and licenses is one of the main barriers for entrepreneurs in Argentina. It involves dealing with multiple government agencies, waiting for inspections, and paying fees and taxes at each step. The excessive and redundant regulations not only discourage domestic and foreign investments but also increase the cost of doing business, affecting the competitiveness of Argentine firms.

In 2020, Argentina ranked 135th out of 190 countries in the World Bank's Ease of Doing Business report.

The lack of a transparent and stable regulatory framework, coupled with the high political instability, makes Argentina an unfavorable destination for foreign investors. The difficulty of obtaining financing, the weak protection of property rights, and the frequent changes in policies and regulations have caused significant damage to the economy.

In 2019, Argentina spent 5.5% of its GDP on energy subsidies, which benefited mostly the wealthiest quintile of the population.

The subsidies for public utilities, particularly energy, have been a significant burden on the state budget, absorbing funds that could have been allocated to other areas, such as health, education, or social programs. Moreover, the subsidies have distorted the market incentives, encouraging overconsumption and waste, and discouraging investments in renewable and clean energy sources.

Case Studies: Successful Reduction of the State Size

Reducing the size of the state is not an easy task, as it involves political, social, and economic challenges. Nevertheless, several countries have successfully undergone a process of downsizing their public sector, with positive outcomes. Here are two case studies:

Canada

Canada is often cited as an example of successful fiscal consolidation and reduction of the public sector. In the 1990s, Canada faced a severe fiscal crisis, with high deficits, public debt, and inflation. The government implemented a comprehensive program of spending cuts, tax reforms, and privatization, reducing the public debt from 70% of GDP in 1996 to 28% in 2008. The reforms also led to a more efficient and affordable public service, with shorter waiting times for medical services, reduced crime rates, and a higher-quality education system.

"The Canada case shows that fiscal adjustment can indeed be conducive to stronger growth. By improving public finances, Canada was also able to reduce borrowing costs, lower the risk of turbulence in the financial markets, and enhance confidence in the economy" - International Monetary Fund (2008)

New Zealand

New Zealand is another example of a country that successfully reformed its public sector in the 1980s and 1990s. The government pursued an agenda of deregulation, privatization, and devolution, reducing the number of public servants from 116,000 in 1987 to 38,000 in 2000. The reforms led to significant improvements in the efficiency and effectiveness of the public service, such as shorter processing times for licenses and permits, better health outcomes, and lower inflation rates.

"The New Zealand case demonstrates that there are alternative paths to well-being other than those that are based on traditional models of economic growth - models that rely on the state to provide for the vast majority of services and to insure against most risks" - Roger Douglas, former Minister of Finance of New Zealand (1992)

Conclusion

The current size of Argentina's state is unsustainable and threatens the country's economic stability and prosperity. To address this problem, the government needs to implement a comprehensive program of fiscal consolidation and public sector reform. This program should include:

  • Reducing the number of public employees and the size of the bureaucracy
  • Streamlining regulations and procedures for doing business
  • Reforming the subsidies for public utilities and redirecting the funds to social programs and investment in sustainable energy
  • Promoting transparency, accountability, and efficiency in the public sector
  • Fostering a culture of innovation, entrepreneurship, and private sector development

Argentina has the potential to become a competitive and prosperous economy, but it needs to tackle the challenges of an oversized public sector. By learning from the experiences of other countries and implementing bold reforms, Argentina can create a more favorable environment for domestic and foreign investment, job creation, and social inclusion.
