The Indo Pacific Is Embracing Multi Alignment Over U S China Competition

Once upon a time, there was a small island nation in the Indo Pacific that found itself in the middle of a great power rivalry. For years, this nation had relied heavily on the United States for security and economic reasons. But as China's influence in the region grew, the nation began to feel the pressure to choose sides.

On the one hand, there was the U.S. - a long-time ally with a proven track record of providing aid, protection, and investment. On the other hand, there was China - a big, powerful neighbor with deep pockets and growing economic ties.

As the pressure mounted, the nation's leaders decided to chart their own course. They refused to be drawn into the U.S.-China rivalry, instead opting for a strategy of multi-alignment. They began forging new relationships with other countries in the region, seeking to balance their ties and diversify their options.

The Rise of Multi Alignment

The story of this small island nation is playing out across the Indo Pacific, as countries increasingly look for alternatives to the U.S.-China rivalry. Multi-alignment is emerging as a dominant strategy, as countries seek to balance their relationships with major powers and forge alliances with new partners.

There are several reasons for this shift. One is the recognition that the U.S.-China competition is becoming more intense and potentially destabilizing. Many countries fear being caught in the crossfire and are eager to avoid taking sides. Moreover, the rise of China has created new opportunities for economic and diplomatic engagement, providing an alternative to the U.S.-led order.

There are also structural changes happening in the region that are prompting countries to explore new alignments. The Indo Pacific is undergoing a major transformation, as new infrastructure projects, trade deals, and alliances are reshaping the strategic landscape. In response, many countries are seeking new partners and building new alliances that reflect these changes.

Quantifying Multi Alignment

The rise of multi-alignment can be seen in several key examples. One is the Quad, a loose grouping of Australia, India, Japan, and the U.S. that is seeking to counter China's growing influence in the region. Originally formed in 2007, the Quad was revived in 2017 and has been gaining momentum ever since, with regular meetings and joint military exercises.

Another example is the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), China's massive infrastructure project that aims to connect Asia, Europe, and Africa through a series of ports, highways, and railways. While the BRI has faced criticism and pushback from some quarters, many countries in the region see it as an opportunity to boost their own infrastructure and deepen ties with China.

Yet another example is the growing network of trade deals that are being negotiated in the region. The Trans-Pacific Partnership (TPP), which was originally conceived as a U.S.-led trade pact, has been revived as the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP) and now includes 11 countries, including some that have traditionally been aligned with the U.S. Meanwhile, China has been pushing its own trade deals, including the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP), which includes 15 Asia-Pacific countries.

Conclusions in Three Points

  1. Multi-alignment is emerging as a dominant strategy in the Indo Pacific, as countries seek to balance their relationships with major powers and forge alliances with new partners.
  2. The rise of multi-alignment reflects the recognition that the U.S.-China competition is becoming more intense and potentially destabilizing, as well as structural changes happening in the region that are prompting countries to explore new alignments.
  3. Quantifiable examples of multi-alignment include the Quad, the Belt and Road Initiative, and the growing network of trade deals that are being negotiated in the region.

and Practical Tips

One key takeaway from the rise of multi-alignment is the importance of flexibility and adaptability. Countries that are able to navigate the shifting landscape of the Indo Pacific and build new relationships are likely to be more successful than those that are rigid and inflexible.

A personal anecdote that illustrates this point is the story of a small island nation that was heavily dependent on a single major power for security and economic reasons. When that major power began to falter, the island nation found itself exposed and vulnerable, with limited options for assistance. By contrast, countries that have pursued a strategy of multi-alignment have been able to weather the storms of the U.S.-China rivalry and build diverse networks of support and investment.
