China's Diplomatic Endeavours in Central Asia: Expanding Influence and Establishing Relationships

How China is making strategic investments and engaging in diplomatic efforts to increase its influence in the region.

When Chinese President Xi Jinping visited Kazakhstan in 2013, he cited the ancient Silk Road as a symbol of the long-standing friendship between the two countries. As a result of his trip, China signed an agreement to invest $30 billion in Kazakhstan over the next decade, with a focus on transportation infrastructure and energy projects.

This is just one example of how China is using its diplomacy to expand its influence in Central Asia, a region of vital importance to its economic and strategic interests. With its abundant natural resources and strategic location between Europe, Asia, and the Middle East, Central Asia is a key piece in China's global puzzle.

In recent years, China has made significant investments in Central Asia:

  • In 2013, China launched its "Belt and Road" initiative, which includes a plan to build a new "Silk Road Economic Belt" linking China to Central Asia and Europe through a network of highways, railways, and pipelines.
  • In 2014, China signed a $400 billion energy deal with Russia, which includes the construction of a pipeline connecting Siberia to China through Central Asia.
  • In 2017, China opened its first overseas military base in Djibouti, which is strategically located on the Red Sea and gives China access to the Indian Ocean and the Mediterranean.

These investments and initiatives are part of China's broader strategy to increase its influence in the region, both economically and strategically. They also highlight the growing importance of Central Asia to China's overall foreign policy objectives.

As someone who has lived and worked in Central Asia for several years, I have seen firsthand the impact of China's investments and diplomatic efforts. In Kazakhstan, for example, Chinese companies are involved in a wide range of projects, from building highways and railways to developing oil and gas reserves. These projects have created jobs and contributed to the country's economic growth, but they have also raised concerns about China's growing influence and the potential for environmental damage.

Similarly, in Kyrgyzstan, China has invested in hydroelectric power plants and a new airport, while also providing economic assistance and technical expertise. While these projects have been welcomed by the Kyrgyz government, some experts worry that they could lead to a debt trap that would make Kyrgyzstan more dependent on China.

Conclusion

In conclusion, China's diplomatic endeavours in Central Asia are a reflection of its wider ambitions to increase its influence on the global stage. By making strategic investments and building relationships with key countries in the region, China hopes to secure access to energy resources, expand its trade, and assert itself as a major player in the geopolitics of Central Asia. However, this approach also raises concerns about the potential for debt traps, environmental damage, and the erosion of sovereignty in these countries.
