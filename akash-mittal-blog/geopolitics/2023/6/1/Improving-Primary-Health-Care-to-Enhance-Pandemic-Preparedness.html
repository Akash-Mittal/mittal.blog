How Investing in Primary Health Care Can Help the World Prepare for Pandemics

It was a typical workday for Dr. Maria, a primary health care worker in a rural village in the Philippines until a pandemic hit. Back then, the virus was only a distant threat, and she didn't think much of it. But within months, the situation had changed. Her village was one of the many in the country that had been infected by the disease. With her limited resources, Dr. Maria had to work around the clock to ensure that her community received the care they needed.

Dr. Maria's story is not unique. Primary health care workers around the world have been working tirelessly to keep their communities safe in the face of pandemics. However, the COVID-19 pandemic has shown us that primary health care systems need more investment and support to ensure that they can provide effective pandemic response.

The Importance of Primary Health Care in Pandemic Preparedness

Investments in primary health care can lead to better pandemic preparedness. By improving primary health care systems, countries can detect and respond to outbreaks faster, reducing the risk of pandemics spreading.

For example, in 2018, Nigeria launched an electronic disease surveillance system to improve disease detection and outbreak response. The system, which was developed with support from the World Bank, includes a mobile application that allows health care workers to report suspected cases and receive alerts on potential outbreaks. Since its launch, the system has helped Nigeria respond to various disease outbreaks, including a meningitis outbreak in 2018.

Primary health care investment has also led to improvements in health care service delivery. By investing in primary health care, countries can increase the number of health care workers, improve infrastructure, and ensure essential medicines are available. These improvements can help communities stay healthier, reducing the risk of pandemics.

Investment in Primary Health Care must be prioritized

Despite the importance of primary health care, it is often underfunded and overlooked. According to the World Health Organization, globally, 70% of deaths are due to non-communicable diseases (NCDs), which are best prevented and managed through primary health care. However, according to a report by the Global Health Workforce Alliance, only 23% of the healthcare workforce worldwide is dedicated to primary health care.

Furthermore, funding for primary health care is often inadequate, with some countries spending less than 1% of their gross domestic product (GDP) on primary health care. This underfunding hinders countries' abilities to provide essential health care services, including pandemic response.

The Conclusion

  • Investing in primary health care can lead to better pandemic preparedness.
  • Primary health care investment can improve health care service delivery, making communities healthier and reducing the risk of pandemics.
  • Despite the importance of primary health care, it is often underfunded, and investment in primary health care must be prioritized to ensure that countries can effectively prepare for pandemics.
