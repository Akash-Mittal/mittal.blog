India and China Hold Talks on Disengagement Along LAC In Ladakh

Once upon a time, there was a small village situated in the Himalayas, which was a part of the Indian Territory. The villagers there were living peacefully, and their livelihood was mainly dependent on agriculture and tourism. Life was simple, and the village was untouched by the hustle and bustle of city life.

However, one day, the silence in the village was disturbed by the arrival of a group of foreign soldiers. They were dressed in camouflage and carried weapons with them. It turned out that they were Chinese soldiers who had crossed the Line of Actual Control (LAC) and entered Indian Territory.

The locals were taken aback by their intrusion, and they feared for their safety. The Indian army was alerted, and they rushed to the spot to confront the Chinese soldiers. The stand-off continued for weeks, and the situation escalated to an extent that both sides brought in heavy military reinforcements.

This incident is not just an imaginary story; it is a real incident that happened in the village of Pangong Tso in Ladakh. However, the story does not end here. The incident was just the beginning of a long-standing territorial dispute between India and China.

India and China share a long border of 4,056 km, with most of it being disputed. The disputes between the two countries have escalated multiple times in the past, and the recent stand-off in Ladakh was one of the most significant escalations between the two countries.

The latest round of talks held on 9th August 2021 between India and China aimed to disengage all troops along the LAC in Ladakh.

Quantifiable Examples:

- The disengagement process began in February 2021 and was completed in July 2021, with both sides pulling back troops and weapons from the Pangong Tso lake area in Ladakh.

- During the standoff, India deployed around 50,000 troops in Ladakh, while China had deployed around 50,000 troops on its side of the LAC.

- The standoff caused economic loss to the tourism industry in Ladakh, with a drop in tourism revenue by almost 30%.

India-China: Towards Disengagement and Resolving Border Conflict.

Conclusion:

1. Diplomatic-level Talks: India and China have started the diplomatic-level talks, which have resulted in the disengagement process. This approach should continue to establish an ongoing diplomatic process.

2. Confidence-Building Measures: Establishing confidence-building measures between the two countries, such as information sharing, joint patrolling, and regular dialogue, will go a long way in maintaining peace along the border.

3. Mechanism to Resolve Disputes: Both India and China need to have a mechanism to resolve border disputes, which includes shortening the lengthy and cumbersome bureaucracy, improving communication channels, and reinforcing the importance of non-military means of conflict resolution.
