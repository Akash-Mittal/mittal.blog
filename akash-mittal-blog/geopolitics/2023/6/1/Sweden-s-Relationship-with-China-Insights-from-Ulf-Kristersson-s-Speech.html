Sweden's Relationship with China: Insights from Ulf Kristersson's Speech

During the Stockholm China Forum organized by the German Marshall Fund, Ulf Kristersson, leader of the Moderate Party and former Minister for Social Security in Sweden, delivered a keynote speech on Sweden's relationship with China. He spoke about the challenges and opportunities of building a constructive partnership with China, highlighting the importance of human rights, trade, and cooperation.

An Interesting Story: Balancing Strategic Interests and Human Rights

Kristersson started his speech by sharing an interesting story about a human rights activist, Chen Guangcheng, who in 2012 escaped from house arrest in China and sought refuge in the US embassy in Beijing. The incident put the US-China relationship on edge and raised questions about how to balance strategic interests with human rights. Kristersson emphasized that this dilemma is not unique to the US, but is a challenge for all countries that have economic and political ties with China.

Quantifiable Examples: Sweden's Trade and Investment with China

Sweden has a long history of trade and investment with China, dating back to the 18th century when the Swedish East India Company established a trade route to Canton. Today, China is Sweden's largest trading partner in Asia and second largest outside Europe. Bilateral trade between Sweden and China amounted to SEK 92 billion ($10.6 billion) in 2020. Some of Sweden's major exports to China are machinery, vehicles, and chemicals, while imports from China include communication equipment, textiles, and computer electronics.

Swedish companies have also invested heavily in China, with approximately 600 Swedish companies operating in China and employing over 60,000 people. One of the biggest Swedish companies in China is Volvo Cars, which is now owned by Chinese carmaker Geely. Kristersson noted that while the economic benefits of trade and investment with China are clear, Sweden must also maintain its commitment to human rights and democracy.

Sweden's Relationship with China: Building a Multifaceted Partnership that Balances Strategic Interests and Human Rights

: The Way Forward

As Kristersson concluded his speech, he outlined three points that he believed would be essential for Sweden to maintain a constructive relationship with China:

  1. Promoting Human Rights: Sweden must continue to advocate for human rights in China, including issues such as freedom of speech, assembly, and religion.
  2. Expanding Trade and Investment: Sweden must seize the opportunities presented by China's growing economy and expand trade and investment relations, while also ensuring that Swedish companies operate according to high ethical standards.
  3. Strengthening Cooperation on Global Challenges: Sweden and China must collaborate on global challenges such as climate change, pandemic response, and economic development, in order to create a more sustainable and equitable world.

and Case Studies: Sweden's Approach to Human Rights in China

As a pioneer of feminist foreign policy, Sweden has taken a proactive approach to promoting human rights in China, particularly with regards to gender equality. In 2019, Sweden was one of 22 countries that signed a letter to the UN condemning China's mass detention of Uighur Muslims and other minority groups in Xinjiang. Later that year, Sweden's Minister for Foreign Affairs, Ann Linde, attended the Stockholm Internet Forum in China and raised concerns about internet censorship and online surveillance.

Sweden has also supported civil society organizations in China that work on issues such as women's rights and LGBT rights. In 2018, the Swedish international development agency, Sida, provided funding to a Chinese NGO that focuses on promoting sexual and reproductive health and rights for young people.

Practical Tips: Balancing Economic and Human Rights Interests

For countries that have economic and political ties with China, balancing strategic interests with human rights can be a difficult task. However, Kristersson offered some practical tips for navigating this challenge:

  • Maintain Dialogue: Keep channels of communication open between governments, civil society groups, and business leaders in both countries.
  • Hold Companies Accountable: Ensure that Swedish companies operating in China adhere to high ethical standards and respect human rights.
  • Encourage International Cooperation: Work with other governments and international organizations to pressure China to comply with international human rights norms.
