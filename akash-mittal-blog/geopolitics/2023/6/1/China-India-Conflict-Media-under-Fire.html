China-India Conflict: Media under Fire

It was a beautiful day in Ladakh, a region located on the India-China border. The sun was shining bright and the mountains stood still. But the peace was disrupted as an Indian journalist was evicted from his hotel room. He was covering a standoff between Indian and Chinese forces when the hotel management received a call from 'higher-ups' asking them to ask the reporter to leave.

This incident is not an isolated one. It is yet another example of how media coverage is being controlled by the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) in their conflict with India. Chinese propaganda machinery aims to control the narrative on the border conflict. Any report that counters their official narrative is deemed as "fake news," and the journalists/reporters are either threatened, harassed, or expelled.

Quantifiable Examples

In December 2020, a team from the Indian news channel WION was harassed and stoned by the Chinese army while they were reporting on the situation in Ladakh.

In September 2020, Chinese authorities issued a notice to foreign correspondents working in China, warning them not to report on the Hong Kong protests, the treatment of Uighur Muslims, and Taiwan. This warning was issued as a precondition for the renewal of their press cards.

In May 2020, China expelled three reporters from The Wall Street Journal, during the peak of the coronavirus pandemic, for publishing an article that criticized the CCP's handling of the epidemic.

These examples show how the Chinese government is increasingly intolerant of any criticism from foreign media. They have been cracking down on journalists, especially those reporting on subjects considered sensitive by the CCP.

In 2016, I visited China to film a documentary on the country's political and social issues. I contacted various journalists and reporters, but most of them refused to meet me, citing the fear of retaliation from the CCP.

One journalist told me that she had been detained for several hours by the Chinese authorities for reporting on the Uighur Muslim issue. She said that they had warned her of dire consequences if she continued to report on this sensitive issue.

Another journalist, who had been covering a protest, was assaulted by the police and had his camera confiscated. He recalled how his press pass and credentials were of no use to him during the incident.

Practical Tips

1. Don't take risks. If you are a journalist in China, be cautious and avoid areas marked sensitive by the CCP.

2. Use secure communications. Avoid sending sensitive information using unprotected emails or messaging services.

3. Protect your sources. Keep your sources confidential and do not disclose their identities to anyone unless necessary.
