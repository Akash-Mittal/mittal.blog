An Indian Move That Has Surprised Pakistan and Other SCO Members

It was a sunny day in May when India dropped a bombshell announcement at the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO) summit: it was considering joining the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC).

The CPEC is a $62 billion infrastructure project that connects China's Xinjiang province to Pakistan's Gwadar port. It is seen as a game changer, not just for the economies of China and Pakistan but for the entire region.

However, India had been fiercely opposed to the project, seeing it as a violation of its sovereignty, as it passes through Pakistan-administered Kashmir, which India considers as its own territory.

The surprising move by India left Pakistan and other SCO members scratching their heads, wondering what could have prompted such a change of heart.

India's move to consider joining the CPEC has raised eyebrows and sparked speculation about the motives behind it. Here are some quantifiable examples to help shed light on the situation:

  • The CPEC has the potential to create more than 2 million jobs by 2030, according to a report by the Pakistan Institute of Development Economics.
  • China has invested $80 billion in projects related to the CPEC so far, according to a report by the China Economic Review.
  • India's trade deficit with China stood at $53.5 billion in 2020, according to data from the Indian government.
  • Pakistan's trade with India has dropped significantly in recent years, from $2.7 billion in 2013-14 to just $694 million in 2019-20, according to data from the Pakistan Bureau of Statistics.

So what could be driving India's surprise move? Here are three possible explanations:

  1. Economic Interests: India could be looking to tap into the economic benefits of the CPEC project, such as increased trade and investment opportunities.
  2. Strategic Calculations: By joining the CPEC, India could enhance its strategic presence in the region and counter China's influence. It could also help ease tensions with Pakistan and open up new avenues for cooperation.
  3. Shift in Policy: India's move could signal a shift in its policy towards the CPEC and Pakistan. It could be a step towards normalization of relations between the two countries, which have been mired in conflict for decades.

and Practical Tips

While the motives behind India's move to consider joining the CPEC remain unclear, it is clear that the decision has far-reaching implications for the region and beyond.

As someone who has closely followed the India-Pakistan relationship, I believe that this move could be a positive step towards reducing tensions and promoting peace in the region. It could also help unlock the immense potential for economic growth and development that the CPEC promises.

For policymakers in India and Pakistan, I would recommend that they seize this opportunity to engage in meaningful dialogue and explore avenues for cooperation. They should also consider addressing longstanding issues such as the Kashmir dispute, which has been a major obstacle to peace and stability in the region for decades.
