China Takes Leadership Role in Central Asia

Once upon a time, there was a small village in the heart of Central Asia. The village was surrounded by vast landscapes and great challenges. But there was one man who took the responsibility to lead the village towards a prosperous future. That man was a Chinese businessman.

Over the past decade, China has been gradually increasing its presence in Central Asia by investing in infrastructure projects, such as highways, railways, and power plants. These projects have helped the region to develop and grow, and have brought China closer to its goal of becoming a global superpower.

According to data from the International Monetary Fund, China's trade with Central Asia grew from $1 billion in 2000 to $50 billion in 2017. Additionally, Chinese investments in the region have exceeded $304 billion since 2005, according to the research firm Rhodium Group. These investments have helped to build the region's infrastructure, and have created new markets for Chinese goods and services.

China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) has also been a major factor in the country's increased presence in Central Asia. The BRI is a massive infrastructure project that aims to connect Asia, Europe, and Africa through a network of railways, highways, and ports. Central Asia is a key component of this project, as it sits at the crossroads of Europe, Asia, and the Middle East.

China Takes Leadership Role in Central Asia

Once upon a time, a small landlocked country in Central Asia had been seeking investments for the construction of a new oil refinery for years. Several European and American companies had shown interest in the project, but nothing materialized as they viewed the project riskier and unprofitable. Out of nowhere, a Chinese state-owned company stepped in and offered full funding for the project, promising to complete the construction within record time. The project was a success, and the country became a mini-China with Chinese workers and Chinese goods flooding the markets. This is just one example of how China is aggressively expanding its influence in Central Asia, a region that was once dominated by Russia.

The Central Asian region comprises five former Soviet republics- Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan. These countries have a combined population of roughly 72 million and are rich in natural resources, including oil, gas, minerals, and various agricultural commodities. For many years, Russia was the unchallenged dominant power in the region, with cultural, linguistic, and historical ties shared with the Central Asian countries.

However, the recent economic and geopolitical shifts have created opportunities for China to exert its influence. China, the world's second-largest economy, requires a massive amount of energy and resources to fuel its growth and development. Central Asia has abundant resources, and it is a gateway to Europe, the Middle East, and South Asia, making it a strategically important region for China. Since the early 2000s, China has invested heavily in infrastructure development and energy projects in the region. The Chinese government has formed economic alliances such as the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO), which includes Russia and the Central Asian countries, to increase its regional influence.

Quantifiable examples of China's influence in Central Asia include:

Energy Sector Dominance:

China has become the major stakeholder in Central Asia's hydrocarbon exports. Kazakhstan is the largest producer and exporter of oil in the region and is China's biggest supplier of crude oil. In recent years, China has invested in building pipelines through the region to gain further access to oil and gas reserves. Besides, China has been investing in renewable energy projects in the region, such as wind and solar power plants, to diversify its energy supplies.

Infrastructure Development:

China has undertaken several infrastructure projects in Central Asia, including highways, rail links, and pipelines, to improve connectivity and trade between China and the region. One of China's landmark projects is the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), which aims to connect China with Europe, Central Asia, and Africa through a network of roads, railways, ports, airports, and industrial parks. Central Asia is a crucial part of BRI, and many countries in the region have already joined the project.

Trade and Investment:

China has emerged as a significant partner for trade and investment in the region. The volume of trade between China and Central Asia has increased significantly, reaching $46 billion in 2020. China has also invested in industrial parks, special economic zones, and free trade zones in the region to promote business and investment.

China's expanding role in Central Asia has both positive and negative implications for the region.

Positive Implications:

  • Economic Development: China's investments in infrastructure and energy have created new sources of income and employment opportunities for the Central Asian countries. The investments have also improved the region's connectivity and trade, leading to increased economic growth.
  • Technological Advancements: China's investments in renewable energy projects and infrastructure have brought new technologies and innovations to the region, which can help address the region's environmental and economic challenges.
  • Regional Integration: China's economic alliances, such as SCO and BRI, have helped promote regional integration and cooperation among the Central Asian countries, which can help address common security and economic challenges.

Negative Implications:

  • Limited Local Participation: Many Chinese projects in Central Asia involve Chinese workers, which limit local participation and employment opportunities. Besides, Chinese loans for infrastructure projects often have strict conditions, such as using Chinese contractors and suppliers, which can raise concerns about debt sustainability.
  • Environmental Impact: Some Chinese projects, such as pipelines and mining, can have negative environmental impacts on the region, leading to pollution and degradation of natural resources.
  • Security Concerns: China's increasing influence in Central Asia can raise concerns for Russia, the traditional dominant power in the region. Besides, China's expanding presence in the region can complicate regional security dynamics, especially in the context of the ongoing conflict in Afghanistan.

In conclusion, China's growing influence in Central Asia is a significant geopolitical development that has both positive and negative implications for the region. China's ambitious infrastructure and energy projects in the region have created new sources of income and employment opportunities, improved connectivity, and promoted regional integration. At the same time, Chinese investments have raised concerns, such as limited local participation, environmental degradation, and security challenges. As China continues to expand its presence in the region, it is essential to strike a balance between economic development and sustainable development, taking into account the local needs and interests of the Central Asian countries.

China Takes Leadership Role in Central Asia

As I walked through the bustling streets of Almaty, Kazakhstan, I couldn't help but notice the plethora of Chinese businesses and construction projects dotting the city skyline. From Huawei storefronts to Belt and Road Initiative-funded high-speed rail lines, it was apparent that China had a strong foothold in Central Asia. As I delved deeper into the region's politics and economics, I realized that China's influence was not limited to Kazakhstan, but was spreading throughout all of Central Asia.

According to data from the International Monetary Fund, China has surpassed Russia as Central Asia's largest trading partner since 2014, with trade volumes reaching over $50 billion in 2019. China's economic might is also evident in mega-projects such as the China-Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan railway, which is estimated to cost over $7 billion and will connect China's Xinjiang province to Uzbekistan's populous cities. This project is just one of many infrastructure investments that China has made in the region, aiming to increase connectivity and enhance trade while also cementing its influence in Central Asia.

China's engagement in Central Asia goes beyond just economics, though. The country has also sought to deepen its political relationships with the governments of the five Central Asian states – Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and Tajikistan – through a variety of channels. One such channel is the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO), a security and economic bloc that China co-founded with Russia in 2001 and which includes all five Central Asian states as members. Through the SCO, China has been able to facilitate joint military exercises with Central Asian countries and offer security collaboration on issues such as terrorism and separatism.
