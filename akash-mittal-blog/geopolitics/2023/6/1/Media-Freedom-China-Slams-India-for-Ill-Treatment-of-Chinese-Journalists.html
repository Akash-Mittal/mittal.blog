Media Freedom: China Slams India for Ill Treatment of Chinese Journalists

Journalism has always been considered as the fourth pillar of democracy, and free press is one of the crucial factors that keep our society healthy and informed. However, in recent times, we have seen a decline in media freedom, and governments around the world are trying to control the press through various means. China is one such country that is known for its strict regulations on the media, and it has recently criticized India for its treatment of Chinese journalists.

In 2020, the Chinese embassy in New Delhi accused the Indian government of unfairly targeting and harassing Chinese journalists in the country. The embassy claimed that Chinese journalists were being denied visas, and their applications for extension of visas were being delayed or rejected, which made it difficult for them to carry out their journalistic duties in India. The Chinese embassy warned that such actions could lead to a deterioration of the bilateral relationship between China and India.

The Indian government, on the other hand, denied these allegations and said that it was committed to ensuring the safety and security of all foreign journalists in the country. The Indian Ministry of External Affairs stated that all applications for visas and visa extensions were being processed as per the usual procedure, and there was no discrimination based on nationality.

There are several instances where foreign journalists, including Chinese journalists, have faced difficulties in performing their duties in India. For instance, in 2019, the Indian government revoked the accreditation of a journalist from the Chinese state-run news agency Xinhua after he was found indulging in "disinformation and propaganda activities". Similarly, in 2020, an Indian court asked the police to investigate a Chinese journalist for allegedly spying on Tibetan refugees in India.

Moreover, the 2021 World Press Freedom Index, published by Reporters Without Borders, ranked India at 142 out of 180 countries, citing the country's "intolerance of criticism" and the "attacks on journalists, especially those who are critical of the government". Such rankings reflect the declining media freedom in the country, which is a cause for concern.
