Chinese Hackers Targeting US Infrastructure: A Worrisome Trend



Image Credits https://techmonitor.ai/technology/cybersecurity/chinese-hackers-us-critical-infrastructure-jen-easterly-cisa

Imagine waking up one day to find that your city's power grid is down, transportation systems are haywire, and communication networks are non-existent. This nightmare scenario is not far-fetched, especially in the context of recent cyber-attacks on US infrastructure by Chinese hackers.

In May 2021, the US Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) issued an advisory warning of ongoing attacks by Chinese state-sponsored hackers on US government agencies, critical infrastructure, and technology companies. According to CISA's Jen Easterly, the head of the agency, Chinese cyber-ops are becoming more aggressive and sophisticated in their tactics.

Let's dive deeper into this trend, its impact, and ways to protect yourself from these attacks.

The Trend

Chinese hackers have long been involved in cyber-espionage, theft of intellectual property, and other malicious activities targeting the US. However, what's new is the increased focus on critical infrastructure, which poses a serious threat to national security and public safety.

The recent attacks have targeted a wide range of sectors, including energy, healthcare, finance, transportation, and government agencies. The hackers are using various techniques, such as ransomware, supply chain attacks, and zero-day exploits, to gain access to sensitive systems and steal data.

For instance, in December 2020, a cyber-attack on the US Treasury Department and the Commerce Department was traced back to a China-linked group, which used SolarWinds software as a vector of attack. The incident affected multiple US agencies and private firms, raising concerns about the vulnerability of the supply chain.

In another case, Chinese hackers targeted a US pipeline operator in 2018, compromising its IT systems and disrupting its operations. The attack was traced back to a group known as APT10, which is believed to have links to the Chinese government.

Overall, the trend of Chinese hackers targeting US infrastructure is not new, but it's becoming more frequent, aggressive, and sophisticated, raising concerns about the potential for a major cyber-catastrophe.

Impact

The impact of these attacks can be severe, both in terms of financial loss and disruption of essential services. Here are some examples:

  • In 2017, a ransomware attack dubbed "WannaCry" infected over 300,000 computers in 150 countries, causing billions of dollars in damage. The attack was attributed to North Korea, but some experts suggested the involvement of Chinese hackers as well.
  • In 2020, a ransomware attack on the University of California affected its medical facilities, causing disruptions in patient care and research activities.
  • In 2021, the Colonial Pipeline, which supplies fuel to the East Coast of the US, was shut down for several days due to a ransomware attack by a group believed to have ties to Russia. The incident caused panic buying, fuel shortages, and price hikes across the region.
  • In the same year, a cyber-attack on the Irish health system disrupted its services, including COVID-19 vaccination appointments, causing delays and frustration.

These incidents illustrate the real and immediate threat posed by cyber-attacks on critical infrastructure, which can not only cause economic damage but also endanger human lives and national security.

Protection

The growing threat of Chinese hackers targeting US infrastructure calls for urgent action to protect against these attacks. Here are some ways to do so:

  • Implement strong cybersecurity measures, such as firewalls, intrusion detection, and vulnerability management systems, to detect and respond to cyber-attacks.
  • Stay informed about the latest threats, vulnerabilities, and best practices by following the guidance of trusted sources, such as CISA, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), and cybersecurity experts.
  • Enhance the resilience and redundancy of critical infrastructure systems by adopting backup and disaster recovery plans, testing them regularly, and investing in modernization and innovation.
  • Collaborate with public and private partners, such as government agencies, industry associations, and cybersecurity vendors, to share threat intelligence, coordinate incident response, and promote a culture of security.

By taking these steps, you can reduce the risk of falling prey to Chinese hackers targeting US infrastructure and help protect your organization and community from cyber-disasters.
