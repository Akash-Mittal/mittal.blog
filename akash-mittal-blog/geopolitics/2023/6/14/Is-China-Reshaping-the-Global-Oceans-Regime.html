Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

Oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth's surface and are home to a wealth of biodiversity, mineral resources, and sources of food and energy. As such, they play a crucial role in global affairs and the international community has established a complex legal framework to manage the various activities on and under the seas. However, in recent years, China's expansive maritime claims and growing naval power have raised concerns about its impact on the global oceans regime.

The South China Sea Dispute

One of the most high-profile examples of China's push to assert its maritime claims is the South China Sea dispute. This long-standing dispute involves competing claims from China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan over various islands, reefs, and sea territory. China's claims are based on historic rights and its "nine-dash line" map, which encompasses much of the South China Sea. The other claimant countries argue that these claims violate the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, which gives countries exclusive economic zones up to 200 nautical miles from their coasts.

"China has aggressively pursued its claims through island-building, military exercises, and paramilitary patrols, leading to tension and occasional clashes with other claimants and their allies, including the United States."

China's actions in the South China Sea have been met with widespread criticism and concern from the international community. The United States has conducted freedom of navigation operations in the region to challenge China's claims and support the principle of freedom of the seas. Meanwhile, the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague issued a ruling in 2016 that rejected China's claims to much of the South China Sea, but China has refused to recognize the decision.

The Belt and Road Initiative

Beyond the South China Sea, China is also using its growing economic power to shape the global oceans regime. The Belt and Road Initiative, first announced by Chinese President Xi Jinping in 2013, is a far-reaching plan to improve connectivity and economic integration across Asia, Europe, and Africa through infrastructure development, trade, and investment.

While the initiative has broad goals, it also has significant implications for the oceans. The plan includes the construction of ports, shipping lanes, and other maritime infrastructure that could enhance China's ability to project power and influence across the seas. Some observers worry that these developments could also exacerbate existing tensions and even lead to conflict in maritime hotspots like the South China Sea.

The Arctic

Another area where China's influence on the global oceans regime is growing is the Arctic. As the Arctic ice melts due to climate change, new shipping routes and resource opportunities are opening up. This has sparked interest from countries like China, which is a non-Arctic state but has declared itself a "near-Arctic" power.

China's interests in the Arctic include access to resources like oil and gas, as well as new shipping routes that could significantly reduce transit times between Asia and Europe. China has also explored partnerships with Arctic Council members like Russia, Iceland, and Norway to gain greater influence in the region.

Conclusion

China's growing influence on the global oceans regime has raised concerns about its impact on international law, maritime security, and the environment. While China argues that its actions are justified by historic rights and its legitimate economic interests, its expanding maritime claims and military presence have stoked tensions with other countries in the region and beyond. As China continues to assert itself on the high seas, the international community will need to grapple with how to balance competing interests and ensure that the oceans remain a global commons for all.
