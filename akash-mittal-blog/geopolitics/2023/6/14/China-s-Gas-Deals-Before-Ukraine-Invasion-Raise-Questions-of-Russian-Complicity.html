China's Gas Deals Before Ukraine Invasion Raise Questions of Russian Complicity



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/12/china-natural-gas-russia-ukraine-war-energy/?tpcc=onboarding_trending

It was supposed to be a simple trade deal. China would buy natural gas from Russia in exchange for oil. But as the two sides negotiated the details, questions arose about the timing of the deal and Russia's motivations.

China had been eyeing Russia's natural gas reserves for years, but talks between the two countries had stalled over pricing and delivery terms. In late 2013, the two sides finally sealed a deal that would see China importing 38 billion cubic meters of gas per year from Russia's eastern region starting in 2018. As part of the agreement, China would also loan Russia $25 billion to develop its natural gas fields.

The deal was hailed as a victory for both countries. Russia would have a new market for its gas, while China would secure a long-term source of energy for its rapidly growing economy. But some analysts saw the deal as more than just a simple business arrangement, and wondered if there was something more sinister at work.

Case studies

1. The Ukraine Crisis

In February 2014, just a few months after the gas deal was finalized, Russian troops moved into Ukraine's Crimean Peninsula, sparking a crisis that would eventually lead to the annexation of the region by Russia. The move drew widespread condemnation from the international community, with some accusing Russia of violating Ukraine's sovereignty.

Some analysts saw a connection between the gas deal and the Ukraine crisis. They argued that Russia may have been emboldened to take aggressive action in Ukraine because it had a guaranteed revenue stream from China that would help it weather any economic sanctions or other fallout from the crisis.

2. The Global Energy Landscape

Others saw the deal as part of a broader shift in the global energy landscape. With the United States becoming a major player in the energy sector thanks to the shale gas boom, some speculated that Russia was looking to diversify its customer base and reduce its dependence on European markets, which had traditionally been its main export destination.

If that was the case, the China deal would be a natural fit, as China was hungry for energy and had the financial clout to invest in new infrastructure and support Russia's gas development efforts.

3. Russian-Chinese Relations

The gas deal also highlighted the growing closeness between Russia and China. The two countries had long seen themselves as strategic partners, but the gas deal marked a major milestone in their relationship. With the two countries facing growing isolation from the West, the deal was seen as a way for them to support each other and assert their independence.

Some analysts, however, saw the deal as evidence that Russia was becoming increasingly reliant on China, which could give China more leverage in its dealings with Russia in other areas, such as territorial disputes or cyber espionage.

Conclusion

The China-Russia gas deal of 2013 was a landmark agreement that promised to reshape the global energy landscape. But it also raised questions about Russia's motivations and whether it was using the deal as a way to project power and assert itself on the world stage.

Some saw the deal as evidence that Russia was becoming increasingly isolated and turning to China as a way to offset that trend. But others saw it as a way for Russia to bolster its energy sector and assert its dominance in a new market, while also forging closer ties with a key ally in the region.

Whatever the motivations behind the deal, it is clear that it has had far-reaching implications for Russia, China and the global energy sector. And as tensions between Russia and the West continue to simmer, the deal remains a powerful symbol of the changing dynamics of the international order.
