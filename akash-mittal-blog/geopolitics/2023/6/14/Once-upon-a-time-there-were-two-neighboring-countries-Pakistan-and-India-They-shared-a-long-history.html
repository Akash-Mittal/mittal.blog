Once upon a time, there were two neighboring countries - Pakistan and India. They shared a long history together, including their culture, language, and even economic ties. However, due to political tensions, the relationship soured, and the borders between them became heavily guarded. It's a story that many of us are familiar with, but the consequences of this division still linger.



Image Credits https://morningexpress.in/shehbaz-sharif-fails-pakistans-luck-can-change-trade-with-india-pak-experts-told/

One of the most significant impacts has been on their economies. Pakistan and India are both young countries, experiencing rapid population growth and urbanization. To sustain this growth, they need to modernize their industries, embrace technological advancements, and provide affordable goods and services to their citizens. However, the lack of trade between these two countries has limited their options.

For example, let's consider the textile industry in Pakistan. It is a critical sector for the country, providing employment to millions of citizens. However, Pakistan's textile manufacturers have been struggling to compete with their counterparts in India, which have access to cheaper raw materials, modern machinery, and innovative manufacturing techniques. As a result, the cost of goods produced in Pakistan is higher, and the industry is trapped in a cycle of mediocrity.

But it doesn't have to be this way. Pakistan has the potential to transform its fortunes by reestablishing trade links with India. By importing cheaper raw materials, Pakistan can reduce its production costs and remain competitive in the global market. Similarly, by importing technology and machinery, Pakistan can modernize its industries, increase productivity, and create more job opportunities for its citizens.

The case study of the Wagah-Attari border is a great example of how trade can benefit both countries. Before 2019, Pakistani and Indian trucks would only be allowed to cross the border for unloading and loading purposes. However, after government negotiations, the border remains open 24/7. This has reduced the time and expense of transporting goods, benefiting the businesses and people on both sides. As a result, trade via the Wagah-Attari border has skyrocketed.

In conclusion, Pakistan has a lot to gain from restoring trade with India. By importing cheaper goods, raw materials, and technology, Pakistani industries can thrive, and the country can improve its economic prospects. Additionally, the cultural exchange and person-to-person connections from trade can strengthen diplomatic ties and promote peace. Let us hope that both countries can leave their political differences aside and focus on building a brighter economic future together.
