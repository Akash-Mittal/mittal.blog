Blaming Kyiv: The Geopolitics behind Moscow's Accusations



Image Credits https://geopoliticalfutures.com/daily-memo-moscow-points-the-blame-at-kyiv-brussels-looks-for-migrant-deal/

On a balmy summer evening in 2016, a Ukrainian man named Pavel Sheremet was driving to work as a journalist in the capital city of Kyiv. Suddenly, a car bomb exploded, killing Sheremet instantly. The assassination sent shock waves across Ukraine and beyond, as people wondered who could have carried out such a heinous act.

At the time, many Ukrainians suspected that the Kremlin had a hand in Sheremet's murder, given his outspoken criticism of Russian policies in Ukraine. But the Russian government denied any involvement, and the case remains unsolved to this day.

Fast forward to 2021, and Moscow is once again pointing the finger at Kyiv for a recent incident: the destruction of an ammonia pipeline in Ukraine's Kharkiv region. Russia's Defense Ministry claims that a Ukrainian sabotage group was responsible for the incident, which disrupted ammonia supplies to Russia's agricultural sector.

The Politics of Blame

While it is impossible to know for certain who was behind the pipeline attack, one thing is clear: Moscow has a long history of blaming Kyiv for various incidents, whether it be the downing of Malaysian Airlines Flight MH17 or the ongoing conflict in eastern Ukraine.

Some experts argue that Russia's tendency to blame Kyiv serves several political purposes. For one, it helps to deflect attention away from Moscow's own actions in Ukraine and other parts of the world. By pointing the finger at Ukraine, Russia can portray itself as a victim of aggression rather than an aggressor.

Additionally, blaming Kyiv can help to justify Russia's ongoing support for separatist rebels in eastern Ukraine. By painting the Ukrainian government as a corrupt and illegitimate entity, Moscow can claim that it is merely supporting self-defense forces that are fighting to protect their rights and freedoms.

The Costs of Accusations

But there are also costs to Moscow's habit of blaming Kyiv. For one, it risks further alienating Ukraine and other Western-leaning countries, who see Russia as a destabilizing force in the region.

Secondly, blaming Kyiv for incidents that may or may not have involved Ukrainian actors can create a climate of fear and suspicion, both inside and outside of Ukraine. This could lead to further violence and unrest, as people become more entrenched in their beliefs and more willing to resort to extreme measures.

The Need for Dialogue

So what is the solution to the problem of Moscow's accusations? Some experts argue that the key is to increase dialogue and cooperation between Russia and Ukraine, rather than perpetuating a cycle of blame and recrimination.

By working together to address common threats, such as climate change or terrorism, Moscow and Kyiv may be able to build trust and create a more stable and secure region. Of course, this is easier said than done, given the deep-seated mistrust and resentment between the two countries.

But with tensions on the rise once again, it is clear that something needs to change if the region is to avoid further conflict and instability.
