Top Regional Lender China Struggling with Development Commitments, Study Finds



Image Credits https://asianews.network/top-regional-lender-china-struggling-with-development-commitments-study-finds/

China, one of the world's largest economies, has been a top lender in the region with several development commitments. However, a recent study has found that the country is struggling to keep up with their promises. The findings have raised concerns among the developing countries that rely on financial support from China for infrastructure projects.

The Rising Loans of China

China has been expanding its reach in the region by investing heavily in infrastructure programs, such as railways, highways, ports, and power plants. These projects have been financed by loans from the country, which has been aggressively lending money to developing countries. As per the data from the China-Africa Research Initiative, from 2000 to 2019, China has loaned more than $150 billion to African governments and state-owned entities.

However, while the loans have been useful in improving infrastructure, they have also put a strain on the budgets of the borrowing countries. As per a report by the International Monetary Fund (IMF), one-third of the Sub-Saharan African countries that have borrowed from China are at high risk of debt distress.

The Study Findings

A Washington-based research group, AidData, has recently published a report that has analyzed the Chinese development projects in 136 countries between 2000 and 2018. The report states that China has committed $1.5 trillion to support developing countries over these years, but it has failed to deliver on several of these commitments.

The study found that China has delayed or cancelled several projects that were supposed to be completed between 2014 and 2018. Around 20% of the projects with a cost of $57 billion were either delayed or cancelled, and in some cases, there was no activity at all.

The report also revealed that the Chinese government has failed to provide information on several projects, including their costs and timelines. As per AidData, around 800 projects, with a cost of around $210 billion, lacked information on these matters.

Case Studies

The report has highlighted several case studies, where the Chinese funding has fallen short of expectations:

Malaysia's East Coast Rail Link Project

The East Coast Rail Link Project, worth $16 billion, was cancelled in 2018 due to Malaysia's concerns over high costs. After renegotiations, the project was resumed with a cost reduction, but the Chinese government cut down the project by almost half, causing Malaysia to fund the remaining portion.

Kenya's Standard Gauge Railway

Kenya's Standard Gauge Railway project, worth $3.8 billion, was funded mostly by China. However, it has been underutilized, and the Kenyan government is struggling to repay the loan, which has been taken with a 6.3% interest rate.

Tanzania's Bagamoyo Port Project

The Bagamoyo Port project, worth $10 billion, was set to become Africa's largest port but was cancelled due to concerns over costs and economic viability. The Chinese government was supposed to provide soft loans for the project, but it withdrew from funding, causing the Tanzanian government to indirectly fund it and shelve it.

Conclusion

The study by AidData has raised concerns over China's commitment to development projects and raises questions about its sustainability for the future. The report calls for transparency in funding and timeline commitments to prevent developing countries from falling into the debt trap. It also highlights the importance of the role of international organizations, such as the IMF, in monitoring the funding of such projects.

However, it is essential to note that while the report raises concerns over the Chinese funding of development projects, it also acknowledges that China remains a crucial lender for many developing nations. But it calls for better financial management and transparency to ensure that commitments are fulfilled and countries are not burdened by unsustainable debts.
