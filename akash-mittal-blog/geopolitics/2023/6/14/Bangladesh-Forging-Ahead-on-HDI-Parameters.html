Bangladesh: Forging Ahead on HDI Parameters



Image Credits https://www.weeklyblitz.net/news/bangladesh-has-forged-way-ahead-on-parameters-of-hdi-compared-to-most-countries-including-india-jitendra-kumar-ojha/

Once known as a poverty-stricken and disaster-prone country, Bangladesh has made significant progress in recent years in lifting its people out of poverty and improving their quality of life. As per the 2020 Human Development Index (HDI) rankings published by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), Bangladesh has emerged as a role model for its remarkable improvement in HDI parameters, leaving behind its neighboring countries like India.

Examples of Bangladesh's HDI Progress

Let's look at some examples of Bangladesh's progress in HDI parameters:

  • Economic Growth: Over the past decade, Bangladesh has recorded an average annual growth rate of 6.5%, one of the fastest-growing countries in the world. This impressive growth has been attributed to its booming export-oriented garment industry, which has generated millions of jobs for women and contributed significantly to the country's GDP.
  • Education: Bangladesh has made tremendous strides in education by improving access and quality. The country has achieved gender parity in primary and secondary education, and the literacy rate has increased from 47% in 2000 to 74% in 2019.
  • Health: Bangladesh has also made significant progress in improving healthcare facilities for its citizens. Infant mortality has decreased from 97 per 1,000 live births in 2000 to 23 per 1,000 live births in 2019. Maternal mortality has also decreased from 322 per 100,000 live births in 2000 to 173 per 100,000 live births in 2019.

These are just a few examples of the tremendous progress Bangladesh has made in recent years, thanks to the government's policies and initiatives and the hard work and determination of its people.

Case Studies of HDI Improvement in Bangladesh

Let's take a closer look at some case studies that demonstrate the positive impact of Bangladesh's pro-HDI policies:

Microfinance and Women's Empowerment

Bangladesh is a pioneer in the field of microfinance, which has helped to empower women and lift them out of poverty. The world's first microfinance organization, Grameen Bank, was founded in Bangladesh in 1983 by Nobel Peace Prize winner Muhammad Yunus. Today, microfinance institutions in Bangladesh serve over 30 million people, 90% of whom are women.

Microfinance has enabled women in Bangladesh to start their own businesses, which has provided them with a steady income stream and increased their economic independence.

For example, Rashida Begum, a woman from a poor rural family, started a small business with a loan of $80 from Grameen Bank. She purchased a sewing machine and started making clothes, which she sold in her village. With her profits, she was able to expand her business and now employs four other women. She has also been able to send her children to school and provide for her family.

Improving Maternal Healthcare

Bangladesh has also taken significant steps to improve maternal healthcare, which has contributed to the reduction in maternal mortality rates. The country has implemented a number of initiatives, such as training community health workers to provide basic maternal healthcare services and establishing referral systems for emergency obstetric care.

For example, Rokeya Khatun, a woman from a rural village, received training as a community health worker under the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare's program. She now provides antenatal care and conducts deliveries in her village. Thanks to her efforts, the number of maternal deaths in her village has significantly decreased.

Boosting Agriculture Productivity

Bangladesh's agriculture sector plays a crucial role in the country's economy, accounting for over 14% of GDP and employing over 42% of the total labor force. However, farmers face numerous challenges, such as lack of access to credit, inputs, and markets.

The government has implemented a number of policies and initiatives to support farmers and boost agriculture productivity. For example, the Agricultural Extension Support Activity (AESA) provides agricultural extension services to farmers, including training on modern farming techniques and connecting them to markets.

For example, Emdadul Haq, a farmer from a rural village, received training on modern farming techniques through AESA. He learned how to use fertilizers and pesticides effectively, and how to cultivate crops that are in high demand in the market. With this knowledge, he was able to increase his yields and improve his income.

Conclusion

Bangladesh's progress in HDI parameters is a testament to the country's commitment to improving the lives of its citizens. By investing in education, healthcare, and other critical areas, the government has created an enabling environment for its people to thrive. While there is still much work to be done, Bangladesh's remarkable achievements in recent years are an inspiration to other developing countries.
