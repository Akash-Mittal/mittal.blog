Relations between Saudi Arabia and Iran, the two major powers of the Middle East, have been fraught with tension and conflict for decades. However, recent geopolitical developments and strategic considerations have raised the prospect of a possible rapprochement between the two regional rivals, which could have far-reaching implications for the stability and security of the region.

The Story of Saudi-Iranian Rivalry

The history of Saudi-Iranian rivalry can be traced back to the aftermath of the Iranian Revolution of 1979, which brought to power a Shia clerical regime that challenged the predominance of Sunni monarchies in the Persian Gulf, including Saudi Arabia. The two countries have since been engaged in a bitter struggle for regional influence and power, fuelled by sectarian divides, ideological differences, and strategic competition.

Examples of this rivalry can be seen in numerous regional conflicts and crises, such as the Iran-Iraq War, the Gulf War, the Syrian Civil War, and the Yemeni Civil War, where Saudi Arabia and Iran have supported opposing sides and interests, either directly or indirectly. Moreover, both countries have pursued ambitious foreign policies aimed at expanding their spheres of influence and countering the other's influence, not only in the Middle East but also in other regions of the world, such as Africa, Central Asia, and South Asia.

This longstanding and pervasive rivalry has had a destabilizing effect on the region, exacerbating tensions and conflicts, and hindering regional cooperation and integration. It has also attracted the attention and interference of external actors, such as the United States, Russia, and China, who have sought to exploit or manage this rivalry for their own interests.

The Prospects of Saudi-Iranian Rapprochement

Despite the complex and contentious nature of their relationship, recent developments have raised hopes for a possible rapprochement between Saudi Arabia and Iran, which could have significant implications for the region and the world. Some of these developments are:

  • The Biden Administration's efforts to revive the Iran Nuclear Deal and ease tensions with Iran;
  • The Saudi-led coalition's withdrawal from the Yemeni Civil War and the opening of backchannel talks with the Houthi rebels, who are backed by Iran;
  • The growing recognition among regional powers of the need for collective security and cooperation in the face of common challenges, such as terrorism, extremism, and instability;
  • The economic and strategic benefits of rapprochement, such as the opening of new trade and investment opportunities, the reduction of military expenditures, and the mitigation of external threats;
  • The changing regional and global dynamics, which make it more challenging for Saudi Arabia and Iran to pursue zero-sum policies that ignore each other's interests and concerns.

While these developments do not guarantee a complete or immediate rapprochement, they do indicate a changing political landscape and a growing sense of pragmatism and realism among regional actors. If successful, a rapprochement between Saudi Arabia and Iran could lead to a number of positive outcomes, such as:

  • The easing of sectarian tensions and the promotion of tolerance and coexistence between Sunni and Shia Muslims;
  • The reduction of regional conflicts and the fostering of stability, security, and prosperity;
  • The strengthening of regional institutions and mechanisms for dialogue, cooperation, and conflict resolution;
  • The enhancement of the Middle East's role and voice in the global order, based on principles of multilateralism, human rights, and sustainable development.

The Challenges and Implications of Saudi-Iranian Rapprochement

However, the road to rapprochement is not without challenges and risks. Some of the main challenges and implications are:

  • The deep-rooted mistrust and rivalry between Saudi Arabia and Iran, which could undermine the success and durability of any agreement or arrangement;
  • The opposition and skepticism of internal and external actors, such as hardliners, regional allies, and global powers, who have vested interests in maintaining the status quo or pursuing different agendas;
  • The unresolved issues and conflicts, such as territorial disputes, proxy wars, and human rights violations, which could resurface or escalate if not addressed properly;
  • The potential backlash and resistance from non-state actors, such as extremist groups, who thrive on sectarianism, intolerance, and instability;
  • The geopolitical and strategic repercussions of rapprochement, which could upset the balance of power, provoke counter-reactions, and trigger unintended consequences, especially in the context of regional and global rivalries.

In conclusion, the recent NUST think tank webinar on Saudi-Iranian rapprochement was a timely and insightful discussion on a topic of great significance and complexity. While the prospects of rapprochement between Saudi Arabia and Iran are not yet clear or guaranteed, the growing impetus and momentum for positive change should be encouraged and supported by all stakeholders, both within and outside the region. The pursuit of peace, stability, and cooperation in the Middle East requires bold and visionary leadership, strategic flexibility, and a commitment to dialogue and understanding.

"It is time for Saudi Arabia and Iran to turn the page of history and to start a new chapter of cooperation and mutual respect, built on trust, goodwill, and shared interests. Only then can they unlock the full potential of their peoples and regions, and contribute to a better and safer world for all."
