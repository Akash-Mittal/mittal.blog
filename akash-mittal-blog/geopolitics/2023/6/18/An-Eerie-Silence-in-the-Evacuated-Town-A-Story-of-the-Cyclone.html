The approaching cyclone has prompted India and Pakistan to evacuate over 150,000 people from the coastal areas. As the authorities scrambled to arrange the shelters and transportation, an eerie silence settled over the evacuated towns, with empty streets, shuttered shops, and vacant houses.

For centuries, the coastal communities in the Bay of Bengal have faced the wrath of cyclones, which can bring devastating winds, floods, and landslides. Despite the advances in meteorology, communication, and disaster management, the cyclones still pose a significant threat, especially to the vulnerable populations such as the poor, the elderly, and the marginalized.

Examples of Cyclone Damage in India and Pakistan

The devastating effects of cyclones can be seen from the past incidents that have occurred in India and Pakistan. Cyclone Amphan, which hit India and Bangladesh in May 2020, caused over 100 deaths, displaced millions of people, and damaged crops, infrastructure, and houses. The estimated economic losses were over USD 13 billion.

In 2019, Cyclone Fani hit the eastern coast of India, causing over 80 deaths and affecting millions of people. The government's swift response and the pre-evacuation measures helped to minimize the damage, but the recovery and reconstruction efforts are ongoing.

In Pakistan, the coastal province of Sindh has been vulnerable to cyclones, which can also exacerbate the existing problems of floods, droughts, and poverty. The 1999 Super Cyclone hit the province with winds of up to 300 km/h and caused over 10,000 deaths and extensive damage.

Case Studies of Cyclone Evacuation and Relief Efforts

The current evacuation efforts in India and Pakistan involve a complex coordination of multiple agencies, including the government, the armed forces, the civil society, and the international organizations. The challenges range from securing the shelters and supplies, ensuring the safety of the evacuees, and maintaining social distancing and hygiene protocols in the pandemic era.

In India, the Odisha government has been recognized as a model for cyclone management, thanks to its proactive measures and community mobilization. After Cyclone Phailin in 2013, the government had set up over 8800 cyclone shelters, trained the citizens in early warning systems, and established a disaster fund for relief and rehabilitation. The success of these measures was evident during Cyclone Fani in 2019, where the government had evacuated over 1.2 million people, and there were no casualties reported from the shelters.

In Pakistan, the National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) has been leading the efforts to prepare for the cyclone. It has coordinated with the provinces, the armed forces, and the civil society to identify the vulnerable areas, conduct risk assessments, and provide early warning to the citizens. The NDMA has also set up a 24/7 helpline for emergency assistance and deployed the teams for damage assessment and relief distribution.

Conclusion: How to Protect the Coastal Communities from Cyclones?

The recent evacuation efforts in India and Pakistan highlight the urgent need for a comprehensive strategy to protect the coastal communities from the cyclones. Here are three key points to consider:

  1. Invest in Resilience Building: The governments and the international organizations should invest in long-term measures that can enhance the resilience of the coastal communities. This can include building better infrastructure, improving the warning systems, providing financial safety nets, and empowering the citizens to participate in the planning and implementation of the cyclone management programs.
  2. Ensure Equitable Access to Resources: The vulnerable populations, such as the poor, the elderly, and the marginalized, should have equitable access to the resources and services that can help them cope with the cyclones. This can include providing low-cost shelters, essential supplies, healthcare, and social protection programs.
  3. Adopt a Multisectoral Approach: The cyclone management requires a multisectoral approach, involving the cooperation and coordination of multiple actors from the government, civil society, private sector, and international organizations. The efforts should focus on not only the emergency response but also the recovery and reconstruction, considering the long-term impacts of the cyclones on the social, economic, and environmental development of the coastal communities.
