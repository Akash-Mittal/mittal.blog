Pakistan's Author Lauds India's Growth Story: A Turning Point In Their Relationship



Image Credits https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fozUKpTdP78

A Pakistani author, Sadia Dehlvi, recently shared her views on India's growth story, and it was surprisingly positive. She praised India's rapid economic development in the past decade and predicted that the country will soon become the second-largest economy in the world, surpassing China. This is a significant turning point in the relationship between India and Pakistan, as the two countries have been bitter rivals for decades. In this article, we will explore the history, progress, challenges, and opportunities of India's growth story and its implications for the region and beyond.

History and Background

India and Pakistan gained independence from British colonial rule in 1947, after a long and bloody struggle. However, the two countries have been locked in a bitter rivalry since then, marked by several wars, conflicts, and tensions. The root cause of their animosity lies in the unresolved territorial dispute over the region of Kashmir, which both claim as their own. Moreover, their competing interests, ideologies, and identity politics have further complicated their relationship. Despite some occasional thaw in their relations, such as the recent opening of the Kartarpur Corridor for Sikh pilgrims, India and Pakistan remain divided by mutual distrust and hostility.

Progress and Achievements

India's economic growth has been impressive in the past decade, thanks to several factors such as liberalization, globalization, innovation, and demographic dividend. According to the International Monetary Fund (IMF), India's GDP has been growing at an average rate of around 7% per year, and is expected to reach $3 trillion in 2019. This makes India the sixth-largest economy in the world, behind the US, China, Japan, Germany, and the UK. However, what is more noteworthy is the potential of India to overtake China as the world's second-largest economy in the near future. This is not merely a speculative claim, as several credible sources have projected India's growth trajectory.

For instance, a report by PwC in 2017 estimated that India's economy would grow at an average rate of 7.6% per year from 2016 to 2050, and would surpass the US by 2050 in terms of GDP at PPP (purchasing power parity). Similarly, a report by HSBC in 2018 predicted that India's GDP would reach $5 trillion by 2025, and $9 trillion by 2030, overtaking Japan and Germany. Moreover, India has made significant progress in various fields such as agriculture, automobile, aviation, banking, construction, energy, healthcare, tourism, and IT. It has also launched several initiatives such as Make in India, Digital India, Swachh Bharat Abhiyan, Ayushman Bharat, and Smart Cities Mission, aimed at enhancing its competitiveness, productivity, and inclusiveness.

Furthermore, India's human capital is one of its biggest strengths. It has a young and growing population, with a median age of 28 years, and a large pool of skilled and educated workforce. India produces millions of graduates and professionals in various fields, such as engineering, medical, management, IT, and sciences, every year. This provides a huge potential for innovation, entrepreneurship, and social and economic development.

Another factor that has contributed to India's growth story is its political stability and democratic values. Despite occasional challenges and controversies, India has a robust and inclusive system of governance, with free and fair elections, independent judiciary, vibrant media, and active civil society. This has ensured that India's economic and social policies are based on the principles of accountability, transparency, and participation, and are responsive to the needs and aspirations of its diverse population.

Challenges to Overcome

India's growth story is not without its challenges and constraints. One of the biggest problems is the rising income inequality and poverty. Although India has made significant progress in reducing poverty and improving human development indicators, such as education and health, there remain vast disparities between the rich and the poor, the urban and the rural, and the gender and the caste. This has led to social tensions, political unrest, and economic inefficiencies. Moreover, India faces a huge infrastructure deficit, in terms of transportation, energy, water, and sanitation, which hinders its competitiveness and productivity. To overcome these challenges, India needs to invest more in social welfare, education, and healthcare, as well as in infrastructure and innovation.

Another challenge is the environmental degradation and climate change. India is one of the biggest emitters of greenhouse gases, and faces several ecological and climatic risks, such as water scarcity, air pollution, deforestation, and natural disasters. To address this challenge, India needs to shift towards cleaner and renewable sources of energy, such as solar and wind, and adopt sustainable and eco-friendly technologies and practices.

Moreover, India needs to improve its ease of doing business, and reduce red-tape, corruption, and bureaucracy. Although India has made some progress in improving its business environment, it still ranks low in the global rankings, such as the World Bank's Ease of Doing Business Index. To attract more foreign and domestic investments, India needs to address the structural and policy bottlenecks, and create a conducive and competitive market ecosystem.

Opportunities to Seize

Despite the challenges and constraints, India has many opportunities and potential to leverage. One of the biggest opportunities is its demographic advantage, that is, its young and growing population. By 2020, India is expected to have the largest workforce in the world, with over 600 million working-age people. This provides a huge potential for economic growth, as well as for resolving social and political issues, such as poverty, inequality, and unemployment. However, to tap into this potential, India needs to invest more in education and skills development, and create more employment opportunities, especially in the formal and organized sectors.

Another opportunity is India's vast and diverse market, with over 1.3 billion people and a growing middle class. This provides a huge potential for domestic and foreign businesses, to offer innovative and customized products and services, and to cater to the diverse needs and preferences of the Indian consumers. Moreover, India has a strategic location, that connects South Asia, East Asia, and West Asia, and provides a gateway to the global markets. This makes India an attractive destination for trade and investment, and for regional and international cooperation.

Furthermore, India has a huge potential for innovation and entrepreneurship, especially in the emergent fields of AI, robotics, biotech, fintech, and space technology. India has a vibrant startup ecosystem, with over 40,000 startups, and several incubators, accelerators, and funding platforms. India has also launched several initiatives, such as Startup India, Stand Up India, and Atal Innovation Mission, aimed at fostering innovation and entrepreneurship, and creating a culture of risk-taking and experimentation.

  1. India's growth story is a remarkable progress, that showcases its potential and strengths, as well as its challenges and constraints.
  2. India's growth story has significant implications for the region and beyond, and can serve as a catalyst for regional and global cooperation and development.
  3. India and Pakistan can learn from each other's experiences, and explore new and innovative ways of cooperation, based on mutual respect, trust, and shared interests.
