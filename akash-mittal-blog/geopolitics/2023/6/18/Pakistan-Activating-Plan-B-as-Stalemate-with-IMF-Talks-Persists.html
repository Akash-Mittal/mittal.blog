Pakistan Activating 'Plan-B' as Stalemate with IMF Talks Persists

It was just another day at the Islamabad offices of Pakistan's finance ministry. Yet again, there were no signs of progress in the stalled talks with the International Monetary Fund (IMF) over relief for Pakistan's crippled economy. The government had been hoping to secure a bailout package worth a whopping $6 billion, but the talks had hit a wall.

This was not the first time Pakistan had found itself in this predicament. Back in 2013, Pakistan was in talks with the IMF for a bailout package. However, the IMF, unhappy with Pakistan's progress on its reform agenda, suspended the programme in 2015 just prior to its end date.

But the situation is different this time around. Pakistan is currently grappling with a stagnant economy, a currency crisis, soaring inflation and a balance of payments crisis. The country's fiscal deficit is expected to reach 7.5% of gross domestic product, while the current account deficit is expected to hit $18 billion. All of this has put the government in a tight spot, which is why it has decided to implement its 'Plan-B'.

What is 'Plan-B'?

As Pakistan's attempts to reach a deal with the IMF have failed, the government has been forced to come up with its own contingency plan - the 'Plan-B'. This plan includes a number of measures aimed at reducing the fiscal deficit, stabilising the economy, and attracting foreign investment.

One of the key measures in the plan is the introduction of a tax amnesty scheme for the construction industry. This scheme allows individuals and companies to declare their past income and assets without any questions asked, and pay a nominal tax rate on that income. Additionally, the government has cut down on its development expenditure by more than 20% in order to reduce the fiscal deficit.

The government has also devalued the Pakistani rupee and increased interest rates in order to attract foreign investment. Additionally, it has taken steps to increase exports and decrease imports in order to reduce the trade deficit. The government has also increased taxes on luxury items like cars and mobile phones in order to generate additional revenue.

Case Studies

The introduction of the tax amnesty scheme for the construction industry has been widely hailed as a positive move. In fact, it has already yielded results. The amnesty scheme has brought in over Rs.230 billion, which is equivalent to $1.6 billion, in just a few months. This has helped to reduce the fiscal deficit.

The government's decision to cut down on development expenditure has been met with some criticism, as it could hurt the economy in the long run. However, the government maintains that these cuts are necessary in order to reduce the fiscal deficit.

Meanwhile, the devaluation of the Pakistani rupee has hurt the economy in the short term. However, it has also made exports more competitive, which could help bolster the economy in the long run.

Conclusion

Despite the success of some of its measures, Pakistan still faces an uphill battle in terms of stabilising its economy. The government will have to continue to take bold steps in order to attract foreign investment, reduce the fiscal deficit and stabilise the economy.

One of the biggest challenges facing Pakistan is corruption, which has been a major obstacle in attracting foreign investment and promoting economic growth. The government will have to take concrete steps to address corruption and increase transparency.

Overall, Pakistan's 'Plan-B' is a step in the right direction, but more needs to be done in order to stabilise the economy and promote growth.
