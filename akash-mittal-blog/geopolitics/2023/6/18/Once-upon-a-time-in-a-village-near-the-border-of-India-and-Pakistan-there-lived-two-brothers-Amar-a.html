Once upon a time, in a village near the border of India and Pakistan, there lived two brothers - Amar and Akbar. While Akbar was a farmer, Amar was a skilled artisan who made an exquisite range of handicrafts that were popular across the region. One day, a customer from India entered Amar's shop and admired his work, placing a big order. Amar happily accepted, but soon faced a problem - the Indian government had imposed heavy taxes and tariffs on goods imported from Pakistan, making it difficult for him to make a profit. Despite his brother's advice to abandon the deal, Amar persisted and found a way to smuggle his goods through a secret route. The Indian customer was pleased with the quality of the products and offered to do more business, but Amar realized that he had to keep taking risks and cut costs to sustain the venture. The pressure and uncertainty took a toll on him, and he eventually gave up on his passion.

This anecdote may sound like a simple story, but it reflects the complex realities of trade and diplomacy between India and Pakistan. While the two countries share a long and intertwined history, their recent relations have been marked by hostility and suspicion. The latest bone of contention is the growing economic and strategic partnership between India and the United States, which has been viewed with concern by Pakistan. In this article, we will examine the reasons and implications of this development, and explore how it affects the interests of different stakeholders.

To begin with, let us look at the context and causes of India-US ties. Since the end of the Cold War, India has sought to assert its position as a major power in the international system, and has pursued a policy of economic liberalization and global engagement. The US, on its part, has viewed India as a potential ally and partner in its efforts to shape the balance of power in Asia, and has sought to deepen its ties with the country on multiple fronts - economic, military, cultural, and diplomatic. The two countries have signed several agreements and initiatives in recent years, such as the Civil Nuclear Agreement, the Defence Technology and Trade Initiative (DTTI), the Strategic Energy Partnership, and the Indo-Pacific Vision.

The benefits of India-US partnership are evident for both sides. For India, it means access to capital, technology, markets, and support for its vision of a rules-based global order. For the US, it means a reliable partner in countering China's rise, promoting democracy and stability in South Asia, and expanding its influence in the region. However, the implications of this partnership for other actors in the region, such as Pakistan and China, are more complicated.

Pakistan, as India's immediate neighbor and rival, has been wary of any move that strengthens India's position, especially in areas such as defence, nuclear energy, and connectivity. The recent visit of US Secretary of State Mike Pompeo to India, during which he signed a historic defence agreement and made several statements critical of Pakistan's alleged support for terrorism, has added to these concerns. Pakistan's Foreign Minister Shah Mahmood Qureshi claimed that India was "using the American card to pressurize Pakistan", and warned that his country would "not compromise on its sovereignty, security and strategic interests". He also accused India of "massive human rights violations" in Kashmir and urged the US to play a more "even-handed" role in the region.

China, as a global rival of the US and a close ally of Pakistan, also sees the India-US partnership as a challenge to its interests. China has long viewed India as a potential rival and strategic obstacle in its quest for regional supremacy, and has invested heavily in Pakistan as a counterweight and partner in its Belt and Road Initiative (BRI). China's President Xi Jinping and Pakistan's Prime Minister Imran Khan recently signed agreements worth billions of dollars in infrastructure projects and energy cooperation, including the controversial China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) that passes through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir. China has also provided military aid and support to Pakistan, and has sought to deepen its ties with other countries in the region, such as Nepal and Sri Lanka.

In this volatile and complex scenario, there are several possible scenarios and outcomes that can emerge. Let us consider some of them, based on different factors and scenarios.

- Political Factors: A major factor that can influence the India-US ties is the political stability and continuity of the two countries. In India, the ruling BJP government led by Prime Minister Narendra Modi has been a strong advocate of the US partnership, and has taken steps to align India's foreign policy with US interests. However, the government faces growing challenges from opposition parties, civil society groups, and regional powers who criticize its economic policies, human rights record, and divisive politics. In the US, the administration of President Joe Biden has signaled a shift in priorities from his predecessor Donald Trump, and has sought to restore the traditional alliances and values of the country. However, the political polarization and economic crisis in the US, along with the growing competition from China, can limit the scope and pace of US engagement with India.

- Economic Factors: Another important factor that can shape the India-US ties is the economic interests of both countries. India has been a major beneficiary of US investment and technology, particularly in the fields of information technology, pharmaceuticals, and renewable energy. The US has also been a major market for Indian goods and services, such as textiles, diamonds, and software. However, the recent protectionist measures and visa restrictions imposed by the US, along with the economic recession caused by the pandemic, have affected the Indian economy. On the other hand, the US has sought to reduce its dependence on Chinese goods and diversify its supply chains, which can create opportunities for India's manufacturing sector. However, India faces several hurdles in terms of infrastructure, labour laws, and bureaucratic hurdles that can hinder its competitiveness.

- Strategic Factors: The most critical factor that can impact the India-US ties is the strategic calculus of both countries regarding regional and global issues. India and the US share common concerns and interests in areas such as counterterrorism, maritime security, and democracy promotion. However, there are also differences and divergences in their approaches and priorities, such as the US' inclination towards military interventions and sanctions. Moreover, the India-US partnership can create tensions and challenges for other actors in the region, such as Pakistan and China, who may feel threatened or isolated. However, there are also opportunities for dialogue and cooperation among all parties, such as the recent agreement between India and China to disengage their troops from the border areas.

In conclusion, the India-US ties are a complex and evolving phenomenon, shaped by multiple factors and actors. While the partnership holds great potential for both countries and the region, it also poses challenges and risks that need to be addressed through dialogue, diplomacy, and mutual respect. As the world navigates through the pandemic, economic recession, and geopolitical uncertainties, it is imperative that India and the US work together to promote peace, stability, and prosperity for all.
