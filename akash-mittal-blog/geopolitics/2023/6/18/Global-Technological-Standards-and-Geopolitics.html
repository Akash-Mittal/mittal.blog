Imagine you are the CEO of a leading tech company. You have just developed a revolutionary product that will change the way people around the world communicate and do business. You are thrilled to launch it and capture a sizable portion of the global market, but you realize there is a major problem: your product uses a different technological standard than the rest of the world. In other words, your product will not work with the existing infrastructure and devices that people and businesses are currently using. What do you do?

This scenario illustrates the importance of global technological standards and how they can influence geopolitics. Technological standards are the agreed-upon specifications, protocols, and procedures that enable different devices, systems, and networks to communicate with each other. They are essential for interoperability, compatibility, and seamless global connectivity. However, they are not neutral or universal. They reflect the interests, values, and power relations of the actors that create, adopt, and enforce them. Therefore, they can have political, economic, and strategic implications in addition to technical ones.

Examples of Global Technological Standards

There are many examples of global technological standards that have shaped the world we live in today:

  • The Internet Protocol (IP) standard, which enables the Internet to function as a global, decentralized network of networks
  • The Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS) standard, which enables 3G mobile communications around the world
  • The International Organization for Standardization (ISO) standards, which cover a wide range of products, services, and processes and are used by governments, businesses, and consumers worldwide

Case Studies

The following case studies demonstrate how global technological standards can influence geopolitics:

Case Study: Huawei and 5G

Huawei, a Chinese technology company, has become a global leader in 5G infrastructure, which is expected to enable faster, more reliable, and more widespread mobile connectivity. However, Huawei's dominance has raised concerns among some governments and companies, who fear that the company could use its position to spy on or sabotage their networks, or to advance China's political and strategic interests. These concerns have led to bans, restrictions, or cautionary measures regarding Huawei's participation in 5G deployments in several countries, including the United States, the United Kingdom, India, and Australia. In addition, the controversy over Huawei has highlighted the importance of having trustworthy and transparent global standards for 5G security.

Case Study: E-waste and Sustainability

Electronic waste (e-waste) is a growing problem worldwide, as the rapid pace of technological innovation and consumption leads to the disposal of millions of tons of toxic and non-recyclable materials each year. However, e-waste can also be a source of valuable minerals and components that can be reused or recycled, thereby reducing the carbon footprint and the environmental impact of new electronics. To achieve this, global standards for e-waste management are needed, that define the criteria, procedures, and responsibilities of different stakeholders, such as manufacturers, recyclers, and regulators. Such standards can also have economic and social benefits, such as job creation, innovation, and community engagement. Therefore, the development and adoption of global standards for e-waste should be an integral part of the sustainable development agenda.

Case Study: Climate Change and Green Technology

Climate change is one of the greatest global challenges of our time, as it threatens to destabilize ecosystems, disrupt economies, and harm human health and well-being. However, it also presents opportunities for innovation, growth, and cooperation, especially in the field of green technology. Green technology encompasses a wide range of products, services, and systems that use renewable energy, reduce emissions, conserve resources, and enhance resilience. To achieve the full potential of green technology, global standards are needed that support the deployment, interoperability, and scalability of different solutions, such as solar panels, wind turbines, electric vehicles, smart grids, and energy-efficient buildings. These standards can also promote global trade and investment in green technology, and enable different countries, regions, and sectors to collaborate on common environmental goals.

Conclusion

In conclusion, global technological standards are not only technical but also political, economic, and strategic. They can shape the way we communicate, work, and live, and influence the power relations among governments, companies, and societies. Therefore, it is essential to have transparent, inclusive, and trustworthy processes for developing, adopting, and enforcing global standards, that take into account the needs and interests of all stakeholders, including consumers, regulators, and civil society. Such standards should also reflect the values and goals of the international community, such as sustainability, human rights, and democratic governance. Therefore, the Hudson Institute event on Global Technological Standards and Geopolitics is a timely and important opportunity to discuss these issues and collaborate on solutions.

3 Key Takeaways:

  1. Global technological standards are essential for interoperability, compatibility, and seamless global connectivity, but they are not neutral or universal, and can have political, economic, and strategic implications.
  2. The development and adoption of global standards for pressing global issues, such as 5G security, e-waste management, and green technology, can have significant environmental, social, and economic benefits, and enable international cooperation.
  3. To ensure the transparency, inclusiveness, and trustworthiness of global standards, it is important to have open, participatory, and accountable processes that reflect the needs and interests of all stakeholders, and that uphold international values and goals.
