The Shift of Geopolitics: How China-India Rivalry is Changing Asia



Image Credits https://www.asiasentinel.com/p/china-india-rivalry-change-asian-geopolitics

Once upon a time, a small village near the border of China and India was at the center of a dispute. This village was home to two temples, one Hindu and one Buddhist, and both countries claimed ownership. In 1962, tensions boiled over into a border war, and the dispute has continued to simmer ever since.

But the conflict over this small village is just one small piece of a larger rivalry that is beginning to shift the balance of power in Asia. China and India, the two most populous countries in the world, are both vying for regional dominance. And as they compete, their rivalry is changing the geopolitical landscape.

The Rise of China and India

China and India have both experienced rapid economic growth and modernization over the past few decades. China's GDP has grown by an average of 7% per year since 1990, while India's has grown by an average of 6.8%. Both countries have become major players in the global economy, with China now the world's second-largest economy and India the sixth-largest.

As their economies have grown, so too has their military power. China now has the world's largest army, with more than 2 million active-duty troops, while India has the world's fourth-largest. Both countries have also been investing heavily in modernizing their militaries and developing advanced weapons systems.

Their Rivalry

As China and India have risen, so too has their rivalry. The two countries have long been at odds over their border disputes, which date back to the late 19th century when China ceded parts of its territory to Britain, which later became India.

The tension between the two countries has only increased in recent years, as China has become more assertive in its territorial claims in the South China Sea and India has sought to strengthen its alliances with other countries in the region, such as Japan and the United States.

Another area of competition between the two countries is their quest for energy resources. Both countries are heavily reliant on imported oil and natural gas, and are increasingly competing for access to resources in the Middle East and Central Asia.

The Impact on Geopolitics

The rivalry between China and India is having a significant impact on geopolitics in Asia and beyond. Here are three ways in which it is reshaping the world order:

1. A New Cold War?

Some experts have warned that the rivalry between China and India could lead to a new Cold War, with the two countries competing for influence around the world. This could lead to a new global order in which China and India are the dominant players, with the United States and other Western powers playing a diminished role.

However, others argue that the rivalry between China and India is unlikely to lead to a full-blown cold war, but rather a multipolar world order in which multiple powers compete for influence.

2. A Shifting Balance of Power

The rivalry between China and India is also shifting the balance of power in Asia. As China's power grows, it has become more assertive in its territorial claims and in its efforts to dominate the region. This has led to tensions with many of its neighbors, including Japan, South Korea, and Vietnam.

Meanwhile, India has sought to counterbalance China's power by strengthening its alliances with other countries, such as Japan and the United States. This has led to a new strategic partnership between India and the United States, which is designed to counter China's growing influence in the region.

3. The Future of the Global Economy

The rivalry between China and India is also likely to have a significant impact on the future of the global economy. As the two countries continue to grow, they are likely to become even more important players in the world economy, with the potential to reshape global trade and investment patterns. However, their rivalry could also lead to a more fragmented global economy, with each country seeking to protect its own interests.
