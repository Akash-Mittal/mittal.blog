Interesting story about energy trade between Pakistan and Russia

In late January 2021, Pakistan received its first shipment of oil from Russia, marking a significant moment in the energy trade between the two countries. The shipment consisted of 100,000 tonnes of petrol, which will be used to address Pakistan's growing demand for petroleum products.

This development is a result of a framework agreement signed between the two nations in October 2016, which aims to enhance cooperation in the fields of trade, economy, science, and technology. The agreement also hopes to lay the foundation for future collaboration in the oil and gas sector.

Examples of Energy Cooperation between Pakistan and Russia

This is not the first instance of energy cooperation between Pakistan and Russia. Here are some other examples:

  • In 2015, the two countries signed a $2 billion deal to build a 1,100-kilometre gas pipeline from Karachi to Lahore, which is expected to transport up to 12.4 billion cubic metres of natural gas per year.
  • In 2017, Russia's state-owned energy company, Gazprom, signed a memorandum of understanding with Pakistan LNG Limited to explore the possibility of supplying liquefied natural gas (LNG) to Pakistan.
  • In 2018, Russia's state atomic energy corporation, Rosatom, showed interest in constructing a nuclear power plant in Pakistan.

Case Studies of Energy Dependence and Diversification

The energy cooperation between Pakistan and Russia highlights the importance of energy dependence and diversification. Here are some case studies:

Case Study 1: European Union's Dependence on Russian Gas

Europe depends on Russian gas for around 40% of its imports, making it vulnerable to supply disruptions, price fluctuations, and political tensions. The EU has been trying to diversify its energy sources by investing in renewable energy and building new pipelines, such as the Southern Gas Corridor and the Baltic Pipe. However, it is not clear whether these efforts will be enough to reduce Europe's dependence on Russian gas.

Case Study 2: Japan's Diversification Strategy after Fukushima

After the Fukushima nuclear disaster in 2011, Japan had to shift its energy mix away from nuclear power towards other sources, such as natural gas and renewable energy. This led to a surge in natural gas imports, which made Japan the world's largest importer of LNG. However, Japan has also been promoting energy diversification by investing in coal, oil, and geothermal power, as well as developing technologies for hydrogen and carbon capture, utilization, and storage.

Energy cooperation between Pakistan and Russia is an important step towards diversification and security of supply.

  • It reduces Pakistan's dependence on traditional sources of petroleum products, such as the Middle East.
  • It strengthens Russia's position as a major supplier of oil and gas to Asia, where demand is rapidly growing.
  • It enhances bilateral relations between the two nations, which could lead to further collaboration in other sectors, such as defence, technology, and agriculture.
