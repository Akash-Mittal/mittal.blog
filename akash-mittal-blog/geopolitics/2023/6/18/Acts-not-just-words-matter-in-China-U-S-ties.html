Acts, not just words, matter in China-U.S. ties



Image Credits http://eng.chinamil.com.cn/OPINIONS_209196/Opinions_209197/16231418.html

There is an old Chinese proverb that says, "A picture is worth a thousand words." The same could be said for actions. In the increasingly complex relationship between China and the United States, it's not enough to simply talk the talk. Both sides must walk the walk and demonstrate their commitment to the partnership through concrete actions.

Examples of Acts That Matter

One recent example of the importance of actions in the relationship between China and the U.S. is the trade war that began in 2018. Despite months of negotiations and promises of progress, neither side was willing to back down until tangible progress was made. It took the signing of a "Phase One" trade deal in January 2020, which called for increased purchases of American goods by China, to finally put an end to the conflict.

Another example is the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic. While China and the U.S. initially cooperated on fighting the disease, tensions rose quickly as they began to blame each other for the outbreak. As a result, relationships between the two countries have become strained, further highlighting the need for tangible actions to build trust and cooperation.

Case Studies

Case Study One: Taiwan

The issue of Taiwan is one of the most sensitive and contentious issues in the relationship between China and the U.S. Since Taiwan split from Mainland China in 1949, there has been ongoing tension between the two sides over its sovereignty. The U.S. has pledged to protect Taiwan from Chinese aggression, while China demands reunification and refers to Taiwan as a "renegade province."

In recent years, both China and the U.S. have taken actions that have escalated tensions over Taiwan. For example, China has increased its military presence in the Taiwan Strait, started flying fighter jets over Taiwan's airspace, and pressured foreign governments to sever diplomatic ties with Taiwan. Meanwhile, the U.S. has increased arms sales to Taiwan and has sent high-ranking officials to visit the island, further angering China.

To reduce tensions, both sides need to take concrete actions that demonstrate their commitment to peace and stability in the region. For example, China could halt military exercises near Taiwan and tone down its rhetoric, while the U.S. could reduce the frequency and scale of its military drills with Taiwan and begin to engage in more constructive dialogue with China about the issue.

Case Study Two: Human Rights

Human rights are another sensitive issue in China-U.S. relations. The U.S. has been critical of China's treatment of minority groups such as Uighur Muslims in Xinjiang and pro-democracy activists in Hong Kong, while China has accused the U.S. of meddling in its internal affairs.

To address these issues, both sides need to take tangible actions that promote human rights and respect for the rule of law. For example, China could allow for greater autonomy in Hong Kong and ensure fair treatment of minority groups, while the U.S. could work with international partners to pressure China to improve its human rights record and engage in constructive dialogue with China about the issue.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the relationship between China and the U.S. is complex and multifaceted, and requires more than just verbal commitments to build mutual trust and cooperation. Both sides must take tangible actions that demonstrate their commitment to one another and to peace and stability in the region. Specific actions such as those outlined in the case studies above are needed to move the relationship forward.

Three Key Points:

  1. Actions, not just words, are necessary in the China-U.S. relationship
  2. The trade war and COVID-19 highlight the importance of tangible actions
  3. Case studies on Taiwan and human rights demonstrate specific actions that can be taken
