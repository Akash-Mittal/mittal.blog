Climate Displacement in South Asia and India's Imperative to Act



Image Credits https://southasianvoices.org/climate-displacement-in-south-asia-and-indias-imperative-to-act/

Imagine waking up one day to find that your village, your home, your community, is underwater. The fields you once farmed are now a vast lake, the school your children attended is nothing but ruins, and the only way to survive is to leave everything behind and start anew in a strange land.

The Reality of Climate Displacement

This nightmare scenario is becoming increasingly common in South Asia, where rising sea levels, intensified cyclones, and erratic monsoons are putting millions of people at risk of displacement. According to the Internal Displacement Monitoring Centre, South Asia had the most new internal displacements due to disasters in the world in 2020, with 9.8 million people forced to flee their homes.

But it's not just sudden disasters that are causing displacement. Gradual environmental changes, such as soil degradation, salinization, and desertification, are also making it harder for people to make a living and forcing them to move elsewhere. And with climate change exacerbating these trends, the situation is only going to get worse.

The Impact on India

India, with its vast population, diverse geography, and complex socio-economic fabric, is particularly vulnerable to climate displacement. A recent study by the World Bank estimated that if the global temperature rises by 2°C, India could have as many as 143 million climate migrants by 2050, the highest in the world.

These migrants, often from marginalised communities and with limited resources, will face immense challenges in finding new homes, jobs, and social networks. They are likely to end up in urban slums, where they will be exposed to poor living conditions, health hazards, and social exclusion.

What Can Be Done?

Preventing climate displacement is a complex and long-term challenge, but there are steps that governments, civil society, and individuals can take to mitigate its impact:

  • Invest in Climate Adaptation: By investing in measures such as building sea defenses, improving water management, and promoting crop diversification, governments can reduce the vulnerability of communities to climate risks and increase their resilience.
  • Ensure Access to Basic Services: Climate migrants often lack access to basic services such as housing, healthcare, and education. Governments and civil society should work together to ensure that these services are available to all, regardless of their migration status.
  • Promote Inclusive Development: Climate displacement is often the result of unequal development that leaves certain groups behind. By promoting inclusive development that addresses the needs and aspirations of all, including marginalized communities, governments can reduce the risk of displacement and promote sustainable development.

Conclusion

Climate displacement is not a distant threat, but a reality that is affecting millions of people in South Asia today. India, as a regional leader and a major contributor to global emissions, has a responsibility to act and to protect its citizens from the consequences of climate change. By investing in climate adaptation, ensuring access to basic services, and promoting inclusive development, India can take a proactive role in tackling this urgent issue and build a more resilient and sustainable future for all.
