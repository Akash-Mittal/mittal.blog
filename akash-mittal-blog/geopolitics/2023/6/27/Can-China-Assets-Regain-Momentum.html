The Rise and Fall and Rise Again of China's Assets



Image Credits https://www.ubs.com/us/en/wealth-management/insights/market-news/article.1594166.html

In 2015, a Chinese company called Anbang Insurance Group made headlines when it bought the Waldorf Astoria hotel in New York City for $1.95 billion. At that time, it was just one of many Chinese companies that were investing heavily in foreign assets, ranging from hotels and real estate to technology and entertainment. It seemed like China was taking over the world, one acquisition at a time.

However, things changed quickly. In 2017, the Chinese government cracked down on these overseas investments, citing concerns over capital flight and excessive debt. Many of these acquisitions were canceled or put on hold. This left many investors wondering whether China's assets had lost their momentum.

The History of China's Economy

To understand China's assets and their impact on the global market, we need to look at the bigger picture: the history of China's economy. For decades, China was seen as an underdeveloped and closed-off country, with a command economy and limited foreign investment. However, in the late 1970s, China's leader Deng Xiaoping introduced a series of economic reforms, including the opening up of the country's markets and the encouragement of foreign investment.

These reforms led to a boom in China's economy, with rapid growth and modernization. China became one of the world's largest exporters, and many foreign companies set up operations in the country to take advantage of its low labor costs and huge market. In the early 2000s, China joined the World Trade Organization, which further opened up its economy to the global market.

The Rise of China's Assets

As China's economy grew larger and more open, Chinese companies began looking for opportunities to invest overseas. They saw foreign assets as a way to diversify their portfolios, acquire new technologies and expertise, and increase their global reach. At the same time, many foreign companies were eager to tap into China's huge and growing market, so they welcomed Chinese investments.

The peak of this trend was around 2015, when Chinese companies invested over $100 billion in foreign assets, according to the Financial Times. Some of the notable acquisitions during this time included:

  • Smithfield Foods, the world's largest pork producer, was bought by China's WH Group for $4.7 billion in 2013
  • Legendary Entertainment, a Hollywood studio behind films like Jurassic World and The Dark Knight, was bought by China's Dalian Wanda for $3.5 billion in 2016
  • General Electric's appliance unit was bought by China's Haier for $5.4 billion in 2016

The Fall and Recovery of China's Assets

However, the Chinese government began to worry that these overseas investments were becoming too risky and were putting too much pressure on the country's currency, the yuan. In 2017, the government tightened restrictions on overseas investments, particularly in real estate, entertainment, and sports clubs. Many of the previously announced deals were canceled or delayed.

Despite these setbacks, China's economy has continued to grow, albeit at a slower pace than before. The country's GDP grew by 6.1% in 2019, down from 6.6% in 2018, according to the World Bank. At the same time, China has been investing heavily in its own companies and infrastructure, particularly in the technology sector. This has led to the rise of companies like Alibaba, Tencent, and Huawei, which have become global players in their respective industries.

China's economy has also been boosted by its response to the COVID-19 pandemic, which has been largely successful in containing the virus and restarting the economy. In the first quarter of 2021, China's GDP grew by 18.3%, its fastest rate in decades.

Case Studies: China's Assets Today

So, where do China's assets stand today? Let's look at two case studies.

Real Estate: The Case of China Evergrande Group

China Evergrande Group, a major real estate developer, has been in the news lately due to its mounting debt and the risk of default. The company has over $300 billion in liabilities, making it one of the most indebted companies in the world. It has also been investing heavily in other sectors, including electric cars and health care.

The company's troubles have raised concerns about the stability of China's real estate market and the potential impact on global financial markets. However, experts have also pointed out that the Chinese government is unlikely to let Evergrande fail completely, given its importance to the economy and the risk of a wider financial crisis.

Technology: The Case of Ant Group

Ant Group, a subsidiary of Alibaba and the operator of China's largest mobile payments platform, was poised to go public in late 2020, with an expected valuation of $35 billion. However, the Chinese government abruptly halted its IPO, citing concerns over its business practices and potential risk to financial stability.

The move was seen as a sign of the government's growing control over the country's tech industry, particularly in the wake of the US-China trade war and tensions over cybersecurity. However, experts also noted that the government is still interested in promoting the growth of its tech sector, particularly in areas like artificial intelligence and 5G.

Conclusion

  1. China's assets have had a significant impact on the global market in the past decade, both through its investments in foreign assets and its own economic growth.
  2. The Chinese government has recently tightened restrictions on overseas investments, but the country's economy is still growing and investing heavily in its own companies and infrastructure.
  3. Investors should pay attention to the potential risks and opportunities in China's real estate and tech industries, as well as geopolitical tensions and government regulations.
