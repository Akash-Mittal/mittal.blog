The Rising Numbers of Operational Nukes Globally: A Threat to Humanity



Image Credits https://www.newindianexpress.com/nation/2023/jun/12/numbers-of-operational-nukes-rising-globally-including-in-china-india-and-pakistan-sipri-2584303.html

By The New Indian Express

It's been more than 75 years since the first atomic bomb was dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki, leading to the death of more than 200,000 people and leaving behind a legacy of devastation and destruction. Since then, nations around the world have been racing to build up their stockpiles of nuclear weapons, fearful of a catastrophic global conflict that could wipe out humanity. Despite the efforts of the international community to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons, the numbers of operational nukes are rising globally, posing a grave threat to humanity.

Numbers of Operational Nukes on the Rise

According to a report by the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), the global inventory of operational nukes reached an estimated 12,512 warheads in January 2023. Of these, about 9,576 were in military stockpiles for potential use, which is 86 more than in January 2022. This trend is particularly worrying because it shows that nations are not only continuing to maintain their existing nuclear arsenals, but also expanding them, even as the risks of nuclear war are growing.

The Implications for Humanity

The rising numbers of operational nukes have grave implications for humanity. First and foremost, the risk of nuclear war is growing, as more nations possess nuclear weapons and tensions between them rise. This risk is not theoretical; there have been numerous close calls in the past, and any miscalculation or mistake could trigger a catastrophic global conflict.

Secondly, nuclear weapons are incredibly destructive, capable of killing millions of people in an instant and causing long-term environmental damage. The detonation of just a few nuclear weapons could result in a global humanitarian crisis, as the impacts would be felt not only in the targeted nation, but also around the world.

Thirdly, the proliferation of nuclear weapons undermines the global non-proliferation regime and makes it harder to prevent nuclear weapons falling into the wrong hands. The nuclear black market is a reality, and the more nations possess nuclear weapons, the greater the risk of them falling into the hands of non-state actors, terrorist organizations, or rogue states.

Case Studies: The Global Nuclear Arms Race

To understand the impact of the rising numbers of operational nukes, let's take a look at some of the key players in the global nuclear arms race:

United States

The United States has the most powerful nuclear arsenal in the world, with an estimated inventory of 3,800 nuclear warheads, of which about 1,800 are operationally deployed. In recent years, the US has been modernizing its nuclear arsenal and investing in new delivery systems, despite calls from civil society and some political leaders to reduce their reliance on nuclear weapons.

Russia

Russia is the second-largest nuclear power, with an estimated inventory of 4,500 nuclear warheads, of which about 1,625 are operationally deployed. Russia has also been modernizing its nuclear arsenal, and in recent years has developed new types of nuclear weapons that are designed to evade missile-defense systems.

China

China is rapidly growing its nuclear arsenal, with an estimated inventory of 350 nuclear warheads, of which about 290 are operationally deployed. China has been investing in new delivery systems, including a new hypersonic missile that is capable of penetrating US missile defenses.

India and Pakistan

India and Pakistan are both nuclear-armed nations that have engaged in several military confrontations over the years. India has an estimated inventory of 150 nuclear warheads, of which about 140 are operationally deployed, while Pakistan has an estimated inventory of 160 nuclear warheads, of which about 150 are operationally deployed. Both nations have been expanding their nuclear arsenals in recent years, and the risk of nuclear war between them remains high.

Conclusion

The rising numbers of operational nukes globally are a grave threat to humanity, as they increase the risk of nuclear war, environmental destruction, and nuclear proliferation. It is imperative that nations work towards reducing their reliance on nuclear weapons, engage in dialogue and disarmament negotiations to reduce tensions, and strengthen the global non-proliferation regime to prevent nuclear weapons falling into the wrong hands. Failure to do so could have catastrophic consequences for all humanity.
