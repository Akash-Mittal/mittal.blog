Interest vs alignment - SA's place in the geopolitical maelstrom

An analysis of South Africa's foreign policy

South Africa's complex geopolitical position demands a balance between its national interests and its alignment with international partners. The country's foreign policy has evolved over the years, with a focus on promoting regional integration and stability, advocating for human rights and social justice, and stimulating economic development. However, this policy framework has been challenged by shifting global dynamics, such as the rise of China, the decline of the West, and the resurgence of nationalism and populism.

To illustrate this point, let us examine some examples of how South Africa's interests and alignment have intersected and diverged in recent years:

  1. China and the BRICS: South Africa's participation in the BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) group has been an indication of its desire to engage with emerging economies and diversify its foreign relations. However, the close alignment with China has raised concerns about the balance of power and influence, as well as the impact on local industries and labour standards. As Mcebisi Jonas notes, "South Africa needs to be more strategic about how it leverages the benefits of BRICS while safeguarding its national interests."
  2. The African Union and human rights: South Africa's leadership role in the African Union has been a platform for promoting democracy, governance, and peacekeeping on the continent. However, the country's stance on human rights and humanitarian crises has been inconsistent, particularly in relation to Zimbabwe and Sudan. This has led to criticism from civil society and opposition parties, who argue that South Africa needs to take a stronger stand on issues of human dignity and global solidarity.
  3. The United States and trade: South Africa's long-standing economic ties with the United States have been strained by recent trade disputes, such as the imposition of tariffs on steel and aluminium exports. The Trump administration's "America First" policy has also raised questions about the future of globalisation and multilateralism, which have been central to South Africa's foreign policy. With the upcoming US elections, it remains to be seen how the relationship between the two countries will evolve.

These case studies highlight the need for South Africa to balance its interests and alignment, and to adopt a more nuanced and adaptive approach in its foreign policy. Some of the key factors to consider include:

  1. Domestic priorities: South Africa's foreign policy should be closely aligned with its national development goals, particularly in sectors such as infrastructure, energy, and agriculture. This requires a coherent and coordinated approach between government departments, private sector partners, and civil society actors.
  2. Regional dynamics: South Africa is a key player in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) and the African Union (AU), and should seek to influence regional integration and cooperation. This can be achieved through diplomatic initiatives, trade agreements, and investment partnerships that benefit both South Africa and its neighbours.
  3. Global trends: South Africa should monitor and respond to global trends, such as the rise of China, the growth of India, the fragmentation of Europe, and the impact of climate change. This requires a proactive and strategic approach to diplomacy, foreign aid, and multilateral institutions.
