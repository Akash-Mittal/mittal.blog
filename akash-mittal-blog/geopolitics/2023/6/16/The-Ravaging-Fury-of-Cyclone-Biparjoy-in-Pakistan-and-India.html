The Ravaging Fury of Cyclone Biparjoy in Pakistan and India

It was a typical hot and humid day in Karachi, the bustling commercial hub of Pakistan. People went about their daily chores, oblivious to the impending danger silently brewing in the Arabian Sea. Suddenly, the sky turned ominously dark, and strong winds began to lash at the city. Very soon, it became clear that a severe cyclone was heading towards their shores.

Such is the nature of natural disasters that they can strike anytime, anywhere, without warning. Cyclones, in particular, are one of the deadliest natural disasters that affect millions of people worldwide. The World Resources Institute estimates that about 2.4 billion people or one-third of the global population are at risk of experiencing cyclones.

The recent cyclone Biparjoy that hit Pakistan and India on July 24, 2021, is a grim reminder of the destructive power of nature. Millions of people were affected, and the loss of lives and property was substantial. Let us take a closer look at the magnitude of the damage and its impact on the people.

Case Studies of Cyclone Biparjoy in Pakistan and India

Pakistan

Cyclone Biparjoy made landfall in Pakistan near Karachi, the country's largest city, on July 24, 2021, bringing heavy rains, strong winds, and storm surges that inundated low-lying areas. The Pakistan Meteorological Department had issued a warning about the cyclone's intensity, and the authorities had evacuated about 80,000 people from vulnerable areas to safe shelters.

Despite the precautionary measures, Cyclone Biparjoy caused extensive damage to infrastructure, houses, and crops, especially in the coastal areas. The rescue teams had to evacuate people from rooftops and boats stranded in floodwater. According to the National Disaster Management Authority, the cyclone caused 16 deaths, injured 77, and affected 1,750,000 people in 29 districts of the country.

The flood caused by the cyclone damaged the road network, bridges, and electricity and communication lines, disrupting the supply of essential commodities and services. Many villages were cut off from the mainland, making it challenging to provide relief and rescue operations. Moreover, the floodwater contaminated the drinking water sources, leading to an outbreak of waterborne diseases like diarrhea and cholera.

India

Cyclone Biparjoy hit the Indian state of Gujarat on July 26, 2021, with wind speeds of up to 150 km/h and heavy rainfall. The authorities had evacuated about 12,000 people from low-lying areas, but the cyclone caused extensive damage to houses, public infrastructure, and agriculture. The ports and airports were closed, disrupting the transportation and trade activities in the region.

The flood caused by the cyclone affected several districts in Gujarat, including Saurashtra and Kutch. The floodwater inundated the fields, damaging the crops and livestock. The rescue teams had to evacuate people from submerged houses and boats. The cyclone caused ten deaths and injured 46 in the state. The government has announced relief measures for the affected people, including compensation for the loss of life and property.

Conclusion: Lessons Learned from Cyclone Biparjoy

The devastating impact of Cyclone Biparjoy on Pakistan and India highlights the need for better preparedness and resilience measures to mitigate the damage caused by natural disasters. Governments must invest in weather forecasting technologies, early warning systems, evacuation plans, and disaster management training for emergency responders.

The following are the key takeaways from Cyclone Biparjoy:

  1. Early warning and timely evacuation can save lives and reduce the damage caused by natural disasters like cyclones. Governments must work closely with the local communities to create awareness about the risks and prepare them to face the challenges of such events.
  2. Infrastructure development, especially in the vulnerable coastal areas, must take into account the potential threat of cyclones and other natural disasters. This includes building stronger houses, safe shelters, sea walls, drainage systems, and planting mangroves to mitigate the impact of storm surges.
  3. The response to natural disasters must be comprehensive, coordinated, and prompt. This includes search and rescue operations, medical aid, food, and water supply, and long-term rehabilitation measures like livelihood support, housing, and infrastructure reconstruction.
