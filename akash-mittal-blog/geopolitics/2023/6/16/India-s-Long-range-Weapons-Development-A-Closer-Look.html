India's Long-range Weapons Development: A Closer Look

It was a bright sunny day in May, and the Indian army was conducting a military exercise in the Ladakh region, near the Indo-China border. Suddenly, the calm was shattered by a loud explosion that shook the ground, and thick smoke rose from a nearby hill. The army scrambled to investigate, and they soon discovered that it was a test of the Agni-V, India's most advanced intercontinental ballistic missile (ICBM), capable of reaching targets as far as China.

Case Studies

According to a report by the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), India appears to be expanding its nuclear arsenal and developing new types of nuclear delivery systems. This includes long-range weapons such as the Agni-V and Agni-VI ICBMs and the K-4 submarine-launched ballistic missile. These weapons are designed to provide India with a credible nuclear deterrent against its adversaries, particularly China.

The Agni-V, with a range of up to 5,500 km, is capable of delivering a nuclear payload to targets across China, while the Agni-VI, which is still under development, is said to have a range of up to 10,000 km. Similarly, the K-4 missile, which is deployed on India's nuclear-powered submarines, can strike targets up to 3,500 km away.

India's emphasis on developing long-range weapons is not new. In fact, it has been a stated policy of the country for several years now. India's nuclear doctrine, which was revised in 2003, states that it will maintain a credible minimum nuclear deterrent, and that its retaliatory doctrine will be based on "no first use" of nuclear weapons.

However, India's recent developments in long-range weapons have raised concerns among its neighbors, particularly China. China, which has a much larger and more sophisticated nuclear arsenal, has expressed its displeasure at India's strategic shift. Chinese media has warned of a possible arms race in the region, and has called on India to exercise restraint.

Conclusion

India's focus on developing long-range weapons is driven by several factors, including its desire to maintain a credible nuclear deterrent, and its concerns over China's growing military capabilities. India's strategy of "no first use" of nuclear weapons, coupled with its development of long-range weapons, is aimed at deterring aggression from its adversaries. However, these developments have also raised concerns among India's neighbors, particularly China. It remains to be seen how the strategic balance in the region will evolve in the coming years.
