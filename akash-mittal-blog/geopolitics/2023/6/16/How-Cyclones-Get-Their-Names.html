How Cyclones Get Their Names



Image Credits https://www.wionews.com/photos/as-cyclone-biparjoy-threatens-india-and-pakistan-lets-find-out-how-cyclones-get-their-names-603938

Cyclones are a natural disaster that can cause widespread destruction and devastation. From hurricanes in the Atlantic to typhoons in the Pacific, cyclones are a common occurrence in many parts of the world. But have you ever wondered how these storms get their names? Let's find out.

Interesting Story

In the past, cyclones were named after places, regions, or even the calendar date they occurred on. However, this approach often led to confusion and difficulty in quickly identifying storms. To address this issue, the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) decided to give cyclones human names.

The practice of naming storms started in the 1950s in the United States, where hurricanes were given female names. Later, male names were introduced in 1979 to create a more gender-neutral approach. Today, cyclones around the world are named based on a set of guidelines established by the WMO.

Examples and Case Studies

The WMO maintains a list of names for each basin or ocean around the world. For example, the Atlantic Ocean has six lists of names that are used in rotation. When a cyclone forms, it is given the next name on the list. If a cyclone is particularly deadly or costly, its name is retired and replaced with a new one.

In recent years, several cyclones have made headlines around the world. In 2017, Hurricane Harvey devastated Houston, Texas in the United States. The cyclone caused billions of dollars in damage, and its name was retired from the list of Atlantic hurricane names.

In 2019, Cyclone Idai struck Mozambique, Zimbabwe, and Malawi, causing widespread flooding and leaving over 1,000 people dead. The name Idai was retired from the list of South-West Indian Ocean cyclone names.

Another recent example is Cyclone Amphan, which hit India and Bangladesh in May 2020. The storm caused significant damage to infrastructure and agriculture and resulted in the loss of many lives. The name Amphan will also be retired from the list of North Indian Ocean cyclone names.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the practice of naming cyclones provides a more efficient and organized way of identifying storms. It also allows for easier communication between meteorologists, the media, and the public. Additionally, the retirement of names of particularly devastating storms is a way of honoring the victims and highlighting the importance of storm preparedness and response.
