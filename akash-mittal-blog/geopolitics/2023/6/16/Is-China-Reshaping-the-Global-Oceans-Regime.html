Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

Oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth's surface and are home to a wealth of biodiversity, mineral resources, and sources of food and energy. As such, they play a crucial role in global affairs and the international community has established a complex legal framework to manage the various activities on and under the seas. However, in recent years, China's expansive maritime claims and growing naval power have raised concerns about its impact on the global oceans regime.

The South China Sea Dispute

One of the most high-profile examples of China's push to assert its maritime claims is the South China Sea dispute. This long-standing dispute involves competing claims from China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan over various islands, reefs, and sea territory. China's claims are based on historic rights and its "nine-dash line" map, which encompasses much of the South China Sea. The other claimant countries argue that these claims violate the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, which gives countries exclusive economic zones up to 200 nautical miles from their coasts.

"China has aggressively pursued its claims through island-building, military exercises, and paramilitary patrols, leading to tension and occasional clashes with other claimants and their allies, including the United States."

China's actions in the South China Sea have been met with widespread criticism and concern from the international community. The United States has conducted freedom of navigation operations in the region to challenge China's claims and support the principle of freedom of the seas. Meanwhile, the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague issued a ruling in 2016 that rejected China's claims to much of the South China Sea, but China has refused to recognize the decision.

The Belt and Road Initiative

Beyond the South China Sea, China is also using its growing economic power to shape the global oceans regime. The Belt and Road Initiative, first announced by Chinese President Xi Jinping in 2013, is a far-reaching plan to improve connectivity and economic integration across Asia, Europe, and Africa through infrastructure development, trade, and investment.

While the initiative has broad goals, it also has significant implications for the oceans. The plan includes the construction of ports, shipping lanes, and other maritime infrastructure that could enhance China's ability to project power and influence across the seas. Some observers worry that these developments could also exacerbate existing tensions and even lead to conflict in maritime hotspots like the South China Sea.

The Arctic

Another area where China's influence on the global oceans regime is growing is the Arctic. As the Arctic ice melts due to climate change, new shipping routes and resource opportunities are opening up. This has sparked interest from countries like China, which is a non-Arctic state but has declared itself a "near-Arctic" power.

China's interests in the Arctic include access to resources like oil and gas, as well as new shipping routes that could significantly reduce transit times between Asia and Europe. China has also explored partnerships with Arctic Council members like Russia, Iceland, and Norway to gain greater influence in the region.

Conclusion

China's growing influence on the global oceans regime has raised concerns about its impact on international law, maritime security, and the environment. While China argues that its actions are justified by historic rights and its legitimate economic interests, its expanding maritime claims and military presence have stoked tensions with other countries in the region and beyond. As China continues to assert itself on the high seas, the international community will need to grapple with how to balance competing interests and ensure that the oceans remain a global commons for all.

The oceans cover more than 70% of the earth's surface and are a vital part of global ecosystems, providing food and economic resources for millions of people. However, the regulation and management of these vast areas of water have raised concerns regarding sustainable development and environmental protection. China's rise as a maritime power has raised questions about its role in shaping the global oceans regime and its impacts on regional and global security, environmental sustainability, and economic development.

China's Maritime Development Strategy

China's maritime development strategy is a comprehensive plan that seeks to secure its maritime interests and increase its ocean capabilities, resources, and influence. This strategy comprises three pillars: the maritime economy, maritime security, and maritime governance.

The maritime economy is vital for China's economic development, as the country seeks to sustain its growth through marine resources, transport, and trade. The "Belt and Road Initiative," launched by China in 2013, aims to enhance its connectivity and economic influence by building infrastructure and expanding China's trade links with other countries, particularly those along the maritime Silk Road.

Maritime security is another priority for China, as it seeks to protect its maritime sovereignty, territorial integrity, and interests on land and sea. China's assertive posture in the South China Sea and its military buildup in the region have raised tensions and concerns among its neighbors and other countries that rely on the sea lanes of communication that cross these waters.

Maritime governance is also a key element of China's strategy, as it seeks to play a more active role in shaping the global oceans regime and promoting its own views and interests. China has participated in a range of international initiatives and institutions related to maritime governance, such as the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), the International Maritime Organization (IMO), and the Indian Ocean Rim Association (IORA).

China's Impact on the Global Oceans Regime

China's growing influence in the maritime domain has raised questions about its impact on the global oceans regime and its implications for other countries. Some observers argue that China's rise as a maritime power could lead to a new global order that reflects China's values and interests, such as non-intervention, peaceful development, and mutual benefit.

Others, however, are more skeptical and argue that China's assertiveness and disregard for international norms and rules could undermine the existing global oceans regime and lead to increased tensions and conflicts. For example, China's claims and actions in the South China Sea have challenged the principles of freedom of navigation and overflight, territorial integrity, and peaceful settlement of disputes that are enshrined in UNCLOS.

China has also been accused of engaging in illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing, which undermines the sustainability of fish stocks and harms the livelihoods of coastal communities around the world. In addition, China's construction of artificial islands and military facilities in the South China Sea has raised concerns about its intentions and capabilities in the region.

Case Studies

One case study that illustrates China's impact on the global oceans regime is the South China Sea dispute, which involves China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan. Despite the efforts of regional and global actors to resolve the dispute peacefully and in accordance with international law, tensions have escalated in recent years, as China has expanded its presence and capabilities in the region.

Another case study is China's fishing activities in the Southern Ocean, which have raised concerns about IUU fishing and its impact on the marine environment and biodiversity. The Southern Ocean is a rich area for fisheries, and China is one of the main players in the region, with a large and rapidly growing fleet. However, its fishing practices have been criticized for their unsustainability and negative impact on the ecosystem.

Conclusion

  • China's rise as a maritime power has significant implications for the global oceans regime, as it seeks to promote its own interests and influence the rules and norms that govern the maritime domain.
  • The impact of China's maritime development strategy on the global oceans regime is contentious, with some arguing that it could lead to a new global order that reflects China's values and interests, while others are more skeptical and warn of the risks of conflict and non-compliance with international norms and rules.
  • Case studies such as the South China Sea dispute and China's fishing activities in the Southern Ocean illustrate the challenges and opportunities of managing the complexities of the global oceans regime in the context of China's rise as a maritime power.

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?

Imagine a world where one country has the power to control the world's oceans. This may seem like a far-fetched scenario, but in reality, it is closer to becoming a reality than we might think. China, with its ambitious Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), is making significant strides in reshaping the global oceans regime.

Case Studies

China's expansion in the South China Sea has been well documented, but its reach is not limited to this region alone. In 2018, China signed an agreement with the Maldives for its first-ever free trade deal, which includes a clause allowing Chinese warships to dock at Maldivian ports. While this may seem like a simple trade deal, it is just one example of how China is using its economic power to gain access to ports and maritime resources around the world.

The BRI is also funding port projects in many countries, including Pakistan, Sri Lanka, and Djibouti. These ports are strategically located along key shipping lanes and have the potential to give China significant control over global trade routes, as well as access to valuable resources. For example, China acquired a 99-year lease on the Hambantota port in Sri Lanka after the Sri Lankan government couldn't repay the loans provided by China to build the port. The port is in a critical location, and China has been accused of turning it into a naval base to project its power in the Indian Ocean region.

China is also leading the way in deep-sea mining, with several Chinese companies investing in the technology required to mine the seabed. China is looking to exploit the vast mineral resources found in the deep sea, including cobalt, copper, and rare earth elements. If successful, China could gain a significant advantage over other countries that do not have the technology to mine the deep sea.

Conclusion

In conclusion, China's expanding maritime reach is a cause for concern for many countries, particularly those in the Asia-Pacific region. The BRI has given China access to key ports and resources, allowing it to project its power far beyond its borders. While China claims that it is only interested in peaceful development and trade, its actions suggest otherwise. It is important that the international community work together to ensure that China's maritime ambitions do not threaten global peace and security.
