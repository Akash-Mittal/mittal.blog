Marriages in China Slump to Historic Low: Demographers Say Country Would 'Get Old Before It Gets Rich'



Image Credits https://www.indiatoday.in/world/story/china-marriages-drop-record-low-2022-data-china-birth-rate-population-decline-2392341-2023-06-13

Once upon a time, getting married and having children was considered a rite of passage for many Chinese. However, the traditional norm seems to be fading away with its current generation.

China's marriage rate has been dropping steadily over the years, and in 2022, the country hit an unprecedented low. Only 6.83 million couples completed their marriage registrations, which was down about 8,00,000 from the previous year. This drastic decline has raised concerns among demographers who fear that China might 'get old before it gets rich.'

Why is the Marriage Rate in China Declining?

There are several factors responsible for the decline in marriage rate in China. Let's take a closer look at some of these factors and their impact on society.

The One-Child Policy

The one-child policy, introduced in the 1970s, aimed to control population growth in China. The policy restricted urban couples from having more than one child and rural families from having more than two. This led to a massive gender imbalance with more men than women in the country.

As a result, Chinese men are finding it hard to find partners, which has contributed to the decline in marriage rates.

Economic Factors

The cost of living in urban areas in China is high. Owning a home and providing for a family can be challenging for young working adults. As such, they see marriage as a financial burden, and opt for alternative lifestyles such as cohabitation or remaining single.

Social Pressures

Although society is becoming more progressive, traditional values still hold sway in China. There is a societal expectation that young people should get married and start families. However, changing values and shifting attitudes towards marriage and relationships have made it challenging to meet these expectations.

Case Studies

Case 1: Huang and Zhang

Huang and Zhang met while they were in college and fell in love. However, after graduation, they both realized that owning a home and starting a family was costly. Instead of getting married, they decided to live together, a trend that is becoming increasingly popular in urban areas in China.

Case 2: Li and Wang

Li and Wang met while working in the same company. The two fell in love and decided to get married, but they soon discovered that the cost of living was just too high. They struggled to make ends meet and eventually had to move in with Li's parents. The economic pressures of starting a family led them to decide not to have children.

Case 3: Gao and Liu

Gao and Liu met in college and dated for six years before getting married. They both came from families with strong traditional values and felt pressure to marry and start a family. However, after getting married, they realized that their personalities clashed, and they were not compatible. They got a divorce and decided to remain single.

Conclusion

The decline in China's marriage rate has far-reaching implications for society. With an aging population and fewer young people starting families, there are concerns that China might struggle to support its senior citizens in the future. The Chinese government has responded by relaxing the one-child policy in 2016, but it remains to be seen if this will have a long-term effect on the marriage rate.

Ultimately, the decision to get married and start a family is a personal one, and different factors will influence whether individuals decide to pursue it or not. As society continues to change, we may see new attitudes and values towards marriage emerge.
