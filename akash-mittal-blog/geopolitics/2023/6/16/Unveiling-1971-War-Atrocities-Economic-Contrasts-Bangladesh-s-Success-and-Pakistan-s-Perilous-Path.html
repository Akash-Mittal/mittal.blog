Unveiling 1971 War Atrocities & Economic Contrasts: Bangladesh's Success and Pakistan's Perilous Path

In the early hours of August 25, 1971, the Pakistani army launched a brutal crackdown on the people of East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) which triggered the nine-month-long Bangladesh Liberation War. The Pakistan army brutally killed innocent people, raped women, and torched homes. The war ended with Pakistan's defeat and the birth of Bangladesh.

The war remains a haunting memory for both Bangladeshis and Pakistanis. Bangladesh, however, has risen from the ashes and stands tall as a successful country today. Pakistan, on the other hand, is struggling to cope with its perilous path.

Bangladesh's Success Story

Bangladesh's journey from a war-torn nation to a successful country has been remarkable. The country's economy has been growing at an average rate of 6% for the past decade, making it one of the fastest-growing economies in the world. Bangladesh has also made tremendous progress in social indicators, including healthcare, education, women's empowerment, and poverty reduction.

One example of Bangladesh's success is its ready-made garments (RMG) industry. The RMG industry has been the driving force behind Bangladesh's export-oriented economy. Bangladesh is now the second-largest exporter of apparel, with over $30 billion worth of exports annually.

Bangladesh's success story is not limited to its RMG industry. The country has also made significant progress in the agricultural sector, which employs over 40% of the country's workforce. The country has become self-sufficient in food production and is now exporting food to other countries.

Pakistan's Perilous Path

Pakistan, on the other hand, is struggling to cope with its perilous path. The country's economy is in shambles, with high inflation, rising debt, and a large trade deficit. Pakistan's GDP growth rate has been stagnant for years, and the country is facing a severe energy crisis.

Pakistan's political instability and corruption have contributed to its economic woes. The country has been under military rule for most of its history, and its political leaders have failed to address the fundamental problems of the country.

The lack of investment in human capital and infrastructure has also been a major impediment to Pakistan's economic growth. The country's education system is in disarray, and the healthcare sector is in dire need of reform.

Conclusion

The contrasting paths taken by Bangladesh and Pakistan since the 1971 war are a testimony to the power of effective governance, visionary leadership, and investment in human capital. Bangladesh has shown that with the right policies and leadership, a war-torn nation can transform into a successful country, while Pakistan's struggles highlight the consequences of political instability, corruption, and neglect of human capital.
