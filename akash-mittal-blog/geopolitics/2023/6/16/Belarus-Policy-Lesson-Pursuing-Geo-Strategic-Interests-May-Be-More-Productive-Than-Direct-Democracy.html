Once upon a time, there were two neighboring countries - Country A and Country B. Country A was a strong democracy and believed in promoting democracy around the world. On the other hand, Country B was more focused on pursuing its geo-strategic interests.

In a third country, Country C, both A and B wanted to influence the ruling government. Country A focused on promoting democracy by supporting civil society groups and providing funding for elections. However, Country B focused on providing economic aid and infrastructure development to the government, with the aim of gaining a foothold in the region.

After a few years, the government of Country C was overthrown in a coup supported by Country B. Country A was left wondering what went wrong, despite their efforts to promote democracy.

Examples

The story above is a simplified version of what has been happening around the world for many years. There are several examples of this, but one of the most prominent is the case of Egypt.

In 2011, during the Arab Spring protests, the United States, along with its Western allies, supported democracy activists in Egypt. They provided funding to civil society groups, promoted free and fair elections, and encouraged the military to play a neutral role in the transition to a democratic government.

However, when the Muslim Brotherhood won the elections in 2012, the military, with the support of Gulf countries like Saudi Arabia and the UAE, staged a coup and overthrew the government. The United States was left with limited options since it did not want to alienate its Gulf allies. This led to a change in US policy towards Egypt, where it now focuses more on strategic interests, like counter-terrorism cooperation, than promoting democracy.

Case Studies

Belarus, a former Soviet republic, has been ruled by President Alexander Lukashenko since 1994. Despite its lack of democracy, the European Union (EU) has been providing funding to civil society groups to promote democracy and human rights in Belarus.

However, after the 2020 presidential elections, where Lukashenko was declared the winner amid allegations of fraud and suppression of opposition candidates, the EU imposed sanctions on Belarus. Russia, on the other hand, supported Lukashenko and provided economic aid to the country, with the aim of maintaining its influence in the region.

The lesson here is that pursuing geo-strategic interests may be more productive than direct democracy promotion, especially when dealing with regimes that are unwilling to change or are supported by other major powers.

Conclusion

Based on the examples and case studies above, it is clear that pursuing geo-strategic interests may be more productive than direct democracy promotion, especially when dealing with regimes that are unwilling to change or are supported by other major powers. This does not mean that democracy promotion should be abandoned altogether, but it should be done in a more nuanced and strategic manner.

  • Focus on building strong relationships with local power brokers and elites who are willing to support democracy.
  • Provide economic aid and infrastructure development to win the support of the ruling government, while also ensuring that it does not strengthen their grip on power.
  • Use sanctions and other measures to send a strong message to regimes that violate human rights and suppress democracy.

Belarus Policy Lesson: Pursuing Geo-Strategic Interests May Be More Productive Than Direct Democracy Promotion



Image Credits https://jamestown.org/program/belarus-policy-lesson-pursuing-geo-strategic-interests-may-be-more-productive-than-direct-democracy-promotion/

The ongoing political crisis in Belarus has grabbed the attention of the international community. Protests erupted in the country after the disputed presidential elections in August 2020, which saw the re-election of Alexander Lukashenko. The protests have led to a crackdown by the Lukashenko regime and international condemnation. The crisis has raised questions about the effectiveness of Western efforts to promote democracy in the region. In this article, we explore the lessons that can be learned from the Belarusian case.

A common approach to promoting democracy in authoritarian countries like Belarus is through direct support for civil society, independent media, and opposition political parties. However, in some cases, this approach has backfired. For example, the United States supported pro-democracy movements in the Middle East during the Arab Spring. While initial uprisings were successful in some cases, many countries have descended into chaos and instability. In Egypt, for instance, the military took control after the fall of Hosni Mubarak, leading to a crackdown on political opposition and civil liberties.

Case Study 1: Ukraine

Another way to approach promoting democracy is through geostrategic interests. This can involve the use of economic, political, and military leverage to support democratic and pro-Western forces in authoritarian countries. Ukraine is an example of such an approach. In the aftermath of the Orange Revolution in 2004, which saw the overthrow of the pro-Russian President Viktor Yanukovych, Western countries provided economic aid and political support to the new Ukrainian government. In addition, NATO expanded its military presence in the region, including the deployment of troops to Poland and the Baltics. This helped create a buffer zone between Ukraine and Russia.

Case Study 2: Belarus

However, in Belarus, the West has taken a more cautious approach. The country is deeply intertwined with the Russian economy and has close ties to Moscow. Western countries have imposed economic sanctions on the Lukashenko regime, but have stopped short of providing direct support to the opposition. This has led to criticism from some quarters, who argue that the West is not doing enough to support democracy in Belarus.

Conclusion: What Can We Learn from the Belarusian Case?

  1. Pursuing geostrategic interests can be more effective than direct democracy promotion. Western countries should use their economic and political leverage to support democratic movements in authoritarian countries.
  2. The situation in Belarus is complex and requires a nuanced approach. Western countries can impose sanctions on the Lukashenko regime, but should also engage with the government and opposition to find a peaceful resolution to the crisis.
  3. The Belarusian crisis highlights the need for a strategic approach to promoting democracy. Western countries should prioritize stability and avoid policies that could lead to chaos and instability in authoritarian countries.
