India beats China to take the second spot in Largest Road Networks

The Journey to Second Spot

India has recently surpassed China to become the country with the second-largest road network in the world. This is a remarkable achievement for a country like India which was considered to be lagging behind in infrastructure development for a long time. The journey to the second spot was not an easy one, and it required a lot of planning, investment, and efforts from the government and private sector.

In the last decade, India has been heavily investing in the construction of new highways and upgrading the existing ones to match international standards. The government has also taken significant steps to improve the connectivity of rural areas by building new roads and bridges. Let's take a look at some of the examples and case studies to understand how India achieved this feat.

Examples and Case Studies

Golden Quadrilateral

One of the most significant examples of India's road infrastructure development is the Golden Quadrilateral, a 5,846-kilometer-long network of highways that connects India's four major cities, Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, and Kolkata. Completed in 2006, the project was initiated by the then-Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee in 1999, and it took seven years to complete. The aim of the project was to improve connectivity, reduce travel time and cost, and boost economic growth. The Golden Quadrilateral has helped India to improve its transport infrastructure and increase its competitiveness in the global market.

Bharatmala Project

The Bharatmala project is another example of India's commitment to developing its road infrastructure. Launched in 2017, the project aims to build 83,677 km of highways by 2022, at an estimated cost of $82 billion. The project will connect border areas, coastal regions, tourist destinations, backward and tribal areas, and key economic hubs. The Bharatmala project is expected to create thousands of jobs and boost economic growth by improving connectivity and reducing transportation costs.

Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana

The Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana is a flagship program launched by the government of India in 2000. The aim of the program is to provide all-weather road connectivity to unconnected rural habitations. This program has helped to connect remote and isolated areas with the mainstream and has provided better access to markets, health care, and education. The program has been successful in reducing poverty, improving the quality of life, and promoting inclusive growth in rural areas.

NHAI's Hybrid Annuity Model

The National Highways Authority of India (NHAI) has introduced the Hybrid Annuity Model (HAM) to facilitate the construction of highways on a public-private partnership (PPP) basis. Under this model, the government provides 40% of the project cost as a grant to the developer, and the remaining 60% is funded by a mix of equity and debt. The developer gets a fixed annuity from the government for 15 years, which covers the capital and operational costs. This model has attracted many private players to invest in the development of highways and has helped to expedite the construction process.

Conclusion

India's achievement of becoming the country with the second-largest road network in the world is a testament to its commitment to infrastructure development. The government's efforts to build new highways, upgrade existing ones, and connect rural areas have helped to boost economic growth, create jobs, and improve the quality of life for millions of people. However, there is still a long way to go, and the government needs to continue investing in infrastructure development to support its growing economy and population.
