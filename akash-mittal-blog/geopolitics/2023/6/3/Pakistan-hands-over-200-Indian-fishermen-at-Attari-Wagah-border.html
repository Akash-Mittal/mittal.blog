Pakistan hands over 200 Indian fishermen at Attari Wagah border

Pakistan has handed over 200 Indian fishermen to India at the Attari Wagah border crossing on Wednesday. The fishermen were arrested over the past two years for allegedly crossing into Pakistani waters and fishing illegally.

Impact on the Indian fishing industry

The arrest of Indian fishermen by Pakistan has been an ongoing issue for many years. The fishermen are often poor and illiterate, and they cross into Pakistani waters in search of a catch to sell in local markets. However, they are often caught by Pakistani authorities and charged with illegal fishing.

According to a report by the South Asian Network on Dams, Rivers and People, over 1,000 Indian fishermen are currently in Pakistani jails. This has had a devastating impact on the fishing industry in India, especially in the state of Gujarat, which has the highest number of fishermen arrested in Pakistan.

The report notes that the loss of income has led to a decrease in the standard of living of the affected families, and has forced many fishermen to sell their boats and other equipment. This has also resulted in a loss of jobs, as many small-scale businesses that support the fishing industry have closed down.

Case study: The impact on one family

One such family is that of Ramesh Patel, a fisherman from Gujarat who was arrested by Pakistani authorities in 2018. Patel had gone to sea with a group of fishermen when they were caught by Pakistani coastguards and charged with illegal fishing. Patel was sentenced to three years in prison.

While Patel was in prison, his wife and children struggled to make ends meet. They had to sell their boat and other equipment, and relied on the support of relatives and friends for survival. Patel's wife also had to work as a domestic servant to earn a living.

When Patel was released from prison earlier this year, his family had already suffered a great deal. They had lost their source of income, their home, and their dignity. Patel himself had to undergo counselling to deal with the trauma of being in jail for three years.

The need for a long-term solution

The release of the 200 Indian fishermen by Pakistan is a welcome move, but it is not a solution to the problem. The issue of cross-border fishing is complex, and requires a long-term solution that addresses the root causes of the problem.

One possible solution is to create a joint commission between India and Pakistan that would oversee fishing in the disputed waters. This would require both countries to work together to develop policies that respect the rights of fishermen on both sides of the border, and that ensure sustainable fishing practices.

Another solution is to provide vocational training and alternative sources of income for fishermen who are forced to fish in disputed waters due to poverty. This would require a coordinated effort between the government, NGOs, and the fishing community.

Conclusion

The release of 200 Indian fishermen by Pakistan is a positive development, but it is not enough to solve the recurring problem of cross-border fishing. A long-term solution is needed that addresses the root causes of the issue, and that ensures the rights of fishermen on both sides of the border. This requires a coordinated effort between India and Pakistan, as well as the involvement of NGOs and the fishing community.
