Youth in China Giving Up High-Paying Jobs to Be Baristas



Image Credits https://www.businessinsider.in/policy/economy/news/young-people-in-china-are-giving-up-high-paying-jobs-to-become-baristas-and-waiters-and-theyre-talking-about-it-online/articleshow/100978419.cms

When 23-year-old Zhang Luyao graduated from the prestigious Tsinghua University with a degree in computer science, he had a bright future ahead of him. The hard work had paid off. Luyao secured a high-paying software engineering job with a big company in Shanghai.

But after a year of 12-hour workdays, he began feeling discontented. "I wanted something more than a high salary," he said. So, he quit his lucrative job and began looking for something new.

Eventually, Luyao found work as a barista and he loves it. "I get to interact with different people every day," he said. "And I'm learning so much about coffee and the art of making it.

Luyao is not alone. A growing number of young people in China are walking away from high-paying jobs to work as baristas, waiters and other service industry jobs.

Why Are Youth Giving up High-Paying Jobs?

The primary reason that young Chinese are giving up high-paying jobs is they want more fulfilling work and a better quality of life.

After years of intense schooling and long hours at work, many young Chinese say that they don't want a life in which all they do is work.

They want to pursue careers that they are passionate about and that allow them to interact with different people and explore different cultures.

In addition, they are looking for better work-life balance and the time to pursue personal interests and hobbies.

Examples of Youth in the Service Industry

Meet Shi Lei. She is 24 years old and has a degree in finance. After working for two years in an office job, she quit and started working at a restaurant as a waitress.

"I realized that I wasn't happy working in finance," she said. "It was difficult to find meaning in a job where all I did was look at numbers all day."

Shi Lei loves her job as a waitress because she gets to meet different people every day. "I'm constantly learning new things," she said. "And I love the fast-paced environment."

Another example is Zhang Haoran. He used to work in marketing and public relations for a large Chinese company. After a few years, he felt burnt out and wanted a change. He now works as a bartender in a trendy bar in Beijing.

"I'm much happier now," he said. "I get to connect with customers on a one-on-one basis. And I'm learning so much about different cocktails and spirits."

Case Study: LATTEA Cafe

LATTEA Cafe is a cafe chain in China that has become popular with young people in the country.

The cafes are designed to look like trendy coffee shops in New York City or London with exposed brick walls and rustic decor.

But what sets LATTEA apart is that the baristas are all young and attractive women who wear short skirts and revealing tops.

Despite the criticism from some quarters, LATTEA has grown rapidly over the past few years and has over 50 locations in China.

Conclusion

The trend of young people in China giving up high-paying jobs to work in the service industry is an indication of changing attitudes towards work and life in China.

Here are three takeaways from this trend:

  1. Young people in China are looking for more fulfilling work and a better quality of life.
  2. They want to pursue careers that allow them to explore their interests and passions.
  3. Despite the negative stereotypes and challenges associated with working in the service industry, young Chinese are discovering that they can find happiness and fulfillment in these jobs.
