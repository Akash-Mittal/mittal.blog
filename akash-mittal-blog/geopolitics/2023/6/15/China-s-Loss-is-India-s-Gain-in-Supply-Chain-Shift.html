China's Loss is India's Gain in Supply-Chain Shift



Image Credits https://www.bizzbuzz.news/economy/chinas-loss-is-indias-gain-in-supply-chain-shift-1226207

Have you ever wondered why most of the products we use are made in China? The answer is simple - China was a manufacturing and exporting giant with low labor costs, efficient infrastructure, and accommodating government policies. But things have changed over the years, and the shift in the global supply chain is giving India an opportunity to capitalize on.

Let me tell you a story about Mr. Smith, a small business owner in the United States who used to import cheap plastic toys, keychains, and other novelties from China. He had a good profit margin, and his customers were happy with the quality and pricing. However, the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted global trade, and Mr. Smith faced a shortage of supplies. The lockdown in China halted manufacturing and exports, and soon Mr. Smith realized that he needed to diversify his supplier base and reduce his dependence on China.

The same scenario played out in many parts of the world, as businesses and governments realized the risks of relying too much on a single country for essential goods and services. The term 'decoupling' became popular, which meant breaking away from Chinese dominance in the supply chain and seeking alternatives.

Why India is the Next Manufacturing Hub?

India has the potential to be the next manufacturing hub due to several reasons:

  1. Skilled Labor Force: India boasts of a large and educated population with technical and managerial skills. The government has launched several initiatives to promote vocational training, digital literacy, and startup culture.
  2. Favorable Policies: The government of India has implemented several reforms to attract foreign investment, simplify procedures, reduce compliance costs, and promote export-oriented manufacturing. The Make in India campaign, launched in 2014, aims to make India a global manufacturing powerhouse.
  3. Strategic Location: India's location offers several advantages, such as easy access to large consumer markets in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East, proximity to major shipping lanes, and abundant natural resources.

Examples of Supply-Chain Shift to India:

Many companies have already started shifting their supply chains to India, or at least considering it as an option. Let's look at some examples:

Apple:

In 2020, tech giant Apple announced that it would manufacture the iPhone SE model in India instead of China, as part of its strategy to diversify production to mitigate risks due to the pandemic and trade tensions between the US and China. The move is expected to boost the Indian electronics industry and create jobs.

Samsung:

Samsung, the South Korean conglomerate, has been operating in India for nearly two decades and has invested heavily in its manufacturing and R&D facilities. In 2020, Samsung announced that it would shift some of its production from Vietnam and other countries to India, as India offers better incentives, skilled labor, and emerging demand for consumer electronics.

Nike:

Nike, the American sportswear company, has been sourcing apparel and footwear from China, Vietnam, and other countries. However, due to the US-China trade war and the COVID-19 outbreak, Nike has been exploring alternative sourcing options. In 2020, Nike signed a memorandum of understanding with the government of Tamil Nadu, India, to set up a manufacturing unit and source materials from local suppliers.

Case Studies of Successful Supply-Chain Shift to India:

Wistron:

Wistron is a Taiwanese electronics manufacturer that produces smartphones, laptops, and other devices for companies like Apple, Microsoft, and Acer. In 2018, Wistron set up a manufacturing plant in Bengaluru, India, as part of its expansion plans in Asia. The plant was built with an investment of $160 million and employed over 10,000 workers. Within two years, the plant became one of the largest producers of iPhones in India, with a capacity of 12 million units per year.

Foxconn:

Foxconn is a Taiwanese multinational that is the largest contract manufacturer of electronics in the world. It produces devices for Apple, Xiaomi, and other brands. In 2020, Foxconn announced that it would invest $1 billion to expand its operations in India, including setting up a new manufacturing plant in Tamil Nadu. The investment is expected to create over 6,000 jobs and increase Foxconn's production capacity in India.

Kia Motors:

Kia Motors, the South Korean carmaker, entered the Indian market in 2019 with the launch of its compact SUV model, Seltos. Within a year, Kia became the fastest-growing automobile brand in India, with a market share of over 6%. Kia achieved this feat by setting up a state-of-the-art manufacturing plant in Andhra Pradesh, with an investment of $1.1 billion. The plant has a capacity of producing 300,000 vehicles annually and employs over 12,000 workers.

Conclusion:

The shift in the global supply chain is a golden opportunity for India to emerge as a manufacturing hub. However, India needs to overcome several challenges, such as infrastructure bottlenecks, bureaucracy, labor laws, and skill gaps. Furthermore, India needs to be mindful of not repeating the mistakes that China made, such as environmental degradation, low wages, lack of labor rights, and intellectual property theft. The shift to India should be sustainable, inclusive, and innovative.

  1. India has the potential to be the next manufacturing hub due to its skilled labor force, favorable policies, and strategic location.
  2. Many companies, such as Apple, Samsung, and Nike, have already started shifting their supply chains to India or are considering it.
  3. Successful case studies, such as Wistron, Foxconn, and Kia Motors, demonstrate that India can compete with China in terms of manufacturing and production capacity.
