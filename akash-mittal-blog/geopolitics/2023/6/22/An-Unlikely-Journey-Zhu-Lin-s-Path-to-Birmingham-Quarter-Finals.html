An Unlikely Journey: Zhu Lin's Path to Birmingham Quarter-Finals

There's little glamour in the world of women's tennis outside the top echelons. Even talented players can go their whole careers without breaking into the Grand Slam tournaments or travelling the world on the professional tour. For most, it's a journey of paying dues, fighting for scraps, and celebrating the small victories.

Zhu Lin, a 22-year-old Chinese player, is one of these women. She's currently ranked 140th in the world, with a career high of 126th. She's only played in one Grand Slam tournament – the 2019 Australian Open, where she lost in the first round of qualifying. She's been a professional for four years, and has earned just over $113,000 in career prize money. In short, she's far from a household name in the tennis world.

But this week, Zhu has made a name for herself at the Nature Valley Classic in Birmingham, England. She's won three matches in a row to reach the quarter-finals, including a dominant performance against former world number one Caroline Wozniacki. So how did this unheralded player make it to this point?

Hard Work and Persistence

For Zhu, success hasn't come overnight. Before turning professional, she was a highly-ranked junior player in China, but struggled to make the transition to the senior level. She spent two years playing on the ITF Circuit – the minor league of women's tennis – before earning a wild card into the 2017 Australian Open. She won her first-round match, but lost in the next round to eventual quarter-finalist Anastasia Pavlyuchenkova.

After that early taste of success, Zhu went back to the grind of the ITF Circuit, where she played in dozens of tournaments around the world, often without endorsement deals or a full-time coach. She slowly worked her way up the rankings, cracking the top 200 for the first time in 2018.

But even as she climbed the ranks, Zhu was still facing obstacles. In 2018, she missed several months of the season with a shoulder injury. Earlier this year, she was forced to withdraw from the Australian Open qualifying tournament due to illness.

Through it all, Zhu has maintained a positive attitude and a strong work ethic. In an interview with WTA Insider earlier this year, she acknowledged the challenges of life on the ITF Circuit:

"It's very tough because you don't have a guarantee to play tournaments. You don't have a guarantee to have a sponsor or enough prize money to pay for the travelling costs. But I think if you love the sport and if you have a dream, you will just keep going."

A Breakthrough Moment

Zhu's breakthrough moment came in May of this year, when she reached the final of a $100,000 ITF tournament in Anning, China. She defeated two top-100 players en route to the final, and though she lost in three sets to top seed Zhang Shuai, the tournament marked a major step forward in her career.

Since then, Zhu has continued to build momentum. She won another ITF tournament in Nanning, China in June, and then qualified for the main draw of the WTA tournament in Nottingham, England earlier this month. She won her first-round match in Nottingham, but then lost to top seed and eventual champion Caroline Garcia in the second round.

Still, the experience of playing against a player like Garcia – a top-20 player with multiple WTA titles to her name – gave Zhu a taste of what it's like to compete at the highest level. She took that confidence into this week's Nature Valley Classic, where she's now one win away from the biggest quarter-final of her career.

What Zhu's Success Means

Of course, one player's success doesn't change the tennis world overnight. Even if Zhu goes on to win the Nature Valley Classic – a tournament that lacks many of the top players in the world – she'll still have a long way to go to prove herself on the bigger stages.

But her journey is a reminder of the hard work and sacrifice that goes into a life in professional tennis. It's easy to focus on the glamour and riches of the top players, but every player has to start somewhere. And for most, the road is a rocky one.

Zhu's success is also a reminder of the talent and depth that exists in women's tennis outside of the top 50 or so players. She's far from the only player on the ITF Circuit with the skills to compete at the highest level, but few are lucky enough to get the breaks – or have the persistence – to climb the ranks. Zhu's journey may inspire other players to keep grinding and dreaming, even when success seems far away.

Conclusion

In conclusion, Zhu Lin's path to the quarter-finals of the Nature Valley Classic has been an inspiring story of perseverance and hard work. Her journey is a reminder of the challenges facing players outside the top levels of women's tennis, and of the talent and depth that exists beneath the surface. As Zhu continues her career, she may inspire others to keep fighting for their dreams, no matter how distant they may seem.
