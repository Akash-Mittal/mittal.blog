Envisioning New Nuclear Doctrines – South Asian Voices

Once upon a time, in the midst of the Cold War, the Soviet Union and the United States came dangerously close to engaging in a nuclear war. The year was 1983, and tensions between the two superpowers were high. On September 26th, the Soviet Union's early warning system picked up an incoming missile from the United States. The operations officer, Stanislav Petrov, judged it to be a false alarm and chose not to report it up the chain of command. His decision prevented a retaliatory strike, as the missile did not actually exist. This story underscores the importance of rational thinking and de-escalation when it comes to nuclear weapons.

Despite our best efforts, nuclear weapons remain a reality in today's world. South Asia, in particular, is a region of concern due to the presence of nuclear weapons in India and Pakistan. Both countries have experienced a number of conflicts, and the fear is that there could be a nuclear escalation in the event of another conflict. In order to avoid this outcome, there is a need for a new nuclear doctrine in the region.

Examples

One example of a new nuclear doctrine is the "No First Use" policy. This policy, which India adopted in 1998, states that India will only use nuclear weapons in response to a nuclear attack. This policy is intended to prevent a nuclear escalation by signaling to the world that India will not be the first to use nuclear weapons.

Another example is the idea of "minimum deterrence". This doctrine argues that a country only needs a small number of nuclear weapons to deter an adversary. The idea is that having too many nuclear weapons increases the risk of accidents or unauthorized use, so a smaller nuclear arsenal is safer overall. Pakistan has embraced this approach, arguing that it needs nuclear weapons in order to deter India, but that a small number of weapons is enough.

Case Studies

One case study of the need for a new nuclear doctrine in South Asia is the Kargil War, which took place in 1999. During this conflict, Pakistan and India fought over the disputed region of Kashmir. Both sides mobilized their militaries and there were fears of a nuclear escalation. However, both countries ultimately exercised restraint and did not use their nuclear weapons. This demonstrates that a new nuclear doctrine could be successful in preventing a nuclear war.

Another case study is the ongoing conflict between India and Pakistan in Kashmir. Both sides have made threats regarding the use of nuclear weapons, and there have been a number of skirmishes and terror attacks. The potential for a nuclear escalation remains very real in this situation, and a new nuclear doctrine is needed in order to avoid catastrophe.

Conclusion

In conclusion, South Asia needs a new nuclear doctrine in order to avoid a catastrophic nuclear war. Some possible approaches include a "No First Use" policy, or a focus on "minimum deterrence". Regardless of the specifics, the key is to adopt a rational and de-escalatory approach to nuclear weapons. This will require a shift in thinking on the part of both India and Pakistan, as well as sustained international pressure to encourage responsible behavior. Ultimately, the goal must be to ensure that nuclear weapons are never used, and that peace prevails in South Asia.
