On Cuba-China Ties, the U.S. Should Look in the Mirror



Image Credits https://www.newsweek.com/cuba-china-ties-us-should-look-mirror-opinion-1808259

At a recent press conference about Cuba-China ties, U.S. Secretary of State Antony Blinken expressed concern about China's growing influence in the region. He argued that China was only interested in exploiting Cuba's natural resources and gaining a strategic foothold in the Americas, and warned that this could threaten regional stability and U.S. national security interests.

But is this really a case of the pot calling the kettle black? When it comes to exploiting other countries and meddling in their affairs, the U.S. has a long history and a dubious track record.

The U.S. and Cuba

Let's start with Cuba. The U.S. has a long and complicated relationship with this Caribbean island nation. In the late 1800s, the U.S. intervened in Cuba's war for independence from Spain, and the two countries have been intertwined ever since. The U.S. has supported Cuban dictators, imposed economic sanctions, and even attempted to overthrow the Cuban government through the Bay of Pigs invasion in 1961.

More recently, the U.S. has tightened its economic embargo on Cuba, limiting trade and travel between the two countries. This has caused hardship for ordinary Cubans, who have seen their access to basic goods and services restricted.

The U.S. and China

The U.S. has also had a long-standing rivalry with China, which has grown increasingly tense in recent years. The two countries are locked in a trade war, and the U.S. has accused China of stealing intellectual property and engaging in unfair trade practices. The U.S. has also criticized China's human rights record, particularly with regard to Tibet and Xinjiang province.

But the U.S. has also been accused of meddling in China's affairs. The U.S. government has funded pro-democracy groups in Hong Kong, and has been accused of supporting separatist movements in Tibet and Xinjiang. These actions have caused tension between the two countries and could be seen as a form of interference in China's internal affairs.

Case Studies

Let's take a closer look at two examples of U.S. interference in other countries: Iran and Chile.

Iran

In 1953, the U.S. government orchestrated a coup that ousted the democratically elected government of Iran and replaced it with a pro-Western monarchy. The coup was motivated by a desire to control Iran's oil reserves and prevent Soviet influence in the region. The coup led to the rise of a repressive dictatorship under Mohammad Reza Shah Pahlavi, and contributed to the 1979 Islamic Revolution that brought Ayatollah Khomeini to power.

Chile

In 1973, the U.S. government supported a coup in Chile that overthrew the democratically elected government of Salvador Allende and installed General Augusto Pinochet as dictator. The coup was motivated by a desire to prevent the spread of communism in Latin America, but it led to widespread human rights abuses and a repressive regime that lasted until 1990.

Conclusion

  1. The U.S. should be careful about criticizing other countries for interfering in the affairs of other nations when the U.S. has a long history of doing the same thing.
  2. The U.S. should focus on building constructive relationships with other countries based on mutual respect and cooperation, rather than trying to dictate their policies and behavior.
  3. The U.S. should recognize that other countries may have different political systems, values, and interests, and that it is not the role of the U.S. to impose its own worldview on others.
