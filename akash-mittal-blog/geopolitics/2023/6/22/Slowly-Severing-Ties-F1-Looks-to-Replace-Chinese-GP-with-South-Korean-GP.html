Slowly Severing Ties: F1 Looks to Replace Chinese GP with South Korean GP



Image Credits https://firstsportz.com/f1-is-slowly-trying-to-sever-ties-with-china-sets-eyes-on-south-korean-gp-to-replace-chinese-gp-reports/

The Formula 1 (F1) Grand Prix is one of the most popular and exciting events in the world of motorsports. However, recent tensions between F1 and China have caused F1 to consider severing ties with China and moving the Chinese GP to another location. According to reports, F1 is now eying the South Korean GP as a possible replacement for the Chinese GP.

The Backstory to F1's Tensions with China

Over the past few years, F1 has been gradually trying to distance itself from China due to growing concerns over human rights violations in the country. For instance, in 2019, F1's managing director of motorsports, Ross Brawn, expressed his concerns over the Chinese government's treatment of the Uighur Muslim minority in Xinjiang, China. Brawn was quoted as saying, "We certainly wouldn't want to be seen as being supportive of any actions which we would consider to be against human rights."

Moreover, F1's broadcasting rights deal with China has been a source of tension between the two parties. In 2018, F1's then-CEO, Chase Carey, was quoted as saying that F1 was "not happy" with the deal that they had with CCTV, China's state broadcaster. Carey said, "We're not getting the kind of distribution that we want."

Looking for Alternative Locations

As a result of these tensions, F1 has been exploring alternative locations for the Chinese GP. South Korea has emerged as the leading candidate to replace China, thanks to its impressive infrastructure and strong government support for motorsports.

The South Korean GP was initially held on the newly-built Korea International Circuit in 2010, before dropping off the F1 calendar in 2013 due to spiraling costs. However, the circuit has since undergone a series of upgrades, including track resurfacing and improved safety measures, making it a viable option for F1 to consider.

Beyond South Korea, other locations that F1 has reportedly considered include Malaysia, Vietnam, and Singapore. However, each of these locations has its own set of challenges and limitations that make them less attractive to F1 than South Korea.

Case Study: South Korea's Response to COVID-19

One factor that has made South Korea an attractive option for F1 is its handling of the COVID-19 pandemic. South Korea has been widely praised for its effective response to the pandemic, which has allowed the country to maintain a relatively normal way of life and keep its economy open.

In particular, South Korea's testing and contact tracing capabilities have been recognized as some of the best in the world. As of February 2021, South Korea had tested more than 7 million people for COVID-19, which translates to more than 13,600 tests per 100,000 people.

Moreover, South Korea has implemented strict quarantine and isolation measures for incoming travelers, helping to prevent the spread of the virus. These measures have been enforced by the government with the help of advanced technology, such as wristbands that monitor the wearer's movements and alert authorities if they leave their quarantine location.

By contrast, China's response to COVID-19 has been criticized for its lack of transparency and suppression of information. For instance, in January 2020, Chinese authorities arrested Li Wenliang, a doctor who had raised the alarm about the virus before it became a global pandemic. Wenliang later died from COVID-19, becoming a symbol of Chinese censorship and repression of dissenting voices.

Conclusion

In conclusion, F1's considerations to replace the Chinese GP with the South Korean GP can be attributed to a combination of economic, political, and social factors. Economic viability is clearly an important consideration for F1, as are concerns over human rights and censorship in China, which can damage F1's reputation.

Furthermore, the COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the importance of effective public health management and has made South Korea a strong candidate for hosting the event. In selecting the South Korean GP, F1 can signal its commitment to responsible international relations and show its support for countries that promote and respect human rights and democratic values.
