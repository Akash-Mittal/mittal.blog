Young Chinese Bypass High-Paying Jobs for Barista Positions



Image Credits https://www.businessinsider.in/policy/economy/news/young-people-in-china-are-giving-up-high-paying-jobs-to-become-baristas-and-waiters-and-theyre-talking-about-it-online/articleshow/100978419.cms

The Story

It used to be that getting a high-paying job was the ultimate goal for young people in China. But now, an increasing number of Chinese millennials are choosing to work in the service industry instead of the once-coveted office jobs. They're becoming baristas and waiters, and they're talking about it online. This isn't just a hobby or a stop-gap job for them, but a deliberate career shift. As surprising as it may sound, the trend is real and has even led to a new term coined in China - "Budding Coffee Geeks."

Examples

One example of a young Chinese millennial who chose a career shift from a high-paying job to become a barista is Yang Lu. After working in finance for five years, Yang decided to quit her job and pursue her love for coffee. She enrolled in a barista training program in Guangzhou, where she learned about the intricacies of brewing and serving coffee. She now works as a barista in a cafe in Shanghai, where she can satisfy her passion for coffee and interact with customers, something she couldn't do behind a desk in an office.

Another example is Liu Qian, who had a successful career in marketing before she decided to become a barista. She found that working in marketing was not fulfilling and decided to pursue her dream of becoming a coffee expert. She now runs her coffee shop in Beijing, where she roasts her beans and serves coffee to her regulars. She said the shift made her happier and more satisfied.

Case Studies

A survey conducted by China Youth Daily found that more than 80% of young people in China would consider switching industries for better personal growth. The study also revealed that more than 70% of respondents believed that working in the service industry, including jobs as baristas and waiters, was no longer less respectable than a high-paying office job.

One case study highlighted by the BBC is 25-year-old Liu Xintong, who left her job in public relations after feeling "suffocated" by the office environment. She found solace in making coffee and now works as a barista at Cafe Del Volcan in Beijing. She says that working in a coffee shop gave her a sense of freedom and creativity, and she enjoys interacting with customers, as opposed to her previous job, which was all about pleasing her bosses.

The shift is not limited to baristas alone but even extends to waiters. One case in point is Li Weijie, who used to work in finance before deciding to become a waiter. After working numerous hours in his office, he soon realized that he didn't enjoy his job, which included long hours and desk work. He began searching for other options, which is when he stumbled upon a position opening for a waiter. Li Weijie now works as a waiter at the prestigious Le Noir restaurant in Guangzhou, where he feels happier and more satisfied with his job.

Conclusion

  1. China is witnessing a significant trend shift, where the younger generation is bypassing high-paying jobs to work in the service industry, including jobs as baristas and waiters.
  2. This shift has been fueled by the belief that working as a barista or waiter is no longer less respectable than a high-paying office job.
  3. Young people find careers in coffee and service industry fulfilling, giving them a sense of freedom, creativity, and job satisfaction that working in the office environment doesn't provide.
