Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?

It was just before dawn when Captain Zhao Gang set out on his long journey through the South China Sea aboard the Hainan Province fishing vessel. Gang had been a fisherman since he was a boy, and he knew the waters off the southern coast of China like the back of his hand.

But this time, something was different. As he and his crew sailed past the Spratly Islands, they noticed a new addition to the horizon. Rising up from the sea was a massive structure, a man-made island that looked like a fortress.

They didn't know it at the time, but what they had stumbled upon was a key part of China's strategy for dominating the South China Sea. In recent years, China has been building artificial islands throughout the region, constructing military installations, and staking claim to vast stretches of disputed waters. These actions have alarmed neighboring countries and raised questions about China's intentions in the global oceans regime.

China's Oceans Ambitions

China has long felt that it has been unfairly kept out of the global oceans system. It believes that the current regime was constructed by Western powers and does not reflect the interests of developing countries like China. As a result, China is pushing for a new system that is more equitable and reflects its growing status as a major economic and military power.

One of the keys to China's campaign is the construction of man-made islands throughout the South China Sea. These islands have allowed China to lay claim to reefs and rocks that would otherwise be considered international waters. In addition, these islands have allowed China to construct military installations throughout the region, including airfields and ports.

Case Studies: China in the South China Sea

The South China Sea is one of the most heavily contested bodies of water in the world. It is bordered by China, the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan. All of these countries claim various portions of the sea, leading to numerous conflicts and disputes.

China's actions in the South China Sea have been particularly controversial. In addition to building artificial islands, China has also conducted large-scale land reclamation activities, dredging sand and rocks from the seabed to expand its territory. China has also sent naval vessels and aircraft into disputed waters, risking confrontation with other countries and raising tensions in the region.

Philippines v. China Arbitration

The conflict between China and the Philippines over the South China Sea came to a head in 2013, when the Philippines filed a case against China with the Permanent Court of Arbitration. The case centered on China's claim to a large portion of the South China Sea, which the Philippines argued was in violation of international law.

In 2016, the court issued a landmark ruling in favor of the Philippines. The court held that China's claim to the South China Sea was invalid, and that China had violated Philippine sovereignty by building artificial islands in the region. However, China rejected the ruling and continues to assert its claims in the South China Sea.

U.S.-China Standoff

The United States has also been closely involved in the South China Sea dispute. The U.S. has conducted regular freedom of navigation operations in the region, sending naval vessels and aircraft into disputed waters to challenge China's claims. This has led to several tense incidents between the two countries, including a 2018 incident in which a Chinese destroyer came dangerously close to a U.S. Navy vessel.

Conclusion: How China is Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime

China's actions in the South China Sea are just one part of its broader campaign to reshape the global oceans regime. By building artificial islands and staking its claim to vast stretches of disputed waters, China is challenging the current system and asserting its own vision for the future.

In conclusion, here are three key takeaways:

  • China's quest for dominance in the South China Sea is leading to tensions and conflicts with other countries in the region;
  • China's construction of man-made islands is allowing it to lay claim to vast stretches of disputed waters;
  • China's actions are challenging the existing global oceans regime and raising questions about the future of international maritime law.

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

The world's oceans cover over 70% of the planet's surface and are home to countless species of marine life. They are also essential for international trade and provide significant resources for human consumption. However, with the increasing population and demand for resources, the ocean's health is at risk. Various international organizations and agreements were established to manage the world's oceans, but China's actions in recent years raise questions about whether it is reshaping the global oceans regime.

In 2019, a Chinese fishing vessel collided with a Filipino boat, causing it to sink in the disputed waters of the South China Sea. The Filipino crew claimed that they were left stranded, and the Chinese vessel sailed away without offering any assistance. The incident sparked protests in Manila and tension between China and the Philippines.

This story illustrates the complicated relationship between China and other countries with territorial claims in the South China Sea. China claims almost the entire region, despite objections from other countries such as the Philippines, Vietnam, and Indonesia.

Examples

China's actions in the South China Sea are just one example of how it is reshaping the global oceans regime. Here are a few other ways it is doing so:

1. Illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing

China is the world's largest producer and consumer of seafood. Its fleets of fishing vessels travel all over the world, especially in Africa, Latin America, and the Pacific. However, many of these vessels engage in IUU fishing, which harms the environment and threatens the livelihoods of local fishermen.

China's government has been criticized for not doing enough to crack down on IUU fishing. In 2020, the U.S. government blacklisted several Chinese companies for their involvement in IUU fishing, prompting China to retaliate with its own sanctions.

2. Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)

The BRI is a massive infrastructure and investment plan initiated by China in 2013. It aims to connect Asia, Europe, and Africa through a network of roads, railways, ports, and other facilities. One key aspect of the BRI is the construction of ports and other maritime infrastructure in countries along the route.

This has raised concerns about China's strategic ambitions and its impact on the environment and local communities. For example, in Sri Lanka, the Chinese-built Hambantota Port became a symbol of the country's debt crisis and China's growing influence. Similarly, the Chinese-built port in Gwadar, Pakistan, has been criticized for its lack of transparency and environmental impact.

Case Studies

South China Sea

The South China Sea is a region of intense territorial disputes between China and several Southeast Asian countries. China claims almost the entire sea, including numerous disputed islands and reefs. The other countries, such as the Philippines and Vietnam, contest China's claims and seek to uphold their own.

China has been accused of using aggressive tactics, such as building artificial islands and militarizing the region, to reinforce its claims. These actions have raised concerns about the potential for conflict in the region and the impact on the environment and marine life.

Galapagos Islands

The Galapagos Islands are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the world's greatest biodiversity hotspots. The region is home to numerous rare and endemic species, including the famous giant tortoise.

In 2020, a Chinese fishing fleet was caught operating illegally near the Galapagos Islands. The fleet was reportedly catching large quantities of sharks, which are a protected species in the Galapagos. The incident prompted outrage from Ecuador and conservation groups and underscored the need for better regulation of fishing in the region.

Conclusion

China's actions in the world's oceans are reshaping the global oceans regime in various ways. It is engaging in IUU fishing, promoting its Belt and Road Initiative, and asserting territorial claims in disputed regions such as the South China Sea. These actions raise concerns about the environmental impact, sustainability, and potential for conflict.

In conclusion, here are three key points to consider:

  • China's growing economic and geopolitical power is not limited to land but extends to the sea.
  • The world needs to find ways to ensure sustainable use and management of the ocean's resources.
  • A cooperative approach that involves all stakeholders, including China, is necessary for the effective management of the world's oceans.
