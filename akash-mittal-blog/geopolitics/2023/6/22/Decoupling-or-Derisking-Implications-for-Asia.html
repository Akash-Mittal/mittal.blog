Decoupling or Derisking: Implications for Asia



Image Credits https://www.theedgesingapore.com/views/geopolitics/whether-decoupling-or-derisking-there-are-big-implications-asia

Meet John, a businessman based in Asia. His company has been thriving due to its close ties to the US market. However, recent tensions between the US and China have made him worried about the future of his business. He wonders if he should consider decoupling from the US market or derisking his investments in Asia. But what does it mean?

Decoupling refers to the process of reducing economic interdependence between two countries or regions. It often happens when there are political or economic tensions between them. On the other hand, derisking is the process of reducing exposure to financial risks by diversifying investments or reducing them altogether.

The implications of these two trends are enormous for Asia, a region that heavily relies on trade and foreign investments. Let's explore some examples of their impact:

Example 1: Decoupling from China

The US has been implementing policies to reduce its dependence on China, particularly in the tech sector. For instance, it banned Huawei, a Chinese tech giant, from doing business with US companies. This has made some Asian countries, such as South Korea and Taiwan, worried about their tech exports to China. A decoupling between the US and China could result in a domino effect that would shrink their economies and disrupt global trade.

Example 2: Derisking Investments in Asia

Global investors have been pulling out money from emerging markets, including Asia, due to the economic slowdown caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. This has made some Asian countries, such as India and Indonesia, vulnerable to financial instability. To mitigate this risk, some investors have been diversifying their portfolios to include less volatile assets, such as gold and US Treasury bonds. This could result in a reduction of foreign investments in Asia, which would have negative consequences for the region's growth.

Let's look at some case studies that illustrate the impact of decoupling and derisking in Asia:

Case Study 1: Japan and South Korea's Trade War

Japan and South Korea had a trade war in 2019, triggered by a dispute over wartime compensation. Japan, the world's third-largest economy, imposed export restrictions on three key high-tech materials needed by South Korean tech companies. This forced South Korea to look elsewhere for alternative suppliers, such as China and Taiwan. The trade war caused a decline in their economies and disrupted the global supply chain.

Case Study 2: Chinese Investors in Southeast Asia

Chinese investors have been pouring money into Southeast Asia in recent years, particularly in the real estate and infrastructure sectors. However, the COVID-19 pandemic has made some of their investments riskier, as it has affected the demand for these assets. This has prompted some Chinese investors to derisk by diversifying their portfolios, as well as to renegotiate their investment terms. This has put pressure on Southeast Asian countries, such as Malaysia and Thailand, to revise their policies towards Chinese investments.

In conclusion, decoupling and derisking have significant implications for Asia, a region that is deeply integrated into the global economy. To sum up, here are three points to remember:

  1. Decoupling can disrupt global trade and shrink economies, as seen in the US-China trade war.
  2. Derisking can reduce foreign investments and slow down growth, as seen in the COVID-19 pandemic.
  3. Both trends can cause political tensions and financial instability, as seen in the case studies of Japan-South Korea and China-Southeast Asia.
