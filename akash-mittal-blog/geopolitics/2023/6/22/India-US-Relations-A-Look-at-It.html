India-US Relations - A Look at It



Image Credits https://www.brookings.edu/podcast-episode/a-look-at-india-us-relations-as-prime-minister-modi-visits-the-white-house/
"The United States has no closer ally than the Indian people," said US President Joe Biden in his first address to the American people after taking office. "We will stand with India in confronting the threats it faces in its own region."

These words were more than just a diplomatic gesture. They came after a long and complex history of India-US relations that includes both cooperation and confrontation, trust and suspicion, support and criticism. In fact, the story goes back even before India gained independence from British rule in 1947 and the US recognized it as a sovereign nation.

But let's fast forward to the present, to the recent visit of Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi to the White House in September 2021. This was the first in-person meeting between him and President Biden since the latter's election in November 2020. It was also seen as an opportunity to reset and revitalize the bilateral ties that had faced some challenges in the previous years.

Examples of India-US Relations

One of the key areas of India-US cooperation is trade. According to the Office of the US Trade Representative, the two-way goods and services trade between the two countries totaled $146.1 billion in 2019, with exports from the US to India reaching $58.5 billion and imports from India to the US reaching $87.6 billion. However, the trade balance is heavily tilted in favor of India, which has been a point of contention for the US. The US has also accused India of engaging in unfair trade practices and violating intellectual property rights.

Another area of strategic partnership is defense. The two countries have been conducting joint military exercises and sharing intelligence and technology, particularly in the maritime domain. Recently, India became part of the Quad, a security alliance involving the US, Japan, and Australia, seen as a counterbalance to China's assertiveness in the Indo-Pacific region. However, India has its own concerns about the Quad and wants to maintain its strategic autonomy.

Cultural and people-to-people exchanges have also played a role in shaping India-US relations. India has a large diaspora in the US, with over 4 million people of Indian origin living there. They have contributed to the US economy, society, and politics, and have also helped to bridge the gap between the two cultures. Indian Americans have been represented in various fields, such as science, technology, business, arts, and politics, including Vice President Kamala Harris, who is of Indian descent.

Case Studies of India-US Relations

Case 1: The Nuclear Deal

In 2005, India and the US signed a landmark civilian nuclear deal that allowed India to access nuclear technology and fuel from the US and other countries, despite not being a signatory to the Non-Proliferation Treaty. The deal was seen as a major achievement of the then Indian Prime Minister Manmohan Singh and his US counterpart George W. Bush, who had overcome many obstacles to push it through.

The deal was not without controversy, however, as it raised concerns about nuclear proliferation and the balance of power in South Asia. Critics in India accused Singh of compromising the country's sovereignty and giving in to US pressure, while critics in the US accused Bush of undermining the nonproliferation regime and setting a bad precedent for other countries.

Nevertheless, the deal was eventually approved by both countries and implemented over the years. It has been touted as a symbol of the growing partnership between India and the US, as well as an example of how two democracies with different histories and interests can find common ground.

Case 2: The H-1B Visa Issue

The H-1B visa is a non-immigrant visa that allows US companies to hire foreign workers in specialty occupations, such as science, engineering, and IT. Many Indian professionals, including engineers, doctors, and software developers, have been beneficiaries of the H-1B program, which has helped them to live and work in the US and contribute to the US economy.

However, the H-1B program has also been criticized as a way for US companies to hire cheap foreign labor and displacing American workers. This has led to calls for reforming the program and increasing the minimum wage and skill requirements for H-1B workers. The Trump administration was particularly hostile to the H-1B program, accusing it of taking away jobs from Americans and cracking down on fraud and abuse.

The H-1B issue has been a sore point in India-US relations, as it affects the livelihoods and aspirations of many Indians. The Indian government has been lobbying the US to maintain a fair and liberal H-1B policy, while also trying to improve the domestic job market and skills of its workforce. The Biden administration has promised to review and reform the H-1B program, but it remains to be seen how it will balance the competing demands and interests.

  • India-US relations have come a long way since their inception and have evolved into a multifaceted partnership that encompasses trade, defense, security, culture, and values. However, the relationship also faces challenges, such as trade imbalances, strategic divergences, visa issues, and human rights concerns. The two sides need to engage in frank and constructive dialogue to address these issues and build on their shared interests and goals.
  • The visit of Prime Minister Modi to the White House in 2021 has provided a renewed impetus to the India-US relations and has set the stage for greater cooperation and collaboration in the coming years. The two leaders have agreed to focus on areas such as climate change, healthcare, education, digital innovation, and regional stability, among others. They have also reaffirmed their commitment to democracy, pluralism, and human rights, which are the pillars of their societies.
  • India-US relations are not only important for the two countries but also for the region and the world at large. They can contribute to peace, stability, and prosperity in the Indo-Pacific and beyond, as well as to the global challenges such as COVID-19, climate change, terrorism, and economic recovery. India and the US have a shared responsibility to promote cooperation, not conflict, and to uphold the principles of justice, equality, and dignity for all.
