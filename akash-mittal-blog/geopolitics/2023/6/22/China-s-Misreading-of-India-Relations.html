China's Misreading of India Relations



Image Credits https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/editorials/archives/2023/06/22/2003801955

Once upon a time, there were two powerful neighbors in Asia – China and India. They shared a long border and ancient cultural and economic ties. However, their relationship has been marked by a history of conflict and suspicion, such as the Indo-China War of 1962 and the recent Doklam stand-off near Bhutan. Despite their mutual interests and potential for cooperation, China and India have failed to build a stable and friendly partnership.

The Problem of Misreading

One reason for this failure is the problem of misreading – China is misreading India relations. Instead of understanding India's aspirations and concerns, China has viewed India through the lens of its own strategic interests and ideology, thus leading to strategic miscalculations and diplomatic blunders. Let's examine some examples.

Examples of Misreading

Example 1: China's "String of Pearls" Strategy. This strategy aims to encircle India with a network of ports and naval bases stretching from Myanmar to Pakistan. While China claims that this is a commercial and peaceful initiative, India sees it as a strategic move to gain military influence in its neighborhood and pose a threat to its security. China's misreading lies in its assumption that India will not resist its expanding presence in the Indian Ocean Region and will eventually submit to Chinese dominance.

Example 2: China's One Belt One Road (OBOR) Initiative. This global infrastructure project, which seeks to connect China with Europe, Africa and Asia, includes the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) that passes through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir, a disputed region claimed by India. China's misreading is in its disregard for India's sovereignty and territorial integrity, and in its belief that India will not oppose its OBOR project, which it regards as a challenge to its own regional integration plan.

Example 3: China's Support for Pakistan. China has a long-standing strategic alliance with Pakistan, which includes military cooperation, nuclear technology transfer and economic assistance. While India has no objection to China's normal relations with Pakistan, it opposes China's support for Pakistan's terrorism and its obstruction of Indian efforts to bring Pakistan to justice. China's misreading is in its failure to recognize India's legitimate concerns about Pakistan-based terrorism, and its tendency to equate India and Pakistan as equally responsible for their disputes.

Case Studies

Case Study 1: Doklam Stand-Off. In 2017, China and India faced a military stand-off near the border of Bhutan, where both countries had competing claims. China attempted to construct a road in the area, which India saw as a violation of the status quo and a threat to its security. India intervened to stop the construction, leading to a tense confrontation between the two sides. China misread India's resolve and mistakenly believed that India would back down as it did in 1962. However, India's strong response and diplomatic maneuvering eventually led to a peaceful resolution of the stand-off, with both sides agreeing to disengage and withdraw their troops.

Case Study 2: India's Response to OBOR. India, unlike most other countries, has refused to join China's One Belt One Road project, citing concerns over its economic and strategic implications. India views the OBOR as a Chinese attempt to establish a new world order based on its own terms, and as a threat to India's own regional integration plans such as the Bay of Bengal Initiative for Multi-Sectoral Technical and Economic Cooperation (BIMSTEC) and the International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC). India's misgiving is in its belief that OBOR will benefit China more than its partners and will lead to a debt trap and strategic dependence for the latter.

Case Study 3: India's Relations with Taiwan. India, like many other countries, does not officially recognize Taiwan due to its "One China" policy, which obliges countries to accept the People's Republic of China (PRC) as the sole legitimate representative of China. However, India has maintained informal ties with Taiwan and allowed its officials to visit India as part of cultural and economic exchanges. China misread India's intentions and demanded that India stop such activities, warning of "serious consequences" in case of non-compliance. India refused to be cowed and reiterated its commitment to engage with Taiwan based on shared interests and values.

Conclusion

In conclusion, China's misreading of India relations is a major hurdle to their bilateral cooperation and peace in Asia. China needs to address India's legitimate concerns and interests, and avoid imposing its own strategic and ideological paradigm on India. India, on the other hand, needs to engage China on equal terms and balance its national interests with regional and global aspirations. The two countries can benefit from their complementary strengths and markets if they can overcome their mutual suspicions and build trust and cooperation.

Here are three key points to keep in mind:

  • China needs to respect India's sovereignty and territorial integrity, and avoid pursuing projects and policies that harm India's security and interests.
  • India needs to keep an open mind and engage with China on issues of mutual concern and benefit, such as climate change, terrorism, and trade.
  • International and regional actors, such as the United States, Japan, and ASEAN, need to encourage and support China and India's peaceful coexistence and cooperation, rather than taking sides or exacerbating their differences.
