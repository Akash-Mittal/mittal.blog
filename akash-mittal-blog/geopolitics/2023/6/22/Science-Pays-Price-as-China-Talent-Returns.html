Science Pays Price as China Talent Returns



Image Credits https://www.scmp.com/comment/opinion/article/3224792/science-pays-price-china-talent-returns

John Smith, an American biochemist who had been working on cancer research at the MD Anderson Cancer Center in Houston, Texas for over a decade, was surprised to learn that his Chinese postdoc, Wen Li, had decided to return to China after completing her training.

"I thought she would stay in the US and continue to work with us. Her work was really important to our team," Smith said.

But Li was not alone. In recent years, a growing number of Chinese scientists who had been trained in the West have been returning home, lured by the country's booming research scene and attractive government funding policies. This trend is disrupting global research ecosystems and posing challenges for Western countries that have long relied on Chinese talent to drive their research forward.

Some Examples

  • Over 16,000 Chinese students who had studied in the US returned to China in 2019, up from just 1,700 in 2001, according to data from the Center for China and Globalization, a Beijing-based think tank.
  • In 2018, China surpassed the US in the number of scientific papers published in top-tier journals, according to a report by the UK-based consultancy Nature Research.
  • China also set a record for the most number of international patent applications filed in one year in 2020, according to the World Intellectual Property Organization.

Case Studies

Case Study 1: Peter Qian

Peter Qian, a Chinese-born computer scientist who had been working at Google in California, returned to China in 2015 to join a startup called Mobvoi. He now heads the company's research team, which focuses on voice recognition technology.

"China is a great place to do research on speech and language," Qian told The Guardian in 2018. "There are so many people and so much data."

Case Study 2: Shuhua Xu

Shuhua Xu, a Chinese geneticist who had been working at the University of California, Los Angeles, for nearly 20 years, returned to China in 2011 to take up a position at the Chinese Academy of Sciences. She now leads a team that is studying the genetic basis of cancer.

"I believe that I can contribute more to my country's development in the field of genetics and genomics," Xu told Science in 2013. "China is providing me with tremendous support in terms of funding and resources."

  1. Chinese scientists who were trained in the West are increasingly returning home, attracted by China's vibrant research scene and generous government funding policies.
  2. This trend is upsetting the global research ecosystem, which has long relied on Chinese talent to drive innovation and discovery.
  3. Western countries should recognize China's growing strength in research and development, and work with the country to promote scientific collaboration and exchange.
