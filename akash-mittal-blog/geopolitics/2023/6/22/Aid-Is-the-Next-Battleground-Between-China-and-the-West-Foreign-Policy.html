Aid Is the Next Battleground Between China and the West



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/22/debt-aid-imf-g20-global-south-development-china-west-us-geopolitics/

An article by Foreign Policy

In the early 2000s, a small African country named Zambia was facing an acute shortage of healthcare facilities. The government approached the West for help in building hospitals and clinics, but they were turned down due to lack of funds. That's when China stepped in with an offer to build 27 hospitals and clinics across the country. The Zambian government, left with no other choice, accepted the offer. However, the catch was that China would use their own companies and labor force to carry out the construction, which they did quickly and efficiently.

This is just one example of how China has been providing aid to developing countries in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. Unlike the West, China does not attach any political or governance conditions to their aid, which has made them very popular among the recipient nations. Moreover, China's aid comes in the form of infrastructure projects, which have a direct and visible impact on the lives of the people.

Case Study: Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port

In 2007, China provided aid to Sri Lanka to build a port in Hambantota, which is strategically located on the Indian Ocean. However, when the Sri Lankan government could not repay the loan, they were forced to lease the port to China for 99 years. This move has caused concern among the West, as it is seen as an attempt by China to increase its presence in the Indian Ocean region.

Case Study: US Aid to Pakistan

For years, the US has been providing aid to Pakistan with the aim of promoting democracy and stability in the region. However, the aid has been marred by corruption, mismanagement, and lack of tangible results. As a result, Pakistan has turned to China for aid, which has been providing large-scale infrastructure projects such as the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC).

In conclusion, aid is becoming the next battleground between China and the West. China's approach of providing aid without any political conditions and in the form of infrastructure projects has made them very popular among developing countries. On the other hand, the West's approach of linking aid to good governance and democracy has been criticized for being too intrusive. However, concerns have been raised over China's increasing presence in strategic locations and their use of debt-trap diplomacy. The West needs to find a middle ground that promotes development without compromising on democracy and human rights.

  • The West needs to reassess their approach to aid and be more flexible in their conditions.
  • The West needs to collaborate with China and find common ground that promotes sustainable development.
  • The recipient nations should be empowered to make their own decisions and not be forced to choose between China and the West.
