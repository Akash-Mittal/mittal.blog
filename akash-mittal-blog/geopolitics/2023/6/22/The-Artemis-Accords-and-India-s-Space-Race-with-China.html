The Artemis Accords and India's Space Race with China



Image Credits https://breakingdefense.com/2023/06/india-signs-artemis-accords-tightening-ties-with-us-in-space-race-with-china-sources/

On September 22, 2021, India signed the Artemis Accords, a set of principles for international cooperation in space exploration, developed by the United States. The accords seek to establish a framework for responsible behavior in space, promote transparency and cooperation among nations, and pave the way for a sustainable and peaceful exploration of the cosmos.

The Artemis Accords take their name from NASA's Artemis program, which aims to return humans to the Moon by 2024 and establish a sustainable presence there by 2028. The program is seen as a stepping stone to future crewed missions to Mars and beyond, and as a symbol of the United States' leadership in space exploration.

India, which has its own ambitious space program, has been a partner of NASA in some of its missions, including the Mars Orbiter Mission (MOM) in 2014 and the Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution (MAVEN) mission in 2013. India's space agency, the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), has made significant advances in areas such as satellite technology, remote sensing, and space launch vehicles, and is working on its own crewed space mission by 2022.

India's signing of the Artemis Accords can be seen as a recognition of the growing importance of space exploration and collaboration, as well as a strategic move to strengthen its ties with the United States and counterbalance China's expanding presence in space.

Examples of India's Space Program

India's space program has its roots in the establishment of the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR) in 1962, which later became ISRO in 1969. Since then, ISRO has achieved a number of milestones in space exploration, including:

  • The launch of Aryabhata, India's first satellite, in 1975
  • The launch of Rohini, India's first satellite launched by an Indian-made launch vehicle, in 1980
  • The launch of Chandrayaan-1, India's first lunar mission, in 2008
  • The launch of Mangalyaan, India's first Mars mission, in 2013
  • The launch of 104 satellites in a single mission, a world record, in 2017

ISRO has also contributed to international space missions, such as the European Space Agency's Mars Express and ExoMars missions, and has established partnerships with countries such as France, Russia, Japan, and the United States.

Case Studies of India-US Collaboration in Space

India and the United States have a long history of collaboration in space, dating back to 1963, when Indian astronaut Rakesh Sharma flew on a Soviet spacecraft with a Soviet cosmonaut and an Indian physician.

Since then, the two countries have cooperated on a number of space missions, including:

  • The launch of the Indian Remote Sensing (IRS) satellites, which have provided valuable data for agriculture, forestry, geology, water resources, and other applications
  • The launch of the Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite (GOES) series, which provide weather data for North America
  • The joint development of a synthetic aperture radar (SAR) on board the NASA-ISRO Synthetic Aperture Radar (NISAR) mission, which will study the Earth's surface and environment

India's participation in the Artemis program, which aims to land the first woman and first person of color on the Moon, is another example of the two countries' partnership in space. India has agreed to provide critical components for the space suits worn by NASA astronauts and to assist in lunar sample analysis.

Conclusion in Three Points

  1. The signing of the Artemis Accords by India highlights the importance of international cooperation and responsible behavior in space exploration.
  2. India's space program has made significant advances in areas such as satellite technology, remote sensing, and space launch vehicles, and is working on its own crewed space mission by 2022.
  3. India's partnership with the United States in space, as exemplified by the Artemis program and other missions, reflects a shared vision of exploration and discovery, as well as a strategic effort to counterbalance China's growing influence in space.
