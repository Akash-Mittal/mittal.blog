Interest vs Alignment: SA's place in the Geopolitical Maelstrom



Image Credits https://www.news24.com/citypress/business/interest-vs-alignment-sas-place-in-the-geopolitical-maelstrom-20230611

Once upon a time, in the midst of the Cold War, South Africa found itself in a peculiar situation. On one hand, it was a staunch ally of the United States, staunchly anti-communist and supportive of America's foreign policy objectives. On the other hand, it was an international pariah, with most Western nations imposing economic sanctions on the country over its policy of apartheid.

This dichotomy highlights the challenge that has always faced South Africa in the realm of geopolitics: how to pursue its own interests in a world where powers align in different ways.

Examples

The clearest example of this dilemma came in the 1970s and 1980s, when South Africa had to balance its alignment with the Western world against its own interests in southern Africa. As communist forces took power in Angola, Mozambique, and Zimbabwe, South Africa saw itself increasingly isolated in the region.

But while the West supported South Africa's anti-communist stance, it was deeply uncomfortable with the country's apartheid policies. So while the US provided arms and other support to South Africa, they also applied pressure on the country to end apartheid.

All of this left South Africa in a precarious position. It needed to maintain its military edge over its communist neighbors, but at the same time it couldn't push the West too far away.

Case Studies

The dilemma facing South Africa has only grown more complex in the years since the Cold War. Now, the country must balance its relationships with not only major powers like the US and China, but also regional players like Nigeria and Egypt.

One case study in this balancing act is South Africa's relationship with China. On one hand, the two countries are close trading partners. China is South Africa's largest trading partner, with two-way trade exceeding $35 billion in 2019.

But China's growing influence in Africa also puts it in competition with South Africa for influence on the continent. Many African nations are increasingly looking to China for investment, aid, and political support, which could erode South Africa's role as a regional power.

One area where this has become particularly clear is in South Africa's relationship with the African Union (AU). The AU has been a key ally for South Africa in pursuing its foreign policy objectives, but China has been investing heavily in the organization in recent years.

Conclusion

In conclusion, South Africa faces an ongoing challenge in navigating the complex web of alliances and rivalries that make up the modern geopolitical landscape. Here are three key takeaways:

  1. South Africa's interests are sometimes at odds with its traditional allies, particularly when it comes to the country's regional aspirations.
  2. The rise of China as a superpower is creating new challenges and opportunities for South Africa on the global stage.
  3. South Africa's ability to effectively navigate these challenges will ultimately determine its place in the world order.

Interest Vs Alignment - SA's Place in the Geopolitical Maelstrom

It was a hot summer day in Pretoria when I met James, a middle-aged businessman who had spent most of his career working in South Africa's mining industry. "The most important thing in this country," he said, sipping his beer, "is knowing who your friends are."

James's words stuck with me long after our conversation ended. It was a simple statement, but it spoke volumes about South Africa's place in the global geopolitical landscape. As a country with rich natural resources and a troubled past, South Africa has had to navigate a complex web of alliances and allegiances while pursuing its own interests.

Interest vs Alignment

At the heart of South Africa's foreign policy is the tension between interest and alignment. Put simply, interest refers to the country's economic and strategic goals, while alignment refers to its relationships with other countries and international organizations.

On one hand, South Africa has a strong interest in maintaining economic ties with major trading partners. As the largest economy in Africa, South Africa relies heavily on exports to countries like China, the United States, and the United Kingdom. At the same time, South Africa must balance these economic interests with its broader strategic goals of maintaining regional stability and promoting human rights and democracy in Africa.

Examples

One example of this dynamic at play is South Africa's relationship with China. China is South Africa's biggest trading partner, and the two countries have a close economic relationship. However, China's growing influence in Africa has raised concerns for South Africa and other countries in the region. South Africa must balance its economic relationship with China against the potential geopolitical risks of aligning too closely with a country that is seen by some as a challenge to the existing global order.

Another example is South Africa's relationship with Zimbabwe. Zimbabwe has long been a close ally of South Africa, but the current government's human rights abuses and political instability have strained the relationship. South Africa must balance its commitment to democracy and human rights with its historical ties to Zimbabwe and the potential regional instability that could result from a breakdown in the relationship.

Case Studies

To better understand these dynamics, we can examine two case studies of South Africa's foreign policy in action.

Case Study 1: The African Union

One of South Africa's key foreign policy goals is to promote regional stability and cooperation in Africa. To achieve this goal, South Africa has been a strong supporter of the African Union (AU) since its inception in 2002.

The AU is a pan-African organization made up of 55 member states. Its mission is to promote economic integration, democracy, and human rights in Africa. South Africa sees the AU as a key partner in achieving its own strategic goals, and has been an active participant in AU initiatives like peacekeeping missions and economic integration efforts.

However, South Africa's relationship with the AU is not without its challenges. The AU is made up of a diverse group of countries with different political and economic interests, and South Africa must balance its support for the AU against the risk of being seen as an outsider or hegemon by other African countries.

Case Study 2: BRICS

In recent years, South Africa has been an active participant in the BRICS group of nations, which includes Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa. BRICS is a consortium of emerging economies that seeks to challenge the existing global order and promote the interests of developing countries.

South Africa sees its participation in BRICS as a way to increase its economic and strategic influence in the world. However, the group's reliance on China as an economic powerhouse has raised concerns for South Africa and other member countries.

Conclusion

As we can see, South Africa's position in the global geopolitical landscape is a complex and evolving one. The tension between interest and alignment is a constant challenge for the country as it seeks to pursue its own economic and strategic goals while maintaining stable relationships with other countries and international organizations.

In conclusion, South Africa's foreign policy can be distilled into three key points:

  • South Africa's interests are primarily economic, but it must balance these interests against the potential geopolitical risks of aligning too closely with any one country or group of countries.
  • South Africa is committed to promoting regional stability and democracy in Africa, and sees the African Union as a key partner in achieving these goals.
  • South Africa's participation in groups like BRICS reflects its desire to increase its economic and strategic influence in the world, but the country must be mindful of the risks that come with aligning too closely with any one member country.
