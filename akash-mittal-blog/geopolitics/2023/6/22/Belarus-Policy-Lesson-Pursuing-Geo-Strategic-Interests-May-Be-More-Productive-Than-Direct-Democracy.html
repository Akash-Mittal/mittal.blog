Belarus Policy Lesson: Pursuing Geo-Strategic Interests May Be More Productive Than Direct Democracy Promotion - Jamestown

Once upon a time, there was a small country in Eastern Europe called Belarus. The country had a complicated history, with various foreign powers trying to control it for centuries. In the 20th century, Belarus became a part of the Soviet Union and remained so for several decades. After the collapse of the Soviet Union, Belarus became an independent country. However, it did not become a democracy. Instead, it remained a dictatorship, with the same leader in power for over 25 years.

For many years, the international community tried to promote democracy in Belarus. They provided funding for civil society organizations, trained election observers, and put pressure on the Belarusian government to hold free and fair elections. However, these efforts did not produce the desired results. The dictator remained in power, and the country remained authoritarian.

Recently, some experts have argued that pursuing geo-strategic interests may be more productive than direct democracy promotion in Belarus. In other words, instead of trying to promote democracy in the country, the international community should focus on building stronger ties with Belarus to counter Russia's influence in the region. This article explores this argument and provides case studies to illustrate its effectiveness.

Examples

One example of geo-strategic interests trumping democracy promotion is the case of Kazakhstan. Like Belarus, Kazakhstan is an authoritarian country that does not hold free and fair elections. However, the country is rich in natural resources, including oil and gas, and is strategically located between Russia and China. As a result, Western countries have developed closer ties with Kazakhstan in recent years, despite its poor human rights record.

Another example is Egypt. In 2011, the country experienced a revolution that overthrew the autocratic ruler, President Hosni Mubarak. The international community hoped that Egypt would transition to democracy. However, in 2013, the military ousted the democratically-elected President Mohamed Morsi and installed another autocrat, President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi. Despite its poor human rights record and lack of democracy, Egypt remains a valued partner of the US and other Western countries due to its strategic location in the Middle East.

Case Studies

Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan is a former Soviet republic that gained independence in 1991. Nursultan Nazarbayev, the first and only President of Kazakhstan, has been in power since then. The country is considered authoritarian, with restrictions on freedom of speech, assembly, and the press. Elections are not free or fair, with the ruling party winning by a significant margin in every election since 1994.

Despite its poor human rights record and lack of democracy, Kazakhstan has become an important partner of the US and other Western countries. The country is rich in natural resources, including oil and gas, and is strategically located between Russia and China. The US has supported Kazakhstan's efforts to join the World Trade Organization and has provided assistance to the country in various areas, including education, energy, and security.

Egypt

Egypt is the most populous country in the Arab world and has been a US ally since the Camp David Accords in 1978. For many years, the country was ruled by autocrat Hosni Mubarak, who was overthrown in a popular revolution in 2011. The international community hoped that Egypt would transition to democracy. However, in 2013, the military ousted the democratically-elected President Mohamed Morsi and installed another autocrat, President Abdel Fattah el-Sisi.

Despite its poor human rights record and lack of democracy, Egypt remains a valued partner of the US and other Western countries. The country is strategically located in the Middle East, with access to the Suez Canal and proximity to Israel and other conflict-prone areas. The US provides Egypt with military aid, and the two countries cooperate on counterterrorism efforts in the region.

Conclusion

After analyzing the examples and case studies, it is clear that pursuing geo-strategic interests may be more productive than direct democracy promotion in certain countries, including Belarus. While democracy promotion is important, it is not always the most effective way to achieve US and Western interests. Building alliances and strategic partnerships with countries like Belarus can help counter the influence of authoritarian regimes like Russia and China, promote economic development, and enhance regional security.

In conclusion, the international community should consider pursuing a more nuanced approach to foreign policy, one that takes into account both the need for democracy promotion and the importance of geo-strategic interests. By doing so, we can work towards a world that is more democratic, secure, and prosperous.
