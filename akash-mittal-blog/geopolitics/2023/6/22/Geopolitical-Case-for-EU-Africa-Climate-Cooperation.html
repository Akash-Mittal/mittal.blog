Geopolitical Case for EU-Africa Climate Cooperation

The Story of Two Continents Fighting Climate Change Together

Europe and Africa are two continents that have been affected the most by climate change in recent years. Both regions have experienced extreme weather conditions, rising sea levels, and loss of biodiversity. The need for urgent action is apparent, and the only way to combat climate change is through cooperation and coordination among nations. In this article, we will make a geopolitical case for EU-Africa climate cooperation and how it can benefit both regions in the long run.

Examples of EU-Africa Climate Cooperation

One of the most significant examples of EU-Africa climate cooperation is the Africa Renewable Energy Initiative (AREI). The initiative aims to achieve universal access to renewable energy in Africa by 2030 and to increase the share of renewable energy in the continent's energy mix to 50% by 2050. The EU has pledged €4.8 billion to support the initiative, which will create jobs, reduce greenhouse gas emissions and improve energy access to millions of people in Africa.

Another example is the EU's partnership with the African, Caribbean, and Pacific Group of States (ACP) under the Cotonou Partnership Agreement. The agreement provides a framework for cooperation on climate change, energy, and sustainable development issues. The EU has committed to allocating at least 50% of its climate-related development assistance to sub-Saharan Africa.

Case Studies

The case of the Lake Chad Basin offers an excellent example of how EU-Africa climate cooperation can bring positive change. The lake, which is shared by four countries, has shrunk by over 90% in the last four decades due to climate change and human activities. The EU has been working with the African Union and the Lake Chad Basin Commission to address the issue. The EU has pledged €50 million to support the creation of an emergency fund to finance projects to address the water scarcity, restore the ecosystem, and promote sustainable agriculture in the region.

Another case study is the EU's collaboration with the African Union and the International Organization for Migration (IOM) to support migrants affected by climate change. The project aims to provide legal and logistical assistance to African migrants stranded in North Africa due to environmental degradation and climate change. The EU has allocated €70 million to the project, which will help vulnerable people rebuild their lives and prevent further displacement.

Conclusion

  1. EU-Africa climate cooperation is vital for the long-term success of both regions in addressing the challenges of climate change.
  2. The cooperation can create jobs, enhance energy access, preserve biodiversity, and prevent further displacement of vulnerable people.
  3. The case studies discussed above demonstrate the potential and effectiveness of EU-Africa climate cooperation in solving complex and urgent climate-related issues.

Climate change is a global issue that requires a global response. EU-Africa climate cooperation can be a significant step in the right direction to achieving a sustainable future for both regions.
