Aid Is the Next Battleground Between China and the West

It was a sunny day in the small village of Mbuji-Mayi, in the Democratic Republic of Congo, when a delegation of Chinese officials arrived with a generous offer: to build a new hospital for the local population. The villagers were thrilled. They had been in need of a new medical facility for years, but the Congolese government had never provided the resources to make it happen. So the arrival of the Chinese delegation seemed like a godsend.

However, as the construction project got underway, many of the villagers began to feel that something was amiss. They noticed that most of the work was being done by Chinese laborers who didn't speak the local language and showed little interest in interacting with the Congolese people. They also observed that the project was not creating many jobs for the local population, and that it was being financed through a loan from the Chinese government at a significantly higher interest rate than what the Congo could have obtained from Western lenders.

The hospital eventually opened, but it did little to alleviate the villagers' concerns. Most of the staff were Chinese, and they primarily treated other Chinese people who had moved into the area to work on the project. Meanwhile, the loan payments became a major strain on the Congolese government's budget, and the hospital was rarely used by the local population.

The Rise of Chinese Aid

The story of Mbuji-Mayi's hospital is just one example of the growing competition between China and the West over global aid. For decades, Western governments and institutions like the World Bank have been the dominant providers of development finance to the developing world. However, in recent years, China has emerged as a major player in the aid arena, with a particular focus on infrastructure projects.

In many respects, China's approach to aid is very different from that of the West. Western aid is typically focused on promoting democracy, human rights, and good governance, as well as on providing humanitarian assistance during crises. By contrast, China's aid is often tied to specific projects, such as roads, railways, ports, and other infrastructure that benefit both the recipient country and China's own economic interests. China also tends to finance aid projects through loans rather than grants, which means that recipient countries are often left with a significant debt burden as a result.

Case Studies

Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port

Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port is often cited as an example of China's controversial approach to aid. In 2010, China provided Sri Lanka with a $1.5 billion loan to build the port, which was then operated by a Chinese company. However, the port failed to attract enough business to be financially viable, and Sri Lanka struggled to make the payments on the loan. In 2017, the Sri Lankan government handed over control of the port to China on a 99-year lease in exchange for debt relief.

Tanzania's Standard Gauge Railway

China is currently financing the construction of a $3 billion railway line in Tanzania that will connect the country with neighboring Rwanda and Burundi. The project is expected to significantly boost trade within the region, but it has also faced criticism for its high cost and the fact that most of the work is being done by Chinese companies with little involvement from the local population.

Conclusion

  • The competition between China and the West over aid is likely to intensify in the coming years, as China continues to expand its global influence and Western countries become increasingly inward-looking.
  • The rise of Chinese aid has both positive and negative implications for developing countries. On the one hand, China's focus on infrastructure projects can help to stimulate economic growth and create jobs. On the other hand, the fact that much of China's aid is provided through loans rather than grants means that many countries are left with a significant debt burden that can be difficult to repay.
  • If Western countries want to maintain their position as the dominant providers of aid to the developing world, they will need to adapt their approach to better compete with China. This may mean focusing more on infrastructure projects, and offering more favorable financing terms to recipient countries.
