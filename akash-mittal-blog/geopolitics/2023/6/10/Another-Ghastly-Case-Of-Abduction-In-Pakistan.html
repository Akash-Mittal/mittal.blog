The Disturbing Trend of Forced Conversions and Marriages in Pakistan

The case of Suha, a 14-year-old Hindu girl from Sindh province, is not an isolated incident. In fact, it is just one of the many cases that are being reported in Pakistan where girls from minority communities such as Hindus, Christians, and Sikhs are being abducted, forcefully converted, and married off to Muslim men.

According to a report published by the Movement for Solidarity and Peace in Pakistan, an estimated 1,000 Christian and Hindu girls are abducted, forcibly converted, and married off annually in Pakistan. These numbers are confirmed by several human rights organizations such as the Human Rights Commission of Pakistan and the International Crisis Group.

Case Studies: The Real-Life Consequences of Forced Conversions and Marriages

Such cases have devastating effects on the lives of the girls who are abducted and their families. Here are a few examples of how these forced conversions and marriages have affected the lives of some of the girls:

  • Aarti Kumari: In 2012, Aarti Kumari, a 14-year-old Hindu girl from Sindh province, was abducted from her home in broad daylight. She was forcibly converted to Islam and married off to a Muslim man. Her family lodged an FIR with the police, but no action was taken. They then approached a court, which ordered the police to bring Aarti Kumari to court and ascertain her true wishes. When she was brought to the court, she told the judge that she had converted to Islam and married of her own free will. The judge, who was sympathetic to the abductor's cause, ruled in his favor, and Aarti Kumari was returned to her abductor.
  • Jasmin: Jasmin, a Christian girl from Lahore, was abducted, forcibly converted to Islam, and married off to a Muslim man in 2015. Her family, who were poor and could not afford a lawyer, approached a human rights organization for help. The organization, after facing several obstacles, finally managed to get Jasmin's case heard by a court. The judge in the case, after questioning Jasmin, ruled in her favor, stating that her conversion and marriage were not free and fair and that she should be returned to her family.
  • Reena: Reena, a 13-year-old Christian girl from Faisalabad, was abducted and forcibly converted to Islam in 2017. Her parents, who were poor and could not afford a lawyer, approached a human rights organization for help. The organization, after several attempts, managed to get Reena's case heard by a court. However, the judge in the case ruled in favor of the abductor, stating that Reena had converted and married of her own free will.

The Need for Urgent Action

These case studies illustrate the urgent need for action to be taken to protect the rights of minority communities in Pakistan. The authorities need to take stringent measures to prevent such abductions, forced conversions, and marriages from taking place. Religious leaders also need to be more actively involved in creating awareness about the importance of respecting the religious beliefs of others.

Furthermore, the international community needs to put pressure on the Pakistan government to take concrete steps to protect the rights of its minority communities.

Conclusion

The situation of minority communities in Pakistan is worsening day by day. The practice of forced conversions and marriages is a violation of human rights and must be condemned in the strongest terms possible. The government and civil society need to work together to find a solution to this problem.
