Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

The oceans have always been a vital source of resources and transportation, and therefore, they have been governed by international laws and regulations. However, some countries have been accused of flouting these laws, particularly China. China's increasing presence and activities in the maritime domain have raised questions about whether it is reshaping the global oceans regime.

The Story of China's Maritime Ambitions

China has always had a long coastline, but it is not until recently that it has taken a more assertive stance in the maritime domain. China's maritime ambitions can be traced back to the early 1990s when it started building up its navy and investing in maritime capabilities. In recent years, China has been involved in various maritime disputes in the South China Sea and the East China Sea, where it has claimed sovereignty over vast areas of waters, including islands and reefs that are also claimed by other countries.

China has also been accused of engaging in various illegal activities, such as illegal fishing, piracy, and smuggling, which have threatened the security and stability of the oceans. China's activities in the maritime domain have not gone unnoticed and have sparked concern among other countries, particularly those that have a stake in the oceans.

Case Studies

China's assertiveness in the maritime domain can be seen through various case studies. One of the most notable is the South China Sea dispute, where China claims sovereignty over almost 90% of the sea, which is also claimed by other countries, such as the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, and Brunei. China has built artificial islands and military facilities on some of the disputed islands, which has raised tension and concerns among other countries.

The East China Sea dispute is another example of China's maritime assertiveness. China and Japan have been engaged in a territorial dispute over the Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands, which has escalated in recent years. China has been conducting military drills and sending coast guard vessels to patrol the waters around the islands, which has raised concerns about potential clashes with Japan.

China's illegal fishing activities are another source of concern for countries that have a stake in the oceans. China has been accused of sending fleets of fishing vessels to waters around other countries, such as in the South Pacific and South America, to catch fish illegally. These activities have not only threatened local fish populations but also the livelihoods of local fishermen.

Conclusion

China's increasing presence and activities in the maritime domain have raised questions about its intentions and whether it is reshaping the global oceans regime. While some argue that China is merely exercising its rights under international law, others argue that its activities have violated international law and threatened the stability and security of the oceans.

In conclusion, it is clear that China's maritime ambitions have consequences not only for China but also for other countries that have a stake in the oceans. It is important for all countries to abide by international laws and regulations and work together to promote the sustainable use and management of the oceans for the benefit of all.

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?

Is China Reshaping the Global Oceans Regime?



Image Credits https://foreignpolicy.com/2023/06/10/china-sea-south-east-maritime-claims-law-oceans-us-disputes/

The oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth's surface and are crucial for life on our planet. They provide us with food, transportation, energy, and many other resources. They also play a key role in regulating the climate and absorbing carbon dioxide. However, as human activities expand, the oceans face increasing pressure from pollution, overfishing, climate change, and geopolitical tensions. To address these challenges, the international community has developed a complex legal framework that governs the use of the oceans and their resources. This framework includes various treaties, conventions, codes of conduct, and organizations that aim to promote cooperation, sustainability, and peaceful relations among nations.

However, in recent years, some observers have raised concerns that China, as a rising global power and a major maritime actor, is challenging the existing oceans regime and promoting its own vision and interests. This article explores some of the ways in which China is reshaping the global oceans regime and what implications it may have for the future of the oceans and international relations.

The Rise of China's Blue Economy

China has a long history of seafaring and maritime trade, dating back to the ancient Silk Road. Today, China has become the world's largest maritime trader, with about 90% of its foreign trade being transported by sea. China is also a major fishery nation, consuming more seafood than any other country and exporting a significant amount to other markets. In addition, China has been investing heavily in ocean-related industries such as shipping, offshore oil and gas, marine biotechnology, marine renewable energy, and seabed mining. All these activities are part of China's "blue economy" strategy, which aims to boost economic growth, secure resources, and enhance national security through ocean development.

However, China's blue economy also raises some concerns about its environmental impact, resource depletion, and competition with other countries. For example, China's distant water fishing fleet is accused of depleting fish stocks in the waters of other countries and engaging in illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing practices. China's massive reclamation and militarization of disputed islands and reefs in the South China Sea have also strained regional relations and triggered territorial disputes.

"China's massive reclamation and militarization of disputed islands and reefs in the South China Sea have also strained regional relations and triggered territorial disputes."

China's Maritime Strategy and Diplomacy

China's blue economy is closely linked to its maritime strategy and diplomacy, which aim to protect its sovereignty, security, and strategic interests in the maritime domain. China's maritime strategy is based on the concept of "active defense", which combines defense, offense, and deterrence. China has been modernizing and expanding its navy and coast guard, building artificial islands and airstrips, and developing anti-access and area-denial (A2/AD) capabilities to counter potential threats from other navies, especially the United States. China has also been pursuing a more assertive diplomacy in the oceans, challenging the existing legal norms and promoting its own interests through bilateral and multilateral channels.

For example, China has refused to accept the ruling of the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague, which invalidated China's claims to most of the South China Sea and confirmed the Philippines' rights over certain areas. China argues that the ruling is null and void and insists on its "historical rights" over the disputed waters. China has also been promoting the concept of "blue partnership" and "blue economy corridors" with other countries, especially in the Belt and Road Initiative, which aims to enhance China's connectivity and influence in the global maritime domain.

The Implications for the Global Oceans Regime

China's growing maritime power and ambitions have significant implications for the global oceans regime, which is based on the principles of freedom of navigation, the rule of law, and cooperation. Some of the key implications are:

  • Challenging the legal consensus: China's rejection of the arbitral ruling on the South China Sea challenges the legal framework of the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) and raises doubts about the effectiveness and legitimacy of international law in resolving maritime disputes.
  • Creating regional tensions: China's assertive actions in the South China Sea and East China Sea have increased regional tensions and raised the risk of miscalculation or conflict among neighboring countries and the US.
  • Shaping the norms and standards: China's promotion of its own maritime norms, such as "green development", "digital ocean", and "marine community with shared future", could influence the future direction and content of the global oceans regime, especially as more countries seek to develop their own blue economies.

In conclusion, China's rise as a maritime power and its pursuit of a blue economy strategy are reshaping the global oceans regime. While China's efforts to secure its maritime interests and contribute to ocean governance are commendable, its assertiveness and disregard for international law and norms raise concerns about the sustainability and stability of the oceans and international relations. It remains to be seen whether China's vision of a "marine community with shared future" can be reconciled with the existing legal and institutional frameworks and the interests of other countries and stakeholders.
