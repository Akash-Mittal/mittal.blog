The Asian Way Key to Building Peace

An interesting story to begin with

In 2019, a border dispute between India and China in Ladakh region turned violent leading to the death of troops on both the sides. The situation remained tense for several months until a breakthrough was achieved through diplomatic talks. The resolution of the conflict was credited to the concept of 'Wu-wei' or non-action, a central tenet of Chinese philosophy, which stresses the importance of adapting to the natural course of events rather than trying to impose one's will on others.

The incident highlights the importance of cultural values and norms in conflict resolution. The ability to understand and appreciate the cultural and social context of a conflict is essential in designing effective peacebuilding strategies.

The Asian way of building peace

Asia is home to diverse cultures, religions, and traditions, which have influenced the way conflicts are resolved in the region. The Asian way of building peace emphasizes:

  • Face-saving: Maintaining one's dignity and respect in a conflict situation is essential in Asian culture. Direct confrontation and public humiliation are avoided as far as possible to ensure that both parties maintain their honor.
  • Harmony: Community welfare and consensus-building are given priority over individual interests. The objective is not just to resolve the conflict but to promote a harmonious relationship between the conflicting parties.
  • Non-interference: The principle of non-interference in internal affairs of other countries is a significant feature of the Asian way of building peace. It encourages countries to respect each other's sovereignty and territorial integrity.

Case studies

Several conflicts in Asia have been resolved or managed successfully using the Asian way of building peace. Some notable examples are:

1. Cambodia peace process

The Cambodian peace process in the 1990s demonstrated the effectiveness of the Asian way of building peace. The peace process was led by Indonesia, which utilized the principles of non-interference and consensus-building to find a peaceful solution to the conflict. The Agreement on the Comprehensive Political Settlement of the Cambodian Conflict, which was signed in 1991, provided a framework for the democratic transition of Cambodia.

2. Aceh peace process

The Aceh peace process in Indonesia is another example of the successful application of the Asian way of building peace. The conflict between the Indonesian government and the Free Aceh Movement was resolved peacefully through a negotiated settlement, which focused on community participation and consensus-building. The Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding, which was signed in 2005, provided a framework for the implementation of the peace agreement.

3. Philippines peace process

The Philippines peace process also reflects the importance of cultural values and norms in conflict resolution. The peace process between the Philippine government and the Moro Islamic Liberation front was successful because it took into account the religious and cultural sensitivities of both the parties. The Bangsamoro Organic Law, which was passed in 2018, provided for the establishment of a political entity that reflects the aspirations of the Moro people.

Conclusion

The Asian way of building peace offers valuable insights into conflict resolution and peacebuilding. It emphasizes the importance of cultural values, community participation, and consensus-building in finding peaceful solutions to conflicts. The successful resolution of conflicts in Cambodia, Aceh, and the Philippines provides evidence of the effectiveness of this approach. However, the Asian way of building peace is not a one-size-fits-all solution and needs to be adapted to the specific context of each conflict. A deeper understanding of the cultural and social norms of the conflicting parties is essential in designing effective peacebuilding strategies.
