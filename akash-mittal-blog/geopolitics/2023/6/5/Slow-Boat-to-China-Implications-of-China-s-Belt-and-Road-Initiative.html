Slow Boat to China: Implications of China's Belt and Road Initiative

Imagine you are a farmer in Ethiopia, struggling to make a living from your small plot of land. You have heard about a new railway that is being built near your village, as part of China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI). You are curious about what this might mean for you.

You have heard stories of how other countries have benefited from Chinese investment. Sri Lanka built a new port with Chinese assistance, and it has become a hub for shipping in the Indian Ocean. Pakistan has received billions of dollars in loans to build new roads, power plants, and other infrastructure. And Kazakhstan has seen a surge in trade with China, thanks to new railways and highways.

But you have also heard concerns from some of your fellow farmers. They worry that the Chinese will bring in their own workers and materials, leaving local people out of the picture. They worry that the projects will create pollution and damage the environment. And they worry that the loans will be too expensive to repay, leading to a debt trap.

You are not sure what to think. On the one hand, you see the potential for new markets and opportunities. On the other hand, you fear the consequences of being left behind or overwhelmed by outside forces.

Case Studies: Sri Lanka, Pakistan, and Kazakhstan

Let's take a closer look at three countries that have been heavily involved in China's BRI: Sri Lanka, Pakistan, and Kazakhstan. Each of these countries has experienced both benefits and challenges as a result of their engagement with China.

Sri Lanka

In 2010, Sri Lanka began construction on a new port in Hambantota, with Chinese financing. The project aimed to boost Sri Lanka's trade and strategic position in the Indian Ocean. However, the project faced numerous delays and cost overruns, and Sri Lanka struggled to repay the loans it had taken out from China to finance the project.

In 2017, Sri Lanka ultimately handed over control of the port to a Chinese state-owned company, as part of a deal to reduce its debt to China. This move sparked concerns among some Sri Lankans about Chinese influence in their country, and fears that Sri Lanka was becoming overly dependent on China.

Pakistan

Pakistan has been one of the biggest recipients of Chinese investment under the BRI, with more than $60 billion committed so far. Much of this investment has gone into infrastructure projects, such as new highways, railways, and power plants.

While these projects have created jobs and economic growth, Pakistan has also faced criticism for its debt burden. Some experts have warned that Pakistan may struggle to repay the loans it has taken out from China, which could lead to a debt crisis.

Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan has also been a major player in the BRI, thanks to its location at the crossroads of Europe and Asia. Chinese investment in Kazakhstan has focused on infrastructure projects, such as railways, highways, and oil and gas pipelines.

This investment has helped to boost trade between China and Kazakhstan, and has created new opportunities for Kazakh businesses. However, there are concerns about the environmental impact of some of these projects, as well as the potential for Chinese companies to dominate the local economy.

Conclusion

The Belt and Road Initiative is a complex and ambitious project that has the potential to transform the economies of many countries. However, as we have seen with Sri Lanka, Pakistan, and Kazakhstan, there are also risks and challenges associated with Chinese investment.

So, what should countries do if they want to engage with China under the BRI? Here are three key takeaways:

  1. Be clear about your goals and priorities. What do you hope to achieve through engagement with China, and how will you measure success? It is important to have a clear and realistic plan before embarking on major infrastructure projects.
  2. Ensure transparency and accountability. It is crucial to involve local stakeholders in decision-making, and to ensure that contracts and agreements are transparent and enforceable. This can help to avoid the sort of controversies that have arisen in Sri Lanka and elsewhere.
  3. Protect against debt traps and other risks. Governments should be cautious about taking on too much debt from China, and should ensure that projects are economically viable and sustainable over the long term. They should also be aware of the geopolitical implications of their relationship with China, and should take steps to ensure that they do not become overly dependent on Chinese support.
