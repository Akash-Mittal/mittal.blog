India Hopes for China's Cooperation in Allowing Indian Media Presence

Once upon a time, two neighbors lived side-by-side in harmony. However, the growing differences between the two soon created a communication gap. This unsubstantiated and unreliable communication directly impacted business and diplomatic relations between the two neighboring countries. India and China have a long-standing issue of mistrust, and the presence of Indian media in China has been a topic of constant debate. China's reluctance to allow Indian media may seem like a small issue, but it is a crucial step towards improving the ceaseless tension between the two Asian giants.

Case Studies

In today's globalized world, media is a weapon. Global politics is now shaped by media discourse, and China has made full use of its media prowess to shape public opinion towards its advantage. As a result, Indian media has been denied access to China, making it difficult to present counter-narratives. One such instance was the Doklam standoff. China's decision to deny Indian media access to the site meant that the only narrative available globally was China's version of events. This created a negative perception of India globally, and it took months to counter this narrative.

Another instance was during the COVID-19 pandemic. China was one of the first countries to face the pandemic, and it took a lot of time for China to report this to the world. China's initial lack of transparency and cooperation with the WHO created a negative perception of China worldwide. The Chinese government refused to allow Indian reporters into the country, making it difficult for India to form a narrative based on verified facts.

The Need for Cooperation

It is essential for Indian media to be allowed in China, as it is important to present facts and counter-narratives. The presence of Indian reporters will lead to a better understanding of both countries and, in turn, improve diplomatic relations. Similarly, China will have better access to report what happens in India, creating a balanced understanding of situations.

Furthermore, it is in China's best interest to have Indian media reporting from their country. The presence of Indian reporters will create an accurate representation of India, presenting a clear picture of its economy, culture, politics, and society. This understanding will lead to increased business opportunities between the two countries and improved diplomatic relations.

Conclusion

In conclusion, India's request for cooperation from China is not a demand, but a need. The freedom of press is a fundamental right under international law, and it should be respected. An authoritarian approach towards the media will not create a positive perception of the country. It is essential that China allows the presence of Indian media, so the right message is conveyed by both countries. Mutual trust and respect are required to maintain a healthy relationship between states.
