Pakistan: How Khanism is Fighting Monkeyism

When Imran Khan became the Prime Minister of Pakistan in 2018, he promised to transform the country's political culture and governance system. He called his ideology "Khanism," which he defined as a movement to create a "new Pakistan" based on meritocracy, transparency, and accountability. The opposite of Khanism, according to him, is "Monkeyism," or the politics of nepotism, corruption, and dynastic rule.

Khanism is not just a political slogan or a catchy phrase. It is a comprehensive reform agenda that aims to tackle the root causes of Pakistan's chronic problems, such as poverty, inequality, and terrorism. Khanism has three main components: institutional reform, social justice, and national security.

Institutional Reform

Khanism's first priority is to reform Pakistan's state institutions, such as the judiciary, the police, and the civil service. These institutions are notorious for their inefficiency, corruption, and politicization. To address this, Khan has introduced several measures, including:

  • Appointment of independent and competent judges and police officers;
  • Revival of the National Accountability Bureau (NAB) to tackle corruption;
  • Introduction of e-governance to reduce red tape and bureaucratic delays;
  • Strengthening of local government to empower citizens at the grassroots level.

These reforms have already started to yield results. For example, the NAB has recovered billions of rupees from corrupt politicians and businessmen, including former Prime Minister Nawaz Sharif and his family. The judiciary has also become more independent and active in protecting the rights of citizens, such as in the case of Asia Bibi, a Christian woman who was falsely accused of blasphemy and sentenced to death.

Social Justice

Khanism's second component is to promote social justice and equality in Pakistan. This involves addressing the root causes of poverty, such as lack of education, healthcare, and employment opportunities. Khan has launched several initiatives to achieve this, such as:

  • Establishment of the Ehsaas Program to provide financial assistance to the poor and vulnerable;
  • Introduction of the Kamyab Jawan Program to support youth entrepreneurship and skill development;
  • Enactment of the Right to Education Act to ensure free and compulsory education for all children.

These initiatives are aimed at creating a more just and inclusive society in Pakistan, where everyone has access to basic needs and can fulfill their potential. They also aim to reduce the influence of feudal lords and dynastic politicians who have perpetuated the culture of Monkeyism in Pakistani politics.

National Security

Khanism's third component is to ensure Pakistan's national security and sovereignty. This involves tackling both internal and external threats, such as terrorism, extremism, and foreign interference. Khan has taken several steps to achieve this, such as:

  • Cutting off support to terrorist groups and cracking down on their networks;
  • Establishing closer ties with China, Turkey, and other friendly countries;
  • Strengthening Pakistan's defence capabilities, such as through the development of the Nasr missile system.

These steps have helped to improve Pakistan's security situation, where terrorist attacks and violence have decreased significantly in recent years. They have also helped to enhance Pakistan's international standing and reduce its dependence on Western powers, who have often used aid and sanctions to coerce Pakistan.

Conclusion

Khanism is not a perfect ideology or a panacea for Pakistan's problems. It has its own limitations and challenges, such as resistance from vested interests, lack of resources, and external pressure. However, Khanism represents an important shift in Pakistan's political culture and governance system, where the focus is on merit, transparency, and accountability, rather than on dynastic politics, corruption, and nepotism.

The success of Khanism depends not only on the leadership of Imran Khan but also on the support and participation of the Pakistani people, who have suffered from years of misrule and neglect. If Khanism can succeed in transforming Pakistan's institutions, society, and security, it could become a model for other countries facing similar challenges in the region and beyond.
