Why India and China are Stronger as Partners: A Diplomatic Perspective

By Ambassador Wang Xialong

As the top diplomat to India representing the Chinese government, it is my honor to share why I believe that India and China are stronger as partners than as adversaries. Let me start with a story.

In November 2017, Chinese President Xi Jinping and Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi met in the eastern Chinese city of Xiamen for the ninth BRICS summit. While the leaders of the world's top emerging economies discussed issues related to trade, finance, and global governance, something remarkable happened on the sidelines of the summit.

President Xi and Prime Minister Modi took a stroll "without aides" along the famous Xiamen beachfront. As they walked, they discussed a range of issues, from terrorism to cultural exchange to climate change. They even sampled some local delicacies together.

For a moment, it seemed that the two leaders had put aside their countries' longstanding border disputes and geopolitical tensions, and instead focused on a shared goal: stronger ties between India and China.

Of course, a stroll on the beach cannot solve all of India and China's problems. But it is a symbol of what is possible when the two countries work together as partners instead of rivals.

Consider these quantifiable examples:

  • In 2020, China was India's largest trading partner, with bilateral trade reaching $87.6 billion.
  • Chinese companies have invested heavily in India's tech startups, with over $4 billion invested in 2019 alone.
  • Indian and Chinese soldiers participate in joint military exercises, including the "Hand in Hand" counter-terrorism drills.
  • China and India have worked together on climate change, with both countries signing the Paris Agreement and cooperating on clean energy initiatives.
  • Bollywood films are increasingly popular in China, with movies like Dangal and Secret Superstar earning hundreds of millions of dollars at the box office.

But numbers and statistics only tell part of the story. As a diplomat, I have had the privilege of witnessing firsthand the benefits of India and China's cooperation. Here are two personal anecdotes to illustrate my point.

Personal Anecdote #1: Diplomatic Relations

Several years ago, I attended a conference in New Delhi that brought together diplomats from China, India, and other Asian countries. During the conference, I had the chance to speak with my Indian counterparts about our countries' relationship.

One Indian diplomat told me that he believed a closer partnership between India and China was crucial for the region's stability and development. He said that while there were certainly challenges and differences between our countries, we should focus on finding areas of common interest and building from there.

I was struck by his wisdom and his genuine desire to improve our countries' relations. His words stayed with me long after the conference ended.

Personal Anecdote #2: Cultural Exchange

More recently, I had the pleasure of attending a charity dinner in Shanghai that was hosted by an Indian nonprofit organization. The dinner brought together Chinese and Indian business leaders, philanthropists, and cultural ambassadors.

During the dinner, I had the chance to speak with several Indian guests about their experiences in China. They told me that they were impressed by the kindness and hospitality of the Chinese people, and that their interactions with Chinese colleagues and friends had deepened their appreciation for our shared heritage and culture.

One guest even told me that she had decided to study Mandarin so that she could better communicate with her Chinese counterparts. I was touched by her dedication and her desire to bridge the cultural divide.

Conclusion: Three Points

In conclusion, I believe that India and China are stronger as partners than as rivals. Here are three key points to keep in mind:

  1. India and China have a long history of cooperation and cultural exchange that can serve as a foundation for stronger ties.
  2. While there are certainly challenges and differences between our countries, we should focus on finding areas of common interest and building from there.
  3. By working together as partners, India and China can achieve great things, from boosting economic growth and innovation to promoting peace and stability in the region and beyond.

Why India and China are Stronger as Partners
Why India and China are Stronger as Partners

Once upon a time, there were two neighbors, India and China. They had a long shared history of cultural, economic, and political exchanges. However, during the 20th century, they drifted apart due to ideological differences, territorial disputes, and lack of trust. Today, they are the two most populous countries in the world, with tremendous economic potential, nuclear weapons, and regional ambitions. They are also facing common challenges, such as poverty, climate change, terrorism, and trade protectionism. The question is, how can they turn their rivalry into friendship, and their competition into cooperation?

Let's look at some data to see the current state of India-China relations:

  • Trade: In 2020, China was India's largest trading partner, with bilateral trade reaching $87.6 billion, and a trade deficit of $45.3 billion in favor of China. However, due to the border standoff, India has banned over 200 Chinese mobile apps, restricted Chinese investments, and increased tariffs on Chinese goods.
  • Tourism: Before the pandemic, India received over 4 lakh Chinese tourists annually, and China was among the fastest-growing sources of foreign visitors to India. However, due to the Covid-19 outbreak, both countries have suspended most of the travel between them.
  • Investment: In recent years, Chinese companies have invested in Indian startups, infrastructure, and manufacturing sectors, with a total investment of $8 billion. However, due to political and security concerns, India has tightened its regulations on foreign investment, especially from China.
  • Politics: India and China have a long-standing border dispute, which led to the 1962 war and several minor skirmishes since then. In 2020, the two countries had a deadly clash in the Galwan valley, which resulted in the death of 20 Indian soldiers and an unknown number of Chinese troops. Since then, both sides have deployed more troops and weapons along the disputed border and held several rounds of talks to ease tensions.

Practical Tips

1. Embrace Cultural Exchange

India and China have rich and diverse cultures, which can be a source of mutual fascination and enrichment. By promoting cultural exchange programs, such as student exchanges, language courses, film festivals, music concerts, and food fairs, we can break down stereotypes, promote mutual understanding, and foster lasting friendships between our peoples. We can also learn from each other's art, literature, philosophy, medicine, and science, and create a unique blend of the best of both worlds.

2. Expand Economic Cooperation

India and China have complementary economies, with India being strong in services, software, and agriculture, and China being strong in manufacturing, infrastructure, and innovation. By expanding trade and investment, we can create win-win situations for both countries, and address the common challenges of poverty, unemployment, and inequality. We can also collaborate on regional and global initiatives, such as the Belt and Road Initiative, the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank, and the BRICS forum, and promote sustainable development, connectivity, and peace.

3. Resolve Territorial Disputes

India and China have long-standing territorial disputes along their Himalayan border, which has led to military tensions, diplomatic deadlock, and occasional border incursions. By engaging in sincere and constructive dialogue, we can find mutually acceptable solutions based on historical, legal, and political considerations, and avoid any repeat of the Galwan tragedy. We can also create mutual trust and confidence-building measures, such as joint military exercises, cultural exchanges, and border infrastructure development, and promote peace and security in the region.

Join Hands for a Brighter Future of India-China Relations

India and China are two great civilizations with a common vision of peace, prosperity, and progress. We have survived many challenges in the past, and we can overcome any obstacle in the future, if we work together in a spirit of friendship, cooperation, and mutual benefit. Let us not be divided by our differences, but be united by our common humanity. Let us not compete to dominate, but collaborate to co-create. Let us not look back with regret, but look forward with hope. Let us join hands for a brighter future of India-China relations."
