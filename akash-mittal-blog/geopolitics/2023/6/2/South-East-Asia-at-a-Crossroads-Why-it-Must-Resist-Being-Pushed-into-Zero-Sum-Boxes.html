South East Asia at a Crossroads: Why it Must Resist Being Pushed into Zero Sum Boxes

When I was living in Southeast Asia, I had the opportunity to visit a small village near the Thai-Cambodian border. The village was home to both Thai and Cambodian families who lived side-by-side and shared resources and traditions. Despite their differences in language and culture, they had found a way to coexist peacefully and sustainably.

This village is just one example of the many ways in which Southeast Asia resists being pushed into zero sum boxes. From economic cooperation through the ASEAN Free Trade Area, to cross-border educational exchange programs, to shared environmental protections, Southeast Asia has demonstrated a commitment to collaborative solutions that benefit all parties involved.

One personal anecdote I can share is from my time working with a group of young entrepreneurs in Indonesia. They were developing a social enterprise that aimed to provide job training and placement for marginalized youth. To achieve their goals, they partnered with a local NGO that had similar objectives, but different strengths and resources. Together, they were able to create a sustainable program that addressed multiple social issues in the community.

Practical Tips

  • Collaborate with others who have different perspectives, strengths, and resources to find innovative solutions to shared problems.
  • Be open to learning from other cultures and traditions, and find common ground that can be built upon.
  • Advocate for policies and programs that prioritize cooperation over competition, and aim to benefit all parties involved.

  1. Southeast Asia is at a crossroads, facing increasing pressures from global and regional powers.
  2. To resist being pushed into zero sum boxes, Southeast Asia must continue to prioritize collaboration and shared benefits.
  3. Through practical tips and personal anecdotes, Southeast Asia can build sustainable and peaceful relationships that benefit all parties involved.
