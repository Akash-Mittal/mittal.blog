The Long-Term Impact of Conflict in Sudan on China

Once upon a time, there was a Chinese company named PetroChina that invested heavily in Sudan's oil industry. PetroChina built a 1,000-mile pipeline that transported oil from the southern part of Sudan to the Port of Sudan. They had dreams of making massive profits, but their investments came at a steep cost.

Sudan has been in a state of intermittent conflict since the 1980s, and the situation has only worsened in recent years. The conflict has claimed the lives of countless people, displaced millions, and wreaked havoc on the country's infrastructure. As China's investments in Sudan continue to be affected by this conflict, the long-term impact on China is becoming clearer.

The Quantifiable Impact

China's investments in Sudan have made it the country's largest trading partner. According to data from the Chinese Ministry of Commerce, bilateral trade between China and Sudan reached $13.77 billion in 2019. However, with the ongoing conflict, China has seen a decline in profits from its investments in Sudan. In 2018, China's CNPC, the largest shareholder in Sudan's oil industry, reported a five percent year-on-year drop in production.

Beyond the financial costs, China has also been impacted by the conflict in Sudan in other ways. The unrest has led to kidnappings and killings of Chinese citizens working in the country. For example, in 2012, two workers from a Chinese company were kidnapped. One died during the rescue mission, and the other was held for 10 months before being released.
