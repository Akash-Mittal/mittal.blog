China's Reporters in India to Drop to Zero: An Unfolding Story of Mutual Expulsions

It all started with a border dispute in the western Himalayas – an area known as the Galwan Valley – between India and China in June 2020. The dispute, which resulted in the loss of lives on both sides, escalated the tensions between the two world powers.

As the diplomatic relations between India and China soured, mutual accusations of espionage and media manipulation added fuel to the fire. Following reciprocal expulsions, China says its reporters in India are about to drop to zero. This story is all about the unfolding events that led to this outcome.

Mutual Expulsions

In September 2020, two Chinese journalists working for Xinhua news agency were expelled from India allegedly for carrying out activities beyond their journalistic brief.

In March 2021, India expelled another three Chinese journalists, also accusing them of indulging in activities beyond their journalistic brief. Following the expulsions, China says that its reporters in India are about to drop to zero.

These expulsions have effectively reduced the media presence of both countries in the other. The two neighbors are now left with a handful of correspondents who are faced with increasing challenges not only in reporting from the field but also in accessing information from their sources.

Practical Tips for Journalists Reporting in Conflict Zones

  • Always have a backup plan in place in case of emergencies or if you have to quickly evacuate a conflict zone
  • Understand the local customs and traditions to build a rapport with local sources and avoid cultural misunderstandings
