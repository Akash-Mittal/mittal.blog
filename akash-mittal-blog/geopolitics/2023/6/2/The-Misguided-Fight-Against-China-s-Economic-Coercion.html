The Misguided Fight Against China's Economic Coercion

Once upon a time, there was a small country that relied heavily on exports to a neighboring giant. The giant decided to flex its economic muscles and began putting pressure on the small country to comply with its demands. In response, the small country rallied its allies and tried to fight back, but it only ended up hurting itself more.

This story is not a fairytale, but a real-life situation that many countries are facing today. China's economic coercion on its trading partners is a controversial and complex issue, but the West's response to it has been misguided and misleading.

The Problem with the West's Response

The West's approach to dealing with China's economic coercion is often based on retaliation and protectionism. The US, for example, has imposed tariffs on Chinese goods, restricted Chinese investment in certain industries, and blacklisted Chinese companies. Similarly, the UK, Australia, and Canada have taken measures to limit Chinese investment, screen Chinese acquisitions, and ban Huawei from their 5G networks.

While these actions may seem like a tough stance against China, they are misguided for several reasons. First, they are unlikely to change China's behavior because China is not easily intimidated or coerced. Second, they harm the West's own interests by disrupting supply chains, raising consumer prices, and reducing market access. Third, they push China closer to other countries like Russia and Iran, which undermines the West's efforts to isolate those countries.

The negative impact of the West's fight against China's economic coercion can be seen in various sectors and regions.

  • The US-China trade war has cost the US economy at least $57 billion and has reduced US exports to China by 22%.
  • The UK's restrictions on Huawei have delayed the country's 5G rollout and will cost telecom companies up to Â£2 billion.
  • Australian wine exports to China have dropped by 96% after China imposed tariffs on them.
  • The EU's ban on Chinese solar panels has led to a 30% decrease in solar installations in Europe.
  • The Philippines' decision to cancel a $1.5 billion loan from China has led to delays in infrastructure projects and increased borrowing costs.

The Alternative Approach

Instead of fighting China's economic coercion with protectionism, the West should adopt a more nuanced and strategic approach that focuses on engagement, diversification, and rules-based cooperation.

Engagement

The West should engage with China on issues of mutual interest and concern, such as climate change, pandemic response, and regional security. By engaging with China, the West can build trust, create dialogue, and find common ground.

Diversification

The West should diversify its trade and investment partnerships to reduce its reliance on China and create alternative sources of growth. This can be done by expanding trade with emerging markets, building regional partnerships, and investing in innovation and technology.

Rules-based Cooperation

The West should work with China to strengthen the rules-based international system and promote fair competition, transparency, and accountability. This can be done by reforming existing institutions, creating new frameworks, and engaging civil society and private sector stakeholders.

In Conclusion

The West's fight against China's economic coercion is misguided and misleading. Instead of resorting to protectionism and retaliation, the West should adopt a more nuanced and strategic approach that focuses on engagement, diversification, and rules-based cooperation.
