India's Best Bet in Pakistan

India's relationship with Pakistan has always been a rocky one. Over the years, there have been multiple attempts at peace talks and diplomatic efforts. However, the sad truth is that both sides have failed to establish a healthy relationship. But, as they say, hope springs eternal. There is a need for a new approach towards Pakistan and India's best bet lies in building stronger trade and cultural ties. Let's explore how:

Historical Context

India and Pakistan were once the same country. However, after India's independence in 1947, Pakistan was formed out of Muslim-majority regions. Ever since then, there have been tensions between the two countries. From wars to terrorism, the conflict between India and Pakistan has taken a toll on both nations; socially, economically, and politically. However, despite the harsh realities, there have been some positive steps on both sides:

  • In 1986, India and Pakistan launched a joint project to eradicate malaria in their respective countries. This was the first-ever joint project between the two countries and demonstrated the potential for collaboration in the future.
  • In 2003, India and Pakistan signed a ceasefire agreement along the Line of Control (LoC) in the troubled region of Kashmir. This agreement has been mostly successful with only occasional skirmishes and has laid the groundwork for further peace talks.
  • In 2015, Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi made a surprise visit to Pakistan and met with his Pakistani counterpart, Nawaz Sharif. The meeting was hailed as a major breakthrough in the relationship between the two countries.

Trade and Investments

India and Pakistan have a combined population of over 1.5 billion people. Therefore, there is a huge potential for bilateral trade and investments between the two countries. But, currently, the trade relationship is far from ideal. In fact, Pakistan accounts for only 0.83% of India's total trade. However, there is an opportunity to change this:

  • In 2018, Pakistan opened the Kartarpur Corridor, which allowed Indian pilgrims to visit a revered Sikh shrine in Pakistan. This was a significant step in the right direction and demonstrated the value of people-to-people contacts.
  • There is a growing demand in both countries for agricultural products, textiles, and pharmaceuticals. By improving trade ties, both sides can benefit from the interconnectedness and increased economic prosperity.
  • In 2020, India announced a $500 million line of credit for development in Pakistan, which includes projects in the power sector and water conservation.

Education and Cultural Exchange

Education and cultural exchange are powerful tools for building enduring relationships. By encouraging academic exchanges and cultural programs between India and Pakistan, both sides can promote mutual understanding and respect. Here are some examples:

  • The Indus Waters Treaty of 1960 was a landmark agreement between India and Pakistan that divided the water supply of six rivers between the two countries. This treaty was the result of a cooperative effort between experts and policymakers from both countries.
  • In 2018, a Pakistani student from Lahore University of Management Sciences won the Rhodes Scholarship to study at Oxford University. This scholarship is widely considered one of the most prestigious academic awards in the world.
  • Cultural exchange programs such as music concerts, art exhibitions, and food festivals can help bridge the gap between the two countries and foster a sense of common humanity.

Conclusion

India and Pakistan have a long history of conflict, but that does not mean that the future has to be the same. India's best bet in Pakistan lies in building stronger trade and cultural ties. By focusing on economic and cultural engagement, both countries can build enduring relationships and work towards stability and prosperity in the region. In conclusion, here are the top three takeaway points:

  1. Trade and investment are crucial for both India and Pakistan's economic growth. By improving bilateral trade relations, both countries can benefit economically, politically, and socially.
  2. Education and cultural exchange can foster mutual understanding and respect. By encouraging academic exchanges and cultural programs, both countries can promote a sense of common humanity.
  3. The past should not define the future. India and Pakistan have a shared future and a common destiny. It is up to the leaders of both countries to embrace this reality and work towards a peaceful and prosperous future.
