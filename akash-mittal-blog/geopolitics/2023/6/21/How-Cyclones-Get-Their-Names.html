How Cyclones Get Their Names



Image Credits https://www.wionews.com/photos/as-cyclone-biparjoy-threatens-india-and-pakistan-lets-find-out-how-cyclones-get-their-names-603938

Imagine a powerful weather system, brewing and strengthening over the vast expanse of the ocean. It's fierce, unpredictable and capable of wreaking havoc on a massive scale. It deserves a name worthy of its magnitude, don't you think so?

This is where the practice of naming cyclones comes in. It not only makes it easier for meteorologists and scientists to track and study these weather phenomena, but it also helps raise public awareness and preparedness for potential disasters.

Let's take a closer look at how cyclones are named and the significance behind it.

Historical Background

The practice of naming storms and hurricanes has been around for centuries, and it was only in the last century that meteorologists started giving official names to tropical cyclones.

In the beginning, storms were named after the saints on whose feast days they occurred. But as the number of storms increased, it became impractical to keep track of them using this method. In 1953, the United States Weather Bureau started using female names for storms, then male and female names were alternated in 1979.

Current Practice

Nowadays, different regions of the world follow different naming conventions for cyclones.

In the Atlantic basin (which includes the Caribbean Sea and the Gulf of Mexico), storms are named by the National Hurricane Center. They have a pre-determined list of 21 names for each season, and the names are assigned in alphabetical order, skipping Q, U, X, Y and Z. If there are more than 21 storms, the remaining ones are named after letters of the Greek alphabet.

In the Western Pacific, cyclones are named by the Japan Meteorological Agency. They use a much longer list of names, which is revised every few years. The names are short and easy to pronounce, and they typically have a particular meaning in Japanese culture.

In the Indian Ocean, cyclones are named by the Indian Meteorological Department. They have a set of 64 names for storms that originate from the Bay of Bengal and the Arabian Sea. The names are chosen alphabetically, with the first cyclone of the year given a name beginning with the letter A, the second one with the letter B, and so on.

The Significance of Naming Cyclones

Naming cyclones serves several purposes, apart from making it easier to identify and track them. Here are three key benefits:

  1. Raising Public Awareness: When a cyclone has a name, it becomes more tangible and real to people. They are more likely to take it seriously and prepare for it in advance.
  2. Facilitating Communication: When different agencies and organizations refer to a cyclone by the same name, it eliminates confusion and reduces the chances of errors in reporting and planning.
  3. Recognizing Cultures: Cyclone names are often chosen to reflect the cultural and linguistic diversity of the regions affected by them. In this way, they become a way of celebrating and acknowledging the rich heritage of these places.

Case Studies

Cyclone Amphan

Cyclone Amphan was named by Thailand in 2004, and it hit India and Bangladesh in May 2020. It was one of the most powerful cyclones to hit the region in recent years, causing extensive damage to homes, crops and infrastructure, as well as loss of life.

Cyclone Fani

Cyclone Fani was named by Bangladesh in 2004, and it hit India and Bangladesh in May 2019. It was also a powerful cyclone, but thanks to early warning systems and effective preparedness measures, the loss of life was minimized. However, it still caused extensive damage to homes and infrastructure, particularly in the coastal areas.

Conclusion

Naming cyclones is not just a matter of convenience; it carries significant cultural, social and practical implications. By giving these weather systems a name, we are acknowledging their power and unpredictability, and we are also showing respect for the regions and communities that are affected by them.

If you live in an area prone to cyclones, make sure you are aware of the naming conventions and the preparedness measures recommended by your local authorities.
