Interest vs Alignment: SA's Place in the Geopolitical Maelstrom



Image Credits https://www.news24.com/citypress/business/interest-vs-alignment-sas-place-in-the-geopolitical-maelstrom-20230611

It was a sunny day in Johannesburg, South Africa, when I met a Nigerian businessman at a local coffee shop. As we sipped our cappuccinos, we chatted about business, politics, and the state of the world. Eventually, our conversation turned to geopolitics, and my new friend shared his perspective on South Africa's position in the global arena.

"South Africa is caught in a difficult place," he said. "On the one hand, it wants to align itself with other African countries and promote the continent's interests. On the other hand, it wants to maintain its relationships with the West and protect its own economic interests."

My friend's words stuck with me, and I began to delve deeper into the issue. How does South Africa balance its various interests and alignments? And what does this mean for the country's future in the geopolitical maelstrom?

Examples of South Africa's Interests and Alignments

South Africa's interests are numerous and complex, ranging from economic growth and development to social justice and human rights. However, two of its most salient interests are:

  • Oil and Gas Resources: South Africa has oil and gas reserves, but it relies heavily on imports to meet its energy needs. Therefore, it has a vested interest in the stability of global energy markets and in cultivating relationships with energy producers and consumers.
  • Regional Influence: South Africa considers itself a leader in Africa and seeks to play a prominent role in regional affairs. This includes promoting trade, security, and development initiatives and working with the African Union and other organizations to address regional challenges.

At the same time, South Africa's primary alignments can be broken down into three categories:

  • The West: South Africa has historically had close ties with the West, particularly with the United States and the United Kingdom. These relationships are based on shared values such as democracy, human rights, and capitalist economic systems.
  • China: South Africa has been working to deepen its economic and political ties with China, which is a major trading partner and investor. This relationship is based on mutual economic interests and a shared vision of a more multipolar world.
  • Africa: South Africa also seeks to strengthen its relationships with other African countries and promote the continent's interests. This includes supporting regional organizations such as the African Union and participating in peacekeeping missions.

These interests and alignments are not always compatible, and South Africa must navigate a complex and shifting geopolitical landscape in order to promote its own objectives and contribute to the greater good.

Case Studies: South Africa's Role in International Affairs

Case Study 1: BRICS

The BRICS group of emerging economies (Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa) is an example of how South Africa can align itself strategically with other countries while advancing its own interests. South Africa was invited to join BRICS in 2010, and since then it has played a significant role in promoting the group's agenda.

One key area where South Africa has contributed is in the establishment of the New Development Bank, which provides funding for infrastructure and sustainable development projects in BRICS countries and other developing nations. South Africa also supports initiatives to strengthen trade and investment among BRICS countries and to reform international financial institutions to make them more representative of developing countries.

Case Study 2: Syria and the UN Security Council

South Africa's decision to abstain from a UN Security Council resolution on Syria in 2012 highlights the challenges of balancing multiple interests and alignments. The resolution called for the removal of Syrian President Bashar al-Assad and was supported by the United States, the United Kingdom, and other Western countries. However, South Africa believed that the resolution would lead to regime change and violate Syria's sovereignty.

At the same time, South Africa faced pressure from other African countries to support the resolution. In the end, South Africa chose to abstain from the vote, which angered some of its Western allies and strained its relationships in both the West and Africa.

Conclusion: Three Key Points

  1. South Africa's interests and alignments are complex and multifaceted, and it must navigate a complex and shifting geopolitical landscape in order to promote its own objectives.
  2. South Africa has a vested interest in promoting regional stability and development, as well as protecting its own economic interests and cultivating relationships with other global powers.
  3. The examples and case studies discussed here illustrate the challenges and opportunities of South Africa's position in the geopolitical maelstrom.
