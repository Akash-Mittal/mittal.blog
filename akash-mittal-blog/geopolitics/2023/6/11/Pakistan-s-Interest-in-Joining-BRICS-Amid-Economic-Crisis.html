Pakistan's Interest in Joining BRICS: Amid Economic Crisis



Image Credits https://www.business-standard.com/world-news/amid-economic-crisis-pakistan-expresses-interest-in-joining-brics-report-123061000412_1.html

Amid a deepening economic crisis and stalled talks with the International Monetary Fund to get loans, Pakistan has expressed its interest in joining the BRICS. What are the reasons behind this move, and what could it mean for Pakistan's economy?

A Brief Introduction to BRICS

BRICS is an acronym for Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa, five countries that are known for their fast-growing economies and emerging markets. Originally called "BRIC" until South Africa joined in 2010, BRICS was formed as a way for these countries to cooperate and promote trade and investment among themselves.

Since its creation, BRICS has become a powerful force in the global economy, representing nearly half of the world's population and a quarter of its GDP. Its primary focus has been on developing countries, and it has been actively working to create a more equitable and inclusive world economy.

Why is Pakistan Interested in Joining BRICS?

The answer to this question is two-fold: firstly, Pakistan sees BRICS as a way to expand its trade and investment opportunities, and secondly, it hopes that joining BRICS will help it secure much-needed loans from international institutions.

Trade and Investment Opportunities

Pakistan's economy has been struggling for some time now, with weak growth, high inflation, and a huge external debt burden. In this context, joining BRICS could be a game-changer for the country, giving it access to a much larger market and new investment opportunities.

China is already Pakistan's largest trading partner, but joining BRICS would allow Pakistan to strengthen trade ties with other members of the group as well. For example, Brazil and South Africa are both major exporters of commodities such as agriculture and minerals, which Pakistan could benefit from. Moreover, India is a big consumer market, and Pakistan could potentially tap into this market if it improves its political relations with India.

From the perspective of investment, joining BRICS could help Pakistan attract more foreign direct investment (FDI) and diversify its sources of funding. While China has been a major investor in Pakistan's infrastructure projects, such as the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC), other BRICS members could potentially invest in different sectors, such as technology and manufacturing.

Potential Access to Loans

Pakistan's economy has been heavily reliant on loans from international institutions such as the IMF, but talks with the IMF have been stalled recently due to disagreements over economic policies. Given this situation, Pakistan is looking for alternative sources of funding, and joining BRICS could be one way to do that.

BRICS has created its own development bank, the New Development Bank, which is aimed at financing infrastructure and sustainable development projects in developing countries. Pakistan could potentially apply for loans from this bank if it joins BRICS and meets the bank's eligibility criteria. Additionally, BRICS countries have recently expressed interest in creating a common cryptocurrency, which could provide another alternative means of financing for Pakistan.

Implications for Pakistan's Economy

Joining BRICS could be a real game-changer for Pakistan's economy, but it's not without its risks and challenges. Here are three key implications that should be considered:

Challenges in Implementation

Pakistan's path to joining BRICS won't be easy, as it will have to meet certain criteria to become a member. For example, the country will need to comply with the group's economic policies and regulations, such as adopting sound macroeconomic policies, liberalizing its trade regime, and promoting competition and innovation. Moreover, it will have to demonstrate its commitment to BRICS' values and principles, such as promoting sustainable development, democracy, and human rights.

Risks of Dependency

Joining BRICS could also increase Pakistan's dependency on China, which is already its biggest benefactor. If Pakistan becomes too reliant on China for trade and investment, it could potentially face problems if China's economy slows down or if political relations between the two countries sour. Similarly, taking on more loans from Chinese and/or BRICS institutions could increase Pakistan's debt burden and put a strain on its finances in the long run.

Geopolitical Implications

Finally, joining BRICS could have geopolitical implications for Pakistan, as it will strengthen its ties with countries like Russia, India, and Brazil, which have different relationships with Pakistan. For example, India and Pakistan have long-standing tensions over the disputed region of Jammu and Kashmir, and Pakistan's close ties with China have sometimes raised concerns in Russia.

Conclusion

Pakistan's interest in joining BRICS is not surprising, given its economic struggles and the potential benefits of joining a powerful economic bloc. However, joining BRICS is not without its risks and challenges, and Pakistan will need to carefully weigh these before making a final decision.

Ultimately, whether Pakistan joins BRICS or not will depend on several factors, including its ability to meet the group's membership criteria, its relationship with China, and its geopolitical interests. It remains to be seen if Pakistan will take this path, but one thing is clear: joining BRICS could be a game-changer for Pakistan's economy if it plays its cards right.
