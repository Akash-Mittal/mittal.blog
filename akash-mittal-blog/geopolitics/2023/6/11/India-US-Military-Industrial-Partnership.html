Is India-US Military Industrial Partnership on the Verge of a Breakthrough?

The Paradigm of Cooperation is Headed for Transformation

On July 18, 2021, the Indian Navy inducted the first batch of indigenously built Advanced Light Helicopters (ALH) Naval Utility Helicopters (NUH) into its fleet. The aircraft, built by state-run Hindustan Aeronautics Limited (HAL), marks a significant milestone in India's efforts towards self-reliance in defence. What's interesting to note here is that the engines powering these helicopters have been manufactured by the American company, Honeywell International. This collaboration between Indian and American defence firms not only highlights the growing synergy between the two countries but also points towards the immense potential for a breakthrough in the India-US military-industrial partnership.

The Current Scenario: Qualitative and Quantitative Transformation

Over the years, India and the US have strengthened their defence ties through various initiatives, such as the Defence Technology and Trade Initiative (DTTI) signed in 2015, the Logistics Exchange Memorandum of Agreement (LEMOA) signed in 2016, and the Communications Compatibility and Security Agreement (COMCASA) signed in 2018. These agreements have provided the necessary framework for the two countries to collaborate and co-produce defence equipment, with a focus on promoting technology transfer and indigenisation.

The India-US defence partnership has witnessed a qualitative transformation, with a shift from a buyer-seller relationship to a strategic partnership. The two countries have made progress in areas such as joint military exercises, intelligence sharing, counter-terrorism cooperation, and defence research and development. The US has emerged as a key supplier of defence equipment to India and has also supported India's bid for membership in various international forums, such as the Nuclear Suppliers Group and the Wassenaar Arrangement.

On the quantitative front, the India-US defence partnership has witnessed a significant increase in trade and investment, with the US becoming one of the top defence suppliers to India. According to a report by the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), between 2016 and 2020, India was the second-largest importer of major arms, with the US accounting for 16% of India's total arms imports. The bilateral defence trade between India and the US stood at $21.5 billion in 2020, with the potential to reach $40 billion by 2030.

Case Studies: India-US Collaborations in Defence Production

The India-US defence cooperation has yielded some significant results, with collaborations in defence production across various sectors, such as aerospace, defence electronics, and naval systems.

Aerospace

One of the most successful collaborations between India and the US has been in the aerospace sector, with the joint production of the C-130J Super Hercules transport aircraft by Lockheed Martin and Tata Advanced Systems Limited (TASL). TASL is responsible for manufacturing the airframe components for the aircraft, while Lockheed Martin provides the avionics, engines, and other systems. The joint venture has resulted in the indigenisation of the C-130J aircraft in India, with the establishment of a production line in Hyderabad, creating jobs and promoting local expertise in the aerospace industry.

Defence Electronics

India and the US have also collaborated in the field of defence electronics, with the joint production of the AN/TPQ-37 Firefinder radar system by Lockheed Martin and Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL). The system is used by the Indian Army for artillery detection and surveillance and has been instrumental in improving border security and counter-terrorism operations.

Naval Systems

The India-US collaboration in the naval systems sector has been growing, with the joint production of the MH-60R Seahawk multi-role naval helicopters by Lockheed Martin and Tata Advanced Systems Limited. The helicopters are expected to strengthen the Indian Navy's anti-submarine warfare capabilities and provide enhanced situational awareness.

Conclusion: Poised for a Breakthrough?

The India-US military-industrial partnership has come a long way, with significant progress in areas such as defence production, trade and investment, and strategic cooperation. The two countries have overcome several challenges, including differences over intellectual property rights and export control regulations, to establish a strong foundation for collaboration.

However, the potential for further advancements in the India-US defence partnership is immense, with opportunities for joint development and production of advanced technologies and systems. The two countries need to prioritise areas such as cybersecurity, artificial intelligence, and space technology, which are critical in ensuring national security and global stability.

India and the US must also continue to build trust and confidence in each other, by promoting transparency and open dialogue, and by addressing concerns related to technology transfer and intellectual property rights. A more proactive and forward-looking approach is essential to tap into the vast potential of the India-US military-industrial partnership and take it to the next level.
