The Story behind Bolivia's Relation with China

Bolivia is a country located in South America that has always been trying to build better alliances with other countries to enhance its economy. In 2018, the Chinese government offered the Bolivian government a credit line of $1.5 billion to invest in infrastructure projects, which was welcomed by Bolivian President Evo Morales. Since then, the relation between the two countries has been growing stronger.

However, the recent rumors about Bolivia trading in Yuan instead of Dollar have raised some doubts among economists and the general population. Let's explore the political and economic scenarios of Bolivia to understand better why this is not a straightforward decision.

Bolivia's Political Scenario

Bolivia is a plurinational state that consists of 36 indigenous nations and a mixed race population. The country has recently gone through some political changes that have affected its relations with other countries. In 2019, former President Evo Morales was forced to resign after protests against his re-election for a fourth term. The interim president, Jeanine Anez, assumed power and was supposed to hold new elections, but her government has been criticized for its handling of the COVID-19 pandemic and human rights abuses.

This political instability has made it difficult for Bolivia to negotiate with other countries. China, in this scenario, is willing to lend Bolivia the money it needs to invest in infrastructure and gain access to Bolivia's natural resources. However, Bolivia doesn't want to rely too much on China and risk its sovereignty.

Bolivia's Economic Scenario

Bolivia's economy heavily relies on its exports of natural resources such as gas, minerals, and agricultural products. The country has been struggling to diversify its exports and develop other industries to reduce its dependence on natural resources. In this context, China's investment in infrastructure projects is crucial to Bolivia's economic development.

However, Bolivia's economy is currently facing a scarcity of dollars, which is affecting its ability to import goods and services. This is where the rumors of trading in Yuan come from. Yuan is the currency of China, and Bolivia could reduce its reliance on dollars if it starts trading in Yuan. However, many economists doubt the feasibility of this decision.

Case Studies

There are some case studies that can help us understand the risks and benefits of trading in Yuan.

  1. Pakistan: Pakistan started trading in Yuan in 2019 to reduce its reliance on dollars. However, the decision has not been very beneficial for Pakistan's economy as its trade deficit with China has increased, and Pakistan has not been able to keep up with the value of Yuan as compared to the dollar.
  2. Vietnam: Vietnam is one of the largest trade partners of China, and it has been trading in Yuan since 2015. However, Vietnam also faces the risk of inflation and currency fluctuations due to the volatility of Yuan.

Conclusion: Key Points to Consider

  • China's investment in Bolivia's infrastructure is crucial to the country's economic development.
  • The political instability in Bolivia makes it difficult to negotiate with other countries, but at the same time, Bolivia needs to be cautious not to rely too much on China and risk its sovereignty.
  • Trading in Yuan could reduce Bolivia's reliance on dollars, but many economists doubt the feasibility of this decision due to the risks of inflation and currency fluctuations.
