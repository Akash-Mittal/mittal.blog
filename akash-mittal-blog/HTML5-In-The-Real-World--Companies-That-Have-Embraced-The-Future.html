HTML5 In The Real World: Companies That Have Embraced The Future

Once upon a time, there was a company called Netflix. They needed a way to stream their movies and TV shows, but didn't want to rely on any external plugins, such as Adobe Flash or Microsoft Silverlight. And so they turned to HTML5. They built a custom player using HTML5 video and JavaScript, and now millions of people watch Netflix on their computers and mobile devices every day, without having to install any extra software.

Netflix is just one example of a company that has fully embraced HTML5. But why is this technology so important, and what are some other companies that have followed in Netflix's footsteps?

First of all, HTML5 is the latest version of the HTML programming language that underlies the entire Internet. It offers many new features and improvements over previous versions, including better video and audio playback, advanced 3D graphics, offline storage, and mobile device support. This means that websites and web applications built using HTML5 can be faster, smoother, and more interactive than ever before.

One company that has taken full advantage of these benefits is the New York Times. They created a "Snow Fall" article that used HTML5 to tell a stunningly immersive story about a deadly avalanche in Washington state. The article features full-screen photos and videos, animated graphics, and even a custom soundtrack. It's a great example of how HTML5 can be used to create multimedia experiences that are both informative and engaging.

Another company that has embraced HTML5 is Grooveshark, a music streaming service that competes with the likes of Spotify and Pandora. They built a brand-new web app that uses HTML5's audio API to provide seamless playback of millions of songs. The app works in all modern web browsers, and even supports basic media controls from your keyboard or headphones.

Of course, not all companies have jumped on the HTML5 bandwagon just yet. Some still rely on older technologies like Flash, which has been criticized for being slow, buggy, and insecure. But as more and more devices, especially mobile ones, move away from Flash and towards HTML5, it's likely that these companies will start to make the switch as well.

In conclusion, HTML5 is the future of the web, and the companies that have already adopted it are reaping the rewards. From Netflix and the New York Times to Grooveshark and beyond, HTML5 is helping to create web experiences that are faster, smoother, and more interactive than ever before. So if you're not already using HTML5 in your own website or web app, it's time to get on board!
