How to Have Difficult Conversations About Kids' Mental Health

As a concerned parent, it can be difficult to start a conversation with your child about their mental health. The stigma surrounding mental health often prevents individuals from seeking the help they need. However, this conversation is essential in addressing any mental health concerns your child may have. Let me share with you the story of a mother who realized the importance of having this conversation.

Jennifer was a busy working mom, but she noticed that her daughter Emily was becoming more and more withdrawn. Emily's grades were slipping, and she seemed to have lost interest in activities she once enjoyed. Jennifer tried to talk to Emily about it, but she struggled to find the right words.

One day, Jennifer finally mustered up the courage to have a difficult conversation with her daughter about her mental health. Emily opened up about how she was feeling overwhelmed, anxious, and depressed. Jennifer was able to connect her to a mental health professional, and they worked together to find a treatment plan that worked for Emily. Through open communication, Emily was able to get the help she needed to overcome her mental health struggles.

  2. 1 in 5 children and youth will experience a mental health disorder
  3. 64% of young people do not access mental health care when they need it

Practical Tips for Having the Conversation

  1. Find the Right Time and Place: Choose a time and place where both you and your child feel comfortable. Avoid having the conversation during high-stress periods or when either of you is upset.
  2. Listen without Judgement: Give your child a chance to talk openly, without interrupting them or making them feel defensive. Acknowledge their feelings and validate them.
  3. Provide a Plan of Action: Once you've talked about your child's feelings, identify steps you can take together. This might include finding a mental health professional, making lifestyle changes, or seeking support from friends and family.

and Case Studies

My friend's son was having trouble sleeping and was feeling anxious before going to school. His mother noticed but didn't want to upset him by talking about it. Eventually, she decided to speak to him in a gentle way, asking how he was feeling. He opened up and shared his worries about school. Together, they found a solution and were able to address the issue before it became a bigger problem.

In another case study, a school provided free counseling services to its students. Students who utilized these services showed a decrease in mental health symptoms such as anxiety and depression. This is one example of how providing resources and support can make a positive impact on a child's mental health.

Conclusion

  1. Start the Conversation: As a parent, it's important to initiate the conversation about your child's mental health.
  2. Listen and Validate: Make sure your child knows you are there to listen and support them without judgement.
  3. Provide Resources and Take Action: Identify resources and ways to support your child following the conversation.
