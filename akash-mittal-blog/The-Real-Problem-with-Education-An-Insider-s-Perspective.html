The Real Problem with Education: An Insider's Perspective

Published in The Daily Star

As an educator with over a decade of experience, I have seen firsthand the numerous challenges that students, parents, and teachers face when it comes to education. From outdated curriculums and underfunded schools to inadequate resources and overcrowded classrooms, it's no secret that there are many obstacles that make it difficult for students to excel academically.

But as the title suggests, the problem is not with coaching centres or ChatGPT. While these institutions may provide some assistance to students, they are not the root of the problem. To truly understand the challenges facing education today, we need to look deeper.

Let me tell you a story about one of my students, Jamie. Jamie was a bright and eager student who was always willing to learn. However, as the years went by, I noticed that Jamie was starting to struggle in class. She was falling behind in her studies and was becoming increasingly frustrated.

After several conversations with Jamie, I discovered that she was facing numerous personal challenges outside of school. Her parents were going through a divorce, she was being bullied by her classmates, and she was struggling with mental health issues.

It was then that I realized that education is not just about textbooks and exams. It's about providing a safe and supportive environment for students to learn and grow. And unfortunately, this is something that many educational institutions are failing to do.

According to a recent study by the National Center for Education Statistics, nearly one in four students report being bullied at school. This not only affects their academic performance but also their mental health and overall well-being.

In addition, schools with high poverty rates often have inadequate resources and outdated curriculums. This puts students at a disadvantage compared to their peers in more affluent areas. According to the National Assessment of Educational Progress, only 35% of fourth-grade students in high-poverty schools are proficient in reading, compared to 64% in low-poverty schools.

I remember one of my students, Sarah, who was struggling to keep up with the rest of the class. After several conversations with her, I discovered that she was dyslexic and had never received the proper support she needed. With some accommodations and extra help, Sarah was able to thrive academically and even graduated with honors.

Another student, David, had to drop out of school due to financial constraints. However, he was able to continue his studies online through various free resources and eventually went on to attend college.

1. Education is more than just academics.

Students need a safe and supportive environment to learn and grow. This includes providing resources for mental health support, addressing bullying and other social issues, and accommodating students with disabilities or other unique needs.

2. Education should be accessible to all students regardless of income or background.

It's not enough to simply provide textbooks and exams. Schools must also have adequate resources and modern curriculums to ensure that all students have an equal opportunity to succeed.

3. We need to invest in our teachers.

Teachers are the backbone of our education system. Yet, they are often overworked, underpaid, and undervalued. We need to provide better support and training for our teachers and ensure that they have the resources they need to succeed.
