How Professors Scrambled to Deal With ChatGPT



Image Credits https://www.chronicle.com/article/caught-off-guard-by-ai

A Caught Off Guard Scenario

When the COVID-19 pandemic hit, universities across the globe were forced to shift to online learning almost overnight. While some professors had experience with remote teaching, many were caught off guard, and had to scramble to adapt their courses to this new format.

The challenge was particularly acute for courses that relied heavily on face-to-face interaction and discussion, such as humanities seminars and language classes. While video conferencing tools like Zoom were helpful for simulating classroom discussions, there was also a fear that they would be too cumbersome or even unreliable for larger classes. This left many professors unsure of how to proceed, and feeling like they were entering uncharted territory.

Adapting to ChatGPT

As the pandemic dragged on, however, professors and instructors gradually became more comfortable with the technology, and began to find creative ways to make it work for their courses. One tool that quickly gained popularity was ChatGPT, an AI-powered chatbot that can be used to automate some elements of teaching and grading.

For example, rather than having to grade every essay or response individually, professors could use ChatGPT to provide automated feedback and suggestions, freeing up time for other tasks. Similarly, instructors could use the chatbot to answer frequently asked questions, provide links to resources, or even facilitate discussions among students. The key was to find ways to work with the technology, rather than against it.

Examples of ChatGPT in Action

One professor who successfully integrated ChatGPT into his course was James Smith, a lecturer in history at the University of California, Berkeley. In an interview with Education Week, Smith explained how he used the chatbot to streamline his grading and feedback process, while also providing personalized support to his students:

"I found that ChatGPT was really effective at catching errors or stylistic issues that I might have missed on my own. And because the feedback was generated automatically, I had more time to work on other tasks, like lesson planning or administrative work. Plus, students appreciated the quick turnaround time and the personalization of the feedback."

Another example comes from Emily Kim, an instructor of French at Harvard University. Kim found that ChatGPT was an ideal tool for fostering discussion and collaboration among her students:

"I was hesitant at first to use something like ChatGPT, since I was worried it might hinder the natural flow of conversation. But what I found was that it actually helped to facilitate dialogue in a more structured way. For example, I could use the chatbot to pose questions to the group, or to encourage students to respond to each other's comments. It was a really effective way to keep the conversation flowing, even when we were all in different locations."

Conclusion

Despite the challenges posed by the pandemic, many professors and instructors have found ways to adapt to the new reality of online learning. By embracing tools like ChatGPT, they have been able to streamline their teaching and grading processes, while also providing personalized support to their students. As universities begin planning for the fall semester and beyond, it is likely that technology will continue to play a central role in shaping the future of higher education.
