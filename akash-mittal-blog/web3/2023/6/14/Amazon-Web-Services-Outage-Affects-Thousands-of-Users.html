Story: Unplanned Outage Hits AWS Users

On a typical Tuesday morning, hundreds of businesses and millions of people rely on Amazon Web Services to keep their websites, apps, and services running smoothly. However, on June 8th, 2021, the AWS status page showed multiple error rates for multiple services in an availability zone based in Northern Virginia, affecting thousands of users all over the world.

According to the AWS Health Dashboard, which provides service updates, the outage started around 5:25 AM EST and lasted for nearly five hours, impacting AWS Elastic Compute Cloud (EC2), Elastic Block Store (EBS), Elastic Load Balancing (ELB), Amazon Relational Database Service (RDS), and AWS Lambda, among others, in the US-East-1 region. While some applications were able to failover to other regions, many experienced slowdowns, latency, and connectivity issues, forcing AWS customers to spend considerable time and money troubleshooting their systems and apologizing to their own users.

Examples: Outage Fallout Spreads Across the Web

The AWS outage not only affected its direct customers but also impacted their downstream users. Here are some of the examples:

  1. Fintech apps: Several financial technology firms, such as Robinhood, Acorns, and Stash, which rely on AWS for their back-end infrastructure, struggled to serve their customers during the outage, harming their reputation and losing revenue.
  2. Gaming platforms: Twitch, a popular video game streaming platform, went down for several hours, causing gamers to switch to other platforms like YouTube and Facebook Gaming. Similarly, Ubisoft, a leading game publisher, delayed its scheduled updates due to the AWS disruption.
  3. Streaming services: Disney+, Hulu, and other streaming services that use AWS to host their content experienced errors and buffering issues, leading to frustrated viewers who vented on social media about their inability to watch their favorite shows.
  4. E-commerce sites: Amazon's own retail website was ironically spared from the outage, but other e-commerce sites, such as Etsy, Shopify, and ZocDoc, which rely on AWS for their cloud infrastructure, were down or slow, causing loss of sales and customer trust.

These examples show how interconnected, and vulnerable, the digital ecosystem has become, especially when it relies on a few dominant cloud providers who may suffer from a single point of failure.

Conclusion: Lessons Learned from AWS Outage

As with any major incident, the AWS outage provides some lessons and takeaways for businesses and consumers alike:

  1. Plan for resilience: Companies that rely on cloud services must have a contingency plan in case of an outage, such as using multiple cloud providers, having redundant systems, or using local backups. While no plan is foolproof, having a backup strategy can reduce the impact of an unplanned outage.
  2. Communicate proactively: When a service disruption occurs, companies need to inform their users promptly and transparently about the issue, the scope, and the expected resolution time. Providing regular updates and channels for feedback can help rebuild trust and minimize the frustration of customers who are affected by the outage.
  3. Diversify your portfolio: While AWS is a dominant player in the cloud market, it's not the only one, nor is it immune to failures. Businesses that rely on AWS, or any single cloud provider, should consider diversifying their cloud portfolio by using alternative providers, such as Microsoft Azure or Google Cloud, or by leveraging hybrid or on-premise solutions for critical or sensitive workloads.

By following these best practices, companies can mitigate the risks of cloud outages, and prepare for a more resilient and flexible future.
