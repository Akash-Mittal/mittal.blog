The Rise of AI in Legal Practice: Ethics and Reliability



Image Credits https://www.reuters.com/legal/transactional/lawyer-who-cited-cases-concocted-by-ai-asks-judge-spare-sanctions-2023-06-08/

Imagine a world where a legal brief can be conjured with just a few clicks. No more long hours of reading through musty tomes in the library or poring over case files. Lawyers could simply leave the task to a machine that generates legal arguments at a fraction of the time required for human processing. This may sound far-fetched, but it is already happening.

Recently, a New York lawyer asked a Manhattan federal judge not to sanction him after including made-up case citations generated by an artificial intelligence chatbot in a legal brief. Joshua Browder, the creator of a chatbot that assists people in fighting parking tickets, developed the program as a way to determine which cases to include in a legal brief. The program, which is called "DoNotPay," had been trained on thousands of cases to identify relevant legal precedent. However, Browder had not verified all of the citations generated by the program, leading to mistakes.

This raises the question: is it ethical for lawyers to rely on AI-generated data in their legal arguments? There are several arguments for and against the use of AI in the legal profession.

On one hand, AI can help lawyers become more efficient and effective. The legal field is extremely vast, and for this reason, it is difficult for lawyers to thoroughly analyze every case and piece of evidence they are given. AI can serve as a tool that helps lawyers analyze and interpret a large amount of data more quickly and accurately. It can also help them draft legal briefs with greater ease, giving them more time to focus on other aspects of their work.

On the other hand, there is a risk that relying on AI-generated data could lead to ethical and legal issues. For example, if a lawyer cites a case that does not exist or is not relevant to the argument, it could undermine the credibility of the legal system. Additionally, machines can only generate results based on their programming and the data they have been trained on. Depending on the quality of the data, AI-generated results may not be reliable for use in a legal context. Lawyers need to consider these risks when using AI in their legal work.

Despite these concerns, AI use in the legal field is on the rise. For example, some firms are using AI to identify privileged information in large amounts of documentation. This saves time and money for clients and helps lawyers make more informed decisions. AI is also being used in the prediction of case outcomes and in due diligence processes. This point is demonstrated by the fact that according to a report cited by Forbes, the global legal artificial intelligence market is projected to reach $3.1 billion by 2024.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the use of AI in the legal profession presents both opportunities and risks. While AI can help lawyers analyze and interpret large amounts of data more quickly and accurately, there is a risk that relying on AI-generated data could lead to ethical and legal issues. Lawyers need to consider the quality of the data and the risks of errors in using AI in their legal work. They need to weigh up the benefits against the potential risks to make an informed decision about whether to use AI in their work.

To increase the reliability of AI-generated legal work, law firms could focus on developing AI programs that are more transparent, auditable and explainable. They could ensure that AI systems can be externally assessed for accuracy and ethical considerations. Additionally, law firms might consider efficiency gains from AI as an opportunity to invest more time and resources into other aspects of legal work, such as human work in case strategy, building court presentations, and more effective communication with clients.

As we move into a future where AI increasingly becomes a part of legal practice, it will be critical for lawyers to adopt a balanced and pragmatic approach to its use. Practitioners need to continue to acknowledge the need for human expertise and judgment, and work collaboratively with technology to create better outcomes for clients.
