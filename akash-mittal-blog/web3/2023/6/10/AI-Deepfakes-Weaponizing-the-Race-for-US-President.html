The Rise of Deepfakes in US Politics

In the 2020 US presidential election, disinformation and misinformation played a big role in shaping public opinion. One of the most concerning forms of disinformation is deepfakes, which are videos or images created with artificial intelligence (AI) to make it look like someone is saying or doing something they never did.

Although deepfakes have been around for a few years, their use in politics is becoming more pervasive. Political campaigns and interest groups are using deepfakes to spread false or misleading information to voters.

Recently, the campaign for GOP presidential candidate Donald Trump's rival tweeted out a video containing fake images of Trump hugging and kissing Dr Anthony Fauci, the director of the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases, who has been critical of Trump's handling of the COVID-19 pandemic.

This is just one example of how deepfakes are being weaponized in the race for the US presidency, and it's likely to become more common in future elections.

The Dangers of Deepfakes in Politics

The use of deepfakes in politics is dangerous for several reasons. Firstly, it undermines the trust that people have in information sources. If people can no longer trust what they see or hear, it becomes difficult to make informed decisions about who to vote for or what policies to support.

Secondly, deepfakes can be used to spread false or misleading information that can damage a candidate's reputation or hurt their chances of winning an election. This is particularly concerning in an era of social media, where information can spread quickly and have a big impact on public opinion.

Finally, deepfakes can be used to sow discord and division in society. By creating fake videos or images that appear to show one candidate saying or doing something controversial, political opponents can use deepfakes to stoke outrage and hatred among voters.

What Can Be Done to Combat Deepfakes in Politics?

As deepfakes become more sophisticated and more common in politics, it's important to take steps to combat their spread. Here are a few ways we can do that:

  • Invest in AI technology: One way to combat deepfakes is to invest in AI technology that can detect and flag fake images and videos. There are already several companies working on developing such technology.
  • Hold social media platforms accountable: Social media platforms like Facebook and Twitter have a responsibility to ensure that their users are not spreading false or misleading information. They can do this by introducing more rigorous fact-checking standards and algorithms for detecting deepfakes.
  • Encourage media literacy: Media literacy is a crucial skill in an era of disinformation. By teaching people how to identify deepfakes and other forms of disinformation, we can empower them to make informed decisions based on accurate information.

At the end of the day, deepfakes are a dangerous and concerning development in politics. It's up to all of us to stay vigilant and take steps to prevent their spread.
