What is Next-Generation Sequencing?

Next-generation sequencing (NGS) is a powerful tool that allows scientists to read and analyze the genetic code of cancer cells. By understanding the specific mutations and genetic alterations that are driving cancer growth, doctors can develop targeted therapies that are more effective and less toxic than traditional chemotherapy.

To perform NGS, doctors take a sample of the patient's tumor tissue and sequence its DNA. They then compare this sequence to a reference genome to identify any mutations or alterations that are present. This information can then be used to select the most appropriate therapy for each patient.

How Can Next-Generation Sequencing Help?

Next-generation sequencing can help in several ways, including:

  • Identifying driver oncogenes: NGS can help identify the specific mutations and genetic alterations that are driving cancer growth. This information can be used to select targeted therapies that are more effective and less toxic than traditional chemotherapy.
  • Guiding treatment decisions: NGS reports can provide doctors with valuable information about which therapies are most effective for each patient. This can help reduce unnecessary treatments and side effects.
  • Monitoring treatment response: By sequencing a patient's tumor tissue at different points in their treatment, doctors can monitor how well the therapy is working and adjust the treatment plan as needed.

Next-generation sequencing has already helped many patients with non-small cell lung cancer. One example is a patient named Jane, who was diagnosed with non-small cell lung cancer that had spread to her lymph nodes. Her doctor recommended NGS, which revealed that she had a specific mutation called EGFR. Based on this information, her doctor prescribed a targeted therapy called osimertinib. After just six weeks of treatment, Jane's tumor had shrunk significantly, and she experienced fewer side effects than with traditional chemotherapy.

Another example is a patient named John, who had exhausted all standard treatments for his non-small cell lung cancer. His doctor recommended NGS, which identified a rare mutation called ROS1. Based on this information, his doctor prescribed a targeted therapy called crizotinib. After just two months of treatment, John's tumor had shrunk significantly, and he was able to resume many of his normal activities.
