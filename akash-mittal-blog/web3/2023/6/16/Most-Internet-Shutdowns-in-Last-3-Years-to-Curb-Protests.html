A Story of Internet Shutdowns

On a peaceful evening of June 2020, James, a young student in Sudan, was sitting in front of his computer trying to keep up with his studies. Suddenly, his internet connection was lost. He tried to reconnect, but to no avail. He checked with his friends and learned that the entire country's internet was shut down to prevent the spread of anti-government protests. James felt frustrated, helpless, and angry. He realized that the internet was not just a tool for communication but a fundamental human right that must not be taken away arbitrarily.

Examples of Internet Shutdowns

  • India has witnessed the most internet shutdowns globally, with 109 documented cases in 2019 alone, according to a report by the Software Freedom Law Center. The shutdowns were mainly used to curb protests and unrest in regions such as Kashmir, Assam, and Uttar Pradesh.
  • Iran has also resorted to internet shutdowns to quell nationwide protests over fuel price hikes, leaving millions of people without access to information and communication.
  • In Zimbabwe, the government shut down the internet for five days in January 2019 to prevent protests over the skyrocketing fuel prices and the deteriorating economic situation. The shutdown disrupted businesses, education, and health services and resulted in multiple human rights violations.
  • In Egypt, the government shut down the internet for the entire country during the 2011 revolution that ousted Hosni Mubarak. The move was intended to thwart protesters' ability to organize and disseminate information, but it only fueled their anger and determination to bring about change.

Why Internet Shutdowns are Counterproductive

Internet shutdowns violate multiple human rights, including freedom of expression, access to information, and freedom of assembly. They also harm the economy, disrupt education, healthcare, and emergency services, and fuel distrust and resentment towards the government. Moreover, shutdowns are often ineffective in achieving their intended goals, as protesters find alternative ways to communicate and spread their message, and the international community condemns the government's actions.

  • If the goal is to prevent violence or unrest, internet shutdowns may backfire by exacerbating tensions and causing more frustration and anger.
  • If the goal is to control the narrative and suppress dissent, internet shutdowns only make the government appear weak, repressive, and out of touch with its citizens' needs and aspirations.
  • If the goal is to limit exposure to harmful content or prevent cyberattacks, there are other, less damaging ways to address these issues that do not infringe on people's rights and freedoms.

Conclusion

Internet shutdowns are becoming more prevalent worldwide, despite their negative impact on human rights, democracy, and development. Governments must realize that the internet is not a luxury but a basic necessity for people to exercise their rights and participate in society. They should refrain from using shutdowns as a blunt instrument to suppress dissent and instead engage in dialogue with their citizens, address their legitimate grievances, and respect their autonomy and dignity.

  1. Internet shutdowns violate human rights and harm society.
  2. Internet shutdowns are ineffective in achieving their intended goals.
  3. Internet shutdowns must be avoided, and alternatives must be sought.
