Have you ever searched for a product online and then suddenly see ads for that product everywhere you go online? Or do you sometimes find that websites you visit remember your login information even after you close your browser? If yes, then you have probably encountered HTTP cookies. An HTTP cookie (also known as a web cookie, browser cookie, or simply a cookie) is a small text file that a website or web application saves on the user's computer or mobile device when they visit the site. Cookies are designed to be a reliable mechanism for websites to remember stateful information (such as items added to a shopping cart in an online store) or to record the user's browsing activity (such as which pages they visited and when). When a user visits a website that uses cookies, the website sends a cookie to the user's browser. The browser stores the cookie on the user's computer or mobile device. The next time the user visits the same website, the site reads the cookie and recognizes the user. This allows the website to remember the user's preferences or login information, and personalize the user's experience. Cookies are not harmful to the user's computer or mobile device. They cannot be used to identify you personally, and they cannot carry viruses or install malware on your computer. However, the use of cookies has raised concerns about online privacy and online tracking. There are two main types of cookies: session cookies and persistent cookies. Session cookies are temporary cookies that are stored on the user's computer or mobile device only during a browsing session. They are deleted from the user's device when the browser is closed. Persistent cookies, on the other hand, are stored on the user's device after the browsing session has ended. They remain on the device until they expire or are manually deleted by the user. Cookies work by sending and receiving information between the user's browser and the website's server. When the user visits a website, the website's server sends a cookie to the user's browser. The browser stores the cookie and sends it back to the server with every subsequent request. This allows the website to identify the user and provide personalized information or functionality. Cookies can also be used to track the user's browsing activity across multiple websites. This is done by setting a third-party cookie, which is a cookie that is set by a domain other than the one the user is browsing. Third-party cookies are often used by advertisers and analytics companies to track the user's online behavior and target them with personalized ads. Cookies can have a significant impact on the information you see online. By using cookies, websites can personalize the content that is displayed to you based on your browsing history and preferences. For example, a news website might use cookies to show you articles that are relevant to your interests, based on the content you have clicked on in the past. However, cookies can also be used to manipulate the information you see online. Advertisers can use cookies to track your browsing activity and target you with ads that are more likely to be relevant to you.
