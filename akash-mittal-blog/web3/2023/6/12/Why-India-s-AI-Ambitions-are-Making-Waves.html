Why India's AI Ambitions are Making Waves

Recently, Ankur Warikoo, the founder of ed-tech platform Nearbuy and home services start-up SacredHawks, landed himself in hot water after making controversial remarks about Indian Artificial Intelligence (AI) models. During a podcast with chatbot start-up Gupshup's co-founder Beerud Sheth, Warikoo implied that India is not capable of building its own AI models, and that the country will always be a consumer rather than a producer of technology.

Warikoo's comments have been met with backlash, with many experts and industry leaders taking to social media to challenge his claims. One such figure was Union Minister of State for Education Sanjay Dhotre, who hit back at Warikoo on Twitter, stating that India has a wealth of talent in the AI space and is fast becoming a major player on the global stage.

The Current Landscape of AI in India

While Warikoo's remarks may have been misguided and overly critical of India's technological potential, they have brought attention to the current landscape of AI in the country. As it stands, India is one of the fastest-growing AI markets in the world, with a projected growth rate of 45% in the next five years.

One of the reasons for this rapid expansion is the Indian government's push to encourage innovation and entrepreneurship in AI. Initiatives such as the Make in India campaign, launched in 2014 to promote domestic manufacturing, and the National AI Strategy, unveiled in 2018 to establish India as a leader in AI research, have given a boost to the country's tech ecosystem.

The Role of Start-ups in India's AI Growth

Another key factor in India's AI growth is the role of start-ups. India is home to a vibrant start-up ecosystem, with over 30 unicorns (start-ups valued at over $1 billion) and thousands of new companies cropping up every year. Many of these start-ups are focused on AI and are helping to drive the country's innovation in the space.

One of the most notable of these is Bengaluru-based firm, Niramai, which has developed an AI-powered breast cancer screening tool that has been shown to be more accurate than traditional mammograms. Another is Sequoia-backed fintech start-up Lendingkart, which uses machine learning algorithms to provide small business loans in minutes.

The Challenges Facing India's AI Sector

Despite the growth and innovation, there are still challenges facing India's AI sector. One of the biggest is the lack of talent. While India has a large pool of engineers and IT professionals, there is a shortage of those with the skills and training in AI, which has led to fierce competition for top talent.

Another challenge is the lack of funding and investment. While India has seen a surge in start-up activity, funding for AI start-ups still lags behind other countries such as China and the US. This has led to many Indian start-ups being acquired by foreign firms rather than growing and expanding locally.

Conclusion

Despite the challenges, India's AI ecosystem is growing and has the potential to become a major global player in the space. With the government's focus on encouraging innovation and entrepreneurship, and the country's vibrant start-up ecosystem, India is well positioned to build its own AI models and contribute to the global AI industry.

As the country continues to make strides in AI, it is important that individuals such as Ankur Warikoo acknowledge the progress being made and support the growth of the industry rather than spreading misguided criticism.
