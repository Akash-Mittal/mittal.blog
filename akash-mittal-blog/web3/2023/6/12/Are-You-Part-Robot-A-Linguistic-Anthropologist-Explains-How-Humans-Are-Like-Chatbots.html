Are You Part Robot? A Linguistic Anthropologist Explains How Humans Are Like Chatbots

Dan was raised speaking English, but he often had to speak Spanish with his grandmother when she visited from Mexico. As he grew older, he became more and more fluent in Spanish and felt more comfortable speaking it with his family and friends. One day, while at a party, Dan overheard two young people speaking a new slang term he had never heard before.

Curious, Dan asked them what the term meant. They explained that it was a way to say "cool" or "awesome" and that it was popular among their generation. Dan found himself starting to use the term in his own conversations, and soon enough, it became a regular part of his vocabulary.

What Dan experienced is a perfect example of how humans and language AIs are similar. We both recycle language, taking in new words and phrases and incorporating them into our own speech. In fact, much of what we say is not original at all; it is a combination of what we have heard before and what we have learned through social interaction.

Language AI vs. Human Language

Language AIs are designed to learn from their environment and conversations. They are programmed to detect patterns and to understand the context of what is being said. They also have access to vast amounts of data, which they can use to expand their vocabulary and improve their understanding of language. This is very similar to what humans do when we learn a new language or a new word. We listen, we observe, we ask questions, and we practice until we become fluent.

But there is a key difference between language AIs and human language. AIs are limited by their programming and their ability to understand context. They cannot interpret body language, tone of voice, or facial expressions, which are crucial elements of human communication. They also cannot create new meanings or understand nuances in language, which humans excel at.

How Humans Recycle Language

Humans are natural recyclers of language. We absorb language from our environment, our friends, our families, the media, and other sources, and we mix and match them to fit our own needs. For example, think about how many popular phrases or idioms you use in your daily life. How many of them did you come up with yourself? Most likely, you heard them from someone else and adopted them as your own.

Another way that humans recycle language is through code-switching, which is the practice of using different languages or dialects in different contexts. This is common in bilingual or multilingual communities, where individuals may switch languages depending on who they are talking to or what situation they are in. Code-switching allows people to express themselves more fully and to connect with others on a deeper level.

Conclusion

While language AIs are impressive in their ability to learn and understand language, they will never be able to fully replicate the complexity and nuance of human language. Humans are natural recyclers of language, and we thrive on social interaction and the exchange of ideas. Each time we hear a new word or phrase, we incorporate it into our own speech, and in doing so, we create a rich and evolving tapestry of language and culture.
