The Lawyer Who Trusted AI But Wasn't Its Biggest Fool

By John Naughton

Once upon a time, there was a lawyer who had a case that required extensive research of legal precedents and statutes. He had heard about AI technology that could quickly and efficiently gather and analyze data for him, saving him a lot of time and effort. Confident in his abilities as a lawyer and his familiarity with technology, he decided to give it a try.

He found a reputable AI platform called ChatGPT, which promised to deliver accurate and relevant legal research with minimal human input. He entered his queries and waited for the results.

To his surprise, the results were indeed impressive. ChatGPT had gathered a vast amount of data, organized it into categories, and highlighted relevant information that matched his queries. Moreover, it had done so in a matter of minutes, a task that would have taken him or his paralegals hours or even days to accomplish.

"I was amazed by how fast and accurate the results were," he said. "I felt like I had discovered a new world of legal research that could revolutionize the way we practice law."

But then, he decided to test the AI platform further. He asked it to provide him with specific case citations and explanations, expecting the same level of accuracy and comprehension it had shown before. But this time, the results were less satisfactory. Some of the cases it had provided were outdated or irrelevant, and some of the explanations were unclear or incomplete.

The lawyer realized that while AI technology could certainly assist him in his research, it was not a replacement for human intelligence and intuition. He understood that he needed to complement the AI's findings with his own judgment and expertise, and that he needed to be aware of its limitations and biases.

Despite this experience, the lawyer continued to use AI platforms such as ChatGPT for his research, but with a more critical and discerning eye. He found that AI technology could be particularly useful for:

  • Scanning massive amounts of data in a short time
  • Providing general overviews and trends in legal issues
  • Identifying key terms and concepts in legal language
  • Filtering irrelevant or redundant information
  • Notifying updates or changes in legal regulations

However, he also noticed some of the limitations and challenges of AI technology in legal research, such as:

  • Lack of contextual understanding and interpretation
  • Difficulty in determining the credibility and relevance of sources
  • Tendency to reinforce biases and stereotypes
  • Risk of ethical and privacy violations in data collection and use
  • Potential for errors, inaccuracies, and oversights

Nevertheless, the lawyer learned to balance the benefits and risks of AI technology in his legal practice. He also realized that he needed to develop his own digital literacy skills and stay updated on the latest AI developments and regulations, not only for research but also for litigation, negotiation, and client communication.

In conclusion, the lawyer's experience with AI technology taught him three valuable lessons:

  1. AI technology can be a powerful tool for legal research, but it should be used with caution and critical thinking.
  2. AI technology can never replace human intelligence and judgment in legal practice, which requires interpretation, analysis, and advocacy.
  3. AI technology requires ethical and legal considerations in its development, implementation, and use, to ensure that it benefits society as a whole and doesn't create new forms of injustice or inequality.

The lawyer also recognized the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration and knowledge sharing between legal experts, technologists, and policymakers, to uphold the rule of law and protect human rights in the age of AI.
