The Rise of Artificial Intelligence in the US-Mexico Border



Image Credits https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2023/6/9/artificial-intelligence-without-borders

"How do they know?", I ask myself every time I attempt to cross the US-Mexico border. The scrutinizing gaze of border patrol agents and the latest technology they use to track my every move never cease to amaze me. As much as I respect their job, I sometimes feel like AI is on their side.

The US-Mexico border ranks as the busiest land border in the world, making it a prime spot for the homeland security industry. With the Trump administration's crackdown on illegal immigration, the border has become a dynamic testing ground for artificial intelligence and machine learning technologies.

From autonomous surveillance drones and AI-assisted lie detectors to facial recognition cameras and advanced analytics software, the border-industrial complex has joined the AI game. Here are three examples of how AI is being used to up the ante at the border.

Facial Recognition Technology

Using facial recognition technology, agents can identify and track individuals crossing land, sea, and air borders. Customs and Border Protection has installed facial recognition cameras in more than a dozen airports in the US to scan the faces of US citizens leaving the country. Moreover, the agency has started trialing facial recognition cameras in the southern border to speed up processing and provide better security.

However, this technology has its drawbacks. Critics argue that facial recognition is prone to errors, particularly when it comes to matching people of color or with a different gender. Moreover, it raises serious privacy concerns as it can be used for tracking individuals without their consent or knowledge.

Autonomous Drones

Drones have become a vital tool for border patrol, as they can reach remote areas with limited human resources and cover large swaths of land. Thanks to AI, drones can now operate autonomously and detect suspicious activities such as illegal border crossings, drug trafficking, and human smuggling.

Autonomous drones can navigate their way through challenging terrain, analyze data in real-time, and communicate with other drones to coordinate responses to potential threats. However, they also raise concerns about drone attacks and violations of privacy rights as they can capture images of people and property without permission.

Lie Detection Technology

Lie detectors have been around for a while, but AI has made them more efficient and accurate. An AI-assisted system called Avatar is being tested at the Nogales border crossing in Arizona. The system uses an infrared camera and a microphone to analyze changes in an individual's facial expressions and voice, indicating whether they are telling the truth or lying.

Proponents of the technology argue that it can help agents better identify criminals and individuals involved in illegal activities. However, critics say that it is subjective and prone to errors, and that it could lead to profiling and discrimination against certain groups.

Conclusion

The use of artificial intelligence in the US-Mexico border is a double-edged sword. On the one hand, the technology has the potential to enhance security and improve processing times. On the other hand, it raises serious concerns about privacy, bias, and human rights violations.

As AI technology advances, it is essential to ensure that its use at the border complies with ethical standards and human rights principles. Border control agencies should also be transparent about what technologies they use and how they use them. As for me, I hope that one day I won't have to wonder how they know.
